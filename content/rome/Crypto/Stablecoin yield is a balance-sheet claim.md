Stablecoin yield is not interest emitted by a token: it is a contractual or protocol-mediated claim on income from reserves, borrowers, trading strategies, or subsidies, with risks determined by the balance sheet and redemption path behind it.

Up: [[Crypto]]

## Place in the map

This note supplies the yield layer beneath [[Stablecoin reserve and redemption risk]] and operationalizes [[Token emissions and real yield]]. The reserve note asks whether a stable liability can reach par cash; this note asks what additional claim is created when a holder tries to earn on that liability. It also applies [[Wrapped assets and duplicate crypto exposure]] to savings wrappers, lending receipts, exchange balances, and tokenized securities built above a base stablecoin.

“A dollar that earns yield” sounds like a single product category. It is not. The phrase can describe a non-yielding stablecoin held on an exchange that pays a promotional reward, a lending receipt backed by loans, a vault share that appreciates as protocol revenue accumulates, a tokenized money-market fund, a registered debt security, or a crypto strategy that harvests perpetual-futures funding. These instruments may all quote a dollar price and an annual percentage rate. Economically and legally, they are different claims.

The right first question is therefore not “What is the APY?” It is **who owes the holder what, and what activity produces the cash flow?** The second question is whether the holder can turn the position into par dollars on demand. The third is what can interrupt that conversion: asset losses, maturity mismatch, counterparty failure, custody, legal restrictions, smart-contract failure, or simply a crowded exit.

This article develops a balance-sheet method for answering those questions. It complements [[Stablecoin reserve and redemption risk]], which analyzes the par promise itself, and [[Token emissions and real yield]], which separates external income from token dilution. It is an analytical framework, not a recommendation to acquire or deposit any product.

## A yield must have a payer

A stable token does not create income merely by remaining near one dollar. Someone must pay. In simplified form, the holder's gross yield is drawn from one or more of five sources:

$$
Y = R_{reserve}+R_{credit}+R_{trading}+R_{fees}+R_{subsidy}-C,
$$

where reserve income comes from cash, Treasury bills, repo, or other securities; credit income comes from borrowers; trading income includes basis and perpetual-funding strategies; fees come from payments or protocol activity; subsidies come from an issuer, exchange, treasury, or token emissions; and $C$ includes operating costs, losses, hedging, distribution fees, and retained margin. The equation is accounting, not valuation. Two products can display the same yield while taking radically different risks in the terms on the right-hand side.

The Bank for International Settlements distinguishes reserve-based remuneration from activity-based remuneration on centralized exchanges. In the first, an exchange or issuer shares some return generated by reserve assets; rates tend to track monetary-policy rates. In the second, the platform funds rewards from lending, market-making, trading, and other activities; rates can be much more volatile. This distinction is more useful than the marketing label because it identifies the balance sheet bearing the risk.

The common fiat-backed stablecoin shows why yield allocation matters. When an issuer receives dollars for tokens and invests the reserve in short Treasury bills, repo, a government money-market fund, or bank deposits, those assets produce interest. The ordinary tokenholder may have a par redemption claim but no claim to that interest. The issuer and its distribution partners can retain it. A platform may voluntarily pass part of it to selected customers, but then the reward depends on the platform's terms, eligibility rules, and continued commercial decision—not necessarily on the token contract or issuer's redemption obligation.

Calling the reward “native” does not solve the problem. It only means the income is reflected in the instrument without a separate manual deposit. The source can still be an issuer's securities portfolio, a protocol's loan book, or a derivative trade. Native distribution reduces one layer of user action; it does not abolish credit, duration, liquidity, or legal risk.

## The token and the yield claim may be separate

Before evaluating risk, identify the exact position. A wallet balance of USDC, USDT, USDS, sUSDS, USDe, sUSDe, an exchange “earn” balance, and a tokenized Treasury share are not interchangeable versions of digital cash.

Circle's USDC terms illustrate the baseline issuer claim, but the cited terms expressly apply outside the European Economic Area; EEA holders are directed to a separate MiCA white paper. Under the cited terms, only an eligible holder with a Circle Mint account in good standing can redeem directly with Circle, subject to account, compliance, legal, and fee conditions. A wallet holder without an eligible Circle Mint account may have to sell or transfer USDC rather than redeem with the issuer. The terms also say USDC is not designed to generate returns and that holders are not entitled to interest earned on reserves. If an exchange pays a USDC reward, the user must read the exchange agreement to determine whether the stablecoin remains customer property, is lent, is pledged, or becomes a general obligation of the platform.

A lending market changes the claim. The depositor transfers or supplies tokens to a pool and receives an accounting balance or receipt token. Borrowers pay interest, and utilization influences the rate. The depositor's economics now depend on collateral valuation, liquidations, oracle integrity, smart contracts, withdrawal liquidity, bad-debt allocation, and governance. The underlying stablecoin's reserve quality remains relevant, but it is only the first layer. The lender owns a claim on a lending arrangement whose asset is borrower debt.

A savings wrapper changes it differently. Sky's sUSDS is an ERC-4626 vault representation of USDS supplied to the Sky Savings Rate. The share value accrues rather than each unit remaining fixed at one dollar. The holder has a protocol claim whose value depends on Sky's accounting, asset and liability management, governance, contracts, and the continued capacity to fund the savings rate. Calling both USDS and sUSDS stablecoins can hide the transformation: one is the base stable liability; the other is a yield-bearing vault share denominated through that liability.

A security creates still another claim. Figure's YLDS materials explicitly call the instrument a registered fixed-income security rather than a stablecoin. Each certificate has a $0.01 face amount, the variable yield references SOFR less a spread, and redemption depends on Figure Certificate Company's ability to meet an unsecured debt obligation backed solely by its assets. Those descriptions are issuer terms, not an independent conclusion about asset sufficiency: Figure's own disclosure warns that asset losses could leave it unable to pay principal or interest. The relevant analysis resembles short-duration credit and securities analysis: issuer assets, priority, registration, transfer restrictions, valuation, eligibility, and prospectus terms. SEC registration supplies disclosure and a legal framework; it is not a government guarantee and does not remove issuer credit risk.

A tokenized money-market or Treasury fund is generally a fund share, not an issuer's dollar-payment token. The portfolio may consist largely of government paper and cash, but the holder owns an investment security subject to fund documents, fees, transfer-agent records, subscription and redemption procedures, permitted-investor rules, custody, and market hours. A stable net asset value or one-dollar transfer price should not be mistaken for an unconditional, continuously redeemable bank balance.

The practical rule is simple: write the legal noun before the yield—**token redemption claim, platform liability, loan-pool receipt, vault share, debt certificate, or fund share**. If the noun is unclear, the risk analysis has not started.

## Four balance-sheet models

### Reserve pass-through

In a reserve pass-through model, short-duration reserve assets generate income and some portion reaches holders. The stylized balance sheet has cash and securities on the asset side, with redeemable tokens or yield-bearing certificates on the liability side. The key variables are the gross portfolio yield, retained spread, operating cost, credit quality, duration, custody, and redemption liquidity.

This model can be relatively transparent, but “Treasury-backed” remains incomplete. A six-month bill has little credit risk but more duration and settlement exposure than same-day cash. A money-market fund share introduces a fund and transfer-agent layer. Repo introduces counterparties, collateral, haircuts, and settlement. Bank deposits introduce bank credit and access risk. If a product promises continuous redemption while its assets settle only during market and banking hours, it relies on prefunded cash, credit lines, dealers, or a delay provision.

The yield also falls when policy rates fall or when the issuer retains more of the spread. A displayed rate is not a coupon guaranteed for the token's life unless the contract says so and the issuer can pay it. Reserve pass-through is best understood as floating-rate cash management with a specific legal wrapper, not as a permanent crypto return.

### Onchain credit intermediation

In a lending model, deposited stablecoins finance borrowers. The asset side is a portfolio of overcollateralized or undercollateralized loans; the liability side is depositor withdrawals. Yield compensates for credit and liquidity risk, whether or not software automates the process.

Overcollateralization reduces expected credit loss only if collateral prices, oracles, liquidation incentives, blockchain capacity, and market depth perform together. A loan at a safe initial ratio can become bad debt during a gap move or chain outage. Under-collateralized institutional credit depends more directly on underwriting, covenants, legal enforcement, recovery, and concentration. In both forms, a high rate can signal scarce liquidity or elevated demand, but it can also signal that lenders require more compensation for danger.

Utilization creates a maturity-like mismatch even if every loan is callable or floating-rate. When most supplied cash is borrowed, a depositor may have to wait for repayment, new deposits, or liquidations. A protocol can be solvent at model prices and temporarily illiquid. The advertised APY should therefore be paired with utilization, available withdrawal liquidity, borrower concentration, collateral composition, bad debt, and the mechanism for socializing losses.

### Market-neutral trading income

Ethena provides a prominent example of strategy-backed stable-value exposure. Its documentation attributes protocol revenue to staked-asset rewards, perpetual funding, futures basis, and returns on liquid stable backing. USDe holders can stake into sUSDe, whose rewards are distributed through a vault-like mechanism after internal accounting. This is not equivalent to holding Treasury bills.

The core trade pairs crypto collateral with short derivatives intended to offset price delta. If perpetual longs pay shorts, the hedge earns funding; futures contango can also produce basis income. Yet “delta-neutral” refers to first-order price exposure, not to a risk-free balance sheet. The protocol remains exposed to exchange and custodian failure, collateral and derivative basis, funding turning negative, execution, liquidation or margin management, settlement, stablecoin backing, and smart contracts.

Ethena acknowledges funding risk and describes a reserve fund and dynamic allocation toward liquid stable assets during weak or negative funding. Those mitigants are economically meaningful, but they shift rather than erase the analytical question: how large and liquid is the buffer relative to plausible losses, and what happens if several dependencies fail together? Historical positive funding can be consumed by the strategy's own scale or reverse when market positioning changes. A smoothed APY distribution can also lag the period in which revenue was earned, so the displayed rate is not necessarily the current marginal opportunity.

### Subsidized distribution

Some rewards are commercial acquisition spending or token emissions. An exchange may pay selected users to attract balances; a protocol may distribute governance tokens; an issuer may share revenue temporarily. The balance sheet funding the reward can be a marketing budget rather than an earning asset.

Subsidies are not fictitious if the recipient can sell or redeem them, but they are not durable yield unless a durable payer exists. They may expire, be capped, vary by jurisdiction or customer tier, require lockups, or be paid in a volatile token. Annualizing a short campaign makes a finite transfer appear like recurring income. The audit should state the reward asset, vesting, eligibility, end date, funding pool, and dilution.

## Redemption is part of the return

Yield cannot be evaluated separately from exit. A position earning 8% but redeemable only after a seven-day queue is not equivalent to an instantly redeemable 4% position. Nor is a token transferable onchain necessarily liquid: transferability moves the claim to another wallet, while redemption extinguishes it against cash or underlying assets.

For each product, trace the full path:

$$
\text{position} \rightarrow \text{withdrawal request} \rightarrow \text{base token or asset} \rightarrow \text{eligible redeemer} \rightarrow \text{bank cash}.
$$

Every arrow can add a queue, fee, minimum, KYC check, jurisdictional restriction, market sale, blockchain confirmation, bridge, or counterparty. A vault share may first redeem into a stablecoin that itself must be redeemed. A tokenized fund may require an approved transfer-agent account. An exchange balance may be withdrawable only while the exchange and chain are operational. A third-party wrapper may add custody or bridge risk without giving the wrapper holder a direct claim on the original issuer.

Secondary liquidity is not a substitute for primary redemption. Selling near par works while another buyer is willing to assume the claim and arbitrageurs can reach the issuer. In stress, market depth can vanish before ultimate asset value is known. The March 2023 USDC depeg around Silicon Valley Bank demonstrated how uncertainty about access to reserve cash could transmit quickly across stablecoins and DeFi even though the final recovery was much better than the weekend market price implied. The Federal Reserve's later analysis shows that linked one-to-one facilities transmitted the shock into Dai as users drained USDC-facing liquidity.

The relevant return is therefore not the dashboard APY but the cash-on-cash result after exit cost and delay:

$$
R_{realized}=\frac{\text{cash received}+\text{distributions}-\text{cash invested}-\text{fees}}{\text{cash invested}},
$$

measured over the actual holding and redemption period. A small discount or long delay can consume months of quoted yield.

## The risks belong in separate columns

Yield products often compress risk into a single warning. A useful comparison separates at least five dimensions.

**Duration risk** arises when asset value changes with rates or cannot mature in time for redemptions. Short bills have less duration than long bonds; loans and withdrawal queues create effective duration even when their rates float.

**Credit risk** asks who can fail to pay: issuer, borrower, bank, repo counterparty, exchange, derivatives venue, fund, custodian, or bridge. Overcollateralization and reserve funds are buffers, not substitutes for identifying the obligor and loss waterfall.

**Liquidity risk** asks whether assets can become settlement cash at the promised speed and price. Market liquidity, funding liquidity, blockchain liquidity, and banking access are distinct. A solvent portfolio can still gate or delay withdrawals.

**Custody and operational risk** covers private keys, smart contracts, administrators, transfer agents, custodians, oracles, exchange accounts, banking rails, compliance systems, and governance keys. Self-custody of a token removes one wallet intermediary but does not self-custody the offchain Treasury bill, exchange hedge, or bank deposit supporting it.

**Legal-claim risk** asks whether the holder owns property, a beneficial interest, a secured or unsecured debt, a contractual redemption right, or merely an account claim against a platform. It also asks priority in insolvency, segregation, governing law, eligible-holder restrictions, freeze powers, and whether securities, banking, payments, or commodities rules apply. Regulatory status should be described precisely, never translated into “approved” or “insured.”

These risks interact. A custodian failure becomes credit risk if assets cannot be recovered. A legal dispute becomes liquidity risk while withdrawals pause. Duration losses become solvency losses if forced sales crystallize them. A derivative hedge can be economically sound but inaccessible because an exchange suspends withdrawals. Scenario analysis should therefore include correlated failures rather than haircutting each column independently.

## A due-diligence worksheet

An analyst can reject most misleading comparisons by filling one page before looking at APY:

| Question | Evidence required |
| --- | --- |
| What exactly is held? | Contract address, account agreement, prospectus, vault or receipt-token documentation |
| Who is the obligor? | Issuer and affiliate structure; borrower, fund, exchange, or protocol roles |
| What produces gross income? | Reserve schedule, loan book, fee flow, derivatives mechanics, or subsidy budget |
| Who keeps the spread? | Fee schedule, revenue waterfall, distributor share, reserve allocation |
| Is the rate fixed, floating, smoothed, discretionary, or promotional? | Formula, update authority, observation period, caps and termination terms |
| How is principal redeemed? | Eligible party, asset received, queue, fee, minimum, hours, jurisdiction |
| What backs the claim? | Asset composition, valuation, duration, encumbrance, custody, attestations |
| Who absorbs the first loss? | Equity, reserve fund, overcollateralization, junior tranche, depositors |
| What can halt exit? | Pauses, freezes, governance, compliance, bank and chain dependencies |
| What protections do not apply? | Deposit insurance, SIPC, guarantees, bankruptcy remoteness |

Rates should then be decomposed into a base benchmark and a spread. If a product yields much more than short government paper, the difference may be subsidy, leverage, illiquidity, credit, derivative funding, or a temporary imbalance. It is never evidence that blockchain technology manufactured a free premium. If it yields less, the gap may pay for transferability, liquidity, compliance, distribution, operating costs, or issuer profit.

Compare like with like. A directly held Treasury bill, insured bank deposit, government money-market fund, fiat stablecoin, tokenized fund share, DeFi lending receipt, and delta-hedged synthetic dollar solve different access and settlement problems. The highest nominal rate may be rational compensation for a claim the user did not intend to own.

## Connections and classification

This note supplies the yield layer beneath [[Stablecoin reserve and redemption risk]]. The reserve note asks whether a stable liability can reach par cash; this note asks what new claim is created when a holder tries to earn on that liability. It also operationalizes [[Token emissions and real yield]] by locating stablecoin rewards in reserve income, credit, trading, fees, or subsidy rather than accepting “real yield” as a label.

For asset dossiers in the [[Crypto top 100 research universe]], the classification should preserve the base token and every yield wrapper as separate economic exposures. A market-cap list may show only the transferable token, while the practical risk sits in exchange accounts, vault shares, lending receipts, or bridged versions layered above it. [[Wrapped assets and duplicate crypto exposure]] applies here: a wrapper is a claim on a claim and should not inherit the underlying's protections by assumption.

The durable principle is that yield reveals a balance sheet. Find the payer, identify the assets and liabilities, read the legal noun, and trace redemption to final cash. Only then does the percentage become meaningful.

## Open questions

- Which yield wrappers give holders a direct, bankruptcy-remote interest in underlying assets rather than a general claim on an issuer or platform?
- How should a common stress test combine a weekend banking closure, stablecoin depeg, exchange withdrawal halt, and smart-contract congestion without double-counting losses?
- What proportion of displayed stablecoin yield across major venues is reserve pass-through, credit income, trading income, or temporary subsidy as of each reporting date?
- How should Rome measure the option value of continuous transferability against the lower yield or extra intermediary risk that often pays for it?
- Which protocols publish enough position-level data to independently reproduce their claimed yield after hedging costs, realized losses, and reserve-fund allocations?
