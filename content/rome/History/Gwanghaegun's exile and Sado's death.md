[[Gwanghaegun]] was exiled while [[Crown Prince Sado]] was killed because they posed different kinds of dynastic risk.

Gwanghaegun did not go straight to [[Jeju-do]] and simply live quietly there from 1623. After the [[Injo Banjeong]], he was first exiled to [[Ganghwa Island]]. The Encyclopedia of Korean Culture says he later moved through Ganghwa, Taean, Gyodong, and finally Jeju, where he died in 1641 at age 67. His wife and son died within months of the deposition, and the new regime purged or scattered the political network that might have restored him.

That made exile enough. Killing Gwanghaegun would have made [[Injo]] look like a regicide rather than a restorer, while keeping him alive under guard neutralized him without adding a new moral stain to the coup. Exile was not mercy in a soft sense; it was controlled political disposal.

Sado was a different problem. He was not a retired former king; he was the living crown prince, the man who would inherit the throne. By 1762, the official case against him included killings of palace staff, sexual misconduct, erratic violence, unauthorized travel, and alleged danger to [[King Yeongjo]] himself. Our History Net's account of the [[Imo Incident]] says Yeongjo forced Sado to kill himself, then deposed him to commoner status and ordered him confined in a rice chest; Sado died after nine days.

The rice chest was a grotesque workaround. If Yeongjo formally executed Sado as a traitor, Sado's wife and son could be tainted by collective punishment, and [[Jeongjo]]'s succession might be ruined. If Yeongjo merely exiled him, a living crown prince could remain a factional magnet or direct threat. Confinement let Yeongjo remove Sado while later restoring his title and preserving Jeongjo as heir through adoption into the line of [[Crown Prince Hyojang]].

So the contrast is not "Gwanghaegun was forgiven, Sado was not." It is closer to this:

- Gwanghaegun: a defeated former king whose political base could be dismantled.
- Sado: a current heir whose life had become, in Yeongjo's eyes, incompatible with dynastic survival.

The moral horror of Sado's death remains. But politically, Yeongjo was solving a succession problem that exile could not solve cleanly.
