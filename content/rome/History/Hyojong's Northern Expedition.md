[[King Hyojong]]'s [[Northern Expedition]] was more plausible as military-fiscal state-building than as a successful war to defeat [[Qing dynasty|Qing]].

King Hyojong of [[Joseon]] wanted revenge for the [[Qing invasion of Joseon]] and the humiliation of becoming a Qing tributary. As a prince, he had been taken hostage by Qing, and after taking the throne in 1649 he cultivated the project later remembered as [[Bukbeol]]: a northern campaign against the Manchu Qing.

The counterfactual splits into two different questions:

- Could Joseon make its army sharper under Hyojong? Yes.
- Could Joseon defeat Qing or restore Ming power? Almost certainly no.

Hyojong's reforms were not imaginary. Seventeenth-century Joseon had adapted well to gunpowder warfare, and small Korean musketeer detachments performed effectively in the 1654 and 1658 Amur campaigns against Russian forces while serving alongside Qing forces. Hyeok Hweon Kang describes these musketeers as products of a broader Korean "musketry revolution," with firearms infantry, larger armies, and institutional changes around taxation, conscription, and logistics.

But that competence does not scale cleanly into an invasion of Qing. The Amur expeditions involved small specialized units in Qing-led operations; a Bukbeol campaign would have required Joseon to project and supply a large army across the Yalu or Tumen frontier into Manchuria, the dynastic homeland of the ruling Qing house. Qing in the 1650s was no longer just the Manchu force that had invaded Joseon in 1636. After taking Beijing in 1644, it had absorbed large numbers of Han Chinese troops and was building the Banner plus Green Standard military order that gave it far more depth than Joseon could match.

The political context also weakens the war scenario. A Joseon Annals overview says that in March 1659 Hyojong discussed the northern plan privately with Song Si-yeol, but Hyojong emphasized raising troops while Song emphasized moral cultivation and relieving the people. The same overview notes that Hyojong's actual buildup focused heavily on the central army, capital defense, finance, and royal authority. That makes Bukbeol look partly like a mobilizing ideology: anti-Qing revenge gave emotional meaning to military and fiscal strengthening at home.

The most likely outcomes if Hyojong had lived:

1. **Most likely:** he keeps preparing, but never launches. Bukbeol remains a royal project that justifies army reform, fiscal extraction, and anti-Qing legitimacy.
2. **Limited-war best case:** Joseon launches a raid or frontier offensive during a Qing distraction and wins a local success before withdrawing.
3. **Strategic-war case:** Joseon tries to overthrow Qing or restore Ming influence. This probably ends in defeat, Qing retaliation, or forced re-submission.

The only way to make a serious success case is to give Hyojong a much longer life and pair him with a major Qing crisis, such as the later [[Revolt of the Three Feudatories]]. Even then, Joseon would be operating from the northeast while the largest anti-Qing fronts were far to the south and west. Coordination would be weak, and Qing could still treat Korea as an exposed, punishable flank.

So the crisp answer: Hyojong living longer might have made Joseon more militarily capable, but it probably would not have made the Northern Expedition successful. Its ceiling was a symbolic or tactical success; its stated dream of humbling Qing was far beyond Joseon's resources.
