Late Joseon became more commercially connected and more dependent on copper cash, but neither markets nor money dissolved the agrarian state, status hierarchy, or regional inequality.

Up: [[Korean history (MOC)]]

The centuries after the Japanese and Manchu invasions saw a conspicuous thickening of exchange. Periodic markets linked neighboring villages; peddlers and boat merchants connected those circuits to towns and ports; Seoul's licensed shops competed with unlicensed sellers; government procurement put tax receipts into merchants' hands; and the copper *Sangpyeong tongbo* (常平通寶) became an increasingly familiar unit of payment and account. By the early nineteenth century, official compilations listed roughly a thousand periodic markets. That is a major historical change from an economy in which rice and cloth had done much of money's work.

Yet “commercialization” is a description to be explained, not a synonym for capitalism or an automatic prelude to industrialization. Most households remained tied to cultivation, rent, tax, labor service, and kin. Grain and cloth did not disappear as stores of value or means of payment. Market access varied sharply by region, season, transport cost, and household resources. Officials both enabled exchange and defended privileged sellers. Monetization could make transactions easier while making taxes, debts, and subsistence exposure harsher. The central question is therefore not whether late Joseon had markets—it plainly did—but how expanding exchange redistributed risk and bargaining power.

## From local fairs to interlocking market circuits

Periodic rural markets, or *jangsi* (場市), had appeared by the late fifteenth century and spread during the sixteenth. Their later importance came from density and coordination. The common five-day schedule divided a month into six market days: markets opened on dates ending in 1 and 6, 2 and 7, and so forth. Neighboring sites could stagger their days. A traveling seller could move through a circuit while villagers made a round trip in a day. The institution thereby economized on scarcity: a village did not need enough demand to sustain permanent shops if buyers and sellers could concentrate at predictable intervals.

The *Mangi yoram*, an early-nineteenth-century handbook prepared to give the monarch a synoptic view of finance and military administration, records 102 markets in Gyeonggi, 157 in Chungcheong, 68 in Gangwon, 82 in Hwanghae, 214 in Jeolla, 276 in Gyeongsang, 134 in Pyeongan, and 28 in Hamgyeong. These figures total 1,061. They should not be mistaken for a perfect census. What counted as a market, whether small or irregular sites were recorded, and whether the lists describe simultaneous activity all require local checking. Their strongest evidentiary use is comparative: the official state recognized a dense, geographically uneven market network, especially in the three southern provinces.

Markets exchanged much more than surplus grain. Cotton cloth, hemp, paper, metalware, ceramics, tobacco, salt, fish, seaweed, timber, livestock, medicinal ingredients, and craft goods moved through them. Some sites specialized: medicinal markets became associated with places such as Daegu, Jeonju, and Wonju. Such specialization implies links beyond face-to-face barter. Producers needed information about distant demand, carriers who could assemble loads, credit across time, and accepted measures of value.

The network's connective tissue was socially diverse. *Bosang* carried relatively light and valuable wares in bundles; *busang* bore heavier everyday goods on frames or pack animals. The compound term *bubosang* can obscure the distinct merchandise and organizations of these itinerant groups. Boat merchants exploited Korea's coasts and navigable rivers, crucial because moving bulky grain overland was expensive. *Gaekju* and *yeogak* at commercial nodes combined brokerage, lodging, storage, consignment, transport arrangements, and sometimes credit. These were not modern banks or corporations, but neither were they merely shopkeepers. By reducing search, storage, and trust costs, intermediaries made exchange across households and regions more regular.

Market day was also a social institution. Farmers, artisans, servants, women, itinerants, and local elites encountered goods, prices, news, entertainment, religious messages, and political rumor. Women's commercial labor is easiest to undercount because normative texts foreground male household heads and official merchants. Household production, prepared food, textiles, petty retail, and exchange could be essential while leaving a thinner documentary trace than licensed shops. Likewise, a growing number of market transactions does not by itself reveal who kept the margin: the producer, carrier, broker, creditor, landlord, or tax collector.

## Fiscal reform created demand without creating a free market

The [[Joseon land taxation from gwajeon to daedong|Daedong law]] helps explain why state finance and commercial growth cannot be separated. Beginning in Gyeonggi in 1608 and extended province by province over roughly a century, it commuted much household tribute into a more standardized land-based levy, normally payable in rice, cloth, or coin. Licensed procurement agents called *gongin* supplied designated goods to government offices and received payment from the collected revenue.

This arrangement changed incentives. Rather than compelling households to produce or obtain many locally assigned tribute goods through abusive middlemen, the state accumulated standardized receipts and purchased supplies through recognized contractors. Regular official demand encouraged merchants to assemble commodities and could stimulate specialized production, warehousing, and transport. Tax rice moving toward Seoul and other administrative centers reinforced waterborne routes. Payment in cash or convertible goods gave some transactions a common denominator.

But *gongin* were privileged contractors, not proof of competitive laissez-faire. Access depended on licenses, office connections, capital, and the ability to carry inventory. Successful agents could become large wholesalers, or *dogo*, and manipulate supply. Fiscal purchasing could support market integration while also concentrating rents. Nor did Daedong replace the ordinary land tax, eliminate local surcharges, or make assessment fair in practice. A reform that reduced one household's tribute burden might enlarge another actor's opportunity for procurement profit.

The causal arrow also runs both ways. A government could accept more standardized or monetary payments where markets already made conversion feasible. Fiscal commutation then deepened those markets by creating predictable flows. It is therefore too simple to say either that state reform “caused” commercialization or that autonomous merchants forced an agrarian state to modernize. Administrative demand, postwar reconstruction, population and cultivation changes, transport networks, craft specialization, and private accumulation interacted.

## Copper cash and the incomplete monetization of exchange

Joseon governments had repeatedly attempted paper or metal currencies before the seventeenth century, but rice and cloth retained monetary functions. A durable copper-cash regime emerged only after renewed experiments following the invasions. The decisive conventional date is 1678, the fourth year of King Sukjong, when central authorities began sustained issuance of *Sangpyeong tongbo*. “Sustained” matters: some coins bearing the name predated the general issue, and circulation expanded over decades rather than on a single decree day.

The round copper-alloy coin with a square hole belonged to an East Asian cash tradition and could be strung for larger payments. Multiple government offices and military institutions minted issues identifiable by reverse marks; surviving coins vary in weight, dimensions, and alloy. Modern metallurgical study accordingly warns against imagining a perfectly uniform national token. Copper supply, mint practice, counterfeiting, clipping or deterioration, and trust in particular issues affected value.

Cash offered real advantages. It was divisible, portable in small sums, durable, and more standardized than heterogeneous cloth. It let buyers compare unlike goods and facilitated wages, retail, tax payments, rents, interest calculations, and accounting. Once officials accepted coin and merchants quoted prices in it, network effects made it more useful: people accepted cash because they expected others to accept it.

Yet legal issuance did not produce universal use. Grain and cloth continued to circulate as payment and value, especially where coin was scarce or subsistence goods mattered directly. Silver by weight remained important in some high-value and cross-border exchanges. Strings of copper cash were cumbersome for large transactions. Regional price relations between coin, rice, and cloth shifted. A unit of account could appear in a contract even if settlement occurred partly in commodities. “Monetized” therefore has at least three meanings—prices reckoned in money, obligations payable in money, and actual settlement with coins—and the evidence does not always establish all three.

Coin supply itself generated instability known as *jeonhwang*, a term used for monetary stringency or dislocation. When authorities restricted minting or holders hoarded coin, cash could appreciate relative to goods. Debtors and taxpayers who had to obtain it then suffered even if nominal obligations did not change. Heavy minting could push in the other direction and disturb prices or confidence. Officials faced incompatible goals: enough cash for circulation and fiscal payment, stable purchasing power, profitable mint revenue, affordable copper, and protection against forgery. Their alternation between issuance, restraint, and debate was not simply incompetence; it reflected a commodity-money system whose supply was politically and materially difficult to govern.

Contemporaries understood money as morally consequential. Critics such as Yi Ik blamed copper cash for speculation, hoarding, theft, and the conversion of necessary goods into objects of gain. Such arguments should not be dismissed as Confucian ignorance of economics. They identified distributional effects: a liquid means of payment could empower merchants and creditors, transmit scarcity through prices, and expose households to demands that could no longer be met with their own produce. But moral critique is not a neutral measurement of circulation. Elite writers often idealized agriculture and distrusted commerce, so their complaints may testify both to real distress and to money's growing reach.

## Seoul, monopoly, and the 1791 opening

The capital made the conflict between regulation and private exchange especially visible. Licensed *sijeon* shops supplied the court, offices, and urban residents. In return for obligations, leading shops received privileges, including the *geumnanjeongwon*: authority to suppress unlicensed sellers dealing in protected wares. The arrangement offered the state identifiable suppliers and taxable premises, but it also let incumbents exclude *nanjeon* traders and sustain markups.

By the eighteenth century, Seoul's population and private commerce made comprehensive exclusion increasingly impractical. Unlicensed sellers met demands that the official shops could not monopolize without raising prices. In 1791, King Jeongjo's government adopted the *Sinhae tonggong* policy. It removed suppression privileges from most licensed shops while retaining them for the six leading stores, the *yuguijeon*. This was a significant liberalization, not a declaration of a modern free market. State procurement, licenses, remaining monopolies, policing, and guild interests persisted. The reform instead shows a government recalibrating regulation because private exchange had become too important to treat simply as illegal encroachment.

The episode also reveals why “state versus market” is a weak binary. The state built shops, licensed suppliers, issued money, accepted taxes, protected monopolies, and then partially opened competition. Merchants sought official privilege as well as freedom from it. Consumers could benefit from more sellers while remaining vulnerable to wholesalers who cornered supply. Markets were institutional settlements made through law, enforcement, custom, and bargaining—not a natural sphere outside government.

## Commercial crops, rural differentiation, and coercion

Late-Joseon cultivators did not all produce only for household subsistence. Cotton, tobacco, ginseng, vegetables, fruit, and other goods could be grown or processed for sale; coastal communities marketed fish and salt; craft households supplied paper, pottery, metal goods, and textiles. Improvements in cultivation and irrigation in some areas and the spread of transplanting could raise or reallocate output, but their scale and effects varied. Market opportunity was greatest where transport and demand made the price exceed production, rent, credit, and carriage costs.

Commercial participation could widen choice. A household might sell a specialized crop and buy grain, tools, or cloth more efficiently than making everything itself. Cash wages or petty trade could offer income beyond land. Escaped or manumitted people and status claimants operated within this expanding world, contributing to the long contraction described in [[Nobi status and the problem of translating Korean slavery]]. Yet commercialization did not erase status. Capital, land, storage, licenses, official ties, and freedom of movement were unequally distributed. [[Patrilineal transformation of the Joseon family|Household and lineage structures]] shaped access to property and labor, while women and low-status people often traded under constraints that elite men did not share.

The same price mechanism that rewarded surplus sellers could injure net buyers. A poor cultivator forced to sell grain after harvest to meet rent, tax, or debt might buy it back at a higher spring price. A creditor able to wait held an advantage over a debtor facing a deadline. Storage transformed time into bargaining power. In famine, integrated markets might move grain toward scarcity, but merchants might instead move it toward customers who could pay most. Official granaries, price interventions, transport restrictions, and relief therefore remained central to subsistence politics.

The rise of large private merchants should likewise not be equated with an independent bourgeoisie poised to overthrow the social order. Commercial wealth could purchase land, education, titles, office connections, or elite cultural forms. Merchant strategies often sought incorporation into status and state privilege. At the same time, officials depended on merchant logistics they ideologically ranked below farming. Late Joseon's commercial society was dynamic precisely because old hierarchies and new accumulations worked through one another.

## Border and overseas exchange

Domestic circuits connected to controlled foreign trade. Envoys and accompanying merchants exchanged goods in Qing territory; official border markets and illicit trade moved textiles, ginseng, books, metals, and other commodities. The Japan House at Busan structured commerce with Tsushima under close supervision. Shipwreck records showing Koreans carrying *Sangpyeong tongbo* provide unusually concrete evidence that cash traveled with ordinary people, although such exceptional records cannot establish representative holdings.

Foreign commerce mattered disproportionately for scarce inputs and high-value goods, but Joseon was not simply “closed.” The better description is regulated permeability. Authorities tried to channel movement through embassies, designated markets, interpreters, and ports because trade, diplomacy, smuggling, security, and information were inseparable. Restrictions raised profits for licensed intermediaries and smugglers; they did not stop exchange. Nor should growing foreign trade be confused with the treaty-port regime imposed after 1876, which altered tariffs, jurisdiction, shipping, and competitive conditions on a different scale.

## What the evidence can and cannot prove

Four kinds of evidence support the commercialization thesis, each with limits. First, official compilations enumerate markets, mints, taxes, and licensed bodies. They reveal what the state could see and wished to administer, not all illicit or female-coded commerce. Second, chronicles preserve policy debate. They are excellent for reconstructing choices and elite anxieties but poor as direct surveys of household behavior. Third, contracts, account books, price series, petitions, and shipwreck inventories bring transactions closer to view, though surviving examples are regionally and socially selective. Fourth, coins and mint analysis establish production and movement; a coin's presence does not by itself reveal transaction frequency or living standards.

Historiography has often made these materials carry a larger argument. A nationalist “internal development” interpretation emphasized agricultural productivity, private merchants, wage labor, and monetary exchange as indigenous sprouts of capitalism interrupted by imperialism. This was an important corrective to colonial claims that Korea was stagnant and incapable of change. But a search for capitalism's inevitable embryo can flatten regional difference and treat every market as evidence of one destination.

Revisionist economic work asks harder quantitative questions: did real wages or consumption rise, were markets integrated enough for regional price gaps to narrow, did agricultural labor productivity grow, and how broad was capital accumulation? Some reconstructions suggest stagnation or decline in living standards during parts of the nineteenth century rather than an unbroken commercial ascent. Those findings do not make the markets imaginary. Commercialization and welfare growth are different variables. More exchange can accompany population pressure, inequality, fiscal extraction, ecological stress, and falling per-capita income.

The most defensible synthesis is therefore bounded. Between the seventeenth and nineteenth centuries, commodity exchange, specialized intermediaries, state procurement, and copper-cash use became denser and more socially consequential. These changes altered how households met obligations and acquired necessities. They did not create uniform national prices, universal cash settlement, secure property for all, sustained industrial growth, or liberation from hierarchy. Late Joseon was neither a timeless subsistence economy nor a capitalist economy waiting fully formed behind Confucian institutions.

## Why monetization changed power

Money's deepest effect was to make unlike claims commensurable. Grain, cloth, labor, rent, tax, and credit could be translated into a common unit, even when settlement remained mixed. That reduced transaction costs and expanded possible exchange. It also made claims transferable and deadlines sharper. A household with cash could choose among sellers; a household required to find cash could become dependent on the nearest creditor or merchant.

Markets likewise redistributed information. Regular gatherings and merchant circuits transmitted prices and news beyond a village, constraining purely local monopolies. But information remained asymmetric: a wholesaler moving among markets knew more than a producer selling at one. Transport and storage converted that knowledge into profit. State rules could curb or intensify such asymmetry depending on enforcement.

This is why monetization cannot be judged as simply progressive or corrosive. It expanded coordination across distance and supported richer occupational specialization. It also created new routes for extraction, speculation, and crisis transmission. Its historical importance lies in that double movement. The late-Joseon economy became more connected, while the terms of connection remained politically made and socially unequal.

## Open questions

- How closely did recorded market counts track small, irregular, or unofficial markets in each province, and can county gazetteers correct the *Mangi yoram* totals?
- Did women retain a larger share of earnings in particular forms of market work, or did household heads and creditors capture most proceeds?
- Which regional price series are reliable enough to test grain-market integration before the treaty-port period without silently converting quoted units or grades?
- How did mixed settlement in coin, rice, and cloth work inside individual tenancy and debt contracts?
- Did the 1791 *Sinhae tonggong* measurably lower consumer prices, or mainly shift profits from licensed shops to large private wholesalers?
- How much of nineteenth-century monetary distress came from mint policy, copper supply, hoarding, counterfeiting, fiscal demand, or harvest shocks?
