Late-Joseon “tax disorder” was not simply a story of greedy officials: underfunded institutions, defective registers, status exemptions, grain-accounting deficits, and local power shifted public obligations onto households least able to resist, helping turn petitions into the connected but locally distinct uprisings of 1862.

## What “the disorder of the three administrations” means

Nineteenth-century complaints commonly grouped three fiscal systems as the **three administrations** (*samjeong*): field taxation (*jeonjeong*), military taxation or military-service finance (*gunjeong*), and state grain loans (*hwanjeong* or *hwangok*). The phrase is useful because cultivators often experienced them together. A deficit originating in a grain account might be recouped through an extra charge on land; a missing military taxpayer's quota might be reassigned to kin or neighbors; clerical fees and transport costs could attach to a nominally fixed field levy. Yet *samjeong* should not be mistaken for one unified tax code or one sudden collapse. Each branch had a different history, legal purpose, account, and chain of collection.

Field taxation rested on registered cultivated land and the assessment traditions outlined in [[Joseon land taxation from gwajeon to daedong]]. Its statutory forms had been repeatedly standardized, but a fixed nominal levy did not fix what a household actually surrendered. Land registers could omit elite holdings, preserve obsolete classifications, or continue to count land no longer productive. Collection added conversion rates, transport, storage, incidental charges, and local office expenses. When a district had to remit a fixed total despite disaster, abandonment, or concealment, the amount could be redistributed across the land still visible to officials. A landholder's legal assessment, a tenant's rent, and the cost ultimately borne by a cultivating household were therefore different quantities.

Military finance had likewise moved far from a simple roster of men personally performing service. Cloth or monetary payments helped support soldiers and military institutions, while exemptions distinguished status groups. By the nineteenth century, corrupted or outdated registers could retain men who had died or fled. **Kin collection** (*jokjing*) made relatives answer for a missing liable man; **neighbor collection** (*injing*) transferred his obligation to nearby households. Such practices were intelligible to administrators as ways to fill an assigned quota, but they severed liability from the living person supposedly taxed. As commoners acquired exempt status claims, disappeared from registers, or found patrons, those who remained legible bore a greater share. Protesters therefore did not only demand lower military payments. Some demanded that liability cease following dead or absent names and that privileged households share the burden.

The grain system was the most combustible branch in many districts. In principle, public granaries distributed grain during the spring scarcity season and collected it after harvest with an increment intended to cover losses and sustain the stock. The institution combined relief, stabilization, and finance. Over time, central and local agencies treated a portion of the increment as revenue, while provincial governments, military commands, and county offices operated grain accounts of their own. Forced distribution could turn a loan into a tax: households had to accept grain they did not want, sometimes of poor quality, and repay a larger quantity of good grain or its monetary equivalent.

The accounting problem compounded. Famine, nonpayment, embezzlement, fictitious stocks, and repeated bookkeeping devices could create a **deficit** (*pohŭm*): grain or money present on paper but absent from the store. An incoming magistrate still inherited an account that higher authorities expected him to settle. Clerks, officeholders, borrowers, and powerful households might have produced the loss, but collection often spread it among registered residents. The National Institute of Korean History's Jinju case study explains that offices increasingly relied on devices such as additional distribution and paper restoration of principal while unrecoverable stock accumulated. What looked from Seoul like arrears to be collected looked from a village like a debt imposed without a genuine loan.

## A chain of extraction, not a single villain

The language of “corruption” captures embezzlement, fabricated accounts, coercive collection, and illegal surcharges. It becomes misleading when it explains every abuse as individual moral failure. Joseon county government was thinly staffed and inadequately financed relative to its responsibilities. The court demanded remittances, granary balances, military funds, relief, public order, and accurate registers from a rotating magistrate who depended on locally rooted clerks. Village heads and intermediaries had to identify households and divide assessments. This was the institutional triangle described in [[Local magistrates, clerks, and yangban power]].

Every link had both administrative necessity and extractive opportunity. A clerk who knew the register could conceal one parcel or revive a dead man's liability. A magistrate could punish that clerk, but he also needed the office to produce a complete remittance before his evaluation. A village head could bargain for relief, shield a neighbor, favor kin, or pass an imposed total downward. Local *yangban* could protest an outsider's abuse while using status, documents, tenants, and lineage connections to reduce their own exposure. Central authorities could issue prohibitions against forced grain or irregular charges while continuing to expect fiscal resources that made those practices useful.

This helps explain why burdens clustered. Households with land, literacy, office ties, or recognized elite standing had more ways to negotiate an assessment. Poor tenants, smallholders, marginal status groups, and households without secure adult male representation were easier to make visible. Flight then narrowed the base further: each departure could increase pressure on those left behind, encouraging more concealment or departure. A fiscal system could thus enter a feedback loop without any decree ordering that the poor pay more.

Markets and money sharpened some conflicts without replacing grain. As [[Markets and monetization in late Joseon]] explains, taxes, rents, loans, and commodities could be reckoned in coin even where settlement remained mixed. Conversion created room for dispute over rates. A cultivator might have to sell produce after harvest, when prices were low, to meet a cash demand; a clerk or creditor able to wait held an advantage. Protest demands for a fixed money amount per unit of land were consequently double-edged. They accepted monetized payment but sought to stop officials from manipulating conversion and adding open-ended charges.

## Why petitions became uprisings in 1862

The upheavals conventionally called the **Imsul peasant uprisings**, after the cyclical year 1862, were a wave of local movements rather than a single centrally commanded rebellion. They occurred most heavily in the three southern provinces—Gyeongsang, Jeolla, and Chungcheong—but also reached other places later in the year. Their timing and tactics traveled. News of resistance demonstrated that collective action was possible; government investigators' tours created occasions for people to assemble and state grievances. Nevertheless, participants normally organized within a county, targeted local institutions and persons, and formulated demands from a specific account or assessment.

Longer precedents mattered. Hong Gyeong-nae's 1811–1812 rebellion in the northwest had joined regional discrimination, local discontent, commercial networks, and an organized military challenge. Numerous smaller collective petitions, tax refusals, and disturbances followed across the century. The 1862 movements did not simply reproduce Hong's program, and they should not be compressed into a straight line ending in the [[Donghak Peasant Revolution]]. What they shared was evidence that the dynasty's subjects could mobilize beyond an individual lawsuit when normal channels failed.

Jinju makes the causal sequence concrete. Grain deficits had accumulated before 1862, and residents had already appealed collectively. Officials attempted to cover shortages by assessing land through consolidated extra levies. According to the National Institute of Korean History's account, a proposed land charge rose from an earlier 2 *nyang* 5 *jeon* per *gyeol* to 6 *nyang* 5 *jeon*. The Right Gyeongsang Military Command also sought to distribute roughly 60,000 *nyang* of its grain-account deficit among residents. Such figures come through official records and require caution about whether proposed, assessed, and actually collected sums matched. They nonetheless show that the immediate grievance was not an abstract dislike of taxes. It was a contest over who would absorb a named institutional loss.

Organization crossed status lines without erasing them. Meetings involving Yu Gye-chun, Yi Myeong-yun, and village residents helped prepare the Jinju action. Some locally established or declining *yangban* participated because consolidated levies and transferred liabilities reached them too, or because they opposed the officials and elite rivals controlling county politics. Common cultivators, tenants, woodcutters (*chogun*), village functionaries, and other residents brought different stakes. Sun Joo Kim's study of Jinju emphasizes that class, status, village community, and political divisions produced multiple patterns of action rather than one homogeneous “peasantry.”

This coalition could be powerful and unstable. Elite participants possessed literacy, meeting networks, and a language of legitimate remonstrance. Poorer participants supplied numbers and sometimes pushed attacks toward clerks, wealthy households, granaries, or property. A movement could appeal to the king's just rule while assaulting his local office; it could demand equal taxation without demanding an end to monarchy or landed property. Treating such language as false consciousness misses its strategic force. Protesters distinguished an ideal of lawful government from the practices they condemned and used that distinction to justify collective correction.

The wave spread unevenly. The Academy of Korean Studies identifies early connected risings in the Jinju sphere—Danseong, Jinju, Hamyang, and Geochang—followed by movements in northwestern Gyeongsang and around Gyeongju. It records protest in about 38 of Jeolla's 54 counties, concentrated in spring, and a later concentration in inland Chungcheong. These counts depend on surviving official recognition and on what compilers classify as an uprising; they should not be read as a complete census. The regional variation is itself important. In some Gyeongsang districts local gentry joined early action. In parts of Chungcheong, ordinary cultivators and woodcutters more directly attacked powerful local *yangban*. Jeju's movements persisted into early 1863. One national label therefore covers distinct local political societies.

## What protesters demanded and did

Surviving demand lists make the protest more precise than the generic phrase “anti-feudal.” At Indong, demands included not charging residents for clerks' grain deficits, fixing the land-tax conversion, removing roughly a thousand dead or escaped military taxpayers from the rolls, and assessing military-support payments regardless of status. Gongju protesters likewise sought a fixed field-tax amount, less concentration of military charges on poor commoners, elimination of grain abuses, and an end to attaching military and grain deficits to land.

Across localities, four principles recur. First, liability should attach to a real and appropriate base, not to a dead man, an invented borrower, or a neighbor chosen because he could be coerced. Second, stated rates should be predictable rather than enlarged through fees and conversions. Third, officials and clerks should not transfer their missing funds to residents. Fourth, status privilege should not exempt the socially powerful while concentrating obligations on commoners. These demands did not amount to a fully consistent national budget. They did articulate fiscal justice in terms of visibility, regularity, accountability, and capacity to pay.

Collective action ranged from assembly and petition through refusal, occupation, destruction of records, and attacks on people or property associated with extraction. Participants sometimes seized county space long enough to impose their own corrections. Record destruction was not random hostility to writing. Registers and account books made disputed liabilities enforceable; destroying them attacked the administrative memory through which an absent person or fictional stock remained collectible. Attacks on clerks or wealthy households likewise expressed a map of who benefited or controlled information, though that map could be selective and violent.

Women are difficult to recover from movement narratives structured around named male leaders, household heads, interrogations, and official reports. Yet taxation reached household production, food, property, debt, and the labor needed to replace surrendered grain. A male-centered roster of accused leaders cannot establish that women were socially absent. Nor can “peasant” be equated with independent male landowner: tenant cultivators, servants, laborers, woodcutters, local functionaries, and declining status claimants appear in the rural coalitions. The archive makes those who wrote demands or received punishment easier to identify than those who provisioned, relayed news, protected kin, or suffered collection.

## The government's response and the limits of reform

The court combined repression, investigation, punishment of selected officials, concessions, and policy inquiry. It dispatched special investigators and secret royal inspectors, whose arrival could restore authority but also provide a forum for fresh mobilization. The state did not regard every complaint as illegitimate: reports confirmed serious defects, and some officials responsible for abuses were removed or punished. At the same time, leaders and participants faced prosecution, and official categories such as *minran*—a disturbance or uprising by the people—framed collective political action as an order problem.

On May 25, 1862, the government decided to establish a special reform office; the next day it named and organized the **Office for the Rectification of the Three Administrations** (*Samjeong Ijeongcheong*). The king solicited reform memorials, producing hundreds of responses that ranged from tighter operation of existing rules to broader proposals: household or village military levies, replacement of state grain distribution with community granaries, and, in some arguments, changes to landholding or cultivators' capacity to pay. The resulting **Rectification Regulations** (*Samjeong ijeong jeolmok*) recognized that local misconduct had become a systemic political crisis.

Recognition was not implementation. Reforms redistributed costs, so every remedy created opponents. Abolishing a revenue-bearing grain operation left offices needing another income source. Broadening military taxation challenged status privilege. Fixing a land payment required credible registers and prevented local authorities from using surcharges to close other accounts. Community granaries could move control nearer to residents but could also be captured locally. Moreover, central rules still had to pass through the same magistrates, clerks, village heads, and elites whose incentives produced the crisis.

The regulations therefore did not end the disorders or rural protest. Some risings occurred after their promulgation. Later governments revisited many of the same options, and the fiscal changes of the 1894 Gabo Reform addressed problems that 1862 had forced onto the central agenda. That continuity should not become a claim that 1862 “caused” every later reform. It shows instead that the uprising wave converted dispersed grievances into a recognized state problem whose proposed solutions remained politically and administratively unresolved.

## How to interpret the uprisings

Three simplified interpretations should be avoided. The first is dynastic decline as moral rot: weak kings, powerful in-law families, and corrupt officials supposedly allowed an otherwise sound system to decay. Court politics mattered, especially the concentration of appointments and weakened oversight associated with *sedo* government. But moral decline alone cannot explain why deficits were transferable, why offices depended on off-budget income, or why registers concentrated burdens. Those were institutional mechanisms.

The second is a single class war in which a unified peasantry attacked a unified landlord state. Exploitation and class conflict were real, and some protesters attacked wealthy local households. Yet landowners, tenants, clerks, declining gentry, commoners, and laborers divided and allied differently by county. Some local *yangban* helped organize resistance; others were principal targets. The social history is strongest when those differences are explained rather than treated as exceptions.

The third is a rehearsal for inevitable modern revolution. The 1862 wave enlarged the repertoire and memory of popular action, and the unresolved tax order forms part of the background to 1894. But most demands remained county-centered and sought lawful, predictable government. Movements rarely coordinated across county borders into a common national command, and they did not generally reject monarchy, status, or landholding in one program. Their historical importance does not depend on making them incomplete versions of something later.

A better interpretation joins fiscal architecture to political practice. State obligations became intolerable when official totals lost credible connection to land, people, or grain actually present. Protesters then used village networks, elite forms of remonstrance, crowds, and targeted violence to force a re-accounting. They were arguing not merely over how much the state took, but over whose records defined reality and who had authority to distribute a public loss.

## Why it matters

The three administrations connect institutional, economic, and social history. They show the afterlife of earlier fiscal reforms: standardizing tribute or land assessment did not eliminate military finance, granary revenue, local expenses, or unequal power over registers. They also show why commercialization could coexist with immiseration. More coin use and market exchange made conversion possible, but did not guarantee fair prices or secure household income.

The 1862 uprisings reveal Joseon's simultaneous reach and fragility. A territorial state could count land, assign quotas, maintain granaries, send inspectors, and solicit reform schemes across the realm. Yet its information was negotiated locally, and residents could collectively reject the accounts through which it ruled them. This is a more useful diagnosis than saying the state was simply absent.

In the larger map, the article bridges [[Joseon land taxation from gwajeon to daedong]], [[Local magistrates, clerks, and yangban power]], and [[Markets and monetization in late Joseon]] to later nineteenth-century reform and the [[Donghak Peasant Revolution]]. It also establishes a caution for studying Korean popular politics: a national wave can be historically connected without having a national organization, and a demand framed as restoration of proper rule can still redistribute power.

## Open questions

- Which Jinju account figures can be matched claim by claim to surviving *Bibyeonsa* documents, and how much grain or money was proposed, assessed, collected, remitted, or cancelled at each stage?
- How did tax incidence differ among owner-cultivators, tenants, landless laborers, women managing households, and *nobi* or recently manumitted households in counties with surviving registers?
- Can a county-level event database distinguish independent diffusion, investigator-triggered mobilization, and direct organizer contact across the 1862 wave?
- Where did destruction or correction of registers produce durable relief after an uprising, rather than a temporary suspension followed by reconstructed claims?
- How did memories of 1811–1812 and 1862 circulate into the organizations and demands of 1894 without assuming a direct institutional lineage?
