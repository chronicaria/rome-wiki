Japan's wartime mobilization of Koreans was not one undifferentiated roundup: between 1937 and 1945 colonial authorities and firms escalated from pressured recruitment through state allocation to formal labor conscription, embedding coercion in law, police power, company discipline, debt, wage withholding, and the impossibility of freely leaving dangerous work.

## What the terms do—and conceal

“Wartime mobilization” is the broadest useful category. It includes industrial labor, military service, military civilian work, student and women's labor, savings and material drives, and the unpaid work demanded within Korea as well as movements to Japan, Sakhalin, Manchuria, the Pacific, and other war zones. “Forced mobilization” emphasizes that these programs operated under an unrepresentative colonial state whose officials could use police, schools, neighborhood bodies, rationing, and punishment. “Forced labor” asks a narrower question about the work relation: could a person give meaningful consent, refuse placement, leave, receive the promised pay, and live without violence or confinement?

The distinctions matter. Long before 1939, Koreans crossed to Japan for wages, and ordinary migration continued during the war. A worker might initially respond to an advertised job yet encounter deception, confinement, withheld savings, or violence after arrival. Conversely, the formal legal category *chōyō* (labor requisition or conscription) applied only late in the war. Restricting “forced labor” to people drafted under that final ordinance mistakes a bureaucratic label for an account of freedom. Calling every Korean resident of wartime Japan a deportee erases both earlier migration and workers' varied trajectories. The historical task is to reconstruct the channel, the pressures at departure, and the conditions at the destination rather than infer experience from one aggregate number.

This is also a legal and diplomatic vocabulary dispute. Imperial documents represented Koreans as Japanese subjects owing labor to a national emergency. Japan's postwar government stresses legal equivalence and the 1965 claims settlement. South Korean courts have treated Japan's colonial rule as incompatible with the Republic of Korea's constitutional order and have distinguished damages for illegal colonial forced labor from ordinary property claims. The International Labour Organization's supervisory committee concluded that the wartime mass conscription of Koreans and other Asians for private industry under deplorable conditions violated the Forced Labour Convention. These are not interchangeable judgments, but none makes close historical description unnecessary.

## From labor shortage to escalating compulsion

The causal setting was total war. Japan's war in China after 1937, expansion across Asia and the Pacific after 1941, and military conscription removed Japanese men from mines, construction, transport, and heavy industry. The 1938 National General Mobilization Law gave the state sweeping powers over labor and production. Colonial Korea supplied food, savings, soldiers, civilian auxiliaries, and workers while remaining politically subordinated. Administrative capacity built during earlier colonial decades—household registers, police stations, village offices, schools, railways, and cooperating associations—made bodies legible and movable.

Historians commonly distinguish three Japan-bound labor channels, although dates differ slightly by implementation and source:

1. **Company “recruitment” (*boshū*), from 1939 to early 1942.** Japanese firms recruited allotted numbers with authorization and active assistance from the Government-General of Korea, local officials, police, and brokers. The paper form could appear voluntary. In practice, misleading promises, official pressure, economic distress, intimidation, and village-level quotas narrowed refusal. Authorities sought to prevent local resistance and deliver groups intact. The coercion did not begin only if a policeman physically seized someone; state-backed recruitment and deception could produce an unfree journey.
2. **Official mediation or allocation (*kan assen*), roughly February 1942 to September 1944.** Government offices took a more direct role in selecting and assigning workers to firms. Local administrations received quotas and organized collection, transport, and surveillance. The distinction between a corporate recruiter and the colonial state became thinner because firms' manpower demands moved through administrative command.
3. **Formal requisition (*chōyō*), from September 1944 to surrender in August 1945.** The National Labor Conscription framework was extended fully to Koreans. Orders now expressed legal duty openly; refusal could bring punishment. Yet the short formal phase does not mean coercive mobilization began only in 1944. It marks an escalation and juridical clarification of a system already restricting choice.

These channels overlapped with military mobilization. Koreans entered the Japanese army first through a volunteer system for men from 1938 and through conscription implemented in 1944; naval volunteering began later. Others served as military civilian employees, guards, porters, and laborers. Because official datasets classified laborers, soldiers, sailors, and civilian auxiliaries differently, totals cannot safely be combined without identifying the denominator.

## A state–corporate labor regime

The system joined public coercion to private production. Ministries and the Government-General planned allocations; provincial, county, township, and police personnel identified and assembled people; transport networks moved groups; and mines, construction contractors, shipyards, steelworks, and munitions firms put them to work. The Korea Labor Association and state-controlled *Kyōwakai* organizations helped administer, discipline, and assimilate Korean workers. Large conglomerates including Mitsui, Mitsubishi, and Sumitomo were prominent, but thousands of sites and subcontracting relationships make responsibility broader than a few famous corporations.

At the worksite, compulsion took material forms. Managers commonly housed Koreans in segregated dormitories, monitored movement, imposed long shifts and hazardous tasks, and punished disobedience. Deductions for food, lodging, compulsory savings, family remittances, and other charges could consume wages. Promised savings or pay were sometimes withheld and never recovered. Violence, inadequate food, disease, industrial accidents, and poor medical care appear repeatedly in survivor testimony and police or company records. Conditions varied by site and period; variation does not cancel the structure. A worker paid some wages could still be forced, and a nominal contract could coexist with confinement and assault.

Escape is especially revealing. Wartime records show high rates of flight from some mines and companies. Escape demonstrates agency and the porousness of control, not free employment. Workers risked arrest and punishment, and escapees depended on Korean communities, brokers, or irregular jobs to disappear. The combination of repeated flight and efforts to prevent it is evidence that many placements could not retain labor on ordinary contractual terms.

## Geography and unequal danger

Mobilization began in Korean villages and cities but its geography was imperial. Many people worked inside Korea on mines, railways, airfields, military construction, factories, and local labor details. Others crossed through Busan and Shimonoseki into Japan. Coalfields in Kyushu and Hokkaido absorbed large numbers; workers also entered mines on Hashima and Sado, steel and shipbuilding centers, dams, tunnels, ports, and military installations. Specialist research finds Korean miners concentrated in particularly dangerous and labor-intensive sectors. Educational and class differences could influence placement: people with schooling had better chances of factory or clerical work, while poor rural men were disproportionately sent to mines and heavy construction.

Still others went beyond metropolitan Japan. Karafuto/Sakhalin coal and industrial sites left Korean communities stranded when Soviet occupation and the collapse of the Japanese empire disrupted repatriation. Military labor took Koreans to Manchuria, Southeast Asia, and Pacific islands. Death could occur at work, under bombing, at sea, during the atomic bombings, or in the chaotic return after surrender. The failure to identify and repatriate remains is therefore not a marginal afterlife of mobilization; it is evidence of how an empire moved people without creating an adequate system to account for them as persons.

Scale is hard to state in one number. A South Korean fact-finding commission operating from 2004 to 2015 documented 798,043 Koreans forcibly mobilized and sent to Japan between 1939 and 1945 and identified 1,589 worksites, according to Ágota Duró and David Palmer's synthesis of the commission's work. Other widely cited archival reconstructions give lower Japan-bound labor totals, often around 670,000–725,000, because they use different categories and surviving official series. The wartime Korean population in Japan exceeded two million, but that figure includes earlier residents, families, and migrants outside organized labor mobilization. The defensible conclusion is not a false precision: several hundred thousand Koreans moved to Japan through organized wartime labor channels, while the wider number mobilized across military, labor, and geographic categories was larger.

Mortality is even less certain. The commission-derived estimate that more than 10 percent of the nearly 800,000 died or went missing would imply roughly 80,000, but Duró and Palmer stress that only 9,043 deaths in Japan had been identified by name in Takeuchi Yasuto's research and that the precise total is unknown. Destroyed or incomplete records, inconsistent categories, unmarked graves, cremation, post-surrender shipwrecks, and missing repatriation data prevent a secure death count. Estimates should be labeled as estimates, never converted into identified victims.

## Gendered mobilization

The familiar image of a male miner captures only one part of wartime extraction. Colonial policy assigned men and women different duties through gendered ideals. Men were cast as soldiers, miners, construction hands, and household providers whose labor and bodies belonged to the emperor. Women were told to become “mothers of the empire,” maintain households under rationing, join patriotic associations, collect materials and savings, support soldiers, and replace absent men in production. These unpaid and undercounted activities sustained mobilization even when a woman never left Korea.

Paid and compulsory female labor also expanded. Schools, the Patriotic Women's Association and its successors, youth bodies, employers, and officials directed girls and unmarried women into factories, military-related workshops, agriculture, nursing, and other work. The Women's Volunteer Labor Corps (*Joshi Kinrō Teishintai*; Korean *Yŏja Kŭnro Chŏngsindae*) was formalized late in the war, but female labor mobilization preceded the ordinance. “Volunteer” again described an official form more reliably than a free choice. Young age, teacher or police pressure, deceptive promises of schooling or training, separation from family, and withheld wages appear in litigation and survivor accounts, including cases involving Mitsubishi aircraft factories in Nagoya.

The labor corps must not be collapsed into the Japanese military's “comfort women” system. The former organized industrial and other labor; the latter was a system of military sexual slavery. Korean popular memory sometimes used *chŏngsindae* broadly, and fear that labor recruitment could lead to sexual exploitation shaped family responses. Individual paths could involve deception and abuse, but treating the two institutions as synonyms obscures how each operated and makes both harder to document. Gender analysis should instead show their place within the same militarized colonial order while preserving distinct recruitment structures, destinations, and forms of violence.

## Reading survivor evidence critically

Survivor testimony is indispensable because official records encode the mobilizer's categories and often omit beatings, hunger, humiliation, fear, and family rupture. Testimony explains how a “recruitment” visit felt in a village, how advertised wages changed after arrival, why someone escaped, and what unpaid savings meant to a household. It also restores names and moral claims to aggregate labor tables.

Testimony is positioned evidence, not a transcript immune to criticism. Many accounts were recorded decades later, through Korean or Japanese interviewers, activist projects, compensation applications, or lawsuits. Trauma, aging, translation, public vocabulary, and the legal questions asked can shape narration. A responsible method compares testimony with rosters, wage ledgers, police reports, transport lists, company histories, graves, temple remains, local newspapers, and other survivors' accounts. Discrepancies should remain visible. They may reveal faulty memory, but also aliases, clerical erasure, corporate record destruction, or categories designed to hide coercion.

Official evidence needs equal source criticism. A personnel card can establish presence without revealing whether consent was meaningful. A wage entry can record earnings while omitting forced deductions. A company history may celebrate production and silence Korean deaths. Postwar deposits of unpaid wages and savings show that money remained outstanding, but incomplete names and the gap between nominal deposit and family receipt complicate restitution. Material and forensic evidence can correct paperwork, yet cremation and poorly documented grave excavation have foreclosed identification in many cases. No source genre is self-sufficient.

## Liberation without settlement

Japan's surrender ended colonial sovereignty but not the consequences of dispersal. Workers improvised journeys home amid transport shortages, mine and factory collapse, discrimination, violence, and shipwreck. Some remained in Japan; many Koreans in Sakhalin could not return for decades. Families searched for missing relatives, wages, savings, pension deposits, and remains. Cold War division then fractured archives and claimant communities between North Korea, South Korea, Japan, and the Soviet sphere.

The 1965 Agreement on the Settlement of Problems concerning Property and Claims accompanied normalization between Japan and South Korea. Japan provided grants and loans, and Article II declared property and claims questions between the states and their nationals “settled completely and finally.” Japan argues that this disposed of compensation claims. South Korea used much of the funds for national development and later created domestic compensation programs, but the agreement was negotiated by governments, not individual survivors, and did not produce a shared account of colonial illegality or company responsibility.

South Korea's Supreme Court took a different route in 2012 and final 2018 judgments against Nippon Steel and Mitsubishi Heavy Industries. It held that claims for consolation damages arising from illegal colonial rule and forced labor were not the ordinary claims extinguished by the 1965 agreement. Japanese courts and government reject that interpretation, producing a conflict between domestic judgments and Japan's treaty position rather than a simple absence of law. In 2023 the South Korean government announced a foundation-funded “third-party reimbursement” plan for successful plaintiffs, using donations from South Korean companies rather than compelled payments from the defendant Japanese firms. Some victims accepted; others rejected a plan they believed removed apology and corporate responsibility. Compensation is therefore simultaneously about material repair, legal doctrine, diplomatic management, and who has authority to settle a survivor's claim.

## Memory, remains, and industrial heritage

Memory politics concentrates at visible sites: Hashima, Sado, Hokkaido mines, company factories, temples holding remains, and museums interpreting Japan's industrialization. When Japan's Meiji industrial sites entered UNESCO's World Heritage List in 2015, the Japanese delegation said it would explain that a large number of Koreans and others were brought against their will and forced to work under harsh conditions at some sites. Subsequent disputes asked whether exhibitions fulfilled that commitment. The disagreement is not only semantic. A display that celebrates technology while moving coercion outside the site's “relevant period” divides industrial achievement from the colonial labor that sustained its wartime operation.

Local Japanese and Korean researchers, Buddhist institutions, lawyers, unions, former dormitory staff, and families have recovered rosters, excavated graves, returned some remains, and recorded testimony when states and firms did not. Their work is a historical source and a political intervention. Duró and Palmer's study of Hokkaido shows both achievements and limits: community excavation forced forgotten deaths into view, but cremation and inadequate forensic protocols made individual identification and cause-of-death findings impossible in many cases. Commemoration cannot substitute for evidence preservation.

## What can be concluded

The strongest synthesis avoids two shortcuts. The first says only the openly compulsory 1944 requisition phase was forced, treating earlier official pressure, deception, confinement, and inability to quit as irrelevant. The second treats every Korean migration or every workplace as identical, obscuring changes over time and differences among sites. Evidence supports a staged but connected regime: colonial state power and corporate demand increasingly organized labor from 1939; coercion existed throughout the organized program but changed in legal form and intensity; destination conditions ranged widely yet repeatedly included restricted movement, hazardous work, discriminatory control, and unpaid money.

It also supports a wider view than male Japan-bound labor alone. Work inside Korea, female and student mobilization, military civilian service, imperial destinations beyond Japan, household extraction, and failed repatriation belong to the history. Precise totals remain category-dependent, mortality remains underidentified, and individual experiences require site-level reconstruction. Those uncertainties are reasons to preserve archives and testimony—not reasons to retreat to the mobilizers' euphemisms.

## Open questions

- How do South Korean commission case files revise aggregate counts when separated by labor, military civilian, military, and women's mobilization categories?
- Which surviving company wage ledgers can connect postwar Japanese government deposits to named workers and actual family receipt?
- How different were mortality, escape, pay, and punishment across mines, construction sites, shipyards, and factories?
- What evidence survives for labor mobilization inside northern Korea and for claimants who later became North Korean citizens?
- Which remains could still be identified through modern forensic methods without repeating the destructive excavation and cremation practices documented in Hokkaido?
