## “Three Kingdoms” is a retrospective map

The familiar trio—Goguryeo in the north, Baekje in the southwest, and Silla in the southeast—is indispensable for organizing the first seven centuries CE. These courts fought wars, conducted diplomacy, built capitals, ranked officials, patronized Buddhism, and transmitted texts and technologies across Northeast Asia. By the sixth century they were durable states with identities and interests that contemporaries recognized.

But the label becomes misleading when it is projected unchanged back to legendary foundation dates: 37 BCE for Goguryeo, 18 BCE for Baekje, and 57 BCE for Silla in the *Samguk sagi*. That chronicle was completed in 1145, centuries after the events, from earlier records that mostly no longer survive. Its annals impose later dynastic order on a much less consolidated early landscape. Archaeology instead shows uneven, regionally specific growth: central places expanded, elite tombs became more differentiated, weapons and prestige goods circulated, fortifications multiplied, and some communities absorbed their neighbors. There was no single morning on which three finished kingdoms appeared.

Nor were there only three political communities. Chinese histories describe Buyeo and Okjeo in the north and the Samhan groupings—Mahan, Jinhan, and Byeonhan—in the south. Gaya polities remained important in the Nakdong River basin until Silla absorbed the last of them in 562. Smaller powers, local chiefs, mobile groups, and commandery populations were participants rather than scenery. “Three Kingdoms” is most accurate for the mature competitive order, roughly the fourth through seventh centuries, not as a complete census of all earlier societies.

## From coalitions to territorial states

State formation did not mean that a culturally uniform people awakened and selected a king. It meant that rulers and allied elites acquired a greater ability to mobilize labor, collect goods, command soldiers, settle disputes, monopolize prestigious ritual, and reproduce authority beyond a single settlement. That process combined internal bargaining with external danger. The fall of [[Archaeology and the Old Joseon origin problem|Old Joseon]] in 108 BCE and the creation of Han commanderies altered exchange, warfare, and political opportunities without erasing local communities. Iron tools and weapons, horses, writing, seals, luxury goods, and administrative practices moved through networks linking Manchuria, the peninsula, Chinese states, and the Japanese archipelago.

In Goguryeo, communities around the Yalu and upper Hun river region developed amid mountain valleys, fortresses, and powerful neighbors. The early polity is often described as a federation of *bu*, conventionally translated as “tribes” or “departments,” whose leading houses retained political weight. Expansion was not simply settlement of empty land. Goguryeo rulers subordinated or incorporated neighboring populations, competed with the Xuantu and Lelang commanderies, and built a military system adapted to fortified landscapes. The destruction of Lelang in 313 removed a major commandery center, while warfare with Former Yan and other northern regimes demonstrated that consolidation remained vulnerable.

Goguryeo's transfer of its main capital from the Ji'an region to Pyongyang in 427 under King Jangsu marked both strategic reorientation and accumulated capacity. Fortresses, monumental tombs, inscriptions, diplomatic missions, and campaigns show a court able to coordinate extensive resources. The Gwanggaeto Stele, erected in 414 by Jangsu for his father, is exceptional near-contemporary evidence for royal genealogy and conquest. It is also victory rhetoric: its list of submissions and campaigns records what the monarchy wanted carved into political memory, not a neutral ethnic map or a modern deed to every territory named.

Baekje developed from the many communities conventionally grouped as Mahan in the Han River basin. The *Samguk sagi* connects its founders to Goguryeo's royal line, a tradition that mattered for later legitimacy but cannot substitute for early contemporary documentation. Archaeology around the Seoul region, including large settlements, fortifications, and elite burials, indicates increasing centralization. The court absorbed neighboring powers and turned access to the Han River and maritime routes into wealth and diplomatic leverage. Chinese writing, Buddhist institutions, artisans, and court practices were not signs of passive “Sinicization”; Baekje actors used mobile knowledge to strengthen their own polity and became important transmitters to the Japanese archipelago.

Goguryeo's seizure of the Han basin and Baekje capital in 475 forced Baekje south to Ungjin, present-day Gongju, and later to Sabi, present-day Buyeo, in 538. These moves expose the contingency of kingdoms often drawn as timeless blocks. Baekje survived defeat by rebuilding alliances, capitals, and administrative centers. The archaeological sites at Gongju, Buyeo, and Iksan display planned fortifications, royal tombs, temples, workshops, and exchange across East Asia. State capacity lay in people and institutions as much as in a particular homeland.

Silla emerged among Jinhan communities around Gyeongju. Its annals preserve a succession of founding groups and royal titles that may encode competition among elite houses, but their earliest chronology is difficult to verify. Archaeological change is clearer: increasingly lavish wooden-chamber tombs covered by stone and earth concentrated extraordinary goldwork, horse gear, weapons, and imported objects around Gyeongju in the fourth and fifth centuries. Such burials signal a court at the center of far-reaching networks, yet grave wealth reveals elite claims more directly than it reveals everyday administration.

Silla expanded later than its rivals but used that timing effectively. In the sixth century, rulers formalized titles and offices, promulgated law, sponsored Buddhism, and extended power into the Han River basin. The mid-sixth-century stele from Danyang—variously dated between 545 and 551—and other inscriptions show named officials and local communities participating in royal projects more directly than the much later chronicles do. Silla incorporated Gaya polities in stages—Geumgwan Gaya in 532 and Daegaya in 562—often retaining useful local elites while subordinating their political autonomy. This was conquest and negotiated integration, not the natural reunion of co-nationals.

## Institutions were assembled, not inherited whole

The three states converged on some recognizable tools of government without becoming copies of one another. Courts ranked aristocrats, assigned offices, issued commands through writing, organized military service, built walled capitals, and used provincial strongholds. They adopted Buddhism as a royal and aristocratic institution—Goguryeo conventionally in 372, Baekje in 384, and Silla officially in 527 or 528—while older ritual practices continued. Monasteries offered literate personnel, transregional connections, monumental spaces, and doctrines that could represent kingship as morally consequential and cosmically ordered.

This convergence was produced by competition. A court that could recruit literate diplomats, construct fortresses, reward followers with prestige goods, and mobilize agricultural surpluses had advantages against rivals. Borrowing from Chinese dynasties did not abolish local agency: institutions were selected and adapted through embassies, migrants, captives, monks, artisans, and political refugees. Likewise, movement toward Japan was reciprocal and politically positioned. Describing all valuable change as diffusion from one civilizational center recreates an old colonial hierarchy; describing it as purely indigenous erases the networks that made state building possible.

Royal centralization also remained incomplete. Aristocratic houses controlled offices, armed followers, land, and local ties. Goguryeo's *bu*, Baekje's great families, and Silla's ranked elite groupings constrained monarchs even as they staffed states. Succession struggles and elite defections repeatedly changed wars. The mature kingdom was therefore not a modern bureaucracy standing above society. It was a negotiated, coercive coalition whose center had become unusually durable.

## What state building meant below the court

The evidence is heavily tilted toward rulers. Chronicles preserve wars, diplomatic missions, reigns, and prodigies; inscriptions celebrate patrons; lavish graves belong disproportionately to elites. Yet states were built from ordinary people's grain, cloth, iron, transport, construction labor, and military service. Fortress walls, roads, palaces, temples, and tombs materialize organized work even when the laborers remain unnamed. Expansion could bring market access or protection, but also tribute, relocation, conscription, captive taking, and exposure to campaigns.

For local leaders, incorporation offered unequal choices. Some received ranks, marriage ties, gifts, or positions and helped translate central demands into local authority. Others resisted, defected, or lost their bases. Gaya's long persistence demonstrates that small polities were not merely immature versions of Silla; they exploited iron production, river and maritime routes, alliances, and burial display within a competitive field. Their eventual absorption explains Silla's success, but success should not be mistaken for historical inevitability.

Women appear most clearly in dynastic genealogy, marriage diplomacy, and exceptional rulership, while the work and vulnerabilities of most women are difficult to recover. Elite marriages linked houses and could legitimate succession. Silla's later queens show that its elite order did not exclude women from sovereignty in the same manner as some neighboring courts. But neither royal women nor ornate grave goods can stand for all women. Agricultural labor, textile production, household reproduction, ritual, migration, and enslavement are structurally necessary to the story even where the sources provide few individual voices.

Dependent and enslaved people are still less visible. Warfare generated captives, and hierarchical households and states commanded unfree labor. Translating every status through a single modern category risks false precision, but omitting coercive dependence makes elite civilization appear costless. Tomb differentiation also warns against treating shared artifact styles as shared experience: the person buried with a gold crown and the people who mined, smelted, transported, farmed, and constructed inhabited the same political formation from radically different positions.

Religion likewise exceeded court conversion dates. Official Buddhist recognition tells us about political patronage, not an instant replacement of local cults, ancestor rites, healing, divination, or landscape powers. Monks and temples could connect capitals to wider intellectual worlds; local people encountered these institutions through labor demands, festivals, charity, ritual services, and elite display. A social history of the Three Kingdoms must therefore read monumental evidence both as cultural creativity and as concentrated power.

## The source problem changes the narrative

The *Samguk sagi* is the indispensable surviving narrative. Kim Busik and his Goryeo court team organized it in annals, chronological tables, treatises, and biographies, using earlier Korean records and Chinese histories. It preserves material that would otherwise be lost. But its compilers wrote nearly five centuries after Goguryeo and Baekje fell, in a Confucian historiographical form, for a Goryeo court with its own concerns about legitimate kingship and interstate order. Precise speeches, early reign dates, and smooth institutional sequences require corroboration rather than automatic acceptance.

Chinese dynastic histories are often closer in date and provide external descriptions, embassy records, and political geography. They too sort peoples through imperial categories and report what mattered to Chinese courts. Japanese chronicles preserve crucial relations with Baekje, Gaya, and Silla but were compiled within an eighth-century court constructing its own genealogy and claims. The Gwanggaeto Stele is contemporary but celebratory; tomb murals illuminate dress, architecture, labor, belief, and status but do not label every social identity. Archaeology can correct written silence, yet pots, crowns, and burial forms do not speak an ethnicity without an argument.

The strongest reconstruction therefore uses disagreement between sources productively. A later chronicle can propose a sequence; inscriptions can test names, offices, and political language; settlement and burial archaeology can test scale and hierarchy; foreign records can reveal diplomatic recognition and bias. Where these layers converge, confidence rises. Where a foundation story stands alone, it should remain a tradition rather than be promoted into a fact.

## Why national ancestry is the wrong causal model

Modern Koreans can reasonably include all three kingdoms within Korean history. Their languages, institutions, populations, memories, and successors contributed in complex ways to later societies on and around the peninsula. The error is not connection but teleology: imagining that a single homogeneous *minjok* already existed, that the kingdoms knew themselves as its regional branches, and that Silla's victory completed a foreordained national project.

This framework turns interstate war into fratricide, treats changing frontiers as sacred national borders, and makes vanished communities valuable only as ancestors of later winners. It also intensifies modern territorial disputes. Goguryeo's domain crossed today's China–North Korea border, so Korean and Chinese national narratives have sometimes competed to claim it as exclusively “ours.” Ancient political membership, archaeological distribution, modern ethnicity, and present sovereignty are different questions. No fifth-century inscription can settle a twenty-first-century border by ancestry.

Calling the kingdoms states restores harder and more interesting history. Their rulers built coalitions, extracted resources, fought, migrated, borrowed, converted, and failed. Their subjects possessed layered local, kin, status, and political affiliations that changed through incorporation. Silla's eventual victories depended on institutions and a contingent Tang alliance, not on national destiny; their consequences belong in [[Silla unification and the place of Balhae]]. The Three Kingdoms were formative for later Korean history precisely because their convergence and integration had to be made. It was not present at the beginning.

## Open questions

- Which early entries in the *Samguk sagi* can now be independently tested through inscriptions, radiocarbon-dated settlements, or foreign chronicles?
- How did incorporation alter taxation, military service, land access, and household status in former Gaya and Mahan communities?
- What can commoner settlements and archaeobotanical remains reveal that elite tombs systematically conceal?
- How should scholars correlate *bu*, Samhan, and Gaya political names with archaeological groupings without turning either texts or artifact styles into ethnic maps?
- How differently did Goguryeo, Baekje, Silla, and Gaya institutions define dependent labor, captivity, and membership?
