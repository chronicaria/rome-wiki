The Gwangmu reforms gave the Korean Empire more money, information, weapons, and centrally commanded troops than late Joseon had possessed, but those gains remained fiscally fragmented, territorially incomplete, and too vulnerable to court politics and foreign coercion to secure sovereignty.

## The question is capacity, not a reform checklist

The **Gwangmu reforms** usually denote the state-building program pursued under Emperor Gojong after he proclaimed the Korean Empire (*Daehan Jeguk*) in October 1897. “Gwangmu” was the imperial reign name. Lists of achievements—land surveys, factories, schools, railways, telegraphs, modern uniforms, rifles—can make the program look more coherent and complete than it was. Older dismissals can create the opposite illusion: that an autocratic court merely decorated a moribund state while Japan modernized Korea from outside.

A better test asks what the government could actually do. Could it identify taxable land, collect predictable revenue, control money, allocate expenditure through public institutions, mobilize trained force, and make decisions effective outside Seoul? Capacity means repeatable organizational power, not the announced intention to reform. Measured this way, the Korean Empire made consequential advances. It also built them through institutions whose jurisdictions overlapped and whose resources often depended on Emperor Gojong's personal authority. The result was neither an empty performance nor a completed modern fiscal-military state.

The program must also be placed between two coercive contexts. It followed the [[Gabo Reform causal map|Gabo reforms of 1894–1896]], which reorganized offices and status law amid the Sino-Japanese War and powerful Japanese influence. It ended as an autonomous project during the Russo-Japanese War and the imposition of Japanese financial and diplomatic control in 1904–1905. The short window matters. Institutions created in it had limited time to standardize practice before a foreign power took command of crucial levers.

## A sovereign empire built around an enlarged throne

Declaring an empire asserted that Korea was independent of the old Qing-centered hierarchy and equal in formal rank to neighboring emperors. The 1899 constitution, the *Daehan guk gukje*, located broad authority in an “unlimited” imperial sovereign: command, legislation, appointments, diplomacy, and honors converged at the throne. This was state-building, but not parliamentary state-building. Gojong and his allies sought an executive center able to act above factional cabinets and foreign-sponsored reform coalitions.

That choice shaped capacity. The government retained ministries created through the Gabo reforms, including the Ministry of Finance (*Takjibu*) and Ministry of Military Affairs (*Gunbu*), while creating or expanding palace institutions with separate authority. The most important fiscal example was the **Household Department** (*Gungnaebu*) and the offices gathered around the imperial household. The most important military example was the **Board of Marshals** (*Wonsubu*), established in 1899 and directly responsible to the emperor. These arrangements could bypass obstructive ministries and concentrate resources behind the sovereign. They could also divide records, revenues, and accountability between “public” government and palace domains.

This ambiguity is central to the historiography. A palace-centered institution was not automatically private in the modern sense: monarchies commonly used dynastic property and household offices for public power. Yet when mines, ginseng, estates, transport, or other income streams moved under household control, the Ministry of Finance lost some ability to present and execute a unified budget. Personal command could accelerate a favored project while making the whole system less legible and less transferable beyond the ruler.

## Revenue grew, but a unified fiscal state did not

The empire inherited the problem described in [[Nineteenth-century tax disorder and peasant protest]]: old assessments no longer matched cultivation, intermediaries diverted collections, local variation frustrated accounting, and multiple levies burdened households without reliably enriching the center. The Gabo reforms had attempted to centralize tax collection and monetary payments. Gwangmu officials continued that direction rather than returning to an untouched “traditional” order.

Published budget series show a marked increase in nominal central revenue and expenditure from the later 1890s into the early 1900s. The precise meaning of those totals requires caution because accounting categories changed, supplementary appropriations were large, and inflation or currency depreciation distorted nominal comparisons. Still, expansion was real enough to finance new offices, cadastral work, communications, urban projects, foreign purchases, and a much larger military establishment.

The Ministry of Finance developed provincial tax offices and sought to pull revenue away from magistrates and private or semi-private collectors. Cash taxation could make transfers to Seoul more measurable than deliveries in kind. Budgeting supplied an annual statement of anticipated receipts and expenses. These were substantial technologies of rule: a government that counts revenue by office and purpose can compare claims, plan purchases, and detect at least some leakage.

But the budget did not encompass all economically important state-controlled resources. Imperial household finance grew alongside ministerial finance. The court gained control over revenue from crown lands and commercial privileges, while different agencies sometimes claimed the same taxpayers or sources. Officials also used **external assignment** (*oehoek*), directing local revenues to particular claimants or expenditures before those funds entered a consolidated treasury. Such assignments could get money to urgent purposes, but they weakened central accounting and encouraged competition over future collections.

Extraordinary spending further limited predictability. National ritual and imperial display were not merely frivolous: the empress's funeral, Gojong's enthronement, and imperial anniversaries helped materialize the new sovereign order. Nevertheless, reserve and supplementary spending could consume striking portions of annual outlays. The National Institute of Korean History's fiscal survey notes that additional reserve allocations reached about 1.8 million won in 1897—roughly 28.5 percent of that year's total expenditure—and 2.7 million won in 1902, about 35.6 percent. Those figures illustrate both the resources the center could mobilize and the weakness of an ordinary budget as a binding plan.

## Land survey as fiscal intelligence

The most ambitious attempt to repair the revenue base was the **Gwangmu land survey** (*Gwangmu yangjeon*). In 1898 the government established the Bureau of Land Survey (*Yangji Amun*). Surveyors measured fields, recorded location, area, class, and cultivation, and sought to correct registers that omitted newly reclaimed or concealed land. From 1901 a related office issued **land certificates** (*jigye*) in selected surveyed areas.

The immediate purpose was fiscal. Land tax remained indispensable, and the state could not assess it fairly or efficiently without knowing what land existed and who bore responsibility for payment. Surveying was therefore an exercise in information power: it converted locally negotiated fields into standardized entries that officials at a distance could aggregate. Training surveyors and establishing procedural forms also created human and bureaucratic infrastructure that outlasted a single measurement campaign.

The survey was nonetheless not a completed modern cadastre for the whole peninsula. It operated from 1898 to 1904, covered only part of the country, and was interrupted as Japanese influence expanded during the Russo-Japanese War. Techniques and units were not perfectly uniform. The *jigye* identified claims and tax responsibility, but historians disagree about whether it constituted modern, exclusive ownership in the later civil-law sense. Existing landholding involved layered rights among cultivators, landlords, lineages, communities, and the state. A certificate could strengthen one recorded claimant without resolving all those relations.

This is why neither triumphalist nor colonial succession stories work. The later Japanese colonial land survey did not begin from a blank Korean landscape; Korean officials had already pursued measurement, certificates, trained personnel, and a stronger taxable register. But the colonial survey was not simply the neutral completion of Gwangmu policy either. It operated under a different sovereign, with different legal authority, greater coercive reach, and consequences examined in [[Land survey, tenancy, and the colonial economy]]. Institutional continuity in techniques does not erase the political rupture of colonization.

## Monetary capacity and its contradictions

A fiscal state also needs a stable unit in which to levy taxes, write budgets, and pay soldiers. Korea's late nineteenth-century monetary system mixed copper cash, foreign coins, Japanese notes, and newly minted domestic currency. The empire adopted the won and tried to establish modern minting and banking institutions. It also promoted commercial companies, mining, transport, and communications, partly to widen the taxable economy and partly to demonstrate civilized sovereignty.

The notorious weak point was the **nickel coin** (*baekdonghwa*). Large issues generated seigniorage—revenue from creating money—and supplied a government short of other liquid funds. But overissue, variable quality, counterfeiting, and regional differences in acceptance produced depreciation and exchange instability. Taxpayers, merchants, officials, and soldiers did not experience the same nominal won as an identical purchasing power everywhere. A coinage that financed the center in the short run could raise prices and undermine confidence in the longer run.

The monetary problem reveals a recurring Gwangmu pattern. The government possessed enough authority to create instruments and extract resources, yet not enough integrated administrative reach to regulate their circulation consistently. Moreover, foreign banks, merchants, loans, and competing currencies constrained monetary sovereignty. Japan's appointment of the financial adviser Megata Tanetarō in 1904 and the subsequent currency reform displaced Korean control at precisely the point when monetary stabilization was most needed. The reform did stabilize accounting on Japanese terms, but it also made the Japanese yen-centered system and Japanese financial institutions decisive. Technical rationalization and loss of sovereignty occurred together.

## Building a palace-centered army

Military reform had both external and internal purposes. The Korean Empire faced Russian and Japanese troops, foreign concessions, and an international system in which formal sovereignty without force was fragile. It also faced domestic unrest and armed resistance. A modern army could defend borders and the capital, but it could also protect the throne against coups and police the population.

The Board of Marshals placed high command near Gojong. The government expanded the imperial guard and central units, adopted modern ranks and uniforms, established training and military schools, purchased rifles, machine guns, artillery, and ammunition, and sought foreign instructors. Provincial battalions extended the army's nominal reach beyond Seoul. Contemporary and later estimates of total strength vary because authorized establishments, men on rolls, and troops actually present and equipped were not identical. Claims approaching 20,000–30,000 should therefore be read as scale indicators, not exact measures of combat-ready strength.

Budget evidence is more secure about priority than effectiveness. The National Institute of Korean History's synthesis, citing specialist fiscal research, reports military expenditure at about one quarter of total outlays from 1897 through 1900 and approaching 40 percent after 1901. A poor government directing that share to armed force was making a serious fiscal-military effort. It also created an opportunity cost: money spent on guards, weapons, and barracks could not simultaneously complete surveys, schools, or infrastructure.

Numbers and expenditure did not equal an army capable of deterring Japan. Units differed in training and equipment. Ammunition, maintenance, logistics, staff work, and officer education lagged behind the purchase of visible hardware. Palace-centered command encouraged loyalty to Gojong but complicated coordination between the Board of Marshals, Military Ministry, guards, and provincial forces. Foreign instructors represented useful expertise and geopolitical hedging, yet dependence on imported arms and advisers exposed the army to diplomatic pressure and supply disruption.

Most importantly, capacity is relational. A Korean force could be stronger than its predecessor and still be radically outmatched by the conscript army, fiscal base, navy, transport system, and wartime mobilization of Meiji Japan. During the Russo-Japanese War, Japanese troops occupied strategic points and imposed the February 1904 protocol. Korea's declaration of neutrality did not protect its territory. The issue was not that no Korean modernization occurred; it was that Korean gains were insufficient against an imperial power already able to operate inside Korea.

The final destruction of the army shows both weakness and institutional reality. After Gojong's forced abdication, Japan compelled the government to disband much of the Korean military in August 1907. Soldiers resisted in Seoul, and dismissed troops joined or strengthened the [[Righteous armies and the problem of popular sovereignty|righteous-army struggle]]. A purely ceremonial force would not have required coercive dissolution or supplied trained resisters. Yet the Japanese could dissolve it because Korea's command and government were already subordinated.

## Industry and infrastructure were enabling systems

Gwangmu state-building extended beyond taxes and troops. Telegraph, telephone, postal, railway, electrical, mining, and manufacturing projects could lower the cost of moving orders, information, goods, and soldiers. Seoul acquired electric lighting and streetcars through a court-linked joint enterprise. The government granted concessions and chartered companies while attempting to retain leverage over mines and transport.

These projects had mixed ownership and financing. Foreign capital and technology could add capacity faster than domestic revenue alone, but concessions could transfer profit or strategic control abroad. Railways were particularly double-edged: they integrated markets and permitted rapid movement, while foreign control made them tools of imperial military logistics. Infrastructure cannot be counted as Korean state capacity merely because it stood on Korean soil. The relevant questions are who financed it, who governed it, whose personnel operated it, and who could command it in a crisis.

## Why the capacity plateaued

Four constraints interacted.

First, **fiscal fragmentation** prevented increased resources from becoming a fully consolidated treasury. Ministerial budgets, imperial household revenue, earmarks, and local assignments all funded government purposes, but their separation reduced transparency and stable planning.

Second, **territorial incompleteness** limited information and enforcement. Surveys and provincial military units expanded the center's reach unevenly. Local elites, clerks, merchants, and communities remained necessary intermediaries. Their cooperation could make policy work; their resistance or manipulation could redirect it.

Third, **monetary and institutional instability** raised transaction costs. Currency depreciation, frequently reorganized offices, and dependence on foreign expertise made sustained administration harder. A bureaucracy needs routines long enough to become ordinary; the empire repeatedly built under emergency conditions.

Fourth, **imperial competition constrained every reform choice**. Russia, Japan, the United States, Britain, and other powers did not offer a neutral environment in which Korea could patiently develop. Loans, advisers, concessions, troops, and diplomatic recognition linked domestic capacity to external patrons. After Japan's 1904 victory trajectory, reforms in finance and communications increasingly served the protectorate being built over Korea rather than an autonomous Korean program.

These constraints reinforce one another. An incomplete survey restricted revenue; weak revenue encouraged monetary expedients; unstable money impaired soldiers' real pay; divided institutions complicated prioritization; foreign loans or advisers promised solutions but increased leverage over sovereign decisions.

## How to judge the Gwangmu reforms

The correct comparison is not between Korean performance and an abstract finished nation-state. Compared with late nineteenth-century Joseon, the Korean Empire collected and budgeted more revenue, attempted systematic cadastral measurement, trained technical personnel, expanded communications, and fielded a larger modern-armed force. These achievements undermine the colonial claim that Korea possessed no indigenous trajectory of modernization and required Japanese rule to acquire modern institutions.

But indigenous initiative does not prove that the program was socially egalitarian or likely to succeed. The imperial constitution concentrated power rather than creating representative control. Land measurement served taxation and could strengthen proprietors more readily than tenants. Military spending protected the throne and suppressed dissent as well as preparing national defense. Nickel inflation and stronger extraction imposed costs on ordinary people. Reform could be genuinely Korean, genuinely modernizing, and distributively coercive at the same time.

Nor does Japan's conquest prove that every Korean project was futile. Outcomes under extreme external pressure do not retrospectively erase institutional learning. Conversely, cataloguing Korean agency cannot explain away the scale gap with Japan or the empire's internal divisions. The strongest conclusion is asymmetric: Gwangmu reform created a meaningful nucleus of fiscal-military capacity, but it did not consolidate that nucleus into a sovereign system able to survive a great-power war fought across Korean territory.

## Why it matters

The Gwangmu case changes the causal map between the [[Gabo Reform causal map]] and [[From Eulsa to annexation, 1905-1910]]. Korea did not pass directly from failed reform to passive colonization. Between them stood an imperial state that deliberately tried to make land, money, administration, and armed force more governable.

That history also clarifies the difference between **modernization** and **sovereignty**. A government can build modern offices yet lose authority over them; a financial reform can improve accounting while subordinating policy to a foreign adviser; a railway can enlarge productive capacity while carrying an occupier's troops. Institutional form alone does not identify who commands the institution.

Finally, the reforms expose the distributional politics inside “national strengthening.” More legible land and stronger taxation empowered the center, but they also changed relations among cultivators, landlords, local officials, and the palace. A larger army represented national defense, imperial autocracy, and domestic policing simultaneously. Asking who paid, who commanded, and who benefited keeps the story from collapsing into either patriotic celebration or colonial dismissal.

## Open questions

- How much of the rise in nominal revenue between 1897 and 1904 remains after reconstructing consistent accounting categories and deflating by regionally varying currency values?
- What proportion of the land actually surveyed received *jigye*, and how did certificates alter disputes among owner-cultivators, landlords, tenants, lineages, and communal users in different provinces?
- How many soldiers were present, paid, trained, and adequately supplied in each year, as distinct from authorized establishments or names on rolls?
- Did imperial household control allow faster strategic investment than ministerial budgeting would have permitted, or did its fiscal fragmentation impose larger system-wide costs?
- Which Gwangmu personnel, records, and routines were retained under the protectorate and colonial government, and which were deliberately displaced or retrospectively appropriated?
