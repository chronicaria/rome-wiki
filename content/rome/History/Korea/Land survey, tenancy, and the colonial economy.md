Japan's 1910–1918 land survey did not simply confiscate every undocumented Korean farm, but it made land, title, and tax claims legible on colonial terms; together with landlordism, debt, rice commercialization, and unequal access to law and credit, that institutional change intensified insecurity for many cultivators while enabling real—unevenly distributed—agricultural growth.

## The argument in one chain

The colonial agrarian economy is often told through two incompatible slogans. In one, Japan seized Korean land through a fraudulent cadastral survey and reduced independent farmers to tenancy. In the other, Japan supplied secure property rights, irrigation, markets, and modern agricultural growth. Each isolates something real and turns it into the whole story.

A more useful causal chain begins with **political power**. Annexation in 1910 placed Korean land under a state that Koreans did not choose and could not hold electorally accountable. The Government-General then surveyed boundaries, identified persons whose claims it would recognize as ownership, valued fields, and standardized land-tax administration. A registered title could protect an owner, support sale and mortgage, and reduce some boundary ambiguity. The same title also strengthened an owner's position against tenants, subordinate claimants, communal users, and cultivators whose rights had rested in layered custom rather than an exclusive deed. More reliable taxation financed colonial rule; cadastral knowledge and courts made land easier to buy, mortgage, aggregate, and foreclose.

Those legal changes interacted with older inequalities rather than creating rural hierarchy from nothing. Late-Joseon Korea already had landlords, tenants, estate managers, debt, land sales, and contested cadasters. Under colonial rule, Korean landlords remained extremely important; Japanese individuals and companies acquired significant holdings but never became the only landlords. Rising rice production and exports created opportunities for owners and commercially equipped farmers. Irrigation fees, taxes, rent, consumption needs, and costly credit could meanwhile push smallholders into debt and sale. Many families combined tiny owned plots with rented land, wage labor, sidelines, and migration, making a clean owner/tenant binary misleading.

The result was neither development shared evenly nor extraction without production. Output, infrastructure, commercial exchange, and agricultural knowledge changed materially. So did land concentration, dependence on landlords, vulnerability to prices, and the capacity of the colonial state to reach villages. The crucial historical questions are therefore not “Was there modernization?” or “Was there exploitation?” in isolation. They are: **Who controlled the institutions that produced growth, who captured its returns, who bore its risks, and what alternatives were foreclosed by colonial sovereignty?**

## What the Land Survey Project did

The Government-General carried out the Land Survey Project (*tochi chōsa jigyō*, Korean *toji josa saeop*) from 1910 to 1918, building on preparatory surveying under the protectorate. Owners submitted declarations; surveyors measured parcels and fixed boundaries; investigation committees handled disputes; officials classified land and assessed value; cadastral maps and registers became the basis for taxation and legal title. The project was at once geographic, fiscal, juridical, and political. It translated a countryside of locally known boundaries and overlapping practices into records usable by a centralized colonial bureaucracy.

The declaration principle (*shingo shugi*) is central to the controversy. Nationalist accounts emphasized that cultivators unfamiliar with paperwork, fearful of officials, or unaware of deadlines could fail to declare, while landlords, village heads, collaborators, companies, and the state converted procedural advantage into title. Such mechanisms did occur, and the burden of navigating a coercive new administration was not socially neutral. Yet later village-level research, especially Edwin Gragert's study of colonial landownership, disputes a universal scenario in which most cultivators lost farms solely because they lacked modern documents. Declarations could be supported by local land records and testimony; much land remained in Korean ownership; and many ownership changes occurred through market sale, debt, and foreclosure after registration rather than immediate administrative confiscation.

That correction should sharpen, not erase, the colonial critique. Asking only how much land the Government-General directly seized sets too narrow a test. Formal registration selected among claims. A person recognized as owner gained a stronger alienable right; a tenant's continuing cultivation, a widow's practical control, a lineage or village's shared use, and claims to uncultivated commons might receive weaker recognition. State land, royal or institutional holdings, forests, and supposedly ownerless land raised distinct disputes. The legal categories did not merely record a self-evident social reality: they reorganized it.

Nor was “custom” outside politics. Colonial officials investigated Korean customs and decided which would be recognized, often translating flexible or relational practices into rules compatible with an imperial legal order. Local notables and clerks mediated declarations and knew whose testimony counted. Litigation required time, knowledge, and money. The resulting title could circulate in markets whose participants possessed very unequal capital. Legal clarity for a purchaser or lender could mean enforceable dispossession for a debtor.

The fiscal purpose was explicit. A reliable cadaster permitted the state to calculate and collect land tax more consistently and helped provide revenue for the colonial apparatus. The 1914 land-tax revision and the completed survey connected assessed value to a more regular fiscal base. This differed from older Joseon fiscal measurement in form and administrative reach, even though the peninsula did not begin with an unmapped or untaxed countryside. As [[Joseon land taxation from gwajeon to daedong]] shows, Korean states had long maintained land registers and repeatedly struggled over measurement, classification, concealment, and incidence. Colonial claims to have brought order to chaos were therefore self-legitimating as well as descriptive.

## From registered property to agrarian inequality

Registration did not mechanically turn owners into tenants. The path ran through several linked markets. Secure, transferable title made land better collateral. Credit could fund seed, fertilizer, irrigation assessments, taxes, or household survival; it could also end in foreclosure. Owners positioned to borrow on favorable terms could enlarge operations. Small cultivators often relied on expensive private credit and had little reserve against harvest failure or price decline. A household might sell land and lease it back, preserving cultivation while losing the asset and bargaining power.

Japanese settlement and corporate acquisition mattered within this process. The Oriental Development Company, established in 1908 with state support, acquired land, financed settlement, and became a prominent landlord. Japanese settlers and firms enjoyed networks, legal familiarity, and access to capital unavailable on equal terms to most Koreans. Yet an ethnic two-class model—Japanese landlord against Korean tenant—also obscures Korean landed elites, estate managers (*mareum*), merchants, moneylenders, and better-off cultivators who used the colonial order. Colonial inequality worked through both ethnic hierarchy and class relations within Korean society.

Tenancy itself covered different arrangements. Full tenants cultivated entirely rented land; owner-tenants combined owned and rented parcels. Rent might be paid as a share of the crop or in a fixed amount, and contracts varied by region, crop, irrigation, and bargaining strength. Share rent divided some harvest risk but allowed landlords or managers to dispute yields and deductions. Fixed rent made the cultivator bear more price and harvest risk. Short or insecure terms discouraged tenants from making improvements whose returns a landlord could capture by raising rent or replacing them.

Headline tenancy ratios require caution. Colonial surveys changed categories and coverage, and historians reproduce different series. The broad trend is secure: tenancy and owner-tenancy together encompassed a large and generally growing share of farm households, while pure owner-cultivators became a minority in many published series. Exact endpoints depend on whether mixed owner-tenants are classified separately and on which survey years are comparable. Ratios also hide farm size: a nominal “owner” might possess too little land to survive without renting more or selling labor.

This distribution shaped more than income. Landownership supplied collateral and local status, buffered shocks, and helped families finance education. Bogang Jun and Tai-Yoo Kim find that districts with more unequal landownership had lower public-school attainment during the colonial period, even after accounting for observed differences. Their econometric result does not prove every household mechanism, but it shows how agrarian inequality could reproduce unequal human capital rather than remain confined to rent day.

## Rice production, imperial trade, and who ate the gains

The Rice Production Increase Plans of the 1920s linked Korea more tightly to Japan's food economy. Japan's urbanization and the 1918 rice riots made stable supplies politically urgent. Colonial policy promoted irrigation and drainage, land improvement, seed and fertilizer use, and agricultural extension. Railways, ports, warehouses, banks, and merchant networks moved grain and price information. Korean rice output rose, though results varied across phases and fell short of some official targets; shipments to Japan expanded substantially.

This was not simple physical plunder in which soldiers removed a fixed harvest without payment. Rice was sold through markets. But calling the flow ordinary trade is equally inadequate. The market had been constructed inside an empire: Japan controlled tariffs, currency, transport priorities, finance, agricultural policy, and police power. Landlords and merchants could sell rice at favorable prices while cultivators met rent obligations and purchased cheaper grains for consumption. Aggregate production or export values therefore do not reveal the welfare of a tenant household.

Investment also distributed costs. Irrigation associations could increase yields and land values, but fees and debt service burdened fields whether or not a small cultivator had shaped the project. Landlords often captured part of the gain through rent. Fertilizer and improved seed required cash or credit. A household with secure land and reserves could adopt inputs and withstand a bad year; an indebted tenant could be pushed out by the same commercialization that raised regional output.

Price movements reinforced this asymmetry. High rice prices could reward sellers but raise food costs for net buyers. The depression-era collapse in agricultural prices damaged landlords too, yet poorer households had fewer assets and less favorable credit. Research on farm accounts in the 1920s and early 1930s finds that households with little or no land deteriorated faster and on a larger scale. Families responded by cutting consumption, borrowing, combining farm and nonfarm work, sending members to cities or Japan, or migrating to Manchuria. Migration was simultaneously an escape from rural constraint, a search for opportunity, and a source of vulnerable labor for the imperial economy.

The distribution problem is why the long-running “colonial growth” debate cannot be resolved by selecting one aggregate. Some economic historians have reconstructed rising output, population, industrial production, and aspects of consumption, correcting older series and arguing that average living standards did not uniformly collapse. Others emphasize weak rural consumption, anthropometric evidence, land loss, unequal ownership, coerced transfers, and the imperial destination of surpluses. Statistical disagreement matters: colonial data vary in coverage, price deflators, imputations, and incentives. But even a well-measured rise in average output is compatible with severe insecurity among tenants. Means do not disclose distribution, and a colony's national accounts do not answer the moral or counterfactual question of development under Korean sovereignty.

## Tenant conflict and the limits of colonial reform

Tenants were not passive. Disputes over rent levels, deductions, eviction, irrigation charges, and estate management generated petitions, rent withholding, collective bargaining, demonstrations, and organized tenant unions. The somewhat wider associational space after the [[The March First Movement as local and global history|March First Movement]] allowed labor and tenant activism to grow during the 1920s, although police surveillance and repression remained decisive. Socialists, nationalists, religious organizers, local intellectuals, and cultivators brought different languages to agrarian conflict. An immediate quarrel over rent could become a critique of landlordism or empire; conversely, broad ideology depended on specific village grievances.

The prominent 1923–1924 tenancy dispute on Amtae Island illustrates the pattern. Tenants organized against a large Korean landlord, refused or contested high rents, and used collective action and public pressure. The conflict matters precisely because the landlord was Korean: colonial agrarian politics cannot be reduced to bilateral ethnic opposition. Yet the Japanese colonial police and courts structured the arena in which Korean landlord and Korean tenant confronted one another. Class conflict unfolded inside colonial sovereignty.

By the early 1930s, agrarian depression and rural protest made tenancy a governance problem. The Government-General promoted conciliation bodies, rural rehabilitation, household discipline, savings, subsidiary production, and cooperatives. Such programs sometimes delivered material help or mediated disputes. Their language of self-help also recast structural problems—unequal land, rent burdens, volatile prices, and debt—as deficiencies of peasant habits.

The 1934 Korean Farm Tenancy Ordinance (*Chōsen nōchirei*, Korean *Joseon nongjiryeong*) formally regulated agricultural leases. It treated disguised cultivation contracts as leases, required notification and possible removal of estate managers, generally set minimum terms of three years (seven for perennial crops), provided for succession of contractual rights, and regulated county, city, and island tenancy committees and procedures alongside a mediation apparatus whose immediate predecessor dated to 1932. These were meaningful legal recognitions that tenancy could not be left wholly to landlord discretion.

The ordinance did not redistribute land or equalize bargaining power. Its provisions contained exceptions, enforcement depended on colonial local officials, and a tenant challenging a landlord still faced dependence, cost, and possible retaliation. Article 12 nevertheless strengthened a tenant's position by making an unregistered farm lease effective against a person who later acquired a real right once possession or delivery had occurred. The regime sought rural stability and productive discipline, not tenant sovereignty. Reform addressed disruptive practices while preserving landlord property and the agricultural order on which tax and rice policy depended.

## Social experience beyond the model farm household

Colonial statistics commonly organize agriculture around a male-headed farm household, but land relations were lived through gender and generation. Women's labor in transplanting, weeding, harvesting, food processing, sericulture, household management, and market exchange was essential while often undercounted. Property and household-registration rules shaped women's formal claims; widowhood, marriage, and patrilineal inheritance affected practical control. When men migrated for work, women could manage farms while remaining legally or statistically subordinate.

Children's schooling competed with field labor and fees. Young people left villages for domestic service, factories, construction, mines, and overseas destinations. Estate managers stood between absentee landlords and tenants, collecting rent and enforcing local authority; their intimate knowledge could facilitate negotiation or abuse. Regional ecology mattered as well. Paddy zones with irrigation and export access differed from dry-field regions, mountain communities, and the northern frontier. A national tenancy rate flattens these differences.

Rural change also altered political imagination. Tenant organization taught meeting procedure, solidarity, petitioning, and confrontation. Migration carried agrarian grievances into urban labor movements and diaspora communities. Land and rent therefore belong to the history of anticolonial politics, not merely an economic appendix. After liberation, demands for land reform drew force from decades of landlord-tenant conflict. Northern reform in 1946 and southern reform legislated in 1949–1950 took different political forms, but both confronted a colonial agrarian inheritance. The upheaval surrounding liberation and [[Political violence before the Korean War]] cannot be understood without it.

## Historiography: beyond confiscation versus modernization

Three interpretive corrections should be held together.

First, late-Joseon rural society was neither communally egalitarian nor legally blank. Land markets, private claims, tenancy, and cadasters preceded annexation. Korean landlords did not appear because Japanese surveyors invented them. This continuity limits any claim that the survey alone caused every later inequality.

Second, continuity does not make colonial formalization neutral. Japan annexed Korea by force, chose the legal categories, staffed the decisive institutions, and directed fiscal and developmental policy toward imperial priorities. A survey can clarify title and dispossess; irrigation can raise output and increase debt; a railway can integrate markets and carry exports. These are not contradictions when benefits and powers are distributed unequally.

Third, “development” is not a verdict. The strongest scholarship separates measurement from legitimation. It asks whether output rose, then asks who owned assets, consumed output, paid costs, controlled policy, and bore coercion. It does not infer benevolence from a railway or deny material change because the regime was illegitimate. It also treats the sovereign-Korea counterfactual honestly: Korea might have modernized along other paths, but no archive reveals the exact growth it would have achieved without annexation.

The archive itself requires cross-reading. Government-General statistical yearbooks, survey reports, cadastral maps, ordinances, and court files reveal institutional intent and measurable categories, but they were produced by the ruling power. A “resolved” tenancy dispute may mean administrative closure rather than tenant satisfaction. Registered ownership excludes claims the registration system did not name. Output statistics may be stronger than household-consumption estimates. Korean newspapers and movement records recover protest but were censored and politically positioned. Estate accounts reveal calculation from above; petitions and oral histories reveal experiences omitted from official series but have their own selection and memory limits.

The best conclusion is consequently institutional and distributive. The survey gave the colonial state a powerful fiscal map and made exclusive title more enforceable. Markets, finance, and rice policy then operated through unequal starting positions. Some Korean owners and cultivators gained, Japanese capital acquired privileged footholds, output rose in important dimensions, and many tenants experienced high rents, debt, insecurity, and compelled mobility. Colonial rule did not freeze Korean agriculture. It transformed it in ways inseparable from conquest and unequal power.

- How did effective rent after deductions and irrigation charges vary between Japanese-owned and Korean-owned estates?
- Can household-level consumption and anthropometric evidence be reconciled with revised aggregate growth estimates without assuming one national trajectory?
- Which practices created after 1910 survived into North and South Korean cadasters, courts, cooperatives, and post-liberation land reform?
