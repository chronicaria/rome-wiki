`Nobi` named a hereditary, legally subordinated status whose members could be owned, transferred, and inherited, but its changing forms cannot be understood by mechanically substituting either “slave” or “serf.”

Up: [[Korean history (MOC)]]

## The translation is an argument

Calling *nobi* “slaves” immediately places Korean history in a global history of people treated as property. Calling them “serfs” instead emphasizes that many lived apart from an owner, farmed or worked on their own account, transmitted property, formed families, and rendered specified dues rather than working continuously under direct supervision. Neither choice is innocent. “Slave” can make readers import the racial, plantation, and Atlantic features of one particular slave system; “serf” can conceal sale, inheritance, natal status, coercion, and an owner's legally recognized claims over a person.

The safest practice is therefore to keep *nobi* visible as the historical category and then describe the relationship being discussed. Was the person privately or publicly owned? Resident in an owner's household or living independently? Providing labor, cloth, grain, or money? Recorded in a household register, inheritance division, sale document, lawsuit, or moralizing elite text? A translation should follow that evidence instead of deciding the institution in advance.

The dispute matters beyond vocabulary. It asks what features define slavery across societies. If sale and hereditary ownership are decisive, many *nobi* were enslaved even when they possessed land or negotiated their obligations. If social death, pervasive direct control, or exclusion from family and property are treated as necessary, some Korean cases look unlike ideal-typical chattel slavery. Comparative history is most useful when it tests such criteria rather than ranking suffering or declaring that one society was “really” free of slavery.

## A status reproduced through law and households

The institution crossed the Goryeo–Joseon dynastic boundary. Goryeo law and elite practice already recognized inherited low status and ownership claims, while the Joseon state systematized status, registration, adjudication, and service within a more extensively documented legal order. This continuity makes *nobi* a bridge between [[Goryeo as a plural aristocratic and Buddhist polity]] and Joseon's state and family institutions, but it was not an unchanged survival. Rules of descent, the balance of public and private ownership, service burdens, opportunities for manumission, and the state's capacity to find and register people changed over time.

Joseon distinguished government-owned *gongnobi* from privately owned *sanobi*. It also distinguished people residing with an owner from “outside-resident” people who commonly maintained separate households and remitted dues. These axes overlapped but were not identical. A government *nobi* might owe service to an office; a privately held outside-resident *nobi* might farm far from the owner and deliver an annual payment; a resident household *nobi* might work under immediate command. Treating the most autonomous case as representative minimizes coercion, while treating the most tightly controlled household case as universal erases the institution's economic diversity.

Ownership was nevertheless more than metaphor. *Nobi* appeared in transactions and inheritance divisions and could be separated among heirs. A 1405 entry in the *Taejong sillok*, for example, regulated the documentation used when *nobi* were inherited, direct evidence that the state recognized claims transmitted through family property division. Legal status could pass to children. Owners possessed claims enforceable through state institutions, even though law also regulated some treatment and people in *nobi* status could appear in litigation. Regulation did not cancel ownership: slave systems elsewhere also constrained some actions by owners without making the enslaved free.

Descent rules were politically and economically consequential because a child's classification allocated future labor, dues, and taxable population. The general tendency toward inherited status made marriage and maternity matters of state revenue and household wealth. Rules were revised, contested, evaded, and inconsistently recorded. A summary claim about whether status followed the mother, the father, or the lower-status parent must therefore be dated rather than projected across five centuries. A rule tried in 1669 and definitively established in 1731 (Yeongjo 7) made the child of a male *nobi* and a free or commoner woman follow the mother and therefore remain free. It did not newly decree that every child's status followed the mother. The measure reduced one route by which mixed-status unions reproduced slavery and exposed tension between ownership claims and the state's demographic and fiscal interests; see [[Descent law and mixed-status families in Joseon]].

## Work was heterogeneous, unfreedom was shared

There was no single *nobi* occupation. Resident household workers cooked, cleaned, carried messages, cultivated land, produced goods, accompanied owners, or performed skilled tasks. Rural outside-resident *nobi* might manage farms and deliver stipulated products. Government-owned people served central or local offices, supplied labor or goods, and sometimes lived far from the institution claiming them. Women performed domestic and productive labor and also faced sexual vulnerability that administrative categories rarely describe adequately.

Some *nobi* accumulated movable wealth, cultivated land, or even held claims over other *nobi*. That apparent paradox is crucial. Property-holding demonstrates that Korean legal and economic life did not map neatly onto a free/unfree binary derived from another society. It does not by itself prove the absence of slavery. Enslaved people in many systems acquired possessions, traded, hired themselves out, or purchased freedom while remaining legally owned. The analytical question is not whether agency existed but under what constraints, at whose sufferance, and with what risk of separation or confiscation.

Outside residence could give a person substantial control over daily labor. It could also serve an owner's interest by shifting subsistence costs onto the subordinated household while preserving annual claims. Distance created room for concealment, flight, negotiation, or the practical weakening of ownership, but it also generated hunts for absconders and disputes over registers. Autonomy and extraction were not opposites: a decentralized regime could be economically useful precisely because it did not require constant supervision.

The institution also varied regionally. Household-register studies can reconstruct status transmission and mobility with unusual precision, but a village or island sample cannot simply stand for the peninsula. Registers themselves were administrative representations rather than transparent censuses. Families sought advantageous classifications, officials had uneven information and incentives, and status labels could lag behind lived circumstances. Quantitative work is strongest when it treats the register as both data and a product of governance.

## Agency without romanticization

People under *nobi* status fled, litigated, negotiated obligations, formed families, accumulated resources, purchased or received manumission, and sometimes exploited gaps between local knowledge and central records. Owners manumitted people for payment, service, moral reasons, or family relationships. Wartime dislocation and destroyed registers could loosen claims. State policy could also transform status on a large scale.

These actions should be recovered because elite laws and inheritance documents otherwise reduce people to assets. Yet “agency” must not become a euphemism for freedom. A person who bargains over dues does so within an unequal relation; flight reveals both resistance and the danger that made escape necessary. Exceptional wealth or upward mobility should not be allowed to represent those whose lives left fewer records. Sources disproportionately preserve disputes worth recording, prosperous households capable of documentation, and the concerns of owners and officials.

Gender exposes the limits of property-centered records. Rules assigning children's status made women's bodies sites at which household reproduction, ownership, patrilineal ideology, and state policy met. Children born across status lines could become the subject of disputes between an owner's economic claim and a father's lineage interest. Scholarship on parental compassion and law shows that affect was not outside politics: elite fathers' efforts to alter children's status could produce reforms while leaving enslaved mothers' vulnerability intact. A family-centered archive can therefore illuminate intimacy and simultaneously reproduce the viewpoint of those with standing to petition.

## Why the system declined

No single decree explains the long contraction of *nobi* slavery. Changes in labor markets, commutation of service into payments, flight, concealment, manumission, mixed-status families, altered descent rules, expanding claims to commoner or elite status, war and demographic disruption, and the state's fiscal priorities all weakened older forms. The government had to choose between preserving ownership claims and enlarging a legible population from which it could draw taxes and military obligations.

The 1801 emancipation was a major institutional break of specific scope: 66,067 palace and central-government *naesi nobi*—36,974 attached to the royal household and 29,093 to central offices—were made commoners, and their registers were burned. It did not include every provincial-government, school, post-station, or privately owned *nobi*. Nor did formal reform instantly reorder local hierarchy. Household-register research from two Jeju villages finds that descendants of *nobi* great-grandfathers faced reduced odds of attaining high recorded status deep into the nineteenth century. That regional sample should not stand for the peninsula, but it demonstrates how disadvantage could outlive legal change.

Private ownership and residual status distinctions weakened through the nineteenth century. The 1894 Gabo reform resolutions then abolished laws concerning public and private *nobi* and prohibited buying and selling people; see [[Gabo Reform causal map]]. Again, law and life moved at different speeds. Employers, tenants, dependents, former owners, and former *nobi* entered new contractual and social relationships shaped by older distributions of land, literacy, lineage recognition, and reputation. The end of a legal category could leave the advantages accumulated through it intact.

Decline should not be narrated as an automatic triumph of Neo-Confucian morality or linear modernization. Joseon's moral order had long accommodated inherited subordination. Reform emerged from conflicts among fiscal capacity, household interests, labor arrangements, changing family law, and new political claims. Nor should foreign pressure alone receive causal priority: external concepts and the 1894 reform context mattered, but internal erosion and status mobility had a much longer history.

## The historiographical fault line

Older Korean scholarship often translated *nobi* through “serfdom,” partly because many outside-resident people possessed households and economic room absent from familiar images of plantation slavery. Revisionist and comparative scholarship has insisted that sale, inheritance, coercive ownership, and hereditary status justify the word slavery. The strongest recent work does not deny variation; it argues that variation exists within slavery rather than automatically beyond it.

Global comparison helps if it is symmetrical. Atlantic slavery should not become the universal template from which every difference counts as an exemption, and Korean evidence should not be sanitized to defend a national past. At the same time, calling Joseon a slave society does not mean that race organized status in the same way as in the modern Atlantic, that all *nobi* worked in gangs, or that every owner exercised identical power. Comparison should specify dimensions: legal alienability, descent, labor extraction, residence, family recognition, routes to freedom, state enforcement, violence, and the political economy of ownership.

Nationalist teleology distorts both directions. One version minimizes *nobi* because a long Korean slavery history appears shameful or insufficiently modern. Another uses a sensational claim—such as an uninterrupted, uniquely long slavery system—to flatten major institutional changes and turn history into accusation. A source-critical account neither protects nor prosecutes the nation. It reconstructs how particular people were classified, compelled, and able to act at particular times.

## Reading the evidence

Legal codes show normative classifications and remedies, not automatic compliance. Household registers reveal official status at repeated intervals but include strategic reporting and uneven enforcement. Sale and inheritance documents demonstrate alienability especially clearly, yet disproportionately represent claims that owners wanted recognized. Diaries can expose work, intimacy, punishment, and negotiation inside households, although the writer is usually the elite manager. Petitions and lawsuits preserve subordinated voices only through formulae and institutions that selected what could be said.

For that reason, the best reconstruction triangulates. A legal rule should be tested against registers and cases; a demographic trend against local archival practice; an owner's diary against the labor and family arrangements it inadvertently records. Translated excerpts are evidence of a text's wording in an edition, not self-validating evidence that the narrated event occurred exactly as stated. The same caution that governs [[Using the Goryeosa as political evidence]] applies to Joseon administrative abundance: more documents can produce a more detailed official viewpoint without producing neutrality.
