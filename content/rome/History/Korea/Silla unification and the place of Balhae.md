Silla's victories of 660–676 created an unprecedented peninsular state, but “unification” is a qualified description: Silla did not inherit most of Goguryeo's northern lands, and from 698 Balhae formed a second successor polity across northern Korea and Manchuria.

## What was unified?

“Unified Silla” usually names the period from Silla's seventh-century conquests to its surrender to Goryeo in 935. Even its starting date varies. **668** marks the destruction of Goguryeo by the Silla–Tang coalition, whereas **676** marks Silla's success in forcing Tang forces out of their principal positions south of the Taedong River after several more years of war. The first date emphasizes the defeat of the Three Kingdoms rivals; the second emphasizes the creation of an autonomous Silla-led order on most of the peninsula. Neither date means that a timeless Korean nation simply attained its natural borders.

What Silla accomplished was nevertheless enormous. Before the 660s, Silla, Baekje, and Goguryeo were rival states with distinct courts, aristocracies, regional bases, and diplomatic strategies. Their later treatment as three branches of one nation should not be projected backward as if political union were inevitable. Silla first secured the southeastern part of the peninsula, absorbed the Gaya polities by the mid-sixth century, and competed with Baekje and Goguryeo for the Han River basin. Its eventual victory depended not only on internal strength but also on a contingent alliance with Tang China, factional choices at multiple courts, and prolonged military campaigns.

The result by 676 was the first durable government to command most of the Korean peninsula from a single capital, Gyeongju. It brought the former Baekje heartland and much of the southern portion of former Goguryeo under Silla rule. It did **not** place all territory once ruled by Goguryeo under Silla. Much of Manchuria and the northern peninsula remained outside Silla's effective administration; Tang initially claimed former Baekje and Goguryeo territory through protectorates and commanderies, and Balhae later emerged across a large part of the northern zone. “Unified” is therefore defensible if its object is *most of the peninsula*, but misleading if it is understood as all former Three Kingdoms territory or a complete ethnonational union.

## Alliance, conquest, and war with Tang

Silla's success grew out of a strategic bargain. Facing pressure from Baekje and Goguryeo, King Muyeol and the general Kim Yusin worked with Tang emperor Gaozong's government. The coalition destroyed Baekje in **660**. Restoration forces continued to resist, and the defeat in **663** of a Baekje–Japanese force at the Battle of Baekgang helped end the most serious restoration attempt. The allies then turned again against Goguryeo, whose internal divisions after the death of the powerful military ruler Yeon Gaesomun compounded external pressure. Pyongyang fell in **668**.

Coalition victory exposed a basic incompatibility. Silla expected territorial gains and security; Tang attempted to administer conquered areas through institutions such as the Protectorate General to Pacify the East and proposed administrative arrangements that subordinated even Silla in the imperial map. These were claims and institutions whose actual reach varied; they should not be confused with seamless occupation of every mapped district. Silla backed resistance in former Baekje and Goguryeo territory and fought its former ally. The fighting conventionally called the Silla–Tang War ran from about **670 to 676**. Silla's victory at Maeso in 675 and naval success near the Geum River estuary at Gibeolpo in 676 are treated as decisive episodes. Tang relocated its main Goguryeo protectorate northward, while Silla consolidated control to roughly the Taedong River region.

This sequence complicates two simple stories. Silla did not “liberate” an already unified nation from China: it had invited a powerful external ally to defeat rival states and then resisted that ally when their goals diverged. Nor was it merely a Tang instrument. Silla leaders exploited the alliance, incorporated local forces, and ultimately defended an independent political settlement against Tang. The relationship soon normalized because neither side benefited from permanent war. Tribute missions, investiture, study, religion, books, luxury goods, and commercial exchange connected Silla closely to Tang without erasing Silla sovereignty. Premodern tribute and investiture combined hierarchy, diplomacy, and access to trade; translating them as straightforward colonial subjection misses how the system worked.

## Integration was a process, not an instant

Military victory did not automatically turn conquered populations into loyal Silla subjects. The court had to govern regions whose elites had served Baekje or Goguryeo and whose inhabitants had experienced war, displacement, and coercion. Silla incorporated some former royal and aristocratic families, relocated populations, stationed forces, reorganized local administration, and used Buddhist and Confucian institutions to give the enlarged state a shared political language. These policies mixed accommodation with hierarchy.

The older Silla bone-rank system remained central to elite status and office. That continuity helped the Gyeongju aristocracy preserve its dominance but limited full inclusion of newcomers and provincial power holders. The expanded kingdom was not a modern nation-state of equal citizens. It was a stratified monarchy in which court families, monasteries, local elites, commoners, dependent people, and enslaved or unfree groups occupied unequal positions. Former Baekje and Goguryeo identities did not disappear on a battlefield timetable, and later rebellions show that conquest could remain politically salient.

The enlarged realm nonetheless supported consequential integration. Administrative routes connected provinces to Gyeongju; taxation and labor obligations reached across a broader territory; the state fostered a learned bureaucracy through the National Confucian Academy established in **682**; and monks, envoys, students, artisans, and merchants moved through Tang-centered networks. Buddhist projects such as Bulguksa and Seokguram, substantially rebuilt or created in the eighth century, expressed royal and aristocratic power while placing Silla within a cosmopolitan East Asian Buddhist world. “Native” Silla culture and “foreign” Tang influence are poor opposites here: Silla actors selected, translated, and remade institutions and forms circulating across Asia.

Integration also shifted the peninsula's political baseline. Later rulers could imagine most of the peninsula as one governable field, even when control fractured. Yet Silla's concentration of land, office, and privilege around the capital eventually contributed to regional discontent. In the late ninth century, rebellion and the rise of powerful local figures produced the Later Three Kingdoms. Gyeon Hwon established Later Baekje, Gung Ye founded a northern regime later taken over by Wang Geon, and Silla shrank around its old core. Silla surrendered to Wang Geon's Goryeo in **935**; Goryeo defeated Later Baekje in **936**. Thus the political legacy of Silla's unification was real but neither irreversible nor institutionally complete.

## Balhae changes the map

Balhae (Korean *Balhae*, Chinese *Bohai*; older Western spelling *Parhae*) emerged in the upheaval that followed Goguryeo's fall. Dae Joyeong led a population that included people associated with former Goguryeo and Sumo Mohe groups away from Tang control. After defeating a pursuing force near Tianmenling, he founded a state conventionally dated to **698**, initially called Jin or Zhen in the sources. Tang recognized him as prince of Bohai in **713**, after which Balhae/Bohai became the lasting name used for the polity. It survived until conquest by the Khitan in **926**.

The founder's identity and the kingdom's population cannot be reduced to a clean modern ethnic label. Tang histories describe Dae Joyeong and his background differently: formulations connect him both to Goguryeo and to the Sumo Mohe. The contradiction has fueled later national claims, but it may reflect the porous, political character of identities in the borderlands rather than a fact resolvable through modern racial categories. Balhae's ruling house asserted continuity with Goguryeo, and former Goguryeo people mattered in the state. Mohe communities also formed a major part of its population, alongside other groups. Elite institutions, tombs, cities, material culture, and diplomatic language show a polity shaped by Goguryeo inheritances, Tang models, local practices, and Inner Northeast Asian conditions.

At its height Balhae controlled a wide and changing domain spanning parts of today's northeastern China, the Russian Maritime Province, and the northern Korean peninsula. Exact borders are uncertain and changed over time; neat modern maps exaggerate the precision of premodern frontiers. The state developed a multi-capital system, a court and bureaucracy strongly influenced by Tang institutions, agriculture suited to varied environments, and routes linking it to Tang, Japan, Silla, steppe and forest peoples. Balhae fought Tang in the 730s and later maintained regular diplomatic relations with it. It also sent numerous embassies to Japan. Such missions combined diplomacy, status competition, information, and trade.

Silla and Balhae were neither permanently isolated halves of one nation nor simple existential enemies. Their relationship included strategic rivalry, frontier defense, indirect contact, and participation in overlapping Tang and Japanese diplomatic systems. Tang encouraged a Silla attack on Balhae in **733**, but the expedition failed amid severe winter conditions. Later evidence points to routes and contacts across the frontier, though the record is much thinner than for each kingdom's relations with Tang or Japan. Silla sources did not treat Balhae as merely another Silla province, and Balhae acted as an autonomous kingdom. Calling them “north” and “south” is a historian's organizing frame, not proof that contemporaries imagined a single state temporarily divided in the twentieth-century sense.

Balhae's fall also shaped later history. Khitan forces conquered it in 926 and established the Dongdan kingdom. Groups of Balhae people, including crown prince Dae Gwanghyeon, subsequently entered Goryeo. Wang Geon's court received them and presented Goryeo as an heir to Goguryeo. This reception gave Balhae a place in Goryeo's claims and population, but it does not mean that every Balhae inhabitant migrated south or that Goryeo simply annexed Balhae's former territory. Many people remained under Khitan or other regional powers, and political succession diverged from demographic continuity.

## Two period labels, two analytical choices

The conventional **Unified Silla** frame foregrounds a genuine transformation: one court ruled most of the peninsula for roughly two and a half centuries, incorporated former rivals, and generated durable administrative and cultural connections. It is especially useful for studying Gyeongju's monarchy, peninsular institutions, Buddhist culture, relations with Tang, and the long consequences of the seventh-century wars. Its danger is that a convenient period label can silently expand Silla's borders northward or make conquest look like the fulfillment of an ancient national destiny.

The **Northern and Southern States period** (*Nambukguk sidae*) places Balhae and Silla in the same frame, usually from Balhae's foundation in 698 to its fall in 926. The term is associated particularly with Korean historiography and corrects a southern, peninsula-only narrative by treating Balhae as a major successor to Goguryeo and an essential actor in Northeast Asia. It directs attention toward Manchuria, the northern peninsula, migration, frontier peoples, and multiple routes of state formation.

That corrective has its own risk. “Northern and Southern States” can make two heterogeneous kingdoms look like complementary national halves, flatten Balhae's Mohe and other populations, or retroactively map modern Korean unity onto the eighth century. Chinese scholarship has often placed Bohai within a history of Chinese dynastic territory or frontier peoples, while Korean scholarship has commonly emphasized Goguryeo succession and Korean history; Russian and Japanese scholarship bring other archaeological and diplomatic archives to the problem. Modern territorial disputes and national curricula can influence each choice. No present state owns the past simply because Balhae's former lands fall within its borders.

The best practice is therefore additive rather than exclusive. Use “Unified Silla” for the specific history of Silla's enlarged peninsular polity, explicitly qualifying its territorial reach. Use “Northern and Southern States” when the question requires Balhae and the wider Northeast Asian system, explicitly refusing the implication that the two were modern national sections. For a general chronology, show both: Silla's 660–676 conquests created a new southern order; Balhae's emergence in 698 made the post-Goguryeo north a separate center of power. The overlap is not a flaw in periodization. It is the historical reality the labels are trying, imperfectly, to organize.

## Why it matters

This case is a test of how to write national history without treating the nation as eternal. Silla did achieve a major and durable political integration, and minimizing it would obscure the transformation of the peninsula after centuries of interstate competition. But maximizing it into total territorial, ethnic, or cultural unity erases Balhae, the northern borderlands, and the contingency of Silla's Tang alliance.

Keeping both Silla and Balhae in view also changes the geographic scale of [[Korean history (MOC)|Korean history]]. The relevant world was not a sealed peninsula. It included Manchurian plains and forests, the Liaodong corridor, the Russian Maritime region, Tang capitals and ports, the Japanese archipelago, and mobile peoples across Northeast Asia. States gained resources and legitimacy through these networks while asserting distinct political interests.

Finally, the terminology offers a general rule for reading historical maps: a dynasty label is an argument about what counts as the center. “Unified Silla” centers peninsular government; “Northern and Southern States” centers plural succession to the Three Kingdoms; “Tang frontier” centers imperial administration. Each reveals evidence and hides evidence. A responsible account states the scale, preserves uncertainty about borders and identities, and does not turn later national belonging into the cause of earlier events.

## Open questions

- How much can archaeology distinguish the social positions of Goguryeo-descended, Mohe, and other communities within Balhae without assuming that artifact styles map neatly onto ethnicity?
- Which routes across the Silla–Balhae frontier are securely documented, and how did their importance change between the eighth and tenth centuries?
- How differently do the *Old Book of Tang*, *New Book of Tang*, Japanese embassy records, epigraphic evidence, and later Korean histories represent Dae Joyeong's identity and Balhae's claims?
- At what level—court, province, village, monastery, or household—did former Baekje and Goguryeo identities persist most strongly under Silla rule?
