Joseon's fiscal history was not a simple shift from “tax in kind” to “tax in rice,” but a sequence of attempts to identify taxable land, separate public revenue from private claims, make assessments predictable, and control the intermediaries who stood between cultivators and the court.

## Three systems that should not be collapsed

Three reforms anchor the story, but they addressed different fiscal objects.

1. The **rank-land law** (*gwajeonbeop*, 科田法), promulgated in 1391 just before Joseon's founding, reorganized rights to receive revenue from land. It was as much a political settlement over official support and inherited claims as a tax-rate reform.
2. Sejong's **land-tax law** (*gongbeop*, 貢法), completed in 1444, tried to standardize the ordinary land tax (*jeonse*) by classifying fields and harvest conditions. It responded to disputes created by local inspection of crop damage.
3. The **Daedong law** (*daedongbeop*, 大同法), introduced in Gyeonggi in 1608 and extended province by province over roughly a century, commuted much household tribute (*gongnap*) into a land-based levy, usually collected in rice, cloth, or coin. It did not replace the ordinary land tax; it overlaid and reallocated another obligation.

Calling all three “land taxation” is defensible because each tied public extraction to registered acreage or harvest. Yet the common label can obscure the institutional change. *Gwajeon* determined who could collect assigned tax income. *Gongbeop* recalibrated how the state assessed fields. *Daedongbeop* moved a previously household-based tribute burden toward landholders and built a procurement system around the commuted revenue. The transitions therefore reveal not one linear modernization but recurring conflict over registers, assessment, collection, and incidence.

## The 1391 settlement: land did not simply become private property

Late Goryeo reform memorials described a countryside burdened by overlapping claims, powerful families, temples, and officials who diverted revenue and enlarged holdings. Such texts are indispensable evidence of a fiscal and political crisis, but they were also arguments written by victors. They cannot be read as a neutral cadastral survey. The reform coalition led in fiscal matters by Jo Jun first destroyed old public and private land registers in 1390 and then enacted the *gwajeonbeop* in 1391. As [[Why Goryeo became Joseon]] explains, the sequence made existing claims less enforceable and gave the ascendant coalition a new basis for assigning income.

Under the rank-land system, the state retained an ultimate claim over registered land and allocated **tax-collection rights**, not unrestricted modern ownership, to serving and some retired officials according to rank. Most rank land was concentrated in Gyeonggi, near the capital, where beneficiaries and government could supervise it more readily. The cultivator ordinarily remained on the field and delivered the authorized share to the assigned beneficiary. A grant therefore did not necessarily mean that an official personally farmed or possessed the soil; it meant that the state directed a portion of the field's fiscal yield to support officeholding.

This distinction matters. Older descriptions sometimes rendered *gwajeon* as a wholesale “land redistribution” that created a new equal agrarian order. The reform certainly invalidated claims and altered elite fortunes. It helped finance the new bureaucracy, constrained some old beneficiaries, and made Gyeonggi revenue legible to the new regime. But it did not distribute all land equally among peasant households, abolish tenancy, or remove other burdens. Cultivators still supported a ruling apparatus, and the line between an authorized tax right and coercive appropriation could be difficult to enforce locally.

The system also contained a structural tension. A dynasty needed revenue to compensate officials, yet hereditary or long-lived claims could drain the pool available to new officeholders. Widow-support land (*susinjeon*), orphan-support land (*hyulyangjeon*), and merit land (*gongsinjeon*) provided income to specified survivors or meritorious subjects under distinct rules, while officeholders attempted to preserve advantages after service. Over the fifteenth century the government repeatedly narrowed and revised allocations. The **office-land system** (*jikjeonbeop*) of 1466 restricted assignments to serving officials. In 1470 the state moved toward official collection and payment rather than allowing beneficiaries to collect directly; in 1556 even office-land payments were abolished. These changes did not end elite landholding. They ended a particular state assignment mechanism as private estates, tenancy, official salaries, and central taxation evolved around it.

## Sejong's *gongbeop*: measuring land and harvest

The early dynasty's ordinary land tax was tied to the productive capacity and realized harvest of fields. Before *gongbeop*, officials used field inspection—often called the damage-inspection method (*dabeom* or *sonsil dabeom*)—to determine reductions after poor harvests. In principle, inspection matched liability to actual conditions. In practice, it invited bargaining and unequal local discretion. Inspectors could understate damage, demand fees, favor powerful households, or impose the costs of assessment on cultivators. A responsive tax could thus be less predictable and less equitable than a rule-bound one.

King Sejong's government debated reform for years and unusually canvassed opinions widely among officials and local constituencies. The resulting system, completed in 1444, combined two classificatory grids. The **six grades of land** (*jeonbun yukdeung*) rated a field's expected productivity, while the **nine grades of the year** (*yeonbun gudeung*) rated annual harvest conditions. In its ideal form, the intersection of field quality and yearly yield produced a graduated assessment rather than a single nationwide rate. The unit *gyeol* was a fiscal measure of productive capacity, not a fixed modern hectare: better land required less physical area to constitute one *gyeol* than poor land. This made the system sophisticated, but it also makes modern conversions hazardous.

The reform sought a rule that was both sensitive and auditable. It did not eliminate judgment. Someone still had to survey fields, maintain registers, decide the harvest grade, recognize disaster exemptions, and distinguish cultivated land from fallow or abandoned plots. Local magistrates and clerks retained leverage over measurement and relief. The Academy of Korean Studies' account of *gongbeop* emphasizes that powers over land surveys, annual grades, disaster relief, and taxation of fallow fields could still be applied to the advantage of locally powerful people. Standardization shifted the sites of discretion; it did not make discretion disappear.

Over time, the elaborate nine-year scale was often simplified in practice. The **Yeongjeong law** of 1635 stabilized the ordinary land-tax assessment at a low rate—commonly summarized as four *du* per *gyeol*—while supplementary levies and collection costs complicated what households actually paid. It is therefore misleading to infer the total peasant burden from the nominal *jeonse* rate alone. Tribute, military cloth, corvée, transport charges, local surcharges, tenancy rents, debts, and informal exactions could matter more than the headline land tax.

## Why tribute became the seventeenth-century crisis

Joseon's fiscal system conventionally distinguished land tax, household tribute in regional products, and labor or military obligations. Tribute was meant to supply the court and offices with goods associated with particular localities: paper, cloth, fish, medicinal materials, timber, oil, ceramics, and many other items. The assignment was administrative rather than a precise reflection of what every household produced. A locality might owe an item unavailable nearby or in a quality difficult to obtain.

That mismatch fostered **tribute contracting**. Specialist suppliers, commonly called *bangnap* merchants or brokers, delivered required goods to the government and recovered payment from households. Intermediation was not inherently fraudulent: a large state needed procurement, transport, quality control, and storage. The abuse lay in monopoly and political leverage. Contractors and connected clerks could reject household goods, insist on substitution, inflate prices, or collect much more than the official obligation. Because tribute was distributed through household registers, a poor registered household with little land could owe the same assigned product as a richer household. Flight, concealment, obsolete registers, and the shifting of missing households' shares onto those who remained compounded the inequity.

The Imjin War of 1592–1598 made these weaknesses acute. War devastated fields, populations, registers, warehouses, and transport while sharply increasing military and reconstruction needs. The state faced a fiscal paradox: it needed more reliable revenue from a society whose taxable resources and administrative records had been damaged. Proposals to substitute rice for diverse tribute goods existed before and during the war, but the postwar emergency made commutation more compelling.

## The Daedong law as tax reform and procurement reform

In 1608, on the recommendation associated especially with Chief State Councillor Yi Won-ik, Gwanghaegun's government introduced the Gyeonggi **sŏnhye law**, the first durable form of what became the Daedong system. Instead of assigning most ordinary tribute by household and commodity, government assessed a standardized amount per *gyeol* of registered land. The canonical mature rate is often given as twelve *du* of rice per *gyeol*, although rates, media, and supplementary rules varied by province and period. Mountainous districts could pay cloth or coin where rice transport was impractical.

The redistribution was fundamental. Liability moved from the registered household toward the land base. A large landholder owed more; a land-poor household owed less direct tribute. This did not make the law egalitarian in every respect. Tenants might bear some or all of a landlord's assessment through rent arrangements, and officials could add transport or handling charges. Nor did the reform abolish every special tribute item, royal-office demand, local levy, or labor service. But it removed a large field of arbitrary household product assessments and made the levy easier to state, register, and compare.

Daedong also changed how the state obtained goods. Provincial Daedong offices collected the standardized revenue, while licensed procurement agents (*gongin*) supplied specified commodities to central agencies. These agents were paid from tax receipts. The distinction between abusive *bangnap* and licensed *gongin* should not be romanticized as a leap from corruption to a free market. Both were intermediaries, and official procurement could still produce privilege and collusion. The institutional improvement was that the government formalized prices, contracts, and payment streams instead of allowing contractors to recover open-ended charges directly from households.

The system therefore connected taxation to commercialization. Large rice receipts moved through granaries and waterways; goods were purchased for official use; cloth and coin increasingly served where appropriate; merchants gained regular state demand. Some economic histories treat *gongin* as catalysts of commodity production and market growth. That is plausible, particularly near procurement and transport networks, but the causal claim should be bounded. Markets had older roots, commercialization varied regionally, and a fiscal purchasing system could coerce as well as stimulate producers. Daedong created predictable demand and monetizable flows; it did not single-handedly create a national market economy.

## Why nationwide extension took a century

The law's expansion was slow because it redistributed both burdens and rents. After Gyeonggi in 1608, Gangwon adopted a version in 1623–1624. A 1623 attempt to extend the system more broadly after the Injo Restoration encountered resistance and administrative problems; implementation outside Gangwon did not simply proceed in a straight line. The decisive mid-century advocate was Kim Yuk, who argued from experience and bureaucratic calculation for extension to Chungcheong in 1651 and for further southern implementation. Jeolla followed in stages, conventionally dated 1658 for coastal districts and 1662 for inland mountain counties. Hamgyong received a related assessed system in 1666, Gyeongsang in 1677–1678, and Hwanghae in 1708. Different authoritative summaries sometimes give a neighboring year because an order, office establishment, first collection, and full operation were not always simultaneous.

Opposition cannot be reduced to ignorant conservatives resisting progress. Land-rich elites had an obvious distributive reason to object, while tribute contractors and connected officials risked losing income. Yet critics also raised real administrative questions: Was the cadastral register accurate after war? Could rice be transported without spoilage or excessive freight? Would a uniform rate burden poor-quality land? Could the treasury procure the exact goods offices needed? Regional variation required different payment media and schedules. Political faction mattered because sponsors needed appointments, royal support, and provincial compliance, but the controversy joined material interest to genuine design problems.

Kim Yuk's importance accordingly lies not only in having a reform idea but in building a coalition and an implementable apparatus. The long provincial sequence shows state capacity being assembled rather than proclaimed. Surveys, waterways, warehouses, accounting rules, local offices, procurement contracts, and credible enforcement all had to align. “Nationwide by 1708” is itself shorthand: Jeju and some frontier or exceptional districts followed distinct arrangements, and the related *daedong sangjeong* systems adapted assessment to places with little arable land.

## Who gained, who paid, and what remained outside the reform

At the level of formal incidence, Daedong shifted much tribute from households toward owners of registered land. That favored land-poor commoners and exposed larger holders to a greater levy. It also reduced the bargaining power of tribute monopolists and made official demand more predictable. These are meaningful achievements, not merely reform rhetoric.

Actual incidence depended on social relations. A tenant could pay through higher rent or separate agreements. A landlord could conceal fields, exploit outdated registers, or influence disaster exemptions. A village could divide transport costs in ways the central statute did not specify. Local clerks controlled information; magistrates balanced remittance targets against subsistence and elite pressure. Women often encountered taxation through household and property structures rather than as named political actors, while *nobi* households' burdens depended on whether they were registered, independently cultivating, or delivering produce and labor to owners. The fiscal record therefore counts fields and liable units more readily than it reveals intra-household distribution.

The reforms also left major obligations intact. Military service increasingly took fiscal forms such as cloth payments. Corvée survived in changing forms. Grain-loan administration, later notorious under the *hwangok* system, could impose repayment and surcharge burdens. By the nineteenth century, complaints grouped land tax, military tax, and grain loans as the “three administrations” (*samjeong*), whose abuses helped drive rural unrest. Daedong solved a specific tribute-procurement problem; it was not a permanent cure for unequal taxation.

## Evidence and historiography

The strongest narrative evidence comes from the *Veritable Records of the Joseon Dynasty* (*Joseon wangjo sillok*), legal and fiscal codes, royal orders, memorials, and provincial regulations. These documents let historians reconstruct proposals, rates, offices, and arguments with unusual precision. They are less direct about lived incidence. A memorial describing “the people's suffering” may report a real crisis, but it also seeks a policy outcome. A statute gives the intended rate, not proof of uniform collection. Surviving cadasters, household registers, account books, petitions, and local documents can test implementation, though coverage is regionally and socially uneven.

Older institutional histories often narrated a progression from late-Goryeo landed corruption through early-Joseon rationalization to late-Joseon commercial development. That arc identifies real changes but can become teleological. The *gwajeonbeop* was not modern private-property reform; *gongbeop* did not eliminate local discretion; Daedong was neither a single 1608 event nor a complete shift to money taxation. Conversely, emphasizing evasion and elite capture so strongly that reform becomes meaningless also misses the durable change in formal incidence, procurement, and administrative reach.

A better interpretation treats taxation as a relationship among written rule, cadastral knowledge, logistical capacity, and bargaining power. Joseon's government repeatedly sought a base that was visible enough to tax and a collection chain it could supervise. Each solution changed who possessed information and who could extract a margin. Rank land disciplined claims but generated allocation problems. Harvest grading regularized assessment but preserved survey discretion. Daedong curtailed household tribute contracting but created official procurement agents and new transport systems. Institutional change was real precisely because it relocated, rather than abolished, political conflict.

## Why it matters

This fiscal sequence explains how Joseon endured without assuming either an all-powerful Confucian bureaucracy or a static “feudal” countryside. The state could formulate intricate rules and preserve extraordinary archives, yet its revenue depended on local registers, intermediaries, landlords, cultivators, and transport. Reform advanced when court coalitions joined moral language about relieving the people to workable systems of measurement and supply.

The sequence also links dynastic politics to everyday life. The 1391 settlement helped construct the new ruling order; fifteenth-century land grading expressed early Joseon's administrative ambition; war exposed the fragility of household tribute; the century-long Daedong rollout redistributed burdens and supported fiscal markets. None can be evaluated from decree dates alone. The central question is always who was registered, who paid in practice, who carried the goods, who received the revenue, and who could appeal when the rule failed.
