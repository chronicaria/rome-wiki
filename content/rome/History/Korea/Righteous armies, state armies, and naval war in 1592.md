Joseon's recovery in 1592 came not from a vanished state being replaced by spontaneous popular resistance, but from the uneven interaction of surviving government forces, locally raised righteous armies, monk soldiers, and a navy that remained a functioning branch of provincial government.

## The question

The opening months of the Imjin War invite a simple patriotic story. Toyotomi Hideyoshi's veteran armies landed at Busan in the fourth lunar month of 1592, shattered an unprepared Joseon army, and drove King Seonjo from the capital. Ordinary Koreans then supposedly rose as *ŭibyŏng*—“righteous armies”—while Admiral Yi Sun-sin severed Japan's supply line at sea. In that version, society saved a state that had abandoned it.

The sequence contains truths but the categories mislead. “State army,” “righteous army,” and “navy” were not sealed institutions. Provincial commanders and magistrates continued fighting after the central field army collapsed. Former officials and local literati assembled men who included registered soldiers, household retainers, commoners, hunters, enslaved people, and monks. The court appointed or recognized militia leaders and tried to incorporate their forces. Yi's fleet was not a private band attached to a genius: it was a public organization built from provincial naval districts, arsenals, ports, officers, sailors, requisition systems, and coastal intelligence.

The central question is therefore not which force “won” 1592. It is how different mobilizing systems performed different tasks. Regular forces defended fortresses, preserved offices, and supplied a legal chain of command. Righteous armies made occupation locally costly and rebuilt coercive capacity where administration had fractured. Naval squadrons contested Japan's maritime freedom along the southern coast and protected the approaches to Jeolla. Their effects accumulated. None alone could expel the invaders; together they prevented rapid victory from becoming secure rule until large-scale Ming intervention changed the land war in 1593.

## Why the central defense collapsed so quickly

Joseon did possess an army. Its institutions assigned military obligations, maintained provincial commands and fortresses, appointed officers, stored weapons, and expected local forces to concentrate when danger appeared. Yet establishments on paper did not equal trained soldiers already present. Men could evade service, substitute payments, or be dispersed; rosters and actual strength diverged. Long peace had weakened readiness, while mobilization depended on officials receiving warning, gathering men and provisions, and moving them before the enemy arrived.

The Japanese invasion punished every delay. Its divisions were composed of experienced forces mobilized by powerful daimyō after decades of warfare in Japan. Matchlocks were important, but firearms alone do not explain the breakthrough. Japanese commanders combined tactical experience, aggressive tempo, and the ability to move separately along major roads. Busan and Dongnae fell rapidly. Joseon forces were defeated at Sangju and then at Tangeumdae near Chungju, where Sin Rip's destruction opened the central route. The royal court left Seoul before the invaders entered it and continued north toward the Ming frontier.

This was a collapse of the main defense plan and much of the central command network, not the instantaneous disappearance of state power. Some commanders fled; some were killed; some retained soldiers and strategic positions. The study of Left Gyeongsang Province by Korean military historian Kim Kang-sik specifically warns against the conventional picture in which government troops simply vanished and righteous armies replaced them. Provincial commander Pak Chin and local forces defended positions, regrouped, and participated in operations associated with the recoveries of Yeongcheon and Gyeongju. In some localities, men later labeled righteous soldiers were scattered regulars assembled by former officials or literati after their immediate command structure failed.

Royal flight also had two effects. It produced a legitimacy disaster: subjects saw palaces, offices, and communities exposed while the court escaped. But it kept the king and central government from capture. From the north the court could make appointments, solicit Ming aid, demand grain and labor, and authorize local commanders. The dynasty's survival preserved a recognized center around which otherwise fragmented forces could be connected. Condemning the court's failures does not require pretending that royal survival was militarily irrelevant.

## What a “righteous army” was

The term *ŭibyŏng* (義兵) asserted moral legitimacy before it described a uniform organization. “Righteous” located armed action inside a Confucian language of loyalty: subjects took up arms when normal authority could not protect ruler and realm. Local yangban and former officials often supplied leadership because their education, reputations, kinship ties, client networks, and access to grain gave them unusual mobilizing power. Famous leaders included Kwak Chae-u in Gyeongsang, Ko Kyŏng-myŏng and Kim Ch'ŏn-il in Jeolla, and Cho Hŏn in Ch'ungch'ŏng. But a leader's elite biography does not reveal the social composition of his followers.

Units drew on heterogeneous people. Local commoners supplied most manpower and provisions. Existing military personnel could reappear under a militia label. Retainers and servants followed households; hunters brought specialized knowledge and weapons; displaced people joined for protection or subsistence as well as loyalty. Buddhist monks formed armed units under leaders such as Hyujŏng (Sŏsan Taesa) and Yujŏng (Samyŏngdang), complicating any idea that Neo-Confucian Joseon mobilized through literati alone. “Civilian militia” is thus a useful shorthand only if it does not imply that members were all untrained volunteers or that their organizations stood outside public authority.

Mobilization rested on local social power. A respected leader called through academies, kin networks, villages, temples, and former officeholding connections. He needed food, weapons, intelligence, messengers, and a defensible rendezvous. Participation could embody genuine loyalty while also involving hierarchy and pressure. Local elites were able to command labor because Joseon society was unequal. Later national memory often turns righteous armies into the people acting as one; wartime diaries instead expose quarrels over credit, desertion, discipline, supplies, and relations with officials.

The diary *Godae illok*, written by the yangban militia member Chŏng Kyŏng-un, is valuable precisely because it records a participant's group identity and everyday dealings rather than a clean legend. As Nam-lin Hur argues, its writer construed loyalty through royal symbols and a besieged community while observing both Joseon and Ming actors. Such testimony shows what “righteousness” meant to one positioned participant. It cannot establish that every recruit shared the same motives.

## What local forces could—and could not—do

Righteous armies were well suited to exploiting local knowledge. They could gather intelligence, ambush small detachments, attack communications, recover minor positions, protect or conceal grain, and make movement beyond Japanese garrisons dangerous. Kwak Chae-u's activity around the Naktong River has become emblematic of this form of war. In occupied districts, even limited resistance forced Japanese commanders to protect routes and requisition parties rather than treating the countryside as automatically controlled.

Some formations joined larger actions. Forces identified as regular and righteous cooperated in the recovery of Yeongcheon in 1592. At the first siege of Jinju that autumn, magistrate Kim Si-min and government troops defended a strategic fortress while regional support and the wider resistance environment constrained the attackers. These examples make the boundary problem concrete: successful defense often depended on fortress offices and public weapons as well as locally mobilized men.

Local forces nevertheless faced severe limits. Their size in later accounts is difficult to verify. They varied enormously in training, arms, cohesion, and duration. Rival leaders did not automatically accept a common command. A militia able to harass a column might be destroyed in a set-piece engagement against disciplined firearm troops. Ko Kyŏng-myŏng and Cho Hŏn died with many followers in costly fighting. Nor could scattered units by themselves retake Pyongyang, destroy the main Japanese armies, or sustain national logistics. Their strategic contribution was cumulative: they narrowed the invader's practical control and bought time, rather than producing a single decisive victory.

The state soon tried to recognize, rank, supply, and coordinate leaders. This conferred authority but also exposed tensions. Officials worried about unauthorized armed men, while militia leaders criticized officials who had fled or failed. Integration could improve supply and command; it could also subordinate local initiative to the institutions whose collapse had prompted it. The process is better described as reconstruction of the state through local mobilization than as a contest between “people” and “government.”

## Why the navy followed a different trajectory

Joseon's naval forces did not suffer the same initial operational collapse as the central land army. The invasion columns landed on the southeastern coast and advanced inland, while the Left Naval Command of Jeolla under Yi Sun-sin remained outside the first shock. Yi had used the prewar warning period to inspect readiness, discipline crews, repair ships, and gather information. He then coordinated with the Right Jeolla fleet under Yi Ŏk-ki and, less consistently, with Wŏn Kyun's damaged Right Gyeongsang command.

This institutional geography mattered. Jeolla was not yet occupied, its ports and administrative apparatus remained available, and its naval commanders could choose when to concentrate. Korean warships were built as fighting platforms carrying cannon; Japanese transport and combat vessels carried experienced soldiers and matchlock troops but were often most dangerous when they could close and board. Korean commanders generally sought to exploit artillery, maneuver, and local waters while avoiding a boarding contest. The famous “turtle ship” was one specialized component, not a wonder weapon or the bulk of the fleet.

Yi's *Nanjung ilgi* (*War Diary*) strips away the heroic shorthand. Its entries attend to reconnaissance, weather, alarms, punishment, illness, provisions, meetings, and fleet movement. That mundane record shows why command quality mattered: victory was produced through repeated preparation and information, not one inspired invention. The diary is also a personal command document. It gives privileged access to Yi's world but not an impartial view of Wŏn Kyun, court politics, or Japanese intentions. The [[Using the Joseon annals as political evidence|Joseon annals]] add reports and official judgments, yet they too preserve accusations made within wartime struggles over blame.

In May and June 1592, Yi's squadrons struck Japanese shipping around Okp'o, Sach'ŏn, Tangp'o, and Tanghangp'o. In the seventh lunar month the allied Jeolla fleets won their most important 1592 engagement near Hansan Island, then attacked at Angolp'o. Later operations reached toward the Japanese base complex at Busan. Exact ship-loss claims differ between positioned records, and victory lists can disguise the repeated reconnaissance and repair between actions. The secure conclusion is that the Joseon fleet retained the initiative over broad stretches of the southern coastal theater and imposed serious losses.

## What naval victory changed

Japan's invasion depended on crossing from Kyushu to bases around Busan and maintaining the southern lodgment. Yi did not abolish that Korea Strait connection. Japanese forces continued receiving men and matériel through defended southeastern ports, and the main land columns had already advanced using roads and local requisition. It is therefore too strong to say that one battle “cut the Japanese supply line” completely.

The navy changed the more ambitious maritime possibilities. Unescorted movement westward along Korea's indented southern coast became hazardous. Japanese commanders could not freely carry bulk supplies around the peninsula toward the Yellow Sea or support the northern advance from the west coast. Control of those approaches also helped shield Jeolla, whose grain, people, and surviving government became crucial to continued resistance. Hansan should be understood as operational denial: it restricted the invader's choices and preserved a resource base, rather than starving every Japanese soldier immediately.

Naval action also interacted with local war. Coastal residents supplied intelligence, sailors, labor, food, and repair materials. Refugees and reports told commanders where ships gathered. Provincial officials requisitioned resources; military discipline could burden the same population being defended. The fleet's public supply system linked battlefield performance to taxation and administration. This is one reason the navy cannot be separated from “the state,” even though Yi later became a national hero portrayed as fighting despite his government.

## Interaction rather than a three-part scorecard

By late 1592, Japan held Seoul, Pyongyang, and major corridors and garrisons, but conquest had become fragmented. Naval pressure protected the southwest and denied easy coastal expansion. Local resistance attacked the connective tissue of occupation. Surviving regular forces held or recovered strategic nodes and provided offices through which men and supplies could be organized. The court maintained a claimant government and secured Ming intervention. Each contribution made the others more useful.

The complementarity is easiest to see through logistics. A navy could make coastal shipment dangerous, but it could not prevent invaders requisitioning rice inland. Local forces could hide grain and attack foragers, but without protected regions they lacked durable bases. Regular officials could issue orders, but without community cooperation their rosters were fiction. Ming armies could challenge Japan's main formations, but their arrival and maintenance depended on a surviving Joseon government, Korean information, transport, and food. The larger [[Imjin War causal map]] therefore treats the stall as a system of constraints, not as the achievement of one savior.

The same framework corrects two opposite moral simplifications. The first says a heroic people and Yi rescued Korea from a useless court. It erases functioning provincial institutions and turns unequal local leadership into spontaneous nationalism. The second credits only formal states and dismisses militia as militarily marginal. It mistakes the capture of cities for control of territory and misses how occupation depends on daily access to food, roads, labor, and intelligence. Local armed action mattered even when it did not win large battles.

## Memory and the source problem

Righteous-army commemoration served later political needs. Joseon rewarded loyal service and enshrined selected figures; lineages preserved claims to honor. Under Japanese colonial rule and during twentieth-century nation-building, the category offered a genealogy of popular resistance extending from 1592 to later anti-Japanese militias. Yi Sun-sin likewise became an exceptionally powerful national symbol. These memories preserve real resistance, but they select heroes, clarify blurred affiliations, and invite modern national feeling into a sixteenth-century social order.

The archive is structurally uneven. Court records see people when officials report them. Elite diaries disproportionately preserve literate leaders. Meritorious-subject claims reward the inflation of service, while later local histories seek prestigious ancestors. Japanese records may count ships and opponents through their own reporting incentives. Common recruits, coerced carriers, women provisioning households, enslaved participants, and refugees appear only intermittently. Numbers that seem precise should therefore be treated cautiously unless a study reconstructs their provenance.

This uncertainty does not make synthesis impossible. Claims are strongest when kept at the right scale: government defense fractured but persisted unevenly; righteous-army formations were socially mixed and often elite-led; local resistance obstructed occupation; the Jeolla navy retained organizational continuity and denied Japan freedom along the western southern coast; and these pressures did not substitute for the Ming land intervention that followed. More exact claims—total militia strength, the motives of an entire unit, or how much food a naval victory prevented from moving—require regional records rather than patriotic aggregate figures.

## Why it matters and where it fits

This case changes how state capacity should be imagined. A premodern state was not either present or absent. It could lose its capital and main field army while continuing through royal authority, provincial offices, fortresses, naval districts, local elites, temples, and fiscal claims. In 1592, social networks rebuilt military capacity at the edges, while the least-disrupted branch of the armed forces operated from a protected province. Recovery was decentralized without being stateless.

The mobilization also carried costs forward. Wartime extraction damaged communities and registers already devastated by invasion. Rewarding service altered careers and lineage claims. Military lessons encouraged firearms training and new central formations explored in [[The seventeenth-century Korean musketry revolution]]. The problem of financing defense fed the longer fiscal reconstruction traced in [[Joseon land taxation from gwajeon to daedong]]. Finally, the inability of any Korean formation to remove the principal invasion armies unaided explains why Ming intervention became both indispensable during this war and a durable moral memory before [[The Manchu invasions and Joseon's place in the Qing order]].

## Open questions

- Can county-level rosters distinguish registered soldiers who joined *ŭibyŏng* formations from new civilian recruits without accepting later merit claims at face value?
- How did women, enslaved people, and lower-status carriers experience or negotiate mobilization in the best-preserved regional diaries?
- Which surviving provisioning accounts permit a quantitative estimate of how much Yi Sun-sin's 1592 operations reduced coastal shipment, as distinct from their deterrent effect?
- How differently did local communities remember militia leaders who were incorporated into state command and those who remained institutionally marginal?
