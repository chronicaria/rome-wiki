Korean movement under Japanese rule was neither a single voluntary exodus nor one undifferentiated deportation: changing combinations of rural pressure, wage opportunity, family strategy, border policing, racial hierarchy, and wartime compulsion carried people to Japan, Manchuria, and Sakhalin and made mobility itself an instrument of empire.

## One colony, several migration systems

The annexation of Korea in 1910 did not simply remove a border and create free movement within one Japanese state. It placed Koreans under the sovereignty of the emperor while preserving unequal legal, political, and social distinctions between the metropole and colonial subjects. Railways and steamships made movement faster; police, employers, labor brokers, residence rules, and border authorities decided who could move, on what terms, and with what protection. A Korean might formally be a Japanese subject yet still be screened at a port, segregated in housing, paid less, or treated as an alien neighbor.

“Migration across the Japanese empire” therefore names several connected but nonidentical circuits. Movement to metropolitan Japan was chiefly urban and industrial, though it also included students, merchants, domestic workers, and family members. Movement into Manchuria was older than annexation and remained heavily agrarian, crossing a frontier claimed and administered by Chinese states before being increasingly reorganized by Japanese power after 1931. Migration to southern Sakhalin—Japanese Karafuto—was tied especially to mining, forestry, construction, and wartime labor demand. Other Koreans moved through imperial cities and ports such as Osaka, Tokyo, Shimonoseki, Dalian, and Fengtian, or onward to China, the Russian Far East, Hawai‘i, and North America.

These routes belonged to one imperial political economy but cannot be collapsed into one cause. A tenant household sending a son to Osaka in 1925, a farming family crossing into Manchuria, and a miner dispatched to Karafuto in 1943 all acted under colonial rule; the balance between choice and coercion was nonetheless profoundly different in each case.

## Why families moved before mass wartime mobilization

Colonial migration began from conditions that Japanese rule transformed but did not create from nothing. Late-Joseon Korea already contained landlessness, tenancy, seasonal labor, local mobility, and cross-border settlement. From the nineteenth century, famine, taxation, population pressure, and the search for cultivable land had carried Koreans across the northern rivers into Qing Manchuria and the Russian Maritime Province. Annexation inserted those older movements into new imperial strategies.

In colonial Korea, cadastral formalization, expanding landlordism, debt, rent, taxes, commercialization, and fluctuating crop prices placed many rural households under pressure. As [[Land survey, tenancy, and the colonial economy]] explains, the 1910–1918 land survey did not mechanically confiscate every unregistered plot, and Korean landlords remained important. Yet legal title, credit, irrigation charges, and agricultural policy operated through unequal access to power. A household could experience rising aggregate rice output alongside worsening food security. Migration was one way to diversify risk: a member earned wages elsewhere, remitted money, reduced the number eating from the farm, or established a foothold for later chain migration.

Japan’s industrial growth created demand at the other end. After the First World War, construction, docks, factories, mines, transport, and municipal services drew low-wage workers. Steamship routes across the Korea Strait and colonial rail connections reduced travel time. Contractors and brokers recruited through acquaintances and village networks. Migrants often chose destinations where a relative or person from the same locality could provide information, a bed, or access to a foreman. These networks explain why movement persisted even when official restrictions tightened: migration was not only an individual response to an abstract wage gap but a household and community process.

Opportunity was real without being equal. Metropolitan earnings could exceed those available in rural Korea, but travel costs, broker fees, unemployment, wage discrimination, dangerous work, and high urban rents consumed the difference. Employers often concentrated Korean men in casual, dirty, or hazardous jobs. Women migrated in smaller recorded numbers early on, but their labor in factories, domestic service, boarding houses, food selling, and household reproduction was indispensable. Official statistics that classify a male “worker” as the migrant can make wives, children, and women’s paid work disappear.

## Japan: imperial subjects treated as suspect migrants

The Korean population in metropolitan Japan grew from a relatively small student and labor presence before annexation to more than 30,000 by 1920, nearly 300,000 by 1930, and well over half a million in the mid-1930s. Estimates differ because police registration was incomplete, categories changed, and circular or temporary movement was hard to count. The direction is nevertheless unmistakable: by the 1920s, Korean labor had become part of Japanese urban and industrial development.

Colonial subjecthood generated a peculiar migration regime. Koreans did not cross an international boundary in the ordinary legal sense, but Japanese officials repeatedly attempted to regulate departures from Korea and entry into the metropole. Authorities could require identification, proof of employment, travel funds, or a guarantor; police sometimes turned prospective migrants back at Korean ports. The stated reasons included unemployment, homelessness, public order, and protection from deceptive recruiters. The effect was also to manage a racialized labor supply and prevent visibly poor colonial subjects from becoming a metropolitan political problem.

This regulation was inconsistent. Employers wanted labor, local officials feared disorder, Korean colonial officials worried about depopulation or destitution, and migrants learned to evade or satisfy changing rules. Restriction could make brokers and personal connections more important. It could also increase vulnerability: a worker who borrowed for passage or depended on an intermediary arrived with less bargaining power.

In Japan, Korean neighborhoods grew around workplaces and cheap housing. Mutual-aid associations, churches, study groups, labor unions, nationalist organizations, socialist circles, shops, and boarding houses made durable communities. Migrants were not only labor inputs. They organized rent and wage struggles, circulated news from Korea, funded families, pursued education, and argued over assimilation, class politics, national independence, and everyday survival. The diaspora became a political space connecting colonial Korea to metropolitan movements.

Violence revealed the limits of imperial inclusion. After the Great Kantō Earthquake of 1 September 1923, false rumors accused Koreans of arson, poisoning wells, and revolt. Soldiers, police, vigilantes, and civilian groups killed Koreans and others mistaken for them. Exact numbers remain disputed because state investigations were partial and bodies and testimony were concealed, but the massacre is not in serious doubt. It showed how quickly a population governed as imperial subjects could be recast as an internal enemy. Identification practices, language tests, and rumor turned colonial difference into lethal exposure.

Everyday discrimination was less spectacular but persistent. Koreans faced hostile landlords, employment exclusion, segregated tasks, insulting surveillance, and pressure to use Japanese names or pass as Japanese. Yet neither discrimination nor poverty produced one uniform community. Students, professionals, entrepreneurs, skilled workers, activists, families, and the chronically underemployed occupied different positions. Class, gender, generation, locality, and politics divided Korean residents even as racism treated them as a single category.

## Manchuria: settlement between Korean, Chinese, and Japanese power

Korean migration to Manchuria cannot be dated from Japan’s creation of Manchukuo in 1932. Koreans had settled north of the Yalu and Tumen rivers for decades. Some cleared land or leased farms; some fled hunger, debt, prosecution, or political repression; some joined kin already established there. Qing and later Chinese authorities alternately tolerated, registered, naturalized, restricted, or expelled them. Their position was unstable because land claims and nationality did not always align. A family might cultivate a field for years without secure title or become entangled in competing Korean, Chinese, and Japanese claims to jurisdiction.

After annexation, Japan portrayed Koreans as its subjects and used their presence to extend consular and police reach into northeast China. That did not make Korean settlers simple colonizers. Many were poor tenants vulnerable to Chinese landlords, officials, banditry, Japanese agents, and environmental shocks. At the same time, Korean settlement could displace or compete with other cultivators, and some Koreans worked within Japanese-sponsored institutions. “Victim,” “settler,” and “imperial intermediary” were positions that could overlap rather than mutually exclusive identities.

The 1931 Japanese seizure of Manchuria and the establishment of Manchukuo changed the structure of mobility. Borders and jurisdictions were reorganized; railways, agricultural schemes, police registration, anti-guerrilla campaigns, and resettlement projects made populations more legible to the state. Japanese planners valued Korean farmers as producers and as buffers in strategically sensitive areas. Officials and colonial organizations sponsored group settlement and encouraged particular agricultural techniques, while counterinsurgency forcibly regrouped villages or displaced residents suspected of supporting resistance.

Korean nationalist and communist movements also treated Manchuria as a base. Migrant villages supplied recruits, food, intelligence, and targets for competing armed and political organizations. Japanese and Manchukuo security forces consequently viewed ordinary Korean mobility through the lens of insurgency. Families could be pressured simultaneously by guerrillas demanding support and states demanding proof of loyalty. The history of migration here is inseparable from the history of anti-colonial resistance, Chinese sovereignty, and Japanese continental expansion.

By the late 1930s, movement into Manchuria increasingly combined state-directed agricultural settlement, wartime resource extraction, and coercive security policy. Even then, not every migrant was physically deported. Some sought land or followed kin within opportunities created by conquest. The analytical point is not to deny intention but to ask who constructed the choices. A decision made among debt, police pressure, recruiter promises, and imperial settlement subsidies was neither wholly free nor identical to conscription.

## Karafuto and the sharpening of coercion

Southern Sakhalin became the Japanese colony of Karafuto after 1905. Its coal mines, forests, paper industry, fisheries, railways, and construction projects required labor in a harsh and distant environment. Korean workers were present before the full wartime mobilization system. Wage differentials and recruitment brought workers north, and employers valued a workforce that could be paid less and assigned arduous jobs.

After 1937, and especially after labor mobilization extended to Korea in 1939, the distinction between migration and compulsory deployment narrowed sharply. The state and firms recruited, allocated, and later requisitioned Korean labor for mines, factories, military construction, and other strategic work across the empire. Official categories changed from company-led “recruitment” to government-mediated placement and conscription. Those labels matter institutionally, but they do not by themselves reveal a worker’s experience. Recruitment could involve false promises, village and police pressure, withheld wages, restricted movement, dangerous conditions, and punishment for escape before a formal conscription order applied.

Karafuto illustrates why destination shaped the chance of return. Its remoteness, restricted transport, employer control, and the Soviet invasion in August 1945 trapped many Koreans at the end of the empire. Japanese civilians were repatriated under postwar arrangements, while Koreans were often excluded or ambiguously classified. Many had come from southern Korea but found themselves under Soviet rule when the peninsula was being divided. The result was a Sakhalin Korean community whose prolonged separation cannot be explained by wartime recruitment alone; it was produced by the collapse of Japanese sovereignty, Soviet policy, emerging Korean states, and postwar nationality rules.

## From managed mobility to national mobilization

Japan’s war in China transformed the scale and force of Korean movement. The 1938 National General Mobilization Law supplied the legal framework for directing labor and resources. Its application to Korea produced successive systems commonly periodized as recruitment beginning in 1939, official mediation from 1942, and labor conscription from 1944. Military service and military support labor added other channels. [[Forced labor and wartime mobilization]] requires its own treatment because counting victims, distinguishing legal categories, and reconstructing coercion at particular sites are major evidentiary problems.

For migration history, three points are essential. First, pre-1939 wage migration did not make later mobilization voluntary. Existing shipping routes, brokers, Korean neighborhoods, and employer experience became infrastructure that the wartime state could commandeer. Second, formal conscription was not the beginning of coercion. Colonial officials, police, village elites, recruiters, and firms possessed leverage over poor households long before 1944. Third, compulsion did not erase migrant agency. Workers deserted, negotiated, sent remittances, changed jobs illegally, formed relationships, joined organizations, and made plans for return. Agency describes action within constraint; it does not absolve the institutions imposing the constraint.

The wartime population in Japan rose dramatically, reaching roughly two million Koreans by Japan’s surrender, though exact totals depend on date and category. It included long-settled families, recent job seekers, students, mobilized workers, military personnel, and people brought through coercive channels. Treating all two million as either deportees or freely choosing economic migrants substitutes a political slogan for social history.

## Gender, household, and the limits of labor statistics

Migration records privilege the person whom a state or company counted. Household history reveals a different process. Early male movement could become family migration when a worker brought a spouse and children or married in Japan. Women earned wages, ran lodgings and shops, managed remittances, maintained language and ritual life, and navigated schools and welfare offices. They were also exposed to gendered labor exploitation, trafficking, and sexual violence. The military “comfort women” system involved coercive movement across imperial space, but it must not be reduced to a subdivision of ordinary labor migration; its recruitment, confinement, and sexual violence require distinct evidence and concepts.

Children born or raised outside Korea acquired Japanese-language schooling and local attachments. “Return” in 1945 could therefore mean travel to a place they barely knew. Household members also faced different incentives: an older migrant might seek a Korean hometown, while a child, spouse, or business owner feared losing community and livelihood. This helps explain why the end of colonial rule did not simply reverse every route.

## 1945 did not restore the map that migration had crossed

Japan’s surrender dissolved the imperial framework more quickly than it moved people home. Most Koreans in Japan did repatriate during the first postwar years, often amid transport shortages, lost savings, limits on baggage, and uncertainty about conditions in Korea. Several hundred thousand remained. Their reasons varied: established families and businesses, fear of political instability, lack of land or housing, disrupted kin ties, or inability to travel. Those who stayed became the historical core of the *Zainichi* Korean population.

Their status changed from colonial subjects to people whose nationality was disputed and then legally stripped from the Japanese nationality framework. The division of Korea complicated registration and political allegiance. Organizations aligned with South Korea, North Korea, communist politics, or nonaligned community interests competed over schools, welfare, identity, and repatriation. Colonial migration thus flowed directly into postcolonial minority politics.

In Manchuria, Soviet invasion, Japanese defeat, Chinese civil war, land reform, and the formation of new Korean regimes produced new departures and new reasons to stay. Some migrants returned to northern or southern Korea; others remained and became part of the Korean minority in China. Returnees carried political experience, military skills, family networks, and divergent memories into the upheavals traced in [[Political violence before the Korean War]].

Sakhalin generated a different stranded population. Postwar diplomatic nonrecognition and competing state classifications delayed return for decades. The resulting diaspora is evidence that decolonization was not a clean national homecoming. Empire had moved people across territories that successor states partitioned into incompatible citizenship regimes.

## How to describe coercion without flattening it

The vocabulary of “voluntary” and “forced” is necessary but too binary for the full period. It helps to distinguish at least four mechanisms:

1. **Self-directed movement under unequal conditions:** migrants selected a destination and job but did so within colonial poverty and discrimination.
2. **Brokered or employer recruitment:** contracts and promises organized movement, sometimes with deception, debt, or severe penalties.
3. **Administrative direction and community pressure:** officials assigned quotas or used police, village authorities, ration access, and family vulnerability to obtain compliance.
4. **Formal requisition, confinement, or deportation:** legal orders and direct force removed meaningful ability to refuse or leave.

One person could pass through several mechanisms. A man might initially travel voluntarily, become tied to a contractor by debt, be transferred under government mediation, and later be prevented from leaving a mine. Classification should follow the stage and evidence rather than impose one label on an entire life.

Colonial archives add another difficulty. Police records overrepresent surveillance and disorder. Company records may call a worker a recruit while hiding threats or withheld pay. Postwar testimony can recover experiences absent from official files but is shaped by trauma, later legal categories, and the questions interviewers asked. Aggregate population counts cannot identify individual coercion; an individual testimony cannot by itself establish an empire-wide rate. The strongest reconstruction combines official rules, workplace and transport records, local administrative documents, demographic data, and oral histories.

## Place in the map

Migration connects colonial political economy to the lived geography of empire. It shows how the agrarian pressures in [[Land survey, tenancy, and the colonial economy]] became urban labor, frontier settlement, remittances, and diaspora. It turns [[Colonial Korea's changing administrative regimes]] from a sequence of governors and laws into control over passports, ports, police files, recruitment, and residence. It supplies the prehistory of [[Forced labor and wartime mobilization]] without projecting wartime conscription backward onto every earlier journey.

It also changes the unit of Korean history. Colonial Korea was governed from the peninsula, but Korean social life extended through Osaka neighborhoods, Manchurian farms, Karafuto mines, shipping routes, and hometown remittance networks. Empire did not merely act upon a bounded Korean nation; it produced transregional lives that nation-states later struggled to classify. The post-1945 histories of *Zainichi* Koreans, Korean Chinese communities, Sakhalin Koreans, repatriates, and divided families are therefore not epilogues. They are evidence of how colonial rule reorganized population and belonging.

Finally, this history resists two misleading moral shortcuts. Recognizing migrants’ calculations does not vindicate empire, because empire shaped wages, land access, policing, and the menu of possible choices. Recognizing coercion does not reduce migrants to passive cargo, because people formed households, organizations, and strategies inside structures they did not control. The most accurate account keeps power and action in the same frame.

## Open questions

- How did departure-control rules actually vary by Korean port and year, and what proportion of rejected travelers later crossed through another route?
- Can linked village, shipping, and residence registers distinguish temporary circular migration from permanent settlement without treating missing registration as absence?
- How did women’s independent wage migration differ from movement recorded only as family accompaniment in Japan, Manchuria, and Karafuto?
- Which Manchurian settlement projects combined meaningful household choice with administrative pressure, and which involved direct forced relocation?
- How did wages, deductions, remittances, injury, desertion, and mortality compare across Korean, Japanese, and Chinese workers at the same workplaces?
- What exact state decisions after August 1945 prevented particular Sakhalin Korean households from returning, and how did Soviet, Japanese, and Korean records classify them differently?
