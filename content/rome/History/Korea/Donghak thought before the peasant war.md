Before it became inseparable from the 1894 peasant war, Donghak was a persecuted religious discipline that joined an encounter with the Lord of Heaven to ethical self-cultivation, healing ritual, vernacular teaching, and a diagnosis of a world entering dangerous transformation.

## The problem hidden by the name “Donghak rebellion”

Donghak, “Eastern Learning,” is often introduced backward from the [[Donghak Peasant Revolution]] of 1894. That approach supplies a memorable destination but distorts the road. Choe Je-u (1824–1864), known by the religious name Su-un, did not found a peasant army in 1860 or leave a detailed program for overthrowing the Joseon dynasty. He announced a new Way after a revelatory experience; taught recitation, moral cultivation, healing, and reverence for Heaven; organized communities of disciples; and was executed by the state in 1864. His successor Choe Si-hyeong, Haewol, preserved and extended the movement during three decades of illegality. Only in the political crisis of the 1890s did parts of that religious network become infrastructure for mass petition and war.

The distinction matters causally. A religion can make rebellion possible without being a rebellion in doctrinal disguise. Donghak supplied portable practices, trusted local relationships, moral language, and an account of cosmic crisis. Those resources could help ordinary people coordinate and could justify resistance to corrupt power. They did not mechanically dictate when followers should fight, which leaders they should obey, or what state they should build. Indeed, Donghak leaders and local activists disagreed over confrontation in 1894. Early teaching should therefore be read as a field of political possibilities, not as a secret manifesto whose true meaning waited thirty-four years for armed expression.

## A revelation in a world of disorder

Choe Je-u was born near Gyeongju into a yangban lineage but occupied an insecure social position, commonly connected in scholarship to his mother's remarriage or secondary status and to the declining fortunes of his household. Biographical details come partly through later denominational memory and must be handled cautiously. The broad setting is clearer. Mid-nineteenth-century Joseon contained severe local extraction, status conflict, epidemic disease, and dissatisfaction with officials and established elites. The international order also looked unstable: Western powers had defeated Qing China, Catholic communities survived repeated persecution, and reports of foreign military and religious penetration circulated in Korea. These pressures overlapped with the rural grievances traced in [[Nineteenth-century tax disorder and peasant protest]], but they were not reducible to tax rates.

Choe's writings portray a moral and cosmological emergency. People had lost constancy; established learning no longer restored order; the fortunes of the age were changing. In 1860, at Yongdam near Gyeongju, he reported an encounter with a divine voice identified as Sangje or Cheonju, the Supreme Ruler or Lord of Heaven. The revelation furnished a talisman and an incantation and commissioned him to teach. Whether treated devotionally as revelation or historically as a narrated religious experience, the encounter established authority outside the normal Joseon routes of office, examination success, lineage prestige, or monastic ordination.

That claim was socially consequential. A man marginal to secure elite office presented himself as the recipient of a universal Way at a moment when the court's licensed institutions seemed unable to prevent suffering or foreign danger. His authority depended on disciplined efficacy—changed conduct, healing, teaching, and the formation of followers—rather than recognition from the state. The court could therefore see heterodoxy and political danger even where Choe professed loyalty and moral reform.

## “Eastern” did not mean culturally sealed

Donghak defined itself in relation to Seohak, “Western Learning,” the Joseon name associated especially with Catholicism. Choe feared Western military expansion and opposed Catholic teaching. Yet simple descriptions of Donghak as an indigenous reaction to Christianity miss the intimacy of the encounter. Both traditions spoke of a personal Lord of Heaven; both offered truth and discipline beyond hereditary status; both formed communities that officials could not fully supervise. Choe knew at least something about Catholic ideas circulating in Korea, although the exact textual routes and degree of direct borrowing remain debated.

Nor was Donghak merely a mixture of “Confucianism, Buddhism, Daoism, and shamanism,” as textbook shorthand often says. Such a list identifies parallels but does not explain Choe's act of recomposition. His texts use the classical vocabulary of the Way, virtue, sincerity, mind, vital energy, and self-cultivation. He explicitly compared his Way with that of Confucius and presented its differences as limited in some respects. The stress on moral practice, family conduct, and cultivated sincerity made Donghak intelligible in a Confucian world. At the same time, direct divine address, a sacred formula, a talisman, healing claims, song, and bodily-spiritual transformation exceeded the routines of literati Neo-Confucian pedagogy.

“Eastern” was therefore a claim to location and authority, not an inventory of pure origins. Choe answered a global and domestic crisis by reworking materials available in late Joseon Korea. His new teaching competed with Western Learning partly because it occupied comparable ground: universal truth, the Lord of Heaven, salvation from disorder, and a disciplined community. The opposition between East and West was real, but it was produced through contact rather than isolation.

## Serving Heaven within the person

The compact formula most closely associated with early Donghak is *sicheonju* (侍天主), usually rendered “serving,” “attending,” or “bearing the Lord of Heaven.” Each translation reveals and conceals. “Serving” can imply an external sovereign; “bearing” emphasizes divine presence within the practitioner; “attending” captures a continuous ethical relation. Choe's Heaven was personal enough to speak and command, but also present through *gi*, the vital force pervading life. Early Donghak thus resisted an easy choice between a wholly transcendent God and an impersonal cosmos.

The incantation commonly called the twenty-one-character formula made this relationship repeatable. It invokes descending vital energy, attendance on the Lord of Heaven, transformation through divine will, sustained awareness, and the unfolding of the Way. English renderings vary, and the Sino-Korean compression supports more than one interpretation. What matters historically is that recitation joined cosmology to practice. The believer did not merely assent to a proposition about Heaven. Repeated chanting, sincerity, bodily comportment, and ethical reform cultivated an awareness of divine presence. The formula was short enough to travel orally and to be used by people outside advanced classical literacy.

The talisman likewise joined sign, body, and healing. Accounts describe its being inscribed, burned, mixed with water, and consumed. Modern readers may separate “symbolic religion” from “medicine,” but participants inhabited a world in which moral disorder, bodily affliction, vital energy, and cosmic imbalance could be connected. Choe himself limited extravagant expectations: lack of sincerity could explain failure, and the practice belonged within cultivation rather than functioning as an automatic technology. Still, claims of efficacy helped Donghak move from private insight to embodied communal practice—and gave hostile officials material for accusations that he deceived the people.

## A teaching built for different forms of literacy

The early canon exists principally in two collections. The *Donggyeong daejeon* (Great Scripture of Eastern Learning) contains writings in literary Chinese, the prestige language of educated men. The *Yongdam yusa* (Songs of Yongdam) uses Korean vernacular verse and was transmitted in hangul, making teaching more accessible through reading aloud, memorization, and song. Choe Si-hyeong and later followers collected and published these works after the founder's death, so the surviving canon is at once evidence for Choe's thought and the product of a persecuted community preserving it.

The two registers reveal strategy, not two separate religions. Literary Chinese let Choe debate inherited learning in the recognized idiom of cosmology and cultivation. Vernacular verse could address women, farmers, artisans, and less classically educated hearers in familiar rhythms. Accessibility was itself a redistribution of religious authority. It did not abolish hierarchy: teachers led, initiates learned disciplines, and the movement acquired offices. But truth was no longer confined to those who had succeeded in the institutions described in [[The gwageo examination system in practice]].

Donghak's organization magnified that portability. During Choe's brief ministry, local units (*jeop*) and leaders (*jeopju*) connected converts beyond a single master-disciple circle. After 1864, persecution scattered the community and damaged its first organization. Haewol's itinerant leadership rebuilt networks while evading arrest. The combination of concise ritual, vernacular transmission, local leadership, and clandestine trust explains later growth better than doctrine alone.

## Equality: powerful implication, dangerous anachronism

Donghak is frequently summarized as a doctrine of human equality. The description captures something important but needs chronological discipline. If each person attends or bears the Lord of Heaven, rank cannot exhaust human worth. Choe welcomed followers across status boundaries and used forms of teaching that bypassed elite classical education. Later Donghak practice under Haewol developed more explicit reverence for human beings, women, children, and all creation. These commitments could undermine the moral legitimacy of hereditary contempt and abusive officialdom.

But the famous maxim *innaecheon*—“humanity is Heaven” or “people are Heaven”—was systematized and publicized later, especially under Son Byeong-hui in the Cheondogyo era. Treating it as Choe's exact slogan collapses several generations of theological development. Likewise, early Donghak did not articulate equality in the language of modern individual rights, representative government, or class revolution. Its challenge emerged through sacred presence, transformed conduct, and the claim that a universal Way could be practiced by socially humble people.

This distinction strengthens rather than weakens the argument for Donghak's social force. Ideas do not need to reproduce twentieth-century vocabulary to destabilize a hierarchy. When a low-status adherent becomes a site of Heaven's presence, when a woman can learn through vernacular song, or when a local teacher commands trust that once flowed toward a magistrate, relationships change. The result is an ethical leveling whose political consequences depend on circumstance and organization.

## *Gaebyeok*: transformation without a timetable

Another backward projection concerns *gaebyeok*, literally an “opening” or “creation,” often translated as the Great Opening. Donghak interpreted the nineteenth century as a turning of cosmic fortune: an old order was exhausted and a new one could emerge. This was more than optimism. It made present suffering historically intelligible and made disciplined transformation urgent. A believer could understand disease, foreign encroachment, corrupt administration, and moral disarray as connected signs of an age in transition.

Yet *gaebyeok* was not a precise revolutionary calendar. Choe did not specify that a peasant army in 1894 would inaugurate the new world. The concept could sustain personal renewal, patient propagation, petition, social criticism, or militant action. Its ambiguity was a source of durability. It gave later followers a language for radical change while leaving room for leaders who feared that premature violence would destroy the religious community.

The causal bridge to 1894 therefore had several spans. Cosmic crisis made ordinary injustice meaningful beyond a single village. Sacred human worth delegitimized abuse. Ritual and teaching produced committed adherents. Local units built networks. Persecution generated shared grievance and habits of secrecy. Economic and administrative disorders supplied immediate conflicts, while foreign intervention changed the stakes. Remove any one of these and the later mobilization looks different; reduce all of them to theology and the account becomes deterministic.

## Why the Joseon state killed Choe Je-u

Officials arrested Choe in late 1863 and executed him at Daegu in 1864 under charges conventionally summarized as promulgating heterodoxy and deluding the people. The prosecution reflected Joseon's confessional-political order. “Religion” was not a protected private sphere standing apart from government. Correct teaching, ritual hierarchy, family ethics, and political loyalty were connected. An unauthorized movement invoking the Lord of Heaven, healing, talismans, mass following, and organization could resemble both prohibited Catholicism and older categories of dangerous heterodoxy.

The state did not need evidence of a mature insurrectionary plan to perceive a threat. Choe claimed a source of authority the court had not certified; followers gathered around it; and the teaching crossed boundaries of status and literacy. Officials also interpreted unfamiliar practices through hostile categories. Government records that call followers “evil adherents” or accuse them of deception reveal the state's logic, but they cannot serve as neutral ethnography. Conversely, later Cheondogyo accounts had reasons to present Choe as prophet, nationalist, democrat, or martyr. Source criticism requires holding the hostile archive and the devotional archive against each other.

Execution failed as suppression. It eliminated the charismatic founder but gave his followers a grievance, a martyr, and a preservation problem. Haewol's achievement was not merely doctrinal succession. He kept dispersed believers connected, recovered texts and oral teachings, developed practices, and enabled a clandestine movement to survive. The decades between 1864 and 1894 were thus formative, not an empty interval between revelation and revolt.

## From religious capacity to contingent war

By the early 1890s, Donghak adherents could mobilize in large petition movements seeking Choe's posthumous exoneration and an end to persecution. Rural conflicts, official exactions, and local leadership then interacted with the network. In 1894, southern activists associated with Jeon Bong-jun launched an uprising in Gobu; mobilization expanded, government forces faltered, and Qing and Japanese intervention transformed a domestic crisis into the First Sino-Japanese War. The sequence belongs to the history of the peasant war and [[Gabo Reform causal map]], not to Choe's theology alone.

Calling the conflict the “Donghak Peasant Revolution” properly recognizes the religion's importance, but the label can imply a unity the evidence does not support. Not every participant was a doctrinally committed believer. Material grievances and local antagonisms mattered. Northern and southern networks differed, and Haewol was initially cautious about armed action. Once Japanese military intrusion became central, opposition to foreign domination could draw religious and political motives together more tightly.

Early Donghak's historical importance lies precisely in this non-equivalence. It converted diffuse crisis into a teachable cosmology, inward discipline into shared practice, and marginalized people into a community with leaders and routes of trust. Those capacities became available for politics, while political circumstances determined how they were used. Donghak before the peasant war was neither apolitical mysticism nor a rebellion waiting to happen. It was a new religious answer to the breakdown of moral, social, and international confidence in late Joseon—and therefore a generator of possibilities the founder could not control.

## Open questions

- How differently did women, tenant farmers, artisans, and fallen-status families interpret *sicheonju* in the 1860s–1880s, given that most surviving explanations come through male leaders and later compilation?
- Which lines in the surviving scriptures can be securely dated to Choe's lifetime, and where did oral transmission or Haewol's editorial work shape the canon?
- How did local *jeop* organization vary by region before the large petition movements of 1892–1893?
- At what points did anti-Catholic language become anti-Western or anti-Japanese political action, and when is later nationalism being projected backward?
- How should historians translate *gi*, *sicheonju*, and *gaebyeok* without forcing them into either Christian theological or secular revolutionary categories?
