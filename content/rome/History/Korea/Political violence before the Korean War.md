Between liberation in August 1945 and North Korea's invasion on 25 June 1950, Koreans experienced political violence as a connected sequence of occupation, polarized state-building, revolt, counterinsurgency, purge, and border conflict—not as a peaceful interval suddenly broken by war.

Up: [[Korean history (MOC)]]

This sequence matters because it explains how [[Why Korea was divided in 1945|an improvised occupation boundary]] became embedded in institutions and everyday life. It does **not** make the interstate invasion inevitable, excuse it, or reduce every local conflict to instructions from Washington, Moscow, Seoul, or Pyongyang. Political actors made choices at several scales. Armed leftists killed police, officials, soldiers, and civilians; police, constabulary and army units, right-wing youth groups, and later the Republic of Korea's security institutions used collective punishment and unlawful killing on a far larger civilian field in investigated southern cases. In the north, Soviet occupation and the emerging party-state dismantled rival organizations, imprisoned opponents, and drove political and religious refugees south, but surviving access to northern local records is much thinner. The asymmetry of evidence must not be mistaken either for innocence or for permission to invent equivalence.

## A liberation without an agreed state

Japan's surrender removed the colonial sovereign but did not deliver a Korean government accepted across the political spectrum. Local activists formed people's committees and, in Seoul, the Committee for the Preparation of Korean Independence proclaimed a Korean People's Republic. These organizations varied greatly: some were socialist-led, some were broad local coalitions, and some inherited personnel or practices from colonial administration. The Soviet occupation authorities in the north adapted committees into a centralized political order. The United States Army Military Government in Korea (USAMGIK) in the south refused to recognize the self-proclaimed republic and governed through a bureaucracy that retained many experienced Korean officials and a police force deeply compromised, in public memory, by service under Japan.

That institutional choice did not by itself cause all later violence, but it gave everyday disputes a combustible grammar. Police represented order to officials and coercive continuity to many workers and peasants. Returning exiles, demobilized soldiers, landlords, tenants, labor organizers, Christians, communists, and nationalists contested jobs, land, rice collection, and the meaning of independence. Inflation, shortages, and the movement of millions of returnees magnified the stakes. The revived Korean National Police, right-wing youth organizations—especially groups drawing refugees from the north—and left-wing organizations all used intimidation. Arrest, torture, assassination, strikebreaking, and attacks on police stations made politics bodily before two Korean republics existed.

The autumn uprising of 1946 in the south shows how social grievance and organized politics became inseparable. A general strike begun by railway workers was suppressed; protest spread into Daegu and rural districts amid anger over rice policy and police conduct. Demonstrators attacked police and local officials, while police, occupation forces, and right-wing groups suppressed the disturbances. Calling the episode either a spontaneous hunger riot or a centrally directed communist insurrection is too simple. Local grievances supplied participation; left organizations supplied networks and slogans; state repression widened the constituency for resistance. Similar combinations recurred later.

In the north, land reform in March 1946 redistributed land without compensation to landlords and helped the new order mobilize poor peasants while displacing old elites. Nationalization, party consolidation, and pressure on religious and political opponents narrowed legal pluralism. Refugees carried accounts of confiscation and persecution southward, where their experience fed militant anticommunism. The peninsula's political geography was therefore being made not only through superpower agreements but through expulsions, flight, policing, and the destruction of rival local authority.

## Jeju: protest, insurgency, and collective punishment

Jeju's violence grew through a sequence rather than a single outbreak. Police firing during a 1 March 1947 commemoration killed civilians. A general strike followed, and arrests, alleged torture, political dismissals, and the arrival of mainland police and right-wing youth deepened hostility. The South Korean Workers' Party's island organization then launched coordinated attacks on police posts and selected right-wing figures on 3 April 1948. The insurgents opposed repression and the separate southern election scheduled for 10 May, but this political context does not erase killings committed by guerrillas or make every islander an insurgent.

Negotiation briefly appeared possible when constabulary officer Kim Ik-ryeol met rebel leader Kim Dal-sam in late April. It failed amid mutual distrust, continued violence, and a political timetable moving toward a separate state. Jeju's election was disrupted; two of the island's three constituencies failed to produce valid results. After the Republic of Korea was inaugurated in August, counterinsurgency escalated sharply. The security forces sought to separate guerrillas in the uplands from the population by forcing residents toward coastal settlements and treating people remaining in designated interior zones as suspect. Villages were burned. Civilians were detained, beaten, raped, disappeared, or killed; families were punished through association. Guerrillas also murdered civilians labeled collaborators or enemies.

The official Jeju investigation defines the “Jeju 4·3 Incident” broadly, from the 1 March 1947 shootings through 3 April 1948 and the suppression ending in September 1954. That scope is a warning against reducing the history to “the April uprising.” The Jeju 4·3 Peace Foundation's follow-up investigation maps damage across 165 villages and separately examines mass-killing sites, missing prisoners, preventive detention, and casualties among soldiers, police, and right-wing organizations. The first national investigation estimated total deaths in a range rather than producing a complete roll; subsequent victim review has continued to recognize individuals. Counts differ because records were destroyed or concealed, bodies were never recovered, families remained silent, and legal recognition follows categories and evidence rules that are not identical to demographic estimation. The safest claim is therefore that tens of thousands were killed, overwhelmingly civilians, while citing a specific investigation whenever giving a narrower number.

For islanders, “sides” could be imposed by location and kinship. A household might feed guerrillas under coercion at night and answer police questions the next day. Moving to the coast meant crowding and loss of crops; staying inland could be treated as proof of rebellion. Children inherited stigma attached to an accused relative. Silence after the event was not absence of memory but a survival practice under an anticommunist security order.

## Yeosu–Suncheon and the construction of an internal enemy

On 19 October 1948, members of the Fourteenth Regiment at Yeosu refused an order connected to deployment against the Jeju insurgency and revolted. The mutiny spread rapidly to Suncheon and nearby areas, joined by some civilians and leftist cadres. Rebels killed police, officials, right-wing activists, and other civilians. Loyal forces retook the cities within days, then screened inhabitants and executed alleged participants or sympathizers, often through procedures that did not provide reliable adjudication. Some rebels withdrew into the mountains, contributing to a longer guerrilla conflict in the Jirisan region.

Yeosu–Suncheon changed more than the local balance. It exposed organized leftist cells inside the young army, prompted purges, and helped the Rhee government present political opposition as an existential security threat. Martial-law practice preceded a settled statutory framework. The National Security Law, enacted in December 1948, supplied a capacious instrument against organizations and expression deemed to aid the enemy. State formation and counterinsurgency thus reinforced one another: revolt justified expanded coercion; expanded coercion pushed legal and nonviolent left politics underground; underground networks then helped officials portray dissent as indistinguishable from armed conspiracy.

The civilian experience again resists a clean binary. Contemporary photographs and reporting from Suncheon recorded bodies left by rebels and brutal retaliation by returning government forces. Later testimony describes accusations settled through personal enmity, coerced identification, and guilt by family or village. Women were not merely witnesses waiting for male combatants: they searched for bodies, maintained households after detention or execution, carried food and information, suffered sexual violence, and negotiated with authorities. Yet official documents and much early scholarship classified victims through male political identities, making gendered experience harder to reconstruct.

## Guerrilla war, purges, and the approach to 1950

During 1949 the southern government gained the upper hand over guerrillas through army and police operations that combined improved organization with severe coercion. The U.S. Army's own institutional history says the Korean National Police and ROK Army used “brutal and violent tactics” while suppressing insurgents, and also describes increased northern aid and manpower. This source is valuable evidence of how an American military institution later understood the campaign, but its counterinsurgency frame privileges state effectiveness and treats popular politics mainly as an operational problem.

Pyongyang did support southern guerrilla activity and expected indigenous revolt to weaken or topple the ROK. Southern communists were not simply puppets, however: their organizations predated the DPRK and arose from colonial resistance, labor struggles, local disputes, and opposition to separate elections. Nor did guerrilla failure automatically dictate invasion. It altered the northern leadership's assessment of what force would be required, while Kim Il Sung still sought and obtained Soviet authorization for a conventional attack. Distinguishing these propositions preserves responsibility: Korea contained an internal armed conflict before June 1950, and the DPRK nevertheless made a discrete decision to cross the 38th parallel with regular forces.

The border itself was already violent. ROK and DPRK units fought repeated clashes along and near the 38th parallel, especially around the Ongjin peninsula, in 1949 and 1950. Both states claimed national sovereignty and spoke of reunification. Raids, patrol actions, and artillery exchanges accustomed soldiers and residents to intermittent combat. But scale and command matter: scattered border fighting was not equivalent to the coordinated North Korean invasion of 25 June 1950.

Inside the south, surveillance lists turned political identity into an administrative object. The National Guidance Alliance (*Bodo Yeonmaeng*), created in 1949, was intended to register and “re-educate” former leftists and alleged sympathizers. Membership could be coerced or inflated by local quotas. The alliance's most infamous consequence came only after the invasion, when South Korean authorities executed large numbers of registered people and detainees as they retreated. Those killings belong chronologically to [[Civilian experience of the Korean War]], but the machinery—lists, police knowledge, categories of suspect families, and normalized emergency power—was built before the war. This is one of the strongest continuities across 25 June without collapsing the prewar and wartime periods into one event.

## Evidence and the limits of truth commissions

South Korea's democratization made investigation possible after decades in which survivors risked being labeled communist and local records were inaccessible or destroyed. The 2000 Jeju special law created a national investigation process; its 2003 report and presidential apology were landmarks. The Truth and Reconciliation Commission, established under a 2005 framework law, later investigated many civilian-killing and human-rights cases. A second commission operated from 2020 to 2025 and delivered a comprehensive report to the president and National Assembly in November 2025.

These bodies changed the evidentiary baseline. They collected family applications, oral testimony, police and prison material, local records, burial evidence, and foreign archives; identified victims in particular cases; assigned institutional responsibility; and recommended apology, memorialization, record correction, or legal repair. They make it untenable to dismiss mass killing as rumor merely because early official histories omitted it.

Truth-commission findings still require source criticism. They investigate petitions under statutory mandates, deadlines, and standards of proof; their recognized victims are not a census of all dead. Survivor testimony may contain minor chronological error while remaining powerful evidence of a killing pattern. Perpetrator archives preserve the categories and euphemisms of institutions that produced violence. Exhumations can establish identity or manner of death in some cases but cannot reconstruct every command chain. Commission composition and political conditions can affect priorities. Finally, access is uneven: extensive South Korean and U.S. records do not have a northern archival counterpart of comparable openness. A peninsula-wide total that simply adds unlike estimates would pretend to a precision the evidence cannot bear.

## Historiography: origins without inevitability

Cold War histories often separated a legitimate state's suppression of communist disorder from an externally directed invasion. Revisionist scholarship restored colonial continuities, social conflict, American occupation, and southern repression, and framed the war partly as a civil war already underway. That correction made Jeju, Yeosu–Suncheon, and rural guerrilla conflict central rather than peripheral. At its weakest, however, an “already a civil war” formula can blur the change in scale on 25 June or make the northern decision disappear into structure.

Post-revisionist and transnational work combines levels. It asks how local struggles over land, policing, collaboration, and representation interacted with the Soviet-American occupation, formation of rival states, party networks, military aid, and decisions by Kim Il Sung, Stalin, Mao Zedong, Syngman Rhee, and U.S. officials. Microhistory adds another correction: villagers did not experience “the Cold War” as a single ideology. They encountered named policemen, missing relatives, food demands, burned houses, registration cards, informers, and shifting armed visitors. These scales are complementary if causal claims remain explicit.

Naming is itself historiographical. “Incident” can sound accidental; “uprising” can foreground insurgent agency; “rebellion” may reproduce the state's legal category; “massacre” can obscure the armed conflict in which state massacre occurred. This note uses paired descriptive terms and identifies actors rather than forcing Jeju or Yeosu–Suncheon into one label. Vocabulary should clarify responsibility, not decide it in advance.

## Why it matters

The prewar violence explains how division became socially durable. It destroyed organizations that might have bridged the zones, selected coercive institutions, displaced populations, and taught families that political classification could determine survival. It also shows why democratization and truth recovery were not symbolic side projects: they reopened histories that the security state had made dangerous to tell.

The central causal lesson is bounded. Liberation produced political openness and material crisis; occupation authorities empowered rival institutions; separate-state projects narrowed compromise; insurgency and counterinsurgency enlarged violence; purges and security law converted enemies into administrative categories; guerrilla defeat and border clashes changed strategic calculations. None of those steps required the next. The DPRK's invasion was a choice within conditions that earlier violence had made more imaginable and more catastrophic.

## Open questions

- Which county-level police, prison, and court records best show how local quotas and personal denunciations shaped Guidance Alliance enrollment before June 1950?
- How do the first and second Truth and Reconciliation Commissions differ in their evidentiary standards and findings for the same prewar killing sites?
- What can Russian and newly accessible North Korean local records establish about imprisonment, execution, and forced migration in the north without treating refugee testimony as either transparent or disposable?
- How did women describe coercion, survival, and bereavement differently from the male political categories used in contemporary reports and later victim registries?
- Can a village-level comparison across Jeju, the Jirisan region, and a border county separate common mechanisms of political sorting from locally specific chains of violence?
