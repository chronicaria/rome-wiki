Joseon factions were durable coalitions built through offices, scholarly reputations, recruitment, and kinship; they could discipline power and organize policy as well as monopolize posts and turn defeat into purge.

## The wrong question: why were Koreans so factional?

Accounts of Joseon politics often become a procession of named camps—*Sarim*, Easterners, Westerners, Southerners, Northerners, Old Doctrine and Young Doctrine—followed by exiles and executions. Read that way, factionalism looks like a national vice or an inexplicable preference for doctrinal quarrels over government. The framing is misleading. It treats a political outcome as a cultural personality and strips away the institutions that made coalitions both necessary and visible.

Joseon had no legally recognized parties, electoral constituencies, or cabinet responsible to a parliament. It did have a monarch, a highly literate civil bureaucracy, a small number of prestigious offices, formal organs of criticism, regular personnel evaluation, examinations and recommendations, royal lectures, and a political language in which the fitness of a policy was tied to the moral fitness of its advocate. Officials who wanted a proposal adopted, a colleague protected, or a successor promoted therefore needed allies inside a common arena. Those allies were connected by teachers, exam cohorts, service together, marriage, descent, native place, academies, memorial associations, and agreement on particular controversies. A *bungdang* was not simply a modern party under another name. It was a coalition inside the official and literati order, without a separate legal personality, permanent membership roll, mass electorate, or assured right to opposition.

This distinction changes the explanatory task. The question is not whether factional conflict was “good” or “bad.” It is how Joseon's personnel and speech institutions converted networks into governing coalitions; why some coalitions survived exclusion from court; why moral disagreement could become a contest over appointments; and under what conditions competition became reciprocal criticism, winner-take-all displacement, or lethal purge.

## Offices made organized disagreement consequential

At the center stood the king, but royal government did not mean that every decision was a private royal act. The State Council deliberated on major affairs; the Six Ministries administered personnel, taxation, rites, war, justice, and public works; the Royal Secretariat transmitted commands and memorials; specialized offices gathered information and executed policy. Officials obtained leverage from the jurisdiction and documentary routines of the posts they occupied. Control of a ministry mattered differently from access to the king's lectures or the power to challenge an appointment.

Especially important were the institutions conventionally grouped as the Three Offices. The Office of the Inspector-General (*Sahonbu*) investigated official misconduct and guarded bureaucratic discipline. The Office of Censors or Censorate (*Saganwon*) remonstrated with the king and criticized policy. The Office of Special Advisers (*Hongmungwan*) kept books, drafted learned texts, advised the monarch, and participated in royal lectures; its scholarly authority gave its officials a platform for political speech. Boundaries and titles changed across the dynasty, and “Three Offices” should not imply a modern separated branch of government. Together, however, these offices institutionalized an unusual capacity: relatively junior literati in authorized roles could speak against senior ministers, personnel choices, and sometimes the king.

Two practices made this more than advice. In remonstrance, officials framed criticism as loyal correction: because a ruler needed to cultivate judgment and hear unwelcome truth, silence could itself be disloyal. In personnel review, censorial officials could object to an appointment on grounds of character, reputation, procedure, or conflict. The precise force of an objection depended on period and circumstance; it was not an absolute constitutional veto. Yet repeated objections could delay an appointment, damage a career, force a royal response, and publicly define the issue recorded in court documents.

The system rewarded coordination. A lone memorial could be dismissed as eccentric or self-interested. Convergent memorials from offices charged with public criticism carried institutional weight. Conversely, a king or senior coalition could neutralize critics by transferring, demoting, exiling, or prosecuting them and by installing more congenial officials. Personnel control and policy control therefore reinforced each other. A faction did not merely seek office because office brought private reward. It sought offices because occupants decided which allegations entered the record, which nominees were obstructed, which precedents were cited, and which policy acquired the appearance of a learned consensus.

Royal lectures added another arena. They joined classical exposition to current affairs and brought selected scholar-officials into repeated contact with the king. The language was moral and historical rather than programmatic in a modern party sense, but that did not make it ornamental. An interpretation of proper kingship could justify scrutiny of favorites, restraint in punishment, a ritual decision, or a new personnel balance. Because political argument claimed to identify impartial principle, opponents rarely described themselves as one interested group among others. Each side was tempted to call its network the community of upright scholars and the rival network a selfish clique.

## Recruitment turned learning into political capital

The civil examinations supplied a prestigious route into service but never allocated careers mechanically. Eligibility and preparation were socially unequal; the number of graduates exceeded desirable openings; protected appointment, recommendation, irregular examinations, family reputation, and royal favor coexisted with tested achievement. After entry, an official still needed postings, evaluation, promotion, and protection. As [[The gwageo examination system in practice]] shows, selection occurred repeatedly rather than at one meritocratic gate.

That structure made relationships productive without making examination performance fictitious. A teacher could identify talented pupils and transmit an interpretive lineage. A successful senior official could recommend men whose scholarship and character he trusted. Men examined in the same cohort or employed in the same bureau acquired shared experience. A junior official's public defense of a censured teacher might display conviction while also locating him within a network. The resulting tie could be intellectual, affective, strategic, and material at once. Reducing it to “ideas” misses patronage; reducing it to patronage assumes that ideas never constrained conduct.

The rise of *Sarim* scholar-officials illustrates this interaction. The term referred broadly to a “forest of scholars,” especially literati who cultivated claims to moral learning and local scholarly standing, not to a single disciplined party with continuous command. From the later fifteenth and sixteenth centuries, men associated with such networks increasingly entered the central bureaucracy, especially speech and advisory posts, and challenged established merit subjects and powerful officeholders often labeled *Hungu*. The familiar story of virtuous provincial scholars replacing corrupt capital grandees captures the actors' moral geography better than it maps two coherent social classes. Both categories contained officeholding families, and their boundaries shifted. What changed was partly the weight of new recruitment and reputational networks within the institutions of court.

By the later sixteenth century, competition among literati no longer centered on a simple Sarim–Hungu opposition. The Easterners and Westerners emerged around a personnel dispute in 1575 and wider alignments among officials, teachers, and associates. Later divisions produced Southerners and Northerners, and eventually Old Doctrine (*Noron*) and Young Doctrine (*Soron*) within the Westerners. Names should not deceive: “east” and “west” initially referred to residences associated with protagonists, not stable geopolitical platforms. Splits often outlived their triggering controversy because a verdict affected careers, honor, and the status of teachers and the dead. Once an official had signed a memorial, prosecuted an opponent, or defended a condemned scholar, reversal could threaten his credibility and allies.

## Purges were personnel actions with expanding stakes

The literati purges of 1498, 1504, 1519, and 1545 preceded the mature *bungdang* alignments but exposed the same institutional danger: criticism, historical writing, succession, royal affinity, and appointments could be fused into a criminal case. Purge was not simply a debate becoming rude. It involved the machinery of the state—interrogation, legal classification, confiscation, exile, execution, and sometimes retrospective punishment or rehabilitation. The 1498 purge, for example, turned an allegedly disloyal passage associated with Kim Jong-jik and recorded by his disciple Kim Il-son into a prosecution reaching through a scholarly network. Under Jungjong, Jo Gwang-jo's rapid promotion and reform program ended in the 1519 purge when royal confidence collapsed and opponents framed his coalition as dangerously exclusive.

Two cautions follow. First, it is too simple to say that kings merely watched factions destroy one another. Monarchs selected officials, exploited counterweights, authorized punishments, and sometimes used attacks on censorial or scholarly groups to recover room for action. A purge could be a royal strategy against an institutional challenge as well as victory by one elite network. Second, a purge did not erase a coalition. Exile dispersed adherents but could preserve identity. Students maintained a teacher's texts; descendants sought restoration; local scholars commemorated the condemned; later governments reversed verdicts and bestowed posthumous honors. Martyrdom created moral capital that survivors could not have manufactured as easily in office.

Later “changes of government” (*hwanguk*) under Sukjong demonstrate the destructive potential of a court in which factional alternation was not protected. In 1680, 1689, and 1694, sharp reversals displaced governing groups and brought executions, exile, and rehabilitation. Disputes over royal marriage, succession, and the status of consorts were not court gossip detached from institutions: designation of an heir determined the future source of favor, while ritual and legitimacy arguments gave officials grounds to intervene. But narrating these episodes through personalities alone conceals the mechanism. A new royal decision changed appointments; new officeholders reopened the conduct of predecessors; legal punishment made the personnel reversal harder to undo; and each side accumulated injuries that raised the price of coexistence.

## Kinship helped coalitions endure, but did not determine them

Joseon political society became increasingly patrilineal over the dynasty. Elite descent groups compiled genealogies, concentrated ritual around male lines, maintained ancestral sites, arranged marriages, and developed durable local seats. These changes, treated in [[Patrilineal transformation of the Joseon family]], gave political networks memory and resources beyond an individual's career. A punished official's sons, nephews, affines, and disciples could preserve documents, pursue rehabilitation, or avoid a compromised alliance. Marriage connected officeholding houses, while a distinguished ancestor and record of examination success strengthened claims to standing.

Kinship was nevertheless neither a complete faction map nor an automatic command structure. Large descent groups contained branches of unequal wealth and divergent politics. Affinal ties crossed factional lines. Officials sometimes broke with relatives or teachers over succession, ritual, diplomacy, or strategy. A lineage name could conceal different local bases, and later genealogies could tidy relationships that had once been uncertain. It is safer to treat descent and marriage as channels through which trust, resources, liability, and memory traveled. They made some coalitions cheaper to maintain and some betrayals more costly; they did not generate a political position by themselves.

Private academies (*seowon*), local schools, community compacts, and shrine associations also connected central contests to provincial society. An academy educated students and honored selected sages; royal charters and tax privileges could enhance its authority. Scholars gathered around teachers and texts, communicated judgments about public men, and mobilized memorials. A faction excluded from central posts could thus retain a provincial audience and recruit a later generation. Yet no equation—academy equals factional branch office—is adequate. Academies had educational, ritual, economic, and local-status functions, and neighboring elite families could contest their control. Their political importance lay precisely in joining these functions.

## Coalition durability came from repeated games and moral memory

Several mechanisms explain why factions lasted despite lacking formal membership. First, government was a repeated personnel game. Today's junior censor might become tomorrow's minister, examiner, or recommender; a coalition benefited from cultivating careers over decades. Second, Joseon's documentary culture preserved positions. Memorials, verdicts, biographies, genealogies, collected writings, and eventually the Veritable Records made it difficult to pretend that a consequential stand had never occurred. Third, Confucian commemoration extended politics across death. Decisions about posthumous title, shrine inclusion, or rehabilitation altered the moral genealogy claimed by living scholars.

Fourth, exclusion sometimes strengthened boundaries. When one group monopolized desirable posts, outsiders had incentives to coordinate rather than defect individually. When rulers tried “impartiality” or *tangpyeong* policies, they did more than ask officials to be nicer. Yeongjo and Jeongjo sought to manage recruitment across factional divisions, distinguish legitimate principle from selfish party adherence, and restore royal control over personnel. Such balancing could broaden appointments and reduce retaliatory swings, but it also let the monarch define acceptable factional identity. It did not abolish networks or make all appointments neutral.

Coalitions could also decay. Leaders died; disciples disagreed about the proper inheritance; policy shocks split allies; a long spell out of office weakened access to posts; and royal patronage elevated new intermediaries. The Northerners' association with Gwanghaegun's government made the 1623 coup especially devastating to them. The Westerners subsequently divided, while Southerners sometimes returned but did not regain equivalent central dominance after later reversals. By the nineteenth century, politics is often described through *sedo* or in-law government: a narrow set of affinal houses secured extraordinary access around young or weak monarchs. This was not the sudden replacement of institutions by family. It was a changed concentration within the same ecology of royal access, appointments, examination prestige, and kinship, with fewer effective counterweights.

## Who bore the costs?

Most named faction members were male civil literati. That fact reflects both the distribution of formal office and the archive's categories, not the social boundary of political consequence. Purge could affect households through execution, exile, confiscation, blocked careers, and inherited stigma. Wives and other kin managed displaced households, property, ritual continuity, petitions, and marriage prospects, but court-centered records often make this labor visible only indirectly. Royal and elite women could be pivotal in succession and affinity politics while being represented through moralized accounts written by male officials.

Provincial elite families gained or lost access to offices, academies, charters, and central patrons. Local rivals could translate a court verdict into control of a shrine, school, register, or community compact. Commoners, *nobi*, clerks, soldiers, merchants, and tenants rarely belonged to a *bungdang* in the same sense, yet factional governments made fiscal, military, legal, and relief policy that affected them. Competition could open investigation of a corrupt administrator; it could also paralyze appointments, protect an ally, or make local disputes more punitive. A general claim that “factionalism harmed the people” proves little unless linked to a particular decision and its enforcement.

Social incidence therefore has to be traced policy by policy. The long debate over the Uniform Land Tax Law (*Daedongbeop*), for example, involved factional sponsorship but also regional transport, procurement, landholding, official finance, and contractor interests. [[Joseon land taxation from gwajeon to daedong]] shows why labeling one camp reformist and another conservative is insufficient. Coalition alignment affected whether a proposal reached office and survived turnover; it did not supply every participant with the same motive or determine implementation in every province.

## From colonial pathology to institutional history

Factionalism acquired unusually heavy historiographical baggage. Late-Joseon critics themselves condemned selfish party spirit, so the pathology thesis was not invented from nothing. In the colonial period, however, Japanese scholarship and official ideology made endemic factional strife one sign that Koreans were supposedly incapable of autonomous, progressive government. A court-centered sequence of quarrels could then stand for the character of an entire people and help rationalize colonial rule. Some post-liberation narratives retained a moralized “national weakness” even while reversing the political judgment.

Later scholarship has complicated that inheritance in several ways. Institutional history reconstructs the censorial, personnel, and monarchical structures that made alignment rational. Social history connects central officials to marriage, descent, local education, land, and status. Intellectual history takes ritual and classical argument seriously without accepting participants' declarations of pure principle at face value. Political history restores royal agency and shows that the balance changed by reign and crisis. Quantitative work on examination rosters, marriage, officeholding, and genealogies can test whether alleged groups actually clustered, although the survival and construction of those records introduce their own bias.

Calling factional politics an institution does not mean celebrating it or claiming it was formally designed as a party system. “Institution” here means a recurring set of rules, offices, expectations, and relationships that structured action. It directs attention to variation. Censorial opposition could improve information or become coordinated obstruction. Coalition continuity could sustain a policy or enforce exclusion. Moral accountability could restrain a king or turn disagreement into a contest over the opponent's character. Purges were not the essence of all Joseon politics, but they were a recurrent possibility when institutional criticism, personnel monopoly, royal insecurity, and criminal sanction aligned.

## How to read the sources

The *Joseon wangjo sillok* is indispensable for memorials, appointments, arguments, and chronological sequence. It is not a stenographic transcript or a neutral window into faction membership. Historiographers compiled reign records after a monarch's death from daily records and other documents; compilers worked within later political conditions. Recorded speech is selected and formalized. An accusation that someone formed a “party” is evidence that the accusation was politically available, not automatic proof of a bounded organization.

The *Seungjeongwon ilgi*, diaries of the Royal Secretariat, can supply denser daily proceedings for the surviving later period. Official codes describe jurisdictions and authorized procedure, but repetition of a rule may disclose enforcement difficulty rather than compliance. Collected writings, letters, academy records, genealogies, and epitaphs reveal networks omitted by central annals, while also commemorating authors and ancestors selectively. Purge narratives and posthumous biographies are often written by beneficiaries of rehabilitation. Genealogies can clarify descent and marriage but may incorporate later corrections, exclusions, or prestigious inventions.

A defensible analysis therefore separates at least four claims: an actor used a factional label; contemporaries coordinated on an appointment or memorial; a durable relationship connected named people across episodes; and those people shared a policy or philosophical position. These propositions often overlap, but none proves all the others. Network evidence should be dated, not projected backward from a later split. Most importantly, court evidence should not be made to represent society without local, fiscal, legal, and household evidence. That discipline replaces gossip with institutional explanation while preserving the coercion, inequality, and contingency of the politics itself.

## Open questions

- How closely do officeholding and marriage-network reconstructions match the faction labels used in memorials for each reign?
- When did local academy affiliation predict central alignment, and where did local rivalries cut across court factions?
- Which fiscal and military policies survived changes of governing coalition, and what protected them from reversal?
- How did women in exiled or rehabilitated households preserve political memory in sources not organized around formal office?
