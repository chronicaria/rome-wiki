Catholicism survived repeated Joseon persecutions because books, households, women, itinerant teachers, and sacramental contacts made a dispersed community—but those same ties also made believers legible to relatives, neighbors, and interrogators.

Up: [[Korean history (MOC)]]

## The causal core

Catholic persecution in Joseon was neither one continuous campaign nor a simple collision between “Korea” and “the West.” A religion first appropriated by Korean readers became an organized community before a resident foreign clergy existed. Its claims about one creator, salvation, the soul, and the moral equality of human beings gave converts reasons to form ties that could cross some inherited boundaries of lineage, locality, gender, and status. Its ban on sacrifices before ancestral tablets, however, struck at rites through which families reproduced hierarchy and officials recognized proper order. Secret meetings, unauthorized religious specialists, cross-border communication, and later European priests then supplied governments with further grounds for treating the community as an illicit association.

Repression and network formation therefore drove one another. Kin sanctions and arrests pushed believers out of visible elite circles toward trusted households, mountain settlements, market routes, and relations of religious sponsorship. Books and memorized prayers allowed practice without priests. Women could host meetings, teach, carry messages, and maintain devotions within spaces less accessible to unrelated men. Lay leaders recruited and instructed others; guides moved clergy across the border; believers housed strangers because baptism created an obligation that could compete with lineage. Yet the network was never socially weightless. Converts depended on relatives, servants, tenants, and neighbors, and police learned to follow precisely those dependencies. Torture, denunciation, confiscation, and collective liability could turn a chain of trust into a chain of arrests.

The best explanation is thus a feedback loop:

1. Catholic texts and interpersonal teaching created small circles of conviction.
2. Conflict over rites and authority exposed those circles to family discipline and state prosecution.
3. Dispersion made the church less dependent on a few capital elites and encouraged portable, household-based practices.
4. New sacramental and charitable obligations joined people who were not linked only by descent.
5. Those ties enabled survival and expansion, while also giving investigators pathways to books, safe houses, catechists, and priests.
6. Each major persecution altered the community inherited by the next; it did not merely repeat an unchanged “anti-Christian” policy.

## From scholarly reading to a lay church

European learning reached Joseon through the Qing capital long before a Catholic missionary entered Korea. Korean members of tribute embassies encountered books, objects, and clergy in Beijing. Some late eighteenth-century scholars discussed the learning called *Seohak*, or Western Learning, within broader arguments over cosmology, ethics, and evidentiary scholarship. In 1784 Yi Seung-hun was baptized in Beijing and returned to a circle that included Yi Byeok and other Korean readers. They did not wait for a foreign mission to organize worship. Lay believers taught, baptized, selected leaders, and at one point improvised a clerical hierarchy before learning that Catholic canon law did not permit it.

Calling this a “self-founded” or “lay-founded” church captures something important but can mislead. Chinese translations produced by Jesuits and other Catholic writers, the Beijing church, and the annual embassy route were indispensable. The new community was Korean-led, not untouched by transnational institutions. Its early recruits were also not a representative cross-section of Joseon. Literate men from interconnected families supplied access to books and argument. Their social capital accelerated diffusion, while factional association—especially with branches of the Southerners (*Namin*)—made religious affiliation usable in court politics.

The medium soon widened the audience. Oral teaching, vernacular prayer, and Korean-script texts reduced dependence on classical-Chinese literacy. Jeong Yak-jong's *Jugyo yoji* (*Essentials of the Lord's Teaching*) explained doctrine in Korean and became one of the works on which believers relied after 1801. A text was not a mass medium by itself: manuscripts and printed copies required money, copying, custody, readers, and teachers. But a portable book could outlive an executed leader and travel farther than an elite discussion circle. Repeated reading and memorization gave a household a small ritual archive that police could seize but not wholly eradicate.

## Why ancestral rites became a political fault line

In 1790 the bishop of Beijing communicated Rome's prohibition on Catholic participation in ancestral sacrifices. The resulting crisis was not adequately described as private freedom of belief versus superstition. In Joseon, rites before ancestral tablets expressed filial duty, organized descent, distinguished legitimate lines, and connected household morality to state orthodoxy. Elite officeholders were evaluated as sons and ritual actors as well as administrators. Refusal could deprive kin of expected service and publicly suggest that canonical teaching was false.

The 1791 Jinsan incident made the conflict concrete. Yun Ji-chung did not perform the prescribed rites after his mother's death and, with his cousin Gwon Sang-yeon, was implicated in destroying or setting aside the ancestral tablet. Both were executed. The affair divided the early circle. Some renounced Catholicism or tried to separate its moral teachings from prohibited practice; others treated obedience to the church as binding even against lineage demands. The episode exposed a mechanism that recurred: conversion was discovered at funerals, feast days, and memorial observances because nonparticipation could not remain purely inward.

Ritual conflict was central, but it was not the sole cause of every persecution. Officials also used older legal categories for heterodox teaching, illicit gatherings, deceptive specialists, and offenses against hierarchy. Governments feared uncontrolled associations because teaching, money, travel, and loyalty could circulate outside registered institutions. The arrival of foreign clergy added violations of border and residence controls. In the age of Western gunboats, Catholic connections could appear inseparable from imperial coercion. Different regencies and court coalitions combined these concerns in different proportions.

Nor did Catholic life abolish Confucian family feeling. Martyr narratives often represented filial affection, loyalty, chastity, courage, and posthumous honor through a vocabulary intelligible to Joseon readers. Converts could reject sacrifice while caring intensely about parents and kin. The conflict rearranged ritual obligation; it did not make believers socially detached individuals.

## 1801: factional purge, institutional destruction, and dispersal

The Sinyu persecution of 1801 followed King Jeongjo's death and the accession of the child Sunjo under Queen Dowager Jeongsun's regency. The political fall of Southerner rivals and a campaign against Catholic teaching overlapped. Officials arrested leaders, searched for books, demanded apostasy, executed some believers, and exiled or punished others. Zhou Wenmo, the Chinese priest secretly in Korea since 1794, surrendered and was executed. Jeong Yak-jong was executed; other members of the extended Jeong family met death, exile, or lasting suspicion. The repression removed a great part of the capital leadership and interrupted translation and instruction.

Hwang Sa-yeong's Silk Letter intensified the political meaning assigned to foreign connection. Hiding after the arrests, Hwang wrote an extensive report intended for the bishop of Beijing. It catalogued suffering and proposed extraordinary means of external rescue, including pressure by Western power. The letter was intercepted, Hwang was executed, and the court could present the document as evidence that Catholic allegiance endangered sovereignty. It would be wrong to make Hwang's proposals the secret program of all believers; a desperate individual's undelivered appeal was not a collective platform. It would be equally wrong to dismiss its effect. The state possessed a text that connected a clandestine domestic church to imagined foreign intervention.

The surviving documentation requires source criticism. Interrogation records preserve names, routes, meetings, books, kin relations, and statements that may be unavailable elsewhere. They were created to convict. Answers were elicited under unequal and often violent conditions; scribes regularized speech into legal formulas; suspects minimized, redirected blame, recanted, or adopted the language of martyrdom. Later church compilations preserve indispensable testimony too, but their questions favored perseverance, sanctity, and sacramental correctness. A name appearing in both a judicial dossier and a beatification inquiry is not automatically an unmediated voice. The convergence can establish events while the framing of motive remains contested.

The institutional effect of 1801 was paradoxical. The loss of concentrated leadership and priestly access damaged catechesis and sacramental life. It also dispersed survivors. Some moved into remote communities or joined older pockets of believers away from Seoul. A church embedded in a few prominent networks became harder to eliminate as it spread among commoners and socially marginal households. This was not persecution “causing growth” in a mechanical sense: executions, apostasy, fear, and lost books reduced the community. Rather, those who persisted developed forms of organization compatible with illegality.

## What made a clandestine community work

The household was both refuge and danger. A trusted home could conceal a prayer meeting, catechism, traveling teacher, or priest. Marriage and kinship could join Catholic clusters across villages. Widows and unmarried women sometimes possessed more room than wives under a husband's ritual authority, though their economic vulnerability was greater. Female believers could instruct children and other women, prepare candidates, sustain common prayer, offer lodging, and transmit messages. The prominence of women in martyr records is evidence of agency, but also of the church's later selection of exemplary lives; it does not by itself yield a demographic census.

Religious kinship supplemented descent. Baptismal names placed believers in a sacred history, while godparent-like sponsorship, instruction, confession, communion, and care for imprisoned members generated duties beyond the lineage. Catholics sometimes called one another siblings in faith. Teaching that all people shared a creator could challenge claims that moral or spiritual worth followed hereditary rank. People of lower status and women could become respected teachers or martyrs.

Yet equality needs careful qualification. A universal soul did not automatically dissolve slavery, patriarchy, elite manners, or economic dependence. Literate men retained advantages in correspondence and leadership; priests later introduced another male hierarchy. Converts carried status expectations into Catholic communities even when doctrine made those expectations debatable. The new network crossed and bent social boundaries more often than it erased them.

Material practices held it together. Catechisms, calendars, rosaries, sacred images, copied prayers, and memorized formulas standardized belief across distance. Couriers and guides connected villages to Beijing and, after 1836, to clandestine French missionaries. Contributions supported journeys, clergy, prisoners, and bereaved households. Markets and ordinary travel could mask religious movement. Remote settlements offered concealment but also exposed believers to poverty and denunciation. No single secret organization commanded the entire community; it was a layered web with strong local clusters and fragile long-distance bridges.

These same mechanisms explain police success. Authorities seized addressable objects and questioned owners about their provenance. A captured guide exposed safe houses; a household head's arrest endangered dependents; a relative could be punished or pressured to denounce. Officials mapped affiliation through social questions long before “network analysis” existed: Who taught you? Who baptized you? Where did you meet the priest? Who kept the book? Persecution targeted relationships, not abstract belief alone.

## 1839 and 1846: a transnational church under regency politics

After years without resident clergy, lay Catholics repeatedly sought priests through Beijing and Macao. Rome created the Apostolic Vicariate of Korea in 1831 and entrusted it to the Paris Foreign Missions Society. French missionaries entered secretly beginning in 1836, aided by Korean guides and safe houses. This changed Catholic organization. Priests supplied sacraments, trained leaders, counted adherents, corresponded abroad, and linked local communities to a global hierarchy. It also made the church more visibly transgressive of Joseon's border regime.

The Gihae persecution of 1839 occurred amid a court struggle associated with the regency of the Andong Kim clan and the growing influence of the Pungyang Jo clan. Authorities arrested believers and executed three French missionaries—Bishop Laurent Imbert and priests Pierre Maubant and Jacques Chastan—along with Korean Catholics. The records show investigators moving through chains of hosts, catechists, relatives, and intermediaries. They also show that arrest did not produce one response: some confessed faith, some apostatized, some evaded, and some supplied information. Martyrdom is central to Catholic memory, but survival through compromise and flight was at least as important to the community's continuation.

The Byeongo persecution of 1846 was smaller and belongs to a distinct political moment. Its best-known victim, Kim Taegon, had been ordained in Shanghai in 1845 as the first Korean Catholic priest. His work included navigating a maritime entry route for missionaries. Arrest therefore joined priestly identity to illegal mobility and foreign contact. Kim's execution and the killing of other believers again deprived the church of scarce personnel, while letters and testimony carried their stories abroad. The interval between 1839 and 1846, and the two decades before 1866, matters: persecution varied with court conditions and enforcement rather than operating at a constant intensity.

## 1866: security crisis and mass repression

The Byeongin persecution began under the regency of Heungseon Daewongun early in King Gojong's reign. By then the international context had changed sharply. British and French forces had occupied Beijing in 1860; Russia pressed in Northeast Asia; missionaries' presence demonstrated that borders could be crossed; and the Taiping civil war made heterodox Christianity appear politically explosive even though Taiping religion was not Catholicism. Some Korean Catholics explored whether French missionaries could help mediate against Russia. When that possibility collapsed, the contact could look like proof of precisely the foreign entanglement officials feared.

Authorities executed nine of the twelve French missionaries then in Korea and killed large numbers of Korean believers over a campaign that continued beyond its opening year. Exact totals remain uncertain and confessional estimates should not be treated as a complete state census. The scale, geographic reach, and duration nevertheless distinguished Byeongin from 1846. Arrests devastated communities, drove survivors into hiding and migration, and reinforced a Catholic geography of remote villages and refuges.

Repression also became entangled with imperial violence. Missionary Félix-Clair Ridel escaped and reported the executions in China. A French naval expedition attacked Ganghwa later in 1866. As [[Foreign incursions before the opening of Korea]] emphasizes, the expedition followed the Catholic executions, not the *General Sherman* incident. French retaliation did not rescue the church. It validated official claims that foreign priests might bring foreign cannon, while subjecting Koreans to an unauthorized coercive response they did not collectively request. Hwang Sa-yeong's imagined rescue of 1801 and the expedition of 1866 were different events, but later memory could join them into a powerful suspicion.

## Persecution changed the church it failed to destroy

Joseon's anti-Catholic campaigns succeeded in killing leaders, interrupting sacraments, destroying texts, inducing apostasy, impoverishing families, and restricting public organization. Survival should not be romanticized as victory. The costs fell unevenly: people without resources to flee or purchase help were exposed; dependents suffered for a household member's choices; denunciation fractured trust. A martyr-centered narrative can obscure those who recanted under torture, practiced intermittently, protected relatives by silence, or left no heroic dossier.

But suppression did not restore the social world that preceded Catholicism. It helped produce a decentralized religious repertoire: household worship, lay catechesis, memorized prayer, clandestine hospitality, border guides, and support for prisoners and refugees. Successive losses transferred authority to new people and places. Women and lower-status converts did not become socially equal, yet the community gave some of them roles and sacred prestige unavailable through examination office or agnatic ritual. The church's global ties furnished resources and clergy, while repeatedly exposing it to plausible charges of illegal foreign connection.

The causal lesson is broader than a story of tolerant ideas defeating an intolerant state. Joseon's government policed ritual because family order, political loyalty, and cosmic legitimacy were institutionally connected. Catholics formed alternative ties because doctrine required shared practice, not merely private assent. Persecution made those ties costly and clandestine; clandestinity made them resilient in some places and vulnerable in others. By the treaties of the 1880s, formal diplomatic relations and foreign pressure changed the legal setting, and toleration emerged unevenly rather than through a single decree. The communities that entered that new era had been remade by a century of selective violence.

## Why this matters

This history explains why religion belongs inside, not beside, Joseon social and political history. The ancestral-rites dispute reveals how household practices constituted governance. Catholic books reveal that intellectual exchange could generate institutions without conquest or a resident mission. Persecution records expose the reach and limits of the state at village and household scale. The church's survival shows how networks can become robust through dispersal while remaining vulnerable at their bridges.

It also complicates nationalist and confessional stories. Catholicism was neither simply a Western implant nor simply an indigenous democratic movement. Korean actors selected, translated, debated, and organized it; they also sought foreign clergy and sometimes foreign protection. The state was neither reacting to pure fantasy nor confronting a unified conspiracy. Real border violations and imperial danger were interpreted through court rivalry, ritual orthodoxy, and sweeping collective suspicion. Martyr archives preserve courage and community, but they are advocacy archives as well as historical evidence. Judicial archives preserve social relations, but through coercive categories. Reading them against one another makes the network visible without turning testimony into transparent fact.

## Open questions

- How far can surviving interrogation dossiers support a quantitative reconstruction of local Catholic networks without mistaking the police archive for the whole community?
- Which women maintained Catholic teaching between persecutions but disappeared from martyr-centered records because they survived?
- How did Catholic refuge villages differ by region in land tenure, status composition, relations with non-Catholic neighbors, and exposure to officials?
- When did religious sponsorship and marriage ties become more important than the original scholarly and factional connections of the 1780s?
- How should historians reconcile competing estimates for deaths, apostasy, and total membership during the Byeongin persecution?
