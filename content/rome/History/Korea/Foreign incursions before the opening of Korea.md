The French expedition of 1866, the United States expedition of 1871, and Japanese coercion in 1875–1876 formed a connected sequence of gunboat encounters, but only the last converted battlefield pressure into a durable treaty regime.

## The problem was not simple “isolation”

Late Joseon is often described as a “hermit kingdom” that refused all foreign contact until superior force opened it. That shorthand obscures the problem Korean decision-makers were trying to solve. Joseon maintained regular diplomatic relations with Qing China, sent missions to Beijing, received Qing envoys, exchanged missions with Tokugawa Japan, traded through designated border and port channels, and handled castaways. Its policy was better understood as **regulated permeability**: the court distinguished legitimate relations conducted through recognized institutions from unauthorized landing, proselytizing, surveying, commerce, and demands made by armed ships. See [[Markets and monetization in late Joseon]] for the economic side of that distinction.

Western governments brought a different diplomatic grammar. They expected written treaties between formally sovereign states, accredited representatives, commercial access, protection for their nationals, and navigational practices that treated coastal surveying as ordinary. Joseon authorities understood unlicensed approach to the Han River—the route to the capital—as a security breach. Each side therefore interpreted acts such as sailing, sounding channels, exchanging letters, and firing warning shots through incompatible assumptions about jurisdiction.

The three confrontations were not repetitions. France went to punish the killing of missionaries and contemplated regime-changing claims but lacked the authorization and force for conquest. The United States combined inquiry into a destroyed merchant ship with an attempt to open treaty negotiations; after a disputed survey encounter it mounted a punitive assault, won decisively on the ground, yet obtained no negotiations. Meiji Japan possessed something the two distant powers lacked: proximity, prior institutional relations, sustained political attention, knowledge of the peninsula, and the ability to return with a treaty mission backed by warships. It also adapted Western gunboat diplomacy after having experienced it itself.

## 1866: persecution, the *General Sherman*, and French retaliation

The crisis year 1866 contained several different encounters that later became causally entangled. Early that year, the court launched the Byeongin persecution of Catholics. The policy arose from more than a timeless hatred of foreign religion. Catholic refusal of ancestral rites had long raised political and social objections; clandestine missionaries violated border controls; the Anglo-French occupation of Beijing in 1860 demonstrated Western military danger; and Russian approaches in the northeast sharpened fears about imperial penetration. A proposal to use French missionaries as intermediaries against Russia briefly seemed useful, then became politically dangerous. The government arrested missionaries and believers. Nine of the twelve French missionaries then in Korea were executed, along with many Korean Catholics.

In August, the American-owned merchant schooner *General Sherman*, with a multinational crew, entered the Taedong River toward Pyongyang seeking trade. Korean authorities repeatedly indicated that unauthorized commerce was prohibited and ordered it away. The ship grounded or became trapped as the water fell. Accounts differ in detail, and later national narratives simplify responsibility, but the confrontation escalated after coercive acts that included detaining a Korean official and firing from the ship. Local forces and civilians destroyed the vessel by fire and killed its crew. The incident would become one American rationale for the 1871 mission, although Washington initially possessed fragmentary evidence about what had occurred.

The French expedition followed the Catholic executions, not the *General Sherman*. Félix-Clair Ridel, one of the missionaries who escaped, carried news to French authorities in China. Henri de Bellonet, France's acting representative at Beijing, declared in July that the Korean king had forfeited his throne and suggested that France could dispose of Korea. That extraordinary threat exceeded what Paris had authorized. Rear Admiral Pierre-Gustave Roze had naval resources in East Asia, but not an army capable of conquering the peninsula or occupying Seoul.

Roze first conducted a reconnaissance in September. His ships probed the difficult channels toward the Han and produced charts. The main force returned in October with seven vessels and roughly one thousand men. Rather than risk larger ships in the shallow, tidal, fortified approach to Seoul, the French occupied Ganghwa, the island commanding the western approaches to the capital. They seized the administrative center, arms, silver, records, and royal books. The occupation demonstrated a major technological imbalance: modern naval guns and percussion firearms could suppress fixed defenses and defeat troops armed largely with older weapons.

Yet occupation of Ganghwa did not compel the court. Joseon mobilized forces, repaired defenses, and avoided conceding the central French demands. Korean troops contested French movement at Munsusan and elsewhere. On 9 November (dates can vary by calendar conversion), a French detachment attacking Jeongjoksanseong met determined fire from forces under Yang Heon-su and suffered enough casualties to expose the cost of moving inland. Roze withdrew later that month, taking plundered materials, including books from the Oegyujanggak royal library.

Calling the result simply a Korean “victory” or French “victory” confuses levels of analysis. France won most direct engagements, occupied a strategically important island, and removed cultural property. It failed to overthrow the government, secure a treaty, establish religious protection, or open commerce. Joseon preserved its policy autonomy but could not prevent invasion, losses, or plunder. The court drew a politically powerful lesson: disciplined refusal and local resistance could outlast a limited expedition. That lesson was rational given the actual French withdrawal, even though it underestimated what a more persistent neighboring power might do.

## 1871: an American military victory without a diplomatic result

The United States approached Korea through Qing diplomatic channels. Washington wanted information about the *General Sherman*, protection for shipwrecked sailors, and a treaty of amity and commerce. Frederick Low, the U.S. minister to China, and Rear Admiral John Rodgers led the mission. Their instructions and correspondence show an unstable combination: they described peaceful negotiation as the object, but brought a substantial naval force and assumed that a conspicuous display of power would make officials engage.

The expedition arrived near Ganghwa in May 1871. Korean representatives and Americans exchanged communications without establishing shared rules. Language passed through interpreters and Chinese writing; the rank of intermediaries mattered because Joseon sought to keep unauthorized foreigners away from the royal government, while the Americans interpreted lower-ranking contacts as delay. Most consequentially, the Americans announced that they intended to survey the waters. They believed officials had received notice and had not explicitly objected. Korean authorities did not accept that notice as permission to send armed vessels through a defended channel toward the capital.

On 1 June, American vessels entered the Ganghwa narrows to take soundings. Shore batteries fired; American ships returned fire. Both governments could represent the other as the aggressor because their premises diverged. The American report called surveying peaceful and treated fire upon the flag as an offense requiring apology. From the Korean position, armed foreign vessels were penetrating restricted waters past fortifications after the danger had been made evident. The ambiguity did not make force inevitable. It made escalation easier because neither side would accept the other's account of lawful movement.

Low and Rodgers demanded an apology and waited about ten days. When no acceptable response came, Rodgers launched a punitive landing. On 10–11 June, sailors and marines captured a succession of forts, culminating at Gwangseongbo. Korean commander Eo Jae-yeon and many defenders were killed. American reports counted 243 Korean dead and twenty captured, while the U.S. force suffered three killed and ten wounded; exact Korean totals vary by source and counting method. The disparity reflected naval bombardment, modern firearms, and the vulnerability of fortifications once approached from the landward side. Korean soldiers nevertheless fought at close range rather than accept surrender, a fact American participants themselves recorded.

Tactical success produced no treaty. The expedition held forts briefly and took the commander's flag, but it lacked a political partner willing and authorized to negotiate under those conditions. Captured Koreans were not useful bargaining assets; the court did not treat their recovery as grounds for concession. The Americans could raid Ganghwa, but marching on Seoul or maintaining an occupation would have required a different mandate and much larger commitment. They withdrew in July.

The expedition thus exposed a central limitation of punitive gunboat diplomacy: punishment can demonstrate destructive capacity without creating compliance. Low and Rodgers assumed that defeat would raise the cost of refusal. Joseon officials could instead read withdrawal as proof that refusal had worked again. The United States did not return immediately to force the issue; a Korean-American treaty came only in 1882, after Japan had already opened the treaty-port era and Qing officials encouraged wider treaty relations as a strategic counterweight.

The French and American expeditions also strengthened the domestic position of the *Daewongun*, the king's father and dominant political figure. The government erected anti-appeasement stelae (*cheokhwabi*) in 1871 declaring that failure to fight Western invasion meant making peace, and making peace meant selling the country. The slogan compressed a real sovereignty dilemma into a moral binary. Refusal protected jurisdiction in the immediate encounters, but it also made experimentation with selective treaties politically easier to denounce as treason.

## From the Meiji rupture to the *Un'yō* incident

Japan's leverage developed through a separate diplomatic rupture. For centuries, Joseon relations with Tokugawa Japan had been mediated through established forms involving the Tsushima domain, Korean embassies, and carefully negotiated status language. The Meiji Restoration replaced the shogunate and announced a new imperial government. Japanese communications used titles and diplomatic formulations that Joseon officials regarded as incompatible with the existing order—most controversially characters associated with imperial sovereignty. Joseon refused to accept some of these documents. Japanese leaders debated responses, including the coercive project later called *Seikanron* (“debate on conquering” or chastising Korea). The 1873 political crisis did not produce an immediate invasion, but it did not restore the old relationship either.

Japan was now building a centralized conscript state and modern navy while revising its place in a treaty system imposed by Western powers. Korea mattered not only as a commercial opportunity but as a nearby strategic space. Japanese officials feared rival influence on the peninsula, sought recognition of the Meiji order, and increasingly treated Korean refusal as an affront to the emperor and an obstacle to national security. Domestic political competition also made a Korean settlement useful: decisive external action could display the new government's authority.

In September 1875, the Japanese gunboat *Un'yō* approached Ganghwa while ostensibly surveying and seeking water. Its movement reproduced a critical feature of 1871: an armed foreign vessel entered sensitive waters guarding the capital and presented hydrographic activity as normal navigation. A Korean battery fired. The *Un'yō* returned heavier fire, destroyed the position, landed men, and attacked Yeongjong Island, where Japanese forces killed defenders and seized weapons. Whether described as a deliberate provocation or an aggressively conducted reconnaissance, the voyage created a usable incident. Japan framed Korean fire as an unprovoked insult to its flag and demanded redress.

The resemblance to the American encounter makes the different outcome especially revealing. Joseon's coastal-defense doctrine had not changed enough to distinguish between American and Japanese armed surveying. But Japan could connect the clash to a sustained diplomatic campaign. In early 1876, it sent Kuroda Kiyotaka and Inoue Kaoru with warships and troops to Ganghwa. Negotiations occurred under an evident threat of renewed violence. Qing officials offered advice but neither controlled Joseon's decision nor intervened militarily. Within Joseon, opinion was not uniform: rigid refusal risked a war the government was poorly prepared to fight, while negotiation risked legitimating demands that would weaken control of ports and foreigners.

## The Ganghwa Treaty and the sovereignty paradox

Signed on 26 February 1876, the Treaty of Peace and Friendship—usually called the Ganghwa Treaty—declared in Article 1 that Joseon was “an autonomous state” enjoying equal rights with Japan. Read in isolation, that language appears to affirm Korean sovereignty. In context, it served two purposes. It provided a formally equal basis for direct Japan-Korea relations, and it challenged Qing claims or conventions of suzerainty that might justify Chinese intervention. Formal independence could therefore be used as an instrument of unequal leverage.

The treaty opened Busan and provided for two additional ports, allowed Japanese diplomatic representation, gave Japanese vessels freedom to survey Korean coasts, established assistance for shipwrecks, and placed Japanese subjects in the opened ports under Japanese jurisdiction. The treaty and its August regulations also created permissive commercial conditions, including tariff arrangements unfavorable to Korean fiscal control. Some operational provisions were elaborated after February, so the treaty and its implementing regulations should not be collapsed into one text. Together, however, they created a durable structure rather than a one-time settlement.

This was an **unequal treaty**, but that does not mean Joseon ceased to be sovereign in 1876 or that annexation in 1910 was already determined. The court made a constrained choice among dangerous options. Signing avoided immediate war and created space to learn from Japan and other states. It retained a government, diplomacy, armed forces, taxation, and the capacity to make later treaties. At the same time, extraterritoriality removed foreigners from ordinary Korean jurisdiction, port opening limited control over entry and commerce, and hydrographic access normalized a practice previously treated as an armed intrusion. Those were real erosions of sovereign capacity.

The treaty also transformed the strategic environment. Joseon later sought relations with the United States and European powers partly to diversify external ties; Qing officials likewise encouraged some of these relations to counter Japanese predominance. Reformers used missions abroad to gather knowledge, while defenders of established institutions feared that trade and religious access would dissolve political order. The choice after 1876 was no longer “closed” versus “open.” It was how to distribute access, jurisdiction, revenue, expertise, and force among competing powers while rebuilding state capacity.

## Why Japan succeeded where France and the United States did not

Four differences explain the sequence better than a story in which repeated defeats finally broke Korean will.

First, the coercers wanted different things. France's maximal rhetoric outran its practical expedition, and punishment followed by withdrawal could not create a treaty. The United States wanted negotiation but allowed a survey dispute to redirect the mission toward retaliation. Japan arrived in 1876 with a specific diplomatic settlement, commissioners empowered to negotiate it, and military force staged as leverage.

Second, geography shaped commitment. France and the United States operated from regional squadrons with many responsibilities. Japan faced Korea across a narrow sea and could return repeatedly. Its preexisting relationship through Tsushima supplied channels, personnel, and knowledge even as the Meiji government rejected the old diplomatic form.

Third, tactical force mattered only when joined to credible persistence. French and American weapons took forts but did not make their governments willing to occupy the capital or sustain a costly war. Japan did not need to conquer Joseon in 1876. It needed Korean officials to believe refusal might bring a larger and continuing conflict. The *Un'yō* attack, the subsequent warships, and memories of 1866 and 1871 made that threat legible.

Fourth, Joseon politics changed. The *Daewongun* lost power in 1873, and King Gojong and Queen Min's political network had greater room to consider controlled accommodation. This did not produce consensus or make the treaty voluntary in an unconstrained sense. It altered which risks appeared tolerable. Earlier resistance had protected immediate autonomy; by 1876, leaders also had to reckon with Japan's permanence, military modernization, and the danger that another refusal would produce a war without reliable Qing rescue.

## Why it matters

The decade shows that military superiority did not automatically “open” Korea. Coercion succeeded when it was attached to a practicable political objective, sustained proximity, an empowered negotiating mission, and divisions or adaptations within the target government. Joseon's choices were neither passive isolation nor free consent. In 1866 and 1871, resistance preserved treaty autonomy at severe human and material cost. In 1876, negotiation preserved peace and the state while accepting institutions that constrained jurisdiction and invited deeper competition.

The distinction prevents two errors. One is triumphalism: repelling limited Western expeditions did not eliminate the strategic danger revealed by their ships and guns. The other is inevitability: the Ganghwa Treaty did not contain a predetermined road to annexation. It opened a new arena in which Korean officials, reformers, soldiers, merchants, religious communities, Qing statesmen, Japanese factions, and other powers made consequential choices. Later loss of sovereignty must be explained through those subsequent conflicts, not projected backward as the automatic result of a single treaty.
