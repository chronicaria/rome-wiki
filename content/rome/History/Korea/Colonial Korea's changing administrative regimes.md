Japanese colonial rule in Korea was a single imperial sovereignty with changing techniques: military-police coercion after annexation, selective consultation and managed publicity after the March First Movement, intensified developmental administration in the 1930s, and total-war mobilization after 1937; reform of technique never meant Korean political self-government.

## One colonial state, several governing settlements

Japan annexed Korea on 29 August 1910 after five years of protectorate rule and a longer sequence of military, diplomatic, and financial encroachment. The annexation treaty transferred the Korean emperor's governing rights to the Japanese emperor. Korean consent was neither freely solicited nor institutionally available: Japanese forces and resident-general institutions already constrained the court, and prominent opponents had been imprisoned, exiled, or killed. The new Government-General of Korea (*Chōsen Sōtokufu*) answered to Tokyo and stood above a bureaucracy that combined Japanese officials with a much smaller and subordinate Korean staff.

This framework lasted until Japan's surrender in August 1945. Yet treating the whole period as an unchanging block hides how colonial power learned and adapted. Officials altered police practice, schooling, associational rules, economic planning, propaganda, and the political vocabulary through which empire justified itself. Those changes responded to Korean resistance, metropolitan party politics, international criticism, depression, war in China, and Japan's widening imperial ambitions. They also changed who could bargain with the regime and which institutions penetrated everyday life.

Period labels therefore need two tests. First, did formal rules and administrative capacity change? Second, did the change redistribute meaningful power to colonized people? In Korea the first answer is often yes and the second usually no. “Cultural rule” after 1919 relaxed some visible controls and opened a larger public sphere, but the governor-general remained appointed, Koreans lacked representative sovereignty, the police retained formidable powers, and censorship defined what could safely be said. Wartime “imperialization” later promised Koreans a place within the Japanese nation precisely while conscription, labor mobilization, linguistic pressure, and thought control deepened.

## 1910–1919: annexation and military-police government

The first settlement is commonly called *budan seiji*, “military rule.” The governor-general was normally an active-duty army or navy officer, colonial ordinances carried the force of law, and the gendarmerie participated directly in ordinary policing. Regulations enabled surveillance, summary punishment, restrictions on assembly and publication, and intrusion into villages. The saber worn by teachers became an enduring symbol of the period, although the institutional point matters more than the image: education, policing, cadastral survey, courts, and economic administration were built together as instruments of rule.

The 1912 flogging ordinance illustrates differentiated colonial legality. Corporal punishment, abolished in metropolitan Japan, remained available for Korean defendants convicted of specified offenses. Colonial officials defended it as suitable to local conditions and administratively efficient. The rule exposed a central contradiction in assimilationist rhetoric: Koreans were governed in the emperor's name but denied equal legal treatment. Abolition of judicial flogging in 1920 was a real institutional change produced after mass protest and criticism, not proof that the earlier coercive system had been imaginary.

The regime also sought legibility. The land survey conducted from 1910 to 1918 recorded owners, boundaries, land categories, and taxable values. It created more standardized titles and a fiscal map useful to courts, landlords, lenders, companies, and the colonial treasury. Older nationalist accounts sometimes described the survey as a single act of mass confiscation from cultivators unable to produce documents. Later scholarship has qualified that mechanism: many customary claims were registered, and dispossession did not occur everywhere by direct administrative seizure. Qualification does not make the survey neutral. Registration privileged claims that fitted the state's procedures, strengthened ownership against some cultivation rights, increased tax capacity, and supplied information used by landlords and investors. Its consequences varied among proprietors, tenants, communal users, and people already vulnerable to debt.

Schools likewise combined limited provision with political hierarchy. Enrollment expanded from a very low base, but Korean access remained inferior to that of Japanese settlers, curricula were segregated, and schooling emphasized loyalty, practical training, and Japanese language. Mission schools and Korean private education faced tighter regulation. For some families, even a constrained school offered literacy and mobility; for the state, it trained clerks and disciplined subjects. These functions could coexist.

Japanese settlers, companies, and officials benefited most directly from the new order, but colonial society was not divided into two uniform camps. Korean landlords could use titles, courts, and markets; Korean clerks and police occupied intermediate positions; merchants found new opportunities; tenants and day laborers bore rents and insecurity; women entered schools and wage work under strongly gendered conditions; religious organizations offered both collaboration and protected networks for dissent. The categories “resister” and “collaborator” describe real choices but cannot substitute for analysis of constraint, career, survival, belief, and change over time.

## March First and the reconstruction of legitimacy

On 1 March 1919, activists publicly declared Korean independence in Seoul. Demonstrations spread through towns and rural markets, carried by students, religious networks, teachers, merchants, farmers, and local organizers. Colonial police and troops responded with arrests, shootings, beatings, and destruction. Exact casualty totals remain contested because official counts, movement claims, missionary reports, and later commemorations were produced from different positions. The secure conclusion is that the movement was nationwide, socially broad, and met with lethal repression.

March First did not expel Japan, but it demonstrated that naked coercion had failed to produce legitimacy. It coincided with international talk of self-determination after the First World War and embarrassed a Japanese government seeking recognition as a modern great power. Tokyo replaced Governor-General Hasegawa Yoshimichi with Saitō Makoto and announced a new style commonly called “cultural rule” (*bunka seiji*). The gendarmerie police system was formally replaced by an expanded civilian police force; flogging ended; officials loosened some controls on publication, association, and education; and the formal active-duty-officer restriction on eligibility for governor-general was removed. No civilian was ultimately appointed to the post, so the rule change did not civilianize colonial sovereignty.

These were not cosmetic in every respect. Korean-language newspapers such as the *Dong-A Ilbo* and *Chosun Ilbo* appeared in 1920, though under censorship and recurrent suspension. Educational access expanded, civic associations multiplied, and labor, tenant, youth, women's, religious, socialist, and nationalist groups developed more durable organizations. Cultural production flourished in literature, theater, print, scholarship, and consumer life. Activists debated immediate independence, gradual institution-building, socialism, national culture, and transnational alliances. The colonial public sphere was constrained, but it was not fictitious.

The political settlement remained authoritarian. The governor-general did not answer to a Korean electorate. An appointed advisory Central Council gave selected Korean notables no legislative sovereignty. Police numbers and information gathering increased even as uniforms and legal forms changed. The 1925 Peace Preservation Law criminalized attempts to alter the imperial polity or private-property system, giving authorities a powerful instrument against communists and independence activists. Publications were deleted or banned; meetings were monitored; organizations were infiltrated or dissolved. “Cultural rule” is therefore best understood as a shift from conspicuous military policing toward differentiated governance—selective permission, surveillance, co-optation, and calibrated repression.

This settlement also cultivated intermediaries. Colonial authorities sponsored or tolerated Korean elites willing to advocate education, economic self-strengthening, local improvement, or participation inside imperial institutions. Some believed limited cooperation accumulated national capacity; some protected organizations that could not survive underground; some pursued status and profit; some moved between accommodation and opposition. Retrospective moral judgment is unavoidable where people enabled coercion, but a useful history specifies the act, its timing, available alternatives, and its effects rather than turning “collaboration” into an inherited essence.

## Development, depression, and an empire-wide division of labor

During the 1920s the colonial state promoted rice production for the Japanese market. Irrigation, improved seed, credit, transport, and commercial networks raised output in some regions, while exports to Japan grew. The policy linked Korean agriculture more tightly to imperial prices and finance. Landlords and better-capitalized cultivators could gain; many tenants faced high rents, debt, and insecure contracts. Increased aggregate production did not guarantee improved consumption among the rural poor. Migration to cities, Manchuria, Japan, and the northern frontier became one response to pressure and opportunity.

The Great Depression sharpened agrarian distress and challenged the 1920s settlement. Governor-General Ugaki Kazushige's Rural Revitalization Campaign after 1932 promoted debt reduction, savings, sideline production, village discipline, and self-help. Officials presented poverty partly as a problem of household practice. The campaign did organize cooperatives and local projects, but it could not remove the structural burdens of tenancy, volatile prices, and unequal access to land and credit. Its moral language also extended administrative supervision into household budgets and village leadership.

Industrialization accelerated in the 1930s, especially in northern Korea. Hydroelectric development, chemical plants, mines, railways, and heavy industry served entrepreneur Noguchi Shitagau's conglomerate and broader imperial strategy. The state supplied infrastructure, licenses, power arrangements, and access to resources. Korean workers and entrepreneurs participated, and the industrial base was materially significant; it was not simply an illusion imposed on an agrarian colony. Yet ownership, high management, regional distribution, labor conditions, and strategic purpose were unequal. Much heavy industry was Japanese-controlled and oriented toward an empire whose military frontier had moved into Manchuria after 1931.

This is the center of the “colonial modernity” dispute. One interpretation emphasizes that railways, factories, cadastral institutions, schools, public health, markets, and statistical administration contributed to transformations later inherited by both Korean states. A nationalist rebuttal emphasizes coercive annexation, extraction, discrimination, suppressed alternatives, and the destruction caused by war and division. The strongest analysis refuses the forced choice. Colonial rule produced modern institutions, but “modern” does not mean benevolent, inevitable, or optimally developmental. Infrastructure can raise productive capacity and facilitate extraction; schools can spread literacy and discipline imperial subjects; statistics can reveal society and make it governable. The counterfactual—what sovereign Korean development would have achieved—is not directly observable, so it cannot be used as simple proof for either gratitude or total negation.

## 1937–1945: total war and imperialization

Japan's full-scale war in China from July 1937 transformed colonial government. Korea became a logistical base, industrial platform, recruiting ground, and reservoir of labor. The National General Mobilization Law of 1938 provided an empire-wide framework for controlling capital, goods, prices, media, and labor. In Korea, patriotic associations and neighborhood units carried campaigns into workplaces and households. Rationing, savings drives, metal collection, air-raid preparation, military donations, and ideological ceremonies made war administration intimate.

The slogan *naisen ittai*—Japan and Korea as one body—presented intensified assimilation as inclusion. Schools expanded compulsory Japanese-language practice and emperor-centered moral training; Korean-language instruction and print space contracted sharply. Shrine attendance was enforced through schools and institutions despite Christian and other resistance. The 1939–1940 *sōshi-kaimei* measures required households to establish a *shi* (with a default assigned when none was declared), while changing a personal name was formally optional by application. Korean clan surnames and ancestral seats remained recorded in the registry. Official and local pressure surrounding employment, schooling, approval, and demonstrations of loyalty nevertheless made even legally optional choices coercive in practice.

Mobilization also proceeded in stages. Labor recruitment, officially mediated recruitment, and compulsory conscription each operated under changing rules; conflating them erases chronology and differences in coercion. Korean laborers were sent to mines, factories, construction sites, and military facilities across Japan's empire under contracts and controls that could include deception, withheld wages, dangerous work, violence, and inability to leave. Military conscription was announced in 1942 and implemented for Koreans in 1944, after earlier volunteer systems. Women were mobilized for factories and service, while an unknown number of Korean women and girls were coerced into Japanese military sexual slavery. The documentary record includes recruitment systems, official involvement, survivor testimony, and institutional denial; precise totals remain debated, but uncertainty over totals does not negate the system.

Assimilation and discrimination intensified together. The empire demanded Korean blood, labor, language shift, and public loyalty while preserving unequal access to authority and marking Koreans as suspect. Some Koreans joined the military, police, bureaucracy, industry, or mobilization organizations from conviction, ambition, compulsion, or mixtures of these. Others maintained clandestine movements, crossed borders, evaded labor, preserved language and religious practice, or engaged in everyday noncompliance. Many had no heroic option. A worker choosing between mobilization and punishment, or a parent adopting a name to protect a child's schooling, occupied a different moral position from an official who designed or violently enforced policy.

## How the administrative phases fit together

The four phases were cumulative rather than clean replacements. The 1910s built cadastral, police, court, school, transport, and fiscal systems. The 1920s revised the regime's public face, multiplied controlled institutions, and learned to govern through Korean intermediaries. The early 1930s joined village discipline and industrial planning to a continental strategy. Total war then repurposed the accumulated records, organizations, railways, schools, firms, and neighborhood networks for mobilization.

That cumulative view explains why neither “unchanging repression” nor “gradual liberalization” works. Coercion was present throughout, but its objects and techniques changed. Public space genuinely widened after 1919, then narrowed under ideological and wartime controls. Economic capacity genuinely grew, while its distribution and imperial purpose remained unequal. Assimilation rhetoric became most insistent when the state demanded the most extreme sacrifices. Administrative modernization made later extraction more effective because the regime knew more about land, households, skills, movement, and associations.

It also explains the difficult legacy after 1945. Liberation ended Japanese sovereignty abruptly, but not the social and material world built during thirty-five years. Both occupation zones inherited files, police practices, railways, factories, schools, legal categories, trained personnel, ownership disputes, and memories of resistance and collaboration. Industrial concentration in the north and agricultural patterns in the south affected occupation economies. Returning migrants and former imperial subjects competed for housing, jobs, and recognition. Arguments over who had served the colonial state entered struggles over the legitimacy of new governments. [[Why Korea was divided in 1945]] must therefore begin with Allied military decisions, but it cannot treat the peninsula they divided as institutionally blank.

## Reading the colonial archive critically

The Government-General produced abundant annual reports, statistics, ordinances, maps, photographs, and surveys. These sources are indispensable for reconstructing budgets, personnel, schools, production, arrests, and administrative intent. Their precision can mislead. Categories served policy: “reclaimed land,” “tenant dispute,” “ideological offender,” “volunteer,” and “mobilized worker” may conceal conflict over ownership or consent. Aggregate growth figures do not show distribution. Police reports count what police observed and classify dissent through colonial law. Propaganda images document what officials wanted audiences to see.

Korean newspapers, organizational records, diaries, literature, petitions, court documents, missionary reports, foreign diplomatic papers, material evidence, and oral testimony provide other angles, each with limits. Censored newspapers developed indirect vocabularies. Missionaries had institutional and cultural agendas. Activist memoirs could be shaped by later factional struggles. Survivor testimony records experiences excluded from official paperwork but is affected by trauma, time, and interview conditions. Cross-reading is therefore not a ritual demand for false balance; it is how historians distinguish an ordinance from enforcement, an administrative boast from outcome, and a later memory from a contemporary category.

The most defensible periodization keeps the regime's continuous denial of sovereignty in view while marking changes in its means. Japan did not move from “bad” military rule to benign cultural rule and then accidentally into wartime excess. Nor did every reform merely disguise an identical reality. Colonial governance adapted because Koreans resisted it, because empire's goals changed, and because modern administrative institutions created new capacities. That combination—political exclusion, institutional transformation, uneven opportunity, and escalating mobilization—is the causal structure of colonial Korea.

## Open questions

- How did the same ordinance operate differently in Seoul, provincial towns, farming villages, northern industrial zones, and Korean communities elsewhere in the empire?
- Which quantitative series can be reconstructed across changing colonial definitions without turning administrative categories into neutral social facts?
- How should histories of collaboration distinguish institutional responsibility, coercion, career incentives, ideological commitment, and later political uses of accusation?
- What continuities in personnel and police practice crossed the 1945 divide, and where did Soviet and American occupation authorities create genuinely new structures?
- How can Korean-, Japanese-, and English-language scholarship be cross-read without allowing the accessibility of one archive to decide the argument?
