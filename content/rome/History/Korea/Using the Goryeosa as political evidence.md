The *Goryeosa* is indispensable evidence for Goryeo, but its Joseon compilation makes its categories and silences part of the evidence too.

Up: [[Korean history (MOC)]]

## Two historical layers

The chronicle preserves information about Goryeo government, economy, society, and culture, yet it was compiled after Joseon's dynastic victory and completed in the mid-fifteenth century. Its 139-volume annalistic-biographical structure organizes rulers under “hereditary houses” rather than imperial annals, a choice the National Institute of Korean History connects to Joseon's relationship with Ming and Neo-Confucian legitimacy.

That produces a two-layer reading rule. A passage may support a claim about Goryeo only after its date, genre, and transmission are checked; the compiler's classification can separately support a claim about early Joseon's memory politics. The second claim must never be smuggled into the first.
