Korea's division began as a hurried Allied arrangement for accepting Japan's surrender, but it endured because two occupations converted one liberation crisis into rival state-building projects amid Korean political conflict and the emerging Cold War.

Up: [[Korean history (MOC)]]

The line at 38° north was neither an ancient Korean frontier nor the execution of a long-settled Allied plan to create two Korean states. American officers proposed it in August 1945 as an operational boundary between Soviet and American forces, and Moscow accepted. That decision mattered enormously, but it does not by itself explain why a surrender line became a political border. Division hardened through a sequence: colonial institutions collapsed unevenly; Korean activists built competing organizations; the Soviet and American commands installed incompatible occupation regimes; Allied trusteeship negotiations failed; and separate elections and governments followed in 1948. Each stage narrowed alternatives without making the final outcome inevitable from the start.

## A liberation problem created by empire and war

Japan's annexation of Korea in 1910 had abolished Korean sovereignty and placed the peninsula inside a centralized imperial system. By 1945 railways, heavy industry, power generation, mining, and much administrative capacity were concentrated in the north, while the south contained roughly two-thirds of the population and much of the rice-growing land. The peninsula was economically interconnected but politically denied any legal mechanism for an orderly transfer of sovereignty. The Government-General, police, courts, schools, firms, landlords, tenant networks, and neighborhood controls described in [[Colonial Korea's changing administrative regimes]] did not simply evaporate when Emperor Hirohito announced surrender.

Korean independence movements were also plural. The Korean Provisional Government in exile claimed continuity from the 1919 independence struggle examined in [[The March First Movement as local and global history]]. Communists had organized in Korea, China, the Soviet Union, and Japan; nationalists ranged from conservative anti-communists to social democrats and liberals; religious, labor, peasant, youth, and local notables possessed their own networks. Repression and exile meant that no organization had received a free nationwide mandate. Liberation therefore released political energy without supplying an agreed constitutional succession.

Allied promises were real but vague. The United States, Britain, and China declared at Cairo in 1943 that Korea would become free and independent “in due course.” Potsdam in July 1945 reaffirmed Cairo's terms, and the Soviet Union accepted them when entering the Pacific war. “Due course” encoded disagreement: the Allies promised the destination but not the timetable, interim authority, or Korean body entitled to govern. American planners discussed multilateral trusteeship because they doubted that immediate transfer to any exile faction would produce a stable friendly state. Koreans who had lived under claims that colonial rule was tutelage could hear trusteeship as another denial of political capacity.

## The 38th parallel as an emergency military decision

The immediate trigger was the speed of Japan's collapse. The Soviet Union declared war on Japan on 8 August 1945 and its forces advanced through Manchuria into northern Korea. American ground forces were far away. Washington needed a line at which Japanese commanders would surrender to Soviet forces in one zone and American forces in another. On the night of 10–11 August, Colonels Dean Rusk and Charles Bonesteel examined a map and recommended the 38th parallel. The line placed Seoul and major southern ports in the American zone while assigning a substantial northern area to the Soviets. The Joint Chiefs transmitted it through General Order No. 1, and the Soviet government did not reject it.

Later recollections invite two opposite exaggerations. One treats the line as nearly accidental, as though cartographic haste meant geopolitics were absent. The other treats it as proof of a prior conspiracy to partition Korea permanently. The documentary record supports a narrower judgment. The selection was hurried and made without Korean participation; it was also strategic, intended to secure an American occupation zone while Soviet troops were physically better placed to take much more of the peninsula. It divided provinces, transport systems, families, and commercial circuits. Yet the instructions of 1945 still described the zones as interim machinery for surrender and occupation, not sovereign borders between two recognized Korean states.

Soviet forces entered northern Korea in August; United States forces landed at Incheon on 8 September. The asymmetry of arrival mattered. Local Koreans had already formed people's committees in many towns, often under the coordinating initiative of the moderate nationalist Lyuh Woon-hyung. On 6 September activists in Seoul proclaimed a People's Republic of Korea. These bodies varied greatly: some were left-led mass organizations, others coalitions with local notables, and still others improvised committees for food, policing, or the transfer of Japanese property. It is misleading either to imagine a fully functioning unified national government or to dismiss them as Soviet inventions. They were Korean responses to an authority vacuum, with uneven social bases and competing political futures.

## Two occupations made the line institutional

North of the parallel, Soviet authorities worked through and reshaped people's committees, elevated communists with reliable organizational ties, and facilitated Kim Il Sung's emergence while managing other domestic, Chinese-returned, and Soviet-Korean factions. Land reform in March 1946 confiscated Japanese, collaborator, and large-landlord holdings without compensation and transferred ownership to peasant households, subject to restrictions including bans on sale and tenancy. Major industry was nationalized later that year. The reforms attacked colonial and landlord power, gained real support among many poor cultivators and workers, and also drove landlords, Christians, officials, and anti-communists south. Coercion, surveillance, and factional consolidation accompanied social transformation. The northern regime was not merely imported, but Soviet military power decisively set its permissible range.

South of the parallel, the United States Army Military Government in Korea (USAMGIK) declared itself the governing authority. It refused to recognize either the exile Provisional Government or the People's Republic as a sovereign successor. Shortage of trained personnel, unfamiliarity with Korean politics, and fear of communism encouraged the military government to retain administrative forms and employ many Koreans who had served under colonial rule. This preserved capacity but damaged legitimacy: liberation did not feel complete when familiar police and officials remained powerful. The occupation suppressed radical committees and strikes, while conservative politicians—especially Syngman Rhee, returned from the United States—gained room to organize.

Southern policy was not static. USAMGIK tried administrative reform, managed Japanese property, introduced an interim legislature, and eventually implemented limited land measures. But inflation, unemployment, food collection, cholera, refugee flows, and labor unrest turned governance into daily conflict. The September 1946 general strike and October uprisings showed that disputes over wages, grain, policing, and local power could become ideological confrontations. In both zones, people encountered division not first as a diplomatic abstraction but as changes in land ownership, employment, travel, police authority, schooling, and the ability to speak or organize.

The line itself intensified divergence. Movement across it continued at first, then became harder. North-to-south migration changed the political and religious composition of the south; people also moved north for political or economic reasons. Electric power and industrial inputs crossed an increasingly contested boundary. Each occupation interpreted opposition through Cold War categories and pointed to repression in the other zone as proof that compromise was dangerous. Institutions created for temporary control began producing constituencies that could lose land, office, security, or life under reunification on the other side's terms.

## Trusteeship turned diplomacy into a Korean political crisis

At Moscow in December 1945, the United States, Soviet Union, and Britain agreed to seek a provisional democratic Korean government through a joint Soviet-American commission, followed by a trusteeship of up to five years under four powers. The formula attempted to bridge occupation and independence. In practice it made consultation the central dispute. The Soviet side wanted to exclude groups that actively opposed the Moscow decision; the American side argued for wider consultation, which would include many anti-trusteeship organizations. Behind the procedural quarrel lay incompatible expectations about which Korean coalition would govern and how much influence each occupying power would retain.

Trusteeship politics also scrambled Korean alignments. Early reports and translations fostered confusion over which power had proposed what. Much of the right mobilized against trusteeship as a betrayal of immediate independence. Communists, after an initial period of uncertainty, supported the Moscow formula in line with Soviet policy. Kim Kyu-sik and Lyuh Woon-hyung pursued left-right cooperation, seeking land reform, punishment of collaborators, and a united provisional government without civil conflict. Their coalition had genuine Korean agency, but it was squeezed by occupation authorities, ideological militants, and political violence. Lyuh's assassination in July 1947 removed a major bridge figure; it did not alone cause division, but it illustrates how narrowing political space made centrist coordination increasingly perilous.

The Joint Commission met in 1946 and 1947 and failed. Failure was not simply an impersonal consequence of “the Cold War.” Washington and Moscow made choices about consultation, policing, economic systems, and client leadership; Korean actors likewise organized, resisted, collaborated, and sometimes used occupation resources against rivals. Yet power was profoundly unequal. No Korean coalition could require the armies to leave or force the two commands to recognize a common center.

## From failed joint administration to two states

In September 1947 the United States referred the Korean question to the United Nations. General Assembly Resolution 112 (II) established the United Nations Temporary Commission on Korea for a peninsula-wide electoral process, but Soviet authorities denied it access to the north. On 26 February 1948 the General Assembly's Interim Committee advised that elections could proceed in the parts of Korea accessible to the commission; UNTCOK then decided to observe the southern vote. An election on 10 May 1948 produced an assembly and the Republic of Korea, inaugurated in August under President Syngman Rhee. The election involved broad participation but occurred under occupation, exclusion of the north, repression, and a boycott by important opponents of separate statehood. Violence on Jeju, beginning with the 3 April 1948 uprising and a devastating counterinsurgency, exposes the human cost hidden by a purely constitutional chronology.

In the north, a separate political process culminated in the Democratic People's Republic of Korea in September 1948 under Kim Il Sung. Its institutions grew from earlier committees, Soviet-sponsored reforms, party consolidation, and elections conducted through the northern political order. Both governments claimed authority over the whole peninsula. Neither treated the 38th parallel as a legitimate permanent international border, yet each built armed forces, bureaucracies, schools, property regimes, and narratives that made absorption by its rival unacceptable.

Soviet combat forces withdrew in 1948 and most American occupation forces in 1949, but withdrawal did not undo the states they had helped construct. Border clashes, insurgency and counterinsurgency, purges, and competing reunification plans made the line militarized before North Korea's full invasion on 25 June 1950. [[Political violence before the Korean War]] is therefore a necessary next link: division and war were related processes, but explaining the division does not make the later invasion automatic or erase responsibility for particular decisions.

## What caused division—and what did not

A useful causal account keeps four levels together. First, Japanese colonial rule left a centralized but illegitimate state apparatus, severe social conflicts, and no accepted transfer mechanism. Second, the Pacific War created a military geography in which Soviet troops approached Korea and American planners sought an occupation zone. Third, Soviet-American rivalry converted temporary zones into opposed political economies and security systems. Fourth, Korean actors pursued rival visions, drew support from real constituencies, and made consequential choices inside structures they did not control.

This synthesis avoids three traps. The first is inevitability: neither the 38th-parallel proposal nor ideological disagreement required two permanent states in August 1945. Joint administration, a coalition provisional government, neutralization, or a different sequence of elections were imaginable, though each faced formidable enforcement problems. The second is puppet reductionism. Soviet and American military power was indispensable, but northern land reform and southern anti-communism mobilized Korean interests rather than merely staging foreign scripts. The third is symmetrical flattening. Both occupations constrained Korean sovereignty, but their institutions, social reforms, coercive practices, and degrees of political pluralism differed and must be studied rather than balanced rhetorically.

Historians disagree principally over weight and timing. Cold War diplomatic histories emphasize Allied strategic decisions and the failure of great-power cooperation. Revisionist scholarship redirected attention to colonial social conflict, Korean people's committees, southern repression, and civil-war dynamics. Work using Soviet-bloc archives clarified the extent of Soviet supervision and the evolution of northern decision-making. These approaches are most productive when combined: international documents explain why armies and a line appeared; Korean social and political history explains what those occupations encountered and transformed; later archival evidence tests claims that once depended almost entirely on one bloc's records.

## Why it matters

Calling Korea “divided by the superpowers” is true but incomplete. Calling division simply a Korean civil conflict is also incomplete. The tragedy arose from their interaction: an externally imposed military line empowered rivals within a politically mobilized society, while local conflict made foreign officials more willing to build dependable regimes in separate zones. Temporary institutions accumulated coercive power, property stakes, personnel, and fear. By 1948 unification no longer meant merely removing a line; it meant deciding which revolution, police system, land settlement, leadership, and alliance would survive.

This is also why 1945 should not be read backward from the armistice line of 1953. The zones, the two states, the war, and the postwar border were distinct historical stages. Conflating them turns contingency into destiny and makes Koreans appear either passive victims or timeless ideological enemies. A source-critical account instead shows how liberation opened possibilities, how occupation closed some of them, and how institutions made each subsequent reversal more costly.
