The invasions of 1627 and 1636–1637 forced Joseon from an unstable accommodation with the Later Jin into a coercive but negotiated Qing-centered order: military defeat changed external allegiance and imposed hostages, tribute, and service, yet it did not turn Joseon into a province or erase the court's room to govern, bargain, trade, and remember the conquest on its own terms.

## The problem: two wars, one political transition

The label “Manchu invasions” joins two wars whose outcomes were not identical. The **Jeongmyo Horan** (정묘호란, 丁卯胡亂) of 1627 ended in a deliberately ambiguous compact between Joseon and the Manchu-ruled Later Jin. The **Byeongja Horan** (병자호란, 丙子胡亂) began in late 1636, after Hong Taiji had proclaimed the Qing dynasty, and ended with King Injo's personal submission at Samjeondo in early 1637. The first war sought to neutralize a hostile rear and secure recognition without yet obtaining unequivocal subordination. The second destroyed that ambiguity and made Joseon accept the Qing emperor as its superior.

These events belong simultaneously to Korean history, Manchu imperial formation, and the fall of the Ming. They cannot be explained as a timeless “China–Korea” relationship. The aggressor in 1627 was a relatively young conquest state still fighting the Ming; in 1636 the newly named Qing sought credentials as an empire and freedom to concentrate on China. Nor should “tributary state” be mistaken for direct annexation. After 1637 Joseon performed hierarchical diplomacy and bore serious obligations, but retained its king, bureaucracy, law, taxation, and most internal decision-making. Its place was subordinate without being administratively incorporated.

## Why Joseon and the Manchus collided

The Japanese invasions of 1592–1598 had tied Joseon's ruling memory to Ming assistance. Ming troops had helped preserve the dynasty at enormous cost, and many Joseon officials understood loyalty to Ming not merely as expedience but as a moral debt within a Confucian political order. At the same time, the war had damaged fortifications, finances, and population. The court thus inherited a dangerous combination: strong ideological commitments and weak capacity for another continental war.

King Gwanghaegun responded to the rise of Nurhaci's Later Jin with strategic caution. After a Joseon force sent to support Ming was defeated at Sarhu in 1619, commander Gang Hong-rip surrendered and became an intermediary. Gwanghaegun tried to preserve the Ming relationship while avoiding an irreversible conflict with the Manchus. His overthrow in the **Injo Restoration of 1623** changed the signal sent north. The Westerners (*Seoin*) who installed Injo identified the new regime with restoring proper relations with Ming and repudiating Gwanghaegun's rule. “Pro-Ming, anti-Jin” is a useful shorthand, but it can hide divisions at court and the practical compromises that continued.

Three immediate problems made Joseon threatening to Later Jin. First, Ming commander Mao Wenlong operated from islands near the mouth of the Yalu, especially Ka Island, with Joseon support and access to Korean supplies. His forces and refugees created a hostile base on the Manchus' southeastern flank. Second, the rebel Yi Gwal's failed uprising against Injo in 1624 sent survivors north; one, Han Yun, encouraged the Manchus to intervene. Third, Hong Taiji, who succeeded Nurhaci in 1626, needed grain, security, and political leverage while continuing war against Ming. The invasion was therefore neither a random raid nor simply punishment for insulting language. It joined frontier supply, internal Joseon politics, and a larger strategic contest.

## 1627: invasion and an unstable brotherhood

In early 1627 a Later Jin army commanded by Amin crossed the Yalu. It advanced rapidly through northwestern Korea, taking key towns and driving the royal court from the capital toward Ganghwa Island. Injo's government could not stop the advance, but Later Jin also had reasons not to occupy the peninsula indefinitely. Its main war remained against Ming, and a prolonged Korean campaign risked supply problems and resistance. Negotiation served both sides.

The resulting settlement is often summarized as a “brotherly alliance.” That phrase captures the ritual fiction but not a single, comprehensive treaty of the modern kind. Negotiations produced oaths and texts at Ganghwa and later arrangements at Pyongyang, while the parties attached different meanings to them. Joseon recognized a formal relationship with Later Jin, agreed to restrict hostility, and accepted exchanges and material obligations. Yet it did not clearly renounce Ming, and regular tribute and border-market provisions were not fully settled in the initial peace. Joseon could present the agreement as a temporary compact between two rulers rather than surrender to a universal emperor. Later Jin could claim that Joseon had entered its diplomatic orbit.

That ambiguity was functional in 1627 and corrosive afterward. Joseon continued relations with Ming and resisted escalating Later Jin demands. Later Jin expected supplies, fugitives to be returned, and behavior consistent with its growing power. Neither side treated “brotherhood” as equality in a modern sovereign sense; kinship language itself could rank an elder and younger brother. As Hong Taiji's position improved, an intermediate relationship that had ended one war became an obstacle to imperial status.

## From Later Jin to Qing

Between the invasions, the regional balance changed sharply. Hong Taiji consolidated Manchu, Mongol, and Chinese forces. The Ming general Yuan Chonghuan executed Mao Wenlong in 1629, and Later Jin conquered the remaining Ming position on Ka Island in 1631. This sequence steadily reduced Ming leverage around Korea. Hong Taiji assumed the imperial title and renamed his state **Qing** in 1636. The new claim required other rulers to acknowledge more than a bilateral pact with Later Jin.

Qing envoys demanded that Joseon replace the brother relationship with a ruler–subject relationship. They also sought heavier annual gifts and military cooperation. Joseon officials argued fiercely over accommodation and resistance. Moral language was not empty: accepting a Manchu emperor meant repudiating Ming legitimacy and the debt of the Imjin War. But court debate also involved regime security. Injo and the Westerners had justified their 1623 coup partly through foreign-policy orthodoxy. Openly embracing Qing threatened the political story on which their government rested.

Joseon's refusal and the mistreatment or rejection of Qing envoys helped precipitate war, but the causal core was larger. Qing wanted to prevent Joseon from aiding Ming, obtain resources, secure its eastern flank, and stage submission by a respected Confucian monarchy. Joseon hoped geography, fortresses, mobilization, and perhaps Ming relief would make resistance viable. That calculation failed because Qing moved faster than the court's defensive plan.

## 1636–1637: speed, encirclement, and surrender

Hong Taiji personally led the winter invasion that crossed into Joseon in December 1636. A fast Qing vanguard bypassed strongpoints and rushed toward the capital, cutting Injo off from the traditional refuge on Ganghwa Island. The king instead entered **Namhansanseong**, a mountain fortress southeast of the capital. Qing forces surrounded it while another force captured Ganghwa, where members of the royal family and officials had sought safety.

The fortress held for roughly six weeks, but tactical survival did not create a strategic escape. Food and fodder dwindled; relief armies were defeated or dispersed; Ming could not intervene effectively; and the fall of Ganghwa exposed the royal family. Inside Namhansanseong, advocates of continued resistance and advocates of negotiation disputed what loyalty and royal responsibility required. Kim Sang-heon later became emblematic of resistance, while Choe Myeong-gil became emblematic of peace. Their positions should not be reduced to courage against cowardice. Continued resistance defended dynastic honor but risked the court, soldiers, and civilians; negotiation preserved the state by accepting humiliation and burdens.

In early 1637 Injo left the fortress and submitted to Hong Taiji at **Samjeondo**, on the Han River. Accounts conventionally describe him performing three kneelings and nine prostrations. The broad fact of a deliberately humiliating imperial submission is secure; exact descriptions should be tied to particular court and Qing records rather than embellished through later dramatic retellings. The Samjeondo Monument, erected at Qing command, publicly inscribed Qing victory and imperial virtue. It is evidence of the political message the victors required Joseon to display, not a neutral transcript of everything that happened.

The settlement imposed a new relationship. Joseon was to break official ties with Ming, recognize Qing supremacy, send royal hostages—including Crown Prince Sohyeon and Grand Prince Bongnim—to Shenyang, deliver regular tribute and annual gifts, return or control fugitives, and provide military assistance when demanded. The court also faced restrictions related to fortifications and coercive demands affecting people as well as goods. The precise list and implementation varied across negotiations; it is safer to distinguish the submission articles, later diplomatic practice, and exceptional wartime exactions than to imagine one fixed “Treaty of Samjeondo.”

The human cost extended far beyond court ritual. Qing and allied forces killed combatants and civilians, devastated settlements, and carried many captives north. Exact totals in later accounts are uncertain and should not be repeated as firm demographic fact without source criticism. Families tried to ransom returnees, while the label **hwanhyangnyeo**—“women who returned home”—became associated with women captives whose sexual status and chastity were questioned. Later memory sometimes turns the term into a neat origin story for a modern insult; etymological claims of that kind are disputed. What is historically secure is the gendered cruelty: women endured abduction and violence, then could encounter suspicion or rejection on returning to a patriarchal society.

## What Qing overlordship required—and what it did not

The 1637 settlement made hierarchy concrete through repeated practices. Joseon dispatched embassies, presented tribute and annual gifts, received investiture and calendrical authority, hosted Qing envoys, operated regulated border trade, and sometimes supplied troops. Crown Prince Sohyeon's years at Shenyang made the hostage system a channel of surveillance, negotiation, and cultural contact. Joseon later sent forces to assist Qing against Ming positions and, in the 1650s, against Russian forces on the Amur in the campaigns Koreans call the **Naseon jeongbeol**.

These obligations were expensive and politically intrusive. Early demands for goods, animals, captives, and troops strained Joseon's fiscal capacity. Envoy reception itself imposed local burdens. Qing could arbitrate sensitive border and fugitive cases, and the memory of military enforcement gave its commands weight. The relationship was not a harmless ceremony attached to full equality.

Yet Qing did not replace Joseon's government. It did not routinely collect Korean taxes, station a civil administration throughout the kingdom, or legislate ordinary Korean life. Joseon selected and operated officials through its own institutions, subject to dynastic politics and the need for Qing recognition at the top. Embassies also enabled trade, intelligence gathering, book acquisition, and bargaining. Border markets such as Hoeryong linked coercive diplomacy to commerce. Over decades, extraordinary wartime exactions became more regularized, and practical cooperation made the relationship durable.

This is why “tributary system” must be used as a question rather than a complete answer. The term gathers investiture, ritual subordination, diplomatic exchange, trade, and security under one label, but those elements did not always move together. Joseon–Qing relations were hierarchical and unequal; they were also bilateral practices repeatedly negotiated by two distinct courts. The Qing order established in 1637 lasted, with changes, until the late nineteenth century and formally unraveled amid the Sino-Japanese War and the 1895 Treaty of Shimonoseki.

## Adaptation behind a language of resentment

Official defeat did not dictate one Joseon response. Under King Hyojong—formerly Grand Prince Bongnim—court rhetoric included **bukbeol**, a “northern expedition” to avenge the humiliation and attack Qing. Military reforms and fortress repair could be narrated through that aspiration. But a feasible war of revenge never followed. Qing power grew after taking Beijing in 1644, and Joseon increasingly worked within the relationship it publicly resented.

Some literati preserved loyalty to the fallen Ming through ritual and historical writing. Later claims of **sojunghwa**, “Little China,” treated Joseon as a guardian of authentic Confucian civilization after a supposedly barbarian conquest of China. Such ideas mattered, but they were neither a single unchanging national doctrine nor proof that all Koreans rejected Qing culture. Envoys to Beijing observed Qing prosperity, acquired knowledge, and helped stimulate currents later grouped under **bukhak**, “Northern Learning.” Ideological disdain, diplomatic compliance, material exchange, and intellectual curiosity could coexist.

The Crown Prince Sohyeon illustrates the danger of turning memory into a simple morality play. His experience in Shenyang and contact with Qing China made him a symbol in later stories about reform and frustrated possibility. He died soon after returning to Joseon in 1645, and suspicion has long surrounded Injo. The documentary record does not establish the popular claim that Injo murdered him. The secure facts are the prince's hostage experience, return, sudden death, and the subsequent punishment of his family; accusations of royal murder belong to contested interpretation.

## Memory, monument, and the politics of humiliation

Samjeondo became a condensed symbol of national humiliation because it staged bodily submission and left a stone text praising the conqueror. But the symbol has a history. The monument expressed Qing authority when erected; later damage, concealment, restoration, relocation, and public controversy gave it new meanings. Modern nationalism often reads the scene as a timeless humiliation of “Korea by China,” collapsing Manchu imperial formation, Joseon factional politics, and seventeenth-century ritual language into modern nation-states.

A source-critical account keeps three layers apart. **Event fact** includes the invasions, the siege, Injo's submission, hostage-taking, tribute obligations, and captive suffering. **Positioned testimony** includes Joseon memorials defending war or peace and the triumphal inscription Qing compelled Joseon to erect. **Later memory** includes revenge narratives, films and novels, disputed etymologies, and claims about what the defeat reveals about an eternal national character. Memory is historically consequential—it shaped politics and identity—but it cannot silently supply missing facts about 1637.

## Why this matters in the Korean-history map

The invasions are the hinge between the Imjin War's Ming-assisted survival and Joseon's long accommodation to Qing power. They show that foreign relations were not an ornamental sequence of embassies: they reorganized fiscal demands, military planning, royal legitimacy, trade, knowledge, captivity, and gendered social experience. They also expose the limits of a purely court-centered chronology. The Samjeondo ritual mattered because violence, hunger, abduction, and bargaining made the ritual enforceable.

They also complicate easy binaries. Joseon neither heroically resisted forever nor simply “became Chinese.” Qing neither annexed the kingdom nor relied on empty symbolism. Military coercion established a hierarchy; institutions and repeated negotiation made it governable; Joseon actors then interpreted and used that order in conflicting ways. That combination—defeat, retained autonomy, practical adaptation, and resistant memory—is the explanatory core of Joseon's Qing relationship.

## Open questions

- How did tribute and envoy-reception burdens fall differently on the royal treasury, provincial administrations, and common households during the first decades after 1637?
- Which surviving Qing-language records most clearly revise or corroborate the Joseon court's account of the surrender negotiations?
- How did the experiences of captives vary by gender, status, destination, and access to ransom, beyond the court-centered evidence that survives most visibly?
- When did Samjeondo become a mass national symbol rather than primarily a dynastic and literati memory, and how did colonial and postcolonial media reshape it?
