Korean history is easiest to navigate with overlapping political, social, and regional chronologies rather than one dynasty-only ladder.

Up: [[Korean history (MOC)]]

## What periodization does

Dynastic labels are useful because changes of court reorganized archives, official titles, and claims to legitimacy. They are not neutral containers for everything else. The transition from Goryeo to Joseon, for example, did not instantly reset landholding, family practice, religious life, or Korea's position among neighboring states.

A reliable map therefore uses three clocks together:

- a **political clock** for polities, dynasties, occupation regimes, and divided states;
- a **social and institutional clock** for status, taxation, kinship, religion, markets, and administration;
- a **regional clock** that includes Balhae, borderlands, migration, and Korea's changing relations with continental and maritime powers.

This also explains why labels such as “Unified Silla” are arguments as well as dates: they can foreground Silla's rule while obscuring Balhae and the limits of peninsular integration. Period boundaries should therefore be treated as hypotheses about which change mattered most.

## Place in the map

Use this note before [[Goryeo as a plural aristocratic and Buddhist polity]], [[Why Goryeo became Joseon]], or late-Joseon causal claims. It prevents a court succession from becoming an all-purpose explanation and supplies a baseline for comparing long institutional changes.
