Goryeo joined a royal bureaucracy to hereditary elite networks, Buddhist institutions, and regionally rooted power rather than fitting a simple “feudal” or centralized-state label.

Up: [[Korean history (MOC)]]

## How the polity worked

Wang Geon founded Goryeo in 918 and consolidated the Later Three Kingdoms by 936 through conquest, alliance, and marriage. The court used Chinese-derived offices and examinations, yet high office also reproduced itself through protected appointment, kinship, and intermarriage with the royal house. Calling Goryeo only a bureaucracy therefore hides the durable access of great families; calling it only an aristocracy hides examinations, officials, provincial administration, and shifts under military and Mongol power.

Buddhism was both belief and institution. Royal patronage, temples, land, ritual, and clerical careers connected the court to local society and legitimated protection of the realm. Confucian learning supplied much administrative language without displacing Buddhist ritual life. These arrangements changed sharply after the 1170 military coup and again during Mongol intervention, so “Goryeo society” should not be treated as static across 474 years.
