Goryeo Buddhism mattered politically because temples, land, clerical careers, ritual, and patronage made it an institutional part of rule, not merely a court ideology.

Up: [[Korean history (MOC)]]

## Institution rather than label

Royal and aristocratic patrons endowed temples and sponsored rituals intended to protect dynasty and realm. Temples could hold land and labor resources, while members of elite families entered clerical life; Buddhist art and textual projects consequently express both devotion and political economy. “Buddhist state” is useful only if unpacked into these mechanisms.

The institutional field was plural. Textual and Seon traditions competed and adapted, and the 1170 military takeover altered their access to patronage. Later anti-Buddhist Joseon arguments can reveal real concentration of resources, but they are not neutral descriptions of the entire preceding dynasty. Claims about wealth or exemption must be dated and checked against Goryeo-period evidence.

## Place in the map

This note supplies a religious-institutional branch beneath [[Goryeo as a plural aristocratic and Buddhist polity]] and a prerequisite for explaining why early Joseon reformers targeted temples, offices, and land.
