[[Heungseon Daewongun]] was probably involved with the [[Donghak Peasant Revolution]] as a political contact and attempted instigator, while [[Gojong]]'s alleged direct role is plausible but much harder to authenticate.

## Judgment

The credible middle position is that [[Heungseon Daewongun]] and his faction tried to use the Donghak army after Japan seized [[Gyeongbokgung]], and that [[Jeon Bong-jun]] used royalist language and possible palace contacts to legitimate an anti-Japanese rising. That does not mean the Donghak movement was created, controlled, or commanded by the palace.

The strongest version of the claim says Daewongun or Gojong secretly ordered Jeon to drive out the Japanese. The weaker and more credible version says Daewongun-aligned messengers contacted Donghak leaders, urged them to march on Seoul, and may have transmitted or invoked a royal secret order. The evidence supports the weaker version much better than the stronger one.

## Daewongun

Daewongun's involvement is fairly credible after the [[Jeonju Truce]]. The strongest evidence is not a single clean smoking gun, but a cluster:

- [[Song Hui-ok]], a Donghak official and relative of Jeon, reportedly corresponded with Daewongun's close men.
- The [[National Institute of Korean History]] account describes repeated contacts between Daewongun-side envoys and Jeon in late summer 1894.
- Daewongun had an obvious motive: after Japan's palace coup, he was nominally restored but politically hemmed in by Japanese power and pro-Japanese reformers.
- Donghak leaders had their own motive: royalist legitimacy could broaden the anti-Japanese uprising beyond a peasant revolt.

The best reading is contact and attempted exploitation, not command. Jeon did not simply move when Daewongun wanted him to. National History Net's account has Jeon urging caution in July and August 1894 despite pressure, then moving toward renewed uprising only after the Japanese threat and central political situation became clearer.

So: Daewongun-Donghak contact is credible. Daewongun control of Donghak is not.

## Gojong

Gojong's alleged role is murkier. Some source traditions record that Jeon received a "king's secret order" around the moment he decided on the second uprising. National History Net reproduces the substance of such a royal message and treats it as part of the source record for the September 1894 decision to rise.

But the chain of custody is the problem. If a message reached Jeon through Daewongun-linked intermediaries, it is difficult to know whether it was:

- a genuine private appeal from Gojong,
- a message authorized by Gojong but shaped by Daewongun's faction,
- a forged or exaggerated "royal" command,
- or a political story used by Donghak leaders to justify loyalist anti-Japanese mobilization.

Gojong had a motive to oppose Japanese coercion after the palace seizure, but he had also requested outside military help to suppress Donghak earlier and later authorized harsh repression. His position was constrained, inconsistent, and survival-oriented.

So: a royal message tradition is not fantasy, but "Gojong personally directed Jeon" remains unproven.

## What This Means

The relationship was probably triangular:

- Daewongun wanted armed pressure against the Japanese and pro-Japanese cabinet.
- Donghak wanted reform, anti-Japanese mobilization, and legitimacy.
- Gojong was a symbol of sovereignty, but not a reliable revolutionary sponsor.

Jeon seems to have been willing to exploit royalist legitimacy while preserving his own program. His later interrogation statements emphasized anti-Japanese resistance and reform, while denying that he had simply been incited by someone else.

## Credibility Scale

- Daewongun-side messengers contacted Donghak leaders: high.
- Daewongun tried to induce or accelerate the second uprising: moderately high.
- A message claiming royal authority reached Jeon: moderate.
- Gojong personally and knowingly ordered Jeon to drive out the Japanese: low to moderate.
- Daewongun or Gojong created or controlled the Donghak Peasant Revolution: low.

## Open questions

- Can the alleged "king's secret order" be traced to a verifiable original document rather than later copies or hostile intelligence reports?
- How much of Daewongun's outreach was known to Gojong?
- Did Jeon treat royal messages as binding commands or as useful political cover for a decision he was already moving toward?
