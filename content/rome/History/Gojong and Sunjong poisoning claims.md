The poisoning claim is much more credible for [[Gojong]] than for [[Sunjong]], but even Gojong's case remains unproven.

Gojong died suddenly at [[Deoksugung]] on January 21, 1919, after years of confinement following Japan's forced abdication of him in 1907. His death immediately generated rumors that Japanese authorities had poisoned him, and those rumors helped create the emotional climate for the [[March First Movement]]. Robert Neff's Korea Times summary of Yun Chi-ho's diary notes that the death surprised Seoul because Gojong had seemed relatively healthy, and that stories circulated about severe symptoms and disturbing corpse conditions.

There is a serious pro-poisoning case. Yŏng-ho Ch'oe and Yi Tae-jin's Korean Studies article, "The Mystery of Emperor Kojong's Sudden Death in 1919," argues that the evidence strongly supports Japanese official involvement. Their abstract highlights Princess Masako/Yi Bang-ja's later testimony that Japanese officials ordered the poisoning, and Kuratomi Yūzaburō's diary as potentially consistent with involvement by high officials such as Terauchi Masatake and Hasegawa Yoshimichi.

There is also a serious skeptical case. A KCI-indexed article, "A Review of the Gojong Poisoning Theory through Korean and Japanese Press Materials," argues that contemporary medical reports described cerebral hemorrhage, that newspaper descriptions fit cerebral hemorrhage, that corpse deterioration can be explained by delayed preparation of the body, and that Japan had limited political motive to assassinate a powerless ex-emperor at that specific moment. The article allows that medical negligence or concealment may have occurred, but rejects poisoning as established fact.

My judgment on Gojong: credible enough to take seriously, not credible enough to treat as settled. The motive was plausible because Gojong remained a symbol of Korean sovereignty and had tried to escape Japanese control; Japan also had a demonstrated record of violence against the royal house, including [[Empress Myeongseong]]'s 1895 assassination. But the evidence is mostly circumstantial, testimonial, or retrospective, not forensic.

Sunjong is different. The clearest poisoning event involving him was the 1898 [[Kim Hong-ryuk coffee poisoning incident]], when poison was put in coffee intended for Gojong and the crown prince. That was a real assassination attempt, but it was not a Japanese-authorities poisoning in 1926. Sunjong survived but was reportedly left physically damaged and never robust.

When Sunjong died at [[Changdeokgung]] in April 1926, Japanese authorities immediately treated the funeral as a security problem. Choi Eun-jin's 2024 KCI article on Sunjong's death and funeral shows the colonial police tightening security, the state funeral being shortened compared with Joseon royal precedent, and the funeral day becoming the setting for the [[June 10th Movement]]. That shows political control of the funeral, not proof of poisoning.

My judgment on Sunjong: low credibility. His death became politically explosive, but the poisoning claim lacks the specific evidentiary trail that exists for Gojong. The better-supported account is that Sunjong died after a life of trauma, poor health, confinement, and symbolic powerlessness; Japanese authorities then managed the funeral to contain anti-colonial mobilization.

## Open questions

- How reliable is Yi Bang-ja's retrospective testimony about Japanese instructions to poison Gojong?
- What exactly does Kuratomi Yūzaburō's diary say, and how direct is its evidentiary value?
- Are there primary medical records for Sunjong's final illness that clarify whether poisoning rumors existed at the time or arose later?
