[[Heungseon Daewongun]] was clearly used in the [[Eulmi Incident]] as political cover, but the claim that he helped plan or authorize the murder of [[Empress Myeongseong]] is much less credible.

## Judgment

The careful answer is that Daewongun's visible involvement is credible, but his command responsibility is not.

He was brought into [[Gyeongbokgung]] with the Japanese-backed force on the morning of October 8, 1895. He was a useful symbol because he was [[Gojong]]'s father, a long-time enemy of [[Empress Myeongseong]], and a plausible Korean face for an anti-Min court coup. That part is not really in doubt.

What is doubtful is the stronger claim that Daewongun co-designed the assassination or gave meaningful authorization for Japanese agents to murder the queen. The murder operation was planned and directed by Japanese minister [[Miura Goro]] and Japanese legation, military, police, newspaper, and civilian networks. Japanese claims that Daewongun or the [[Hullyeondae]] were the real drivers served an obvious purpose: shifting blame away from Japan.

## Why The Suspicion Exists

The suspicion against Daewongun is not baseless:

- He had a long and bitter rivalry with [[Empress Myeongseong]] and the Min faction.
- He had previously used political violence and opportunistic alliances to return to power.
- Japanese planners deliberately brought him into the operation.
- The post-assassination regime tried to use his prestige and the [[Hullyeondae]] as a Korean frame for the coup.

So it is fair to say he was politically implicated. He benefited from, lent cover to, or at least was made to appear to bless the coup.

## Would He Have Authorized Murder?

It is plausible that Daewongun would have authorized, or at least tolerated, a coup to remove the Min faction from power. It is also plausible that he would not have grieved over [[Empress Myeongseong]]'s death. But that is still different from proving that he knowingly authorized the specific murder carried out by Japanese agents inside the palace.

The most plausible middle scenario is tacit or ambiguous assent: Daewongun may have accepted a plan framed as "restore order," "enter the palace," or "remove the queen's faction," while Japanese planners either concealed the exact method or deliberately kept his assent vague enough to use later.

That distinction matters because Daewongun was politically ruthless, but he was not a simple Japanese client. A foreign-led palace murder could strengthen his faction in the short run while also making him dependent on the very Japanese power he had often resisted. So he had motive to remove the queen, but not necessarily motive to let Japan own the court through a spectacular assassination.

## Why The Strong Claim Is Weak

The stronger claim weakens because the central operational evidence points elsewhere.

The best-supported command chain runs through Miura and the Japanese legation. Contemporary foreign observers saw Japanese troops and armed Japanese participants in the palace. Later scholarship emphasizes that the Japanese narrative blaming Daewongun and Korean soldiers was a cover story. The [[Academy of Korean Studies]] entry on the Eulmi Incident explicitly notes that Japanese accounts recast the event as a Daewongun-Min conflict and that later research traced the planning and execution to Japanese actors.

This does not make Daewongun innocent in a moral sense. It means the available evidence better supports "used as cover" than "mastermind."

## Tainted Records

The records are heavily tainted, but unevenly.

Japanese official explanations, trial records, and later memoirs by participants are the most suspect when they blame Daewongun or the [[Hullyeondae]]. They had a direct incentive to recast a Japanese-run operation as a Korean domestic coup. The Hiroshima acquittals also mean the Japanese legal record cannot be treated as a neutral search for truth.

Korean cabinet records immediately after the incident are also compromised because [[Gojong]] and the court were under coercive pressure, and the pro-Japanese cabinet had reason to preserve the new political order.

Foreign eyewitness and diplomatic records are less tainted by Japanese self-protection, but they are not perfect. Russian, American, British, and other observers saw only parts of the event, and Russia had its own strategic interest in exposing Japanese misconduct. Still, those records are especially valuable because they confirm Japanese armed participation and contradict the Japanese blame-shifting narrative.

Later scholarship is strongest when it triangulates these compromised bodies of evidence against each other. The safest conclusion is that the records can prove Japanese planning and Daewongun's political use much better than they can prove Daewongun's personal authorization of murder.

## Credibility Scale

- Daewongun was physically and politically present in the coup: high.
- Japanese plotters used him to give the operation Korean legitimacy: high.
- Daewongun wanted Queen Min's political power destroyed: high.
- Daewongun knowingly supported a palace coup against the Min faction: moderate.
- Daewongun specifically helped plan or approve her murder: low to moderate.
- Daewongun was the true mastermind, with Japan merely assisting: very low.

## Open questions

- Did Daewongun know before entering the palace that the plan included murder rather than intimidation, dethronement, or forced political reorganization?
- How much of Daewongun's alleged consent comes from Japanese or pro-Japanese sources with an incentive to displace blame?
- Can surviving Japanese trial and diplomatic records distinguish Daewongun's passive use as cover from active political assent?
