The [[Japanese annexation of Korea]] was not inevitable in a deep historical sense, but after 1905 it became highly likely, and after 1907 it was nearly locked in.

## Judgment

The answer depends on the date.

Before the [[Russo-Japanese War]], annexation was a Japanese goal and a serious danger, but not inevitable. [[Korea]] still had some room to maneuver through court reform, balance-of-power diplomacy, and rivalry among Japan, Russia, Britain, China, and the United States.

After Japan defeated Russia in 1905, the situation changed. Russia accepted Japan's paramount interests in Korea through the [[Treaty of Portsmouth]], the United States signaled acceptance through the [[Taft-Katsura Agreement]], and Britain was already aligned with Japan through the [[Anglo-Japanese Alliance]]. The [[Eulsa Treaty]] then stripped the [[Korean Empire]] of diplomatic sovereignty and closed off the normal path of foreign appeal.

After 1907, annexation was close to a fait accompli. The failure of the [[Hague Secret Emissary Affair]] gave Japan a pretext to force [[Gojong]] from the throne. The [[Japan-Korea Treaty of 1907]] placed Korean administration under Japanese direction, Japanese officials entered the bureaucracy, and the Korean army was disbanded. From that point, only a major external shock could realistically have stopped formal annexation.

## What Was Contingent

The exact form and timing were still contingent. Japan might have maintained Korea as a protectorate longer. Annexation might have happened later, or under a different legal pretext. Japanese leaders did not always agree on the best administrative form of control.

The deeper loss of sovereignty, however, had already happened before 1910. The treaty of August 22, 1910, publicly announced on August 29, was the formal legal wrapper around a political reality Japan had built step by step.

## Why Korea Was Vulnerable

Several structural factors made annexation increasingly likely:

- Korea lacked a strong army and modern fiscal base.
- Court politics remained divided and dependent on outside patrons.
- Reform efforts such as the [[Gwangmu Reform]] were real but too late, underfunded, and politically narrow.
- Japan treated Korea as vital to its own security and empire.
- Britain, the United States, and Russia had stronger reasons to accommodate Japan than to defend Korean sovereignty.
- Anti-Japanese resistance was widespread, but fragmented and unable to reverse control of diplomacy, police, army, and bureaucracy.

This does not mean Korea was doomed by weakness or culture. It means Korea faced an imperial system in which sovereignty depended on military capacity and great-power recognition, and by 1905 both had turned decisively against it.

## Counterfactual Openings

The plausible escape routes were narrow:

- Japan loses or stalemates in the [[Russo-Japanese War]].
- Britain or the United States refuses to accept Japan's Korean predominance.
- Russia remains strong enough in East Asia to deter full Japanese control.
- Korean reforms build a credible army, tax base, and bureaucratic state before 1904.
- A domestic coalition unites court, reformers, army, and popular resistance before Japan controls the state machinery.

None of these was impossible. But after 1905, each required more than Korean will; it required a change in the international balance.

## Verdict

Annexation in 1910 was not metaphysically inevitable. It was historically contingent.

But it became progressively more locked in:

- After 1895: likely enough to be a central danger.
- After 1905: highly likely.
- After 1907: nearly inevitable without a great-power rupture.
- By 1910: formal annexation was the last step in an already operating colonial takeover.

## Open questions

- How long could Japan realistically have maintained Korea as a protectorate rather than formally annexing it?
- Could a stronger [[Gwangmu Reform]] have changed foreign perceptions of Korea's viability?
- Was a Russian-backed Korean neutrality settlement ever realistic after 1898, or was it already too late?
