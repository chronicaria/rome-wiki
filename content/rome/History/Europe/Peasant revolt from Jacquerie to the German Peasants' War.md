European peasant revolts were not automatic explosions of misery: they became dangerous when fiscal or seigneurial demands activated institutions, alliances, political languages, and expectations through which rural people could coordinate.

Up: [[European history 500-1914]]

## The comparative problem

“Peasant revolt” groups together events whose participants, objectives, and political settings differed sharply. The Jacquerie of 1358 in northern France was brief, violent, and entangled with war and a crisis of royal government. The English Rising of 1381 joined village mobilization against servitude and taxation to an armed march on London. The German Peasants’ War of 1524–25 spread through leagues that issued programs, negotiated with rulers, besieged institutions, and fought armies across much of southern and central Germany. Between them came the Ciompi revolt in Florence, urban and rural movements in Flanders, the *remences* of Catalonia, the Bundschuh conspiracies, and many smaller refusals that never became general rebellions.

The useful question is therefore not why “the peasantry” periodically rebelled. Rural society contained tenants of different legal status and wealth, landless laborers, artisans, clergy, miners, and small-town inhabitants. Interests could conflict over commons, wages, rent, office, and debt. The question is how grievances became collective action, how coalitions held or broke, and why some movements articulated alternatives to existing rule.

Four distinctions organize the comparison. A **structural condition**—war, lordship, demographic change, or fiscal expansion—created recurring pressure. A **trigger**, such as a new tax collection or an act of coercion, compressed that pressure into a moment. **Mobilizing institutions**—village assemblies, parishes, guilds, military musters, markets, and leagues—made coordination possible. A **political program** defined what participants believed legitimate. None of these alone caused revolt.

## The Jacquerie: war, protection, and elite panic

The Jacquerie erupted in the Beauvaisis in May 1358 during the [[Fourteenth-century crisis as interacting shocks]]. France had suffered defeat at Poitiers in 1356; King John II was a prisoner; soldiers and armed companies preyed on the countryside; and the crown’s fiscal and political machinery was contested. Paris under Étienne Marcel was also challenging the dauphin’s government. Rural communities faced taxation, destruction, and failures of aristocratic protection at the moment nobles still claimed privileges justified partly by military service.

Contemporary narrative sources, including Jean Froissart and Jean le Bel, describe peasants with contempt and dwell on atrocities against noble households. These texts are indispensable but hostile: elite authors converted a frightening social inversion into a story of bestial violence. Their detail cannot be discarded, yet it must be read alongside royal remission letters and the geography of the rising. “Jacques Bonhomme” was an elite label, not evidence of a unified class identity.

The movement attacked castles and nobles and spread rapidly through connected communities. Guillaume Cale emerged as a leader, but the revolt did not create a durable representative organization or surviving common program comparable to the Twelve Articles of 1525. Contacts with Marcel’s Parisian movement did not produce a stable rural-urban alliance. Charles of Navarre defeated the rebels near Mello after negotiations and deception; noble reprisals followed. The revolt’s rapid suppression reflected military asymmetry and coalition weakness, not the absence of political meaning.

Its politics centered on the broken relationship between privilege and protection. Attacking fortified residences could answer both immediate military danger and the claim that nobles extracted without defending. Yet historians should resist inventing a coherent abolitionist platform from hostile chronicles. The Jacquerie demonstrates how state crisis can expose seigneurial relations to sudden challenge while leaving rebels unable to institutionalize a replacement.

## England in 1381: tax refusal became a constitutional encounter

The English Rising followed different conditions. Post-plague labor scarcity had strengthened many workers’ bargaining position, while the Statute of Labourers of 1351 and later enforcement attempted to restrain wages and mobility. Manorial obligations remained uneven: some tenants were legally unfree, others had commuted services or defended custom through local courts. War with France drove repeated poll taxes. The third levy, authorized in 1380, was assessed and then investigated aggressively when receipts disappointed.

Resistance began in Essex and Kent in late May 1381 and moved through county networks toward London. Villages were not shapeless crowds. Hundreds, tithings, parishes, local officials, and market connections supplied information and recognizable leaders. Participants destroyed or seized records, attacked officials and lawyers, opened prisons, and compelled support. Record destruction was a precise political act: court rolls, tax lists, and legal instruments made labor dues and fiscal liabilities enforceable.

At Mile End, rebels demanded the abolition of serfdom and conversion of services into low money rents; the young Richard II issued charters of manumission. At Smithfield, after Wat Tyler was killed, royal authorities dispersed the gathering and revoked concessions. John Ball’s sermons, preserved through hostile reporting, articulated a radical Christian equality, but the movement contained more than one voice. Rebels also professed loyalty to the king while targeting “traitorous” counselors. That idiom was not mere naivety. Medieval petitioners could distinguish an imagined just kingship from corrupt mediation, allowing them to demand sweeping reform without declaring an abstract republic.

The rising joined rural grievances to urban conflict. Londoners admitted rebels through gates; artisans and political factions participated; targets included the Savoy palace of John of Gaunt and institutions associated with government and law. Elsewhere, risings addressed local lords, abbeys, borough conflicts, and university privileges. This breadth gave the rebellion reach but not a single command. The crown recovered because it retained armed support, negotiated long enough to divide concentrations, and punished local leaders after dispersal.

The revolt failed to secure its charters, yet “failure” needs qualification. English serfdom declined over the next century, but 1381 did not mechanically abolish it. Demography, land markets, migration, bargaining, litigation, and lordly choices mattered. The rising revealed the enforcement cost of ambitious labor control and fiscal extraction. It also entered political memory, shaping elite fears and later languages of popular justice.

## Catalonia: revolt as a long institutional struggle

The Catalan *remença* conflicts warn against making a short insurrection the standard form. Some peasants tied to holdings faced the so-called “bad customs,” including payments connected to departure, inheritance, and family status. Organizations of *remences* negotiated, raised funds, petitioned the monarchy, and took part in the Catalan Civil War of 1462–72. Royal policy was inconsistent because the monarchy used peasant organization within a wider contest with landlords and Catalan institutions.

A renewed rising under Pere Joan Sala was defeated in 1485. The Sentencia Arbitral de Guadalupe of 1486, issued by Ferdinand II, then abolished specified servile abuses in return for compensation while confirming other rents and lordly rights. It was neither emancipation by royal gift nor a total peasant victory. Decades of organization, warfare, bargaining, litigation, and monarchical strategy produced a settlement that changed personal status while preserving unequal property relations. The case shows that rural action could succeed when it became a recognized negotiating body and when conflict among elites opened leverage.

## The German Peasants’ War: leagues, gospel, and territorial politics

The revolts of 1524–25 arose in a politically fragmented Holy Roman Empire. Rural grievances included labor services, rents, tithes, restrictions on hunting, fishing, and commons, arbitrary lordly innovations, and disputes over justice. Conditions varied greatly; there was no uniform descent into worsening serfdom. Earlier Bundschuh conspiracies and the “Poor Conrad” rising of 1514 had already developed symbols and networks of resistance.

The Reformation transformed available political language and communication. Arguments about Christian freedom, scripture, godly community, and the authority of the gospel could legitimate scrutiny of lordship. Print helped circulate programs, but preaching, manuscript copying, messengers, taverns, markets, and face-to-face assemblies remained essential; compare [[The printing press and uneven information change]]. Martin Luther’s theology did not cause the war, and rebels did not simply misunderstand him. They selectively joined evangelical claims to older practices of communal right.

In Upper Swabia, armed peasant bands formed larger leagues and negotiated with the Swabian League. The Twelve Articles, printed in 1525, became the best-known common statement. They requested the right of communities to choose pastors, relief from the small tithe, limits on labor and rents according to established agreements, restoration of commons, moderation of death dues, and an end to serfdom, while offering to withdraw any demand disproved by scripture. This was radical and juridical at once. It invoked divine law, community consent, and customary equity rather than rejecting all property or authority.

Programs differed elsewhere. The Memmingen articles were not a constitution for every band. In Franconia and Thuringia, urban allies, miners, artisans, minor nobles, preachers, and territorial rivalries altered the movement. Thomas Müntzer’s apocalyptic politics mattered especially in Thuringia but should not stand for all rebels. Some bands negotiated and disciplined members; others destroyed monasteries and castles or committed violence. The “peasant” label again hides a coalition.

The alliance problem became decisive. Town councils might share opposition to a prince yet fear rural attacks on property. Lesser nobles could bargain with leagues but defect. Reformers who criticized ecclesiastical power could condemn armed resistance. Luther first admonished both lords and peasants, then denounced the rebels violently as the conflict escalated. Territorial rulers and the Swabian League possessed cavalry, artillery, experienced commanders, and the capacity to defeat bands separately. Massacres at Böblingen, Königshofen, and Frankenhausen demonstrated that numerical mobilization did not equal battlefield parity.

Defeat killed tens of thousands and brought executions, fines, and renewed lordly authority in many territories. Results still varied. Some communities negotiated redress; some rulers moderated contested dues; elsewhere repression hardened. The war helped align many Lutheran church settlements with territorial princes, but it neither ended rural protest nor created one German state. Later memory turned 1525 into competing stories of class revolution, religious betrayal, national liberty, or tragic disorder. Each selects a real dimension while flattening the movement’s regional plurality.

## A causal comparison

Across these cases, hardship was abundant but insufficient. Revolt became plausible through a recurring sequence:

1. **Extraction lost legitimacy.** War taxes, labor controls, new dues, or failures of protection made demands appear innovative, unequal, or contrary to custom.
2. **Communities possessed organizational capacity.** Assemblies, offices, parishes, leagues, and local courts supplied trust, records, routes, and leaders. The institutions through which authorities governed could also organize resistance.
3. **A crisis divided elites.** Captivity and civil conflict in France, royal need in Catalonia, minority kingship in England, and territorial-confessional rivalry in the Empire opened opportunities. Rebels rarely faced a perfectly unified ruling order at the start.
4. **Political language connected grievance to legitimacy.** Custom, just kingship, common profit, scripture, and communal election turned complaints into claims about rightful rule.
5. **Coalition breadth increased reach but multiplied fault lines.** Rural producers needed towns, clergy, soldiers, or elite patrons, yet those allies disagreed over property, religion, and authority.
6. **Military and administrative recovery separated outcomes.** Authorities offered concessions, isolated concentrations, revoked promises, prosecuted organizers, and deployed professional force. Durable gains required more than battlefield survival: recognized organizations and bargaining leverage had to outlast the crisis.

This model avoids two teleologies. Revolts were not doomed rehearsals for modern revolution, because participants pursued historically specific corporate, customary, royal, and religious settlements. Nor were they irrational spasms destined to disappear with “modernization.” Rural collective action could alter rents, status, enforcement, and political memory even without sovereign seizure.

## Evidence and representativeness

Most narrative accounts were written by clerics, officials, or elites frightened by rebellion. Judicial records often preserve testimony extracted under threat. Remission letters use formulas designed to obtain pardon. Printed programs tell us what organizers judged defensible, not what every participant believed. Historians must compare genres: chronicles establish perceptions and sequences; fiscal and manorial records identify disputed burdens; petitions and articles show public claims; prosopography and local geography reconstruct networks.

Labels also require care. “Peasant” may translate *rustici*, *villani*, *laboureurs*, *commons*, or *Bauern*, each used strategically. Women provisioned movements, carried information, defended households and commons, participated in crowds, and sometimes appeared as accused organizers, but male-authored programs and courts systematically reduce their visibility. Absence from leadership lists is not absence from revolt.

## Why it matters

Peasant revolts reveal that [[Manorialism, lordship, and peasant bargaining]] operated politically, not only economically. Custom was enforceable because communities remembered, recorded, and sometimes defended it together. State formation likewise did not simply suppress local society. Taxes and courts expanded through village officers and records that could become targets or tools of resistance.

The comparison also bridges medieval and early-modern history. The German Peasants’ War joined long disputes over lordship to the new confessional field examined in [[Reformations and confessional state-building]]. What changed was not the sudden discovery of grievance, but the scale, vocabulary, and institutional alliances through which grievance traveled.
