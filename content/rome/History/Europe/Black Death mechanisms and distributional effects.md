The Black Death was a *Yersinia pestis* pandemic whose biological transmission, commercial movement, and locally specific institutions produced catastrophic but uneven mortality—and redistributed labor, land, income, and coercive power without imposing one uniform European outcome.

Up: [[European history 500-1914]]

## A pandemic, not a single European event

“Black Death” conventionally names the first, exceptionally destructive wave of the second plague pandemic as it crossed much of western Eurasia and North Africa in the later 1340s and early 1350s. In Europe, maritime outbreaks appeared in Mediterranean ports in 1347, followed by rapid movement through Italy, Iberia, France, the British Isles, the Low Countries, central Europe, and Scandinavia. Recurrences then continued for centuries. The label should therefore distinguish the initial mortality shock from the longer plague regime that repeatedly removed people, disrupted households, and changed expectations about risk.

No credible synthesis can reduce the episode to a single death toll. The frequently repeated claim that “one third” or “one half” of Europe died is a continental extrapolation from evidence that is much better for some towns and estates than for others. Parish registers are mostly later. Chronicles favor spectacular urban experiences. Tax rolls, inquisitions post mortem, manorial accounts, wills, vacancies in benefices, and cemetery excavations each observe a selected population. They can establish extreme local mortality and broad demographic contraction, but their denominators and coverage differ. The safest conclusion is that mortality was enormous by the standards of any known premodern epidemic, while its incidence varied markedly within and between regions.

That variation is the explanatory center. A pathogen arrived through connected networks, but networks did not distribute exposure evenly. Local reservoirs and vectors, season, housing, grain storage, transport intensity, household structure, nutrition, prior disease burdens, flight, war, and governing capacity could change the timing or scale of death. Afterward, the same loss of workers operated through different tenures, labor markets, inheritance systems, states, and balances of organized power. Mass mortality made labor scarce relative to land; institutions helped determine who could use that changed ratio.

## What caused it

Ancient DNA has settled the central etiological question more firmly than older arguments based only on symptom descriptions. Researchers recovered *Y. pestis* DNA and reconstructed genomes from plague burials, including East Smithfield in London. These genomes place the medieval bacterium near the ancestral node of many later plague lineages. A 2022 study of cemeteries at Kara-Djigach and Burana in present-day Kyrgyzstan found *Y. pestis* in people buried during a mortality crisis dated by inscriptions to 1338–39. The recovered strain lies directly ancestral to the major diversification associated with the second pandemic, and related present-day lineages cluster in the broader Tian Shan region. This is strong evidence for a central Eurasian source near that region; it is not a complete itinerary from one graveyard to every later outbreak.

Plague is a zoonosis: *Y. pestis* persists in populations of wild rodents and their fleas, sometimes spills into susceptible animal populations, and can reach humans. Bubonic plague typically follows an infected flea bite and produces swollen lymph nodes or buboes. Septicemic infection spreads in the blood; pneumonic plague affects the lungs and can pass through respiratory droplets at close range. Medieval sources describe features compatible with these forms, but retrospective diagnosis from prose is unreliable. Genomic detection supplies the firm identification; it does not by itself reveal the proportion carried by each route.

The familiar textbook chain—black rats, rat fleas, then humans—is plausible in many settings but insufficient as a universal mechanism. Medieval Europe contained commensal rodents, and shipborne grain and goods could move rodents or fleas. Yet the epidemic sometimes appears to have advanced faster, or under climatic conditions less favorable to classic rat-flea epizootics, than a simple modern bubonic-plague analogy predicts. Mathematical models have shown that human ectoparasites such as body lice and human fleas *could* reproduce mortality curves from several European outbreaks better than an exclusively rat-centered model. “Could” matters: a fitted model is not direct proof that lice dominated in 1348. Pneumonic transmission may also have contributed in particular clusters. The most defensible position is plural: vector and transmission mixtures probably differed by ecology and outbreak, and evidence does not license one mechanism everywhere.

## How it moved

The pandemic’s speed depended on infrastructure created for peaceful exchange and imperial power. Caravan routes linked central Eurasian ecological zones to the Black Sea. Commercial shipping connected Black Sea and eastern Mediterranean entrepôts to Constantinople, Sicily, Genoa, Venice, Marseille, and Iberia. From ports, coasting traffic, rivers, roads, fairs, armies, pilgrims, and ordinary household movement carried infection onward. The often-retold siege story at Caffa, in which Mongol forces supposedly catapulted infected corpses into the Genoese colony, rests heavily on one later narrative and cannot bear the causal weight commonly assigned to it. The larger commercial corridor matters more than a dramatic single act.

Spread was neither a smooth wave nor proof of a modern contagion theory. Medieval observers used inherited frameworks of corrupted air, divine judgment, astrology, and bodily imbalance, while also noticing that contact, goods, and travel could be dangerous. Some Italian cities imposed measures that became associated with quarantine, but systematic forty-day detention belongs to the developing response after the initial catastrophe; Ragusa’s 1377 legislation prescribed a thirty-day isolation period. Flight and exclusion sometimes reduced contact, but they could also move infection, abandon dependents, or redirect costs onto poorer people.

Violence was a social mechanism of the disaster even though it did not transmit the bacterium. Accusations that Jews poisoned wells helped incite massacres across parts of the Empire and neighboring regions in 1348–49. The charge was false: Jews also died of plague, and plague struck places without Jewish communities. Authorities sometimes rejected the accusation, but protection was inconsistent and could be overwhelmed by municipal factions, indebted elites, popular fear, or rulers willing to seize property. The epidemic thus intensified the structures described in [[Jewish life within medieval Christian polities]]; disease does not explain persecution without antisemitic institutions, stories, and material incentives.

## Mortality was spatially uneven

Written evidence is densest in western and southern Europe, which has encouraged maps that convert absence of documentation into absence of plague. New palaeoecological work offers an independent, though indirect, check. Researchers compared changes in fossil pollen from 261 sites across nineteen present-day European countries. A sharp reduction in cereal cultivation and regeneration of woodland can indicate labor loss and abandoned land; continued or expanded cultivation suggests lower demographic disruption. The study found strong agricultural contraction in parts of Scandinavia, France, southwestern Germany, Greece, and central Italy, but continuity or growth in areas including much of Ireland and Iberia and portions of central and eastern Europe. This rejects a uniform continental mortality rate.

Pollen is not a body count. Land use can change because of war, prices, pastoral conversion, or sampling geography, and a pollen catchment does not coincide neatly with a polity. The study's windows also include early plague recurrences, so detected contraction cannot always be assigned to the 1347–52 wave alone. Nor does continuity mean nobody died: survivors, migrants, changed crop mixes, or institutional reorganization could maintain cultivation. Its value lies in showing that independent environmental evidence does not fit a homogeneous catastrophe. It also exposes a source problem: dramatic chronicles from Florence or England cannot simply stand for Europe.

Within regions, towns were not automatically deadlier in every outbreak, but density and turnover could sustain exposure. Ports and transport nodes tended to face earlier introductions. Monasteries and clerical communities sometimes provide unusually legible casualty lists, yet their communal routines and age structures make them unrepresentative. Rural districts could be devastated when infection entered households, while isolated places might be spared in one wave and struck in the next. Recurrent plague further altered distribution: a locality lightly affected in 1348 might lose heavily later, while immigration could rapidly refill a town and conceal the original loss in subsequent totals.

## From mortality to bargaining power

The immediate economic mechanism was a change in factor proportions. Death made labor scarcer relative to land and capital. Holdings fell vacant; livestock and tools outlived owners; heirs combined property; employers competed for servants, artisans, harvest workers, and tenants. In many parts of western Europe, nominal wages rose and land became cheaper or available on better terms. Where prices later adjusted and laborers could move, real wages increased. Diets could improve for survivors with access to income and markets, while marginal arable land reverted to pasture or woodland.

These were opportunities produced by mass bereavement, not a universal “golden age.” Households lost earners and caregivers; widows and orphans confronted legal disadvantages; craft knowledge and commercial credit networks disappeared with individuals; demand contracted; and rulers, landlords, and guilds tried to capture gains. England’s Ordinance of Labourers (1349) and Statute of Labourers (1351) attempted to compel able workers to accept employment and to restrain wages near pre-plague levels. Their repeated enforcement demonstrates conflict over scarcity, not successful freezing of the economy. As [[Manorialism, lordship, and peasant bargaining]] explains, landlords could prosecute mobility while also commuting services, leasing demesnes, or bidding for labor because their own institutional tools were costly and incomplete.

Distribution therefore turned on four linked capacities. First was **exit**: could workers leave a manor, village, employer, or marriage market for a better offer? Second was **organization**: could villagers defend custom, withhold labor, or litigate collectively? Third was **lordly and state coercion**: could elites enforce service, wage ceilings, taxes, or residence? Fourth was **market access**: could households sell labor and output or acquire vacant land? Similar demographic pressure generated different settlements because these capacities differed.

In England, weakening demesne cultivation, higher wages, commutation of labor services, and tenant leverage contributed over time to the erosion of villeinage, though neither the plague alone nor the 1381 revolt abolished it at once. Elsewhere in western Europe, outcomes ranged from favorable leases and peasant proprietorship to continued seigneurial dues. In parts of central and eastern Europe, later landlords used political jurisdiction and export opportunities to intensify restrictions on peasants; describing this as an immediate automatic “second serfdom” after 1350 compresses developments that often matured much later. [[Serfdom's divergent paths in eastern and western Europe]] and [[The Brenner debate on agrarian class structure]] clarify why population-to-land ratios cannot predict class outcomes without property and power.

## Inequality, inheritance, and gender

Severe mortality could compress wealth inequality through several channels. Property passed to a smaller pool of heirs; more land entered the market; labor income rose relative to rents; and cheaper real estate allowed some non-elites to acquire assets. Fiscal records from parts of Italy and Germany support post-plague inequality declines, and research on Piedmont finds a substantial leveling after the Black Death that persisted for roughly a century. But surviving datasets are local, measure taxable wealth imperfectly, and often omit the poorest. “Europe became equal” is too strong. Leveling could coexist with windfalls for already prosperous heirs, institutional property accumulation, and renewed inequality as elites adapted.

Women’s outcomes were similarly conditional. Labor scarcity could widen paid opportunities in service, agriculture, brewing, textiles, or retail, and widows might inherit or manage holdings. A smaller cohort could marry later or negotiate differently. Yet guild rules, wage discrimination, coverture, inheritance customs, and moral regulation constrained gains. Legislators sometimes singled out female workers, and elite households could respond to scarcity by tightening control over servants. The post-plague economy changed bargaining positions; it did not dissolve patriarchy.

Nor should higher per-capita resources be confused with aggregate prosperity. Population collapse reduced total output, tax bases, demand, and urban scale even where survivor incomes rose. Some sectors shrank while labor-saving or land-extensive activities expanded. Institutional responses mattered across generations: repeated epidemics, warfare, debasement, taxation, and regional trade shifts continually modified the initial shock. The plague can explain a discontinuity in relative prices and negotiating leverage; it cannot by itself explain the Renaissance, capitalism, European overseas empire, or industrialization centuries later.

## Historiography and limits of evidence

Older plague history often proceeded from narrative sources and the epidemiology of the late nineteenth-century “third pandemic.” That comparison generated the classic rat-flea model and continental mortality totals. Revisionists questioned whether medieval symptoms and speed fit bubonic plague at all. Ancient DNA decisively restored *Y. pestis* as the causal agent, but it did not restore every element of the classic model. Current research integrates genomes, archaeology, climate and rodent ecology, transport history, documents, and models. Each resolves a different question.

Genomes identify pathogen lineages and relationships, but sampling is sparse and biased toward excavated cemeteries where DNA preserves. Documentary series can reveal timing, prices, wages, inheritance, or institutional reaction, but not all deaths. Pollen measures landscape response rather than mortality directly. Epidemiological models test whether proposed routes are plausible under assumptions; several mechanisms can fit imperfect historical curves. Wage series frequently describe adult male skilled or urban workers better than women, children, servants, or subsistence producers. Tax registers omit or undervalue people and assets. Conclusions should therefore be scaled to the evidence: local estimates remain local, correlations are not mechanisms, and silence on a map is not immunity.

## Why it matters

The Black Death is a test case for explaining how natural and social systems meet. The bacterium was indispensable, but the disaster’s geography followed animal ecologies and human connections. Mortality altered the ratio of people to resources, but law and collective power translated scarcity into wages, tenures, coercion, or dispossession. The episode therefore guards against two equal errors: treating disease as an external event with automatic consequences, and treating institutions as if biology and demography supplied no force.

It also changes the scale of medieval European history. Commercial integration made distant ecological disturbances locally consequential. Political fragmentation created experimentation in public-health measures but also uneven protection and scapegoating. Survivors sometimes gained material leverage while living amid grief, recurrent disease, and persecution. The central historical fact is not that plague “ended feudalism” or “created modernity.” It is that one pathogen shock exposed and rearranged existing distributions of connection, property, and power—and that those distributions made its consequences unequal.

## Open questions

- Which combinations of rodent fleas, human ectoparasites, and pneumonic transmission best explain particular outbreaks rather than the pandemic in aggregate?
- How far will additional ancient genomes revise the route between the 1338–39 Tian Shan outbreak and Black Sea epidemics in the 1340s?
- Can pollen, cemetery, fiscal, and documentary evidence be calibrated without turning unlike proxies into a false common mortality rate?
- How durable were post-plague gains for women, landless workers, and poorer tenants outside the unusually well-documented regions of England and northern Italy?
- Why did some political systems convert labor scarcity into mobility and higher earnings while others eventually intensified coercion?
