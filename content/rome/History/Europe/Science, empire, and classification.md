European empires enlarged the scale of natural knowledge between the sixteenth and early twentieth centuries, but they did so through unequal networks in which Indigenous experts, enslaved and coerced workers, sailors, soldiers, missionaries, merchants, and colonial officials gathered and translated observations that metropolitan institutions often reclassified and credited to European savants.

## The argument

“Science and empire” is not a claim that all European science was merely propaganda for conquest, or that reliable knowledge could not emerge from colonial settings. It names a historical relationship. Maritime expansion, chartered companies, plantation regimes, military surveys, missions, and colonial administrations created routes along which specimens, measurements, images, and reports could travel. European states and commercial organizations supplied ships, forts, gardens, observatories, museums, and money. Classification made heterogeneous things portable and comparable: a plant could become a named specimen, a territory a gridded map, a population a census category, and a healing practice an entry in a pharmacopoeia.

Yet these operations depended on people whom published texts often made difficult to see. A metropolitan naturalist rarely discovered a plant simply by walking alone into untouched nature. Guides found paths; hunters and fishers located animals; cultivators identified growing conditions; healers explained uses; artists rendered unfamiliar forms; porters transported collections; and servants preserved them. Some collaborators bargained for pay, prestige, protection, or reciprocal knowledge. Others worked under slavery, corvée, indenture, military discipline, taxation, or the constraints of colonial rule. The archive therefore records both knowledge-making and asymmetrical power.

The relationship was also recursive rather than one-way. Imperial expansion enabled collecting and measurement; classifications then helped officials imagine resources, peoples, and spaces as governable. But schemes devised in Europe changed in the field, competed with local systems, and regularly failed. Colonial knowledge was neither a finished European package exported outward nor raw local “data” simply imported inward. It was made through encounters, translations, refusals, losses, and disputes across many sites.

## Before the high imperial age

The chronology begins before the nineteenth-century partition of Africa. Iberian conquest in the Americas, Portuguese trading networks around Africa and Asia, and Dutch, French, and English commercial expansion all generated demands for information about winds, coastlines, diseases, plants, minerals, and food. Sixteenth-century Spanish officials solicited standardized descriptions of American territories through questionnaires now known as the *Relaciones geográficas*. Local officials assembled answers with Indigenous informants, scribes, and painters. The resulting texts and maps joined European administrative questions to local knowledge and representational practices; they were neither transparent snapshots of America nor purely Spanish inventions.

Materia medica offered another early arena. Europeans sought drugs, dyes, foods, and spices, but knowing that a plant existed was not enough. They needed to learn where it grew, how it was identified, which part was prepared, in what dose, and for what condition. That practical knowledge commonly resided with Indigenous healers, farmers, merchants, craftspeople, and African and Asian specialists. Translation could detach a remedy from its social or ritual setting. European writers might accept an efficacy while rejecting the explanation attached to it, or substitute a familiar name for several locally distinguished organisms.

Commercial companies intensified this circulation. The Dutch and English East India companies were trading and military powers, but their surgeons, factors, gardeners, and correspondents also gathered natural knowledge. Company ships moved letters and specimens while company violence reordered the societies from which they came. Botanical gardens at ports and administrative centers became experimental stations where plants were acclimatized, compared, and redirected. The relevant geography was polycentric: Batavia, Calcutta, Cape Town, Madras, Mexico City, and Caribbean islands were sites of selection and experiment, not passive feeders for London, Paris, Madrid, or Amsterdam.

## Classification as infrastructure

Early modern naturalists inherited ancient and medieval traditions and learned from Arabic, Asian, African, and American bodies of knowledge. They also faced an expanding volume of unfamiliar objects. Catalogues, cabinets, botanical illustrations, herbaria, and gardens helped stabilize this abundance. Carl Linnaeus's eighteenth-century binomial nomenclature was powerful because it offered a relatively compact way to name and arrange species across distance. A specimen labeled with a genus and species could enter correspondence among people who did not share a vernacular language.

That portability should not be confused with neutrality. To classify is to choose which differences count. Linnaean botany foregrounded reproductive structures; other traditions might organize plants by habitat, season, taste, healing action, kinship, sacred association, or practical use. The systems could overlap, but translation was lossy. A single Latin name could facilitate global comparison while stripping away multiple local names and their ecological information. Conversely, European collectors often could not identify or use organisms without those local distinctions. “Universal” taxonomy was built through repeated acts of situated assistance.

Classification also created authority. The published name, type specimen, museum catalogue, or metropolitan herbarium made a claim durable and citable. Credit tended to gather at the point of inscription rather than at the many points of collection. European women, artisans, colonial settlers of modest status, and non-European collaborators could disappear behind the name of an expedition leader or institutional correspondent. This was not absolute: collectors sometimes named informants, local experts published, and colonial intellectuals appropriated learned forms for their own projects. But institutional rules made recognition uneven.

Human classification carried still greater stakes. European naturalists debated whether humanity formed one species or several and arranged human varieties using anatomy, skin color, language, climate, and conjectural stages of civilization. There was no single, continuous doctrine running unchanged from Linnaeus to late nineteenth-century racial science. Biblical chronology, environmental accounts, polygenism, evolutionary theory, linguistics, ethnology, and physical anthropology conflicted. Nevertheless, measurements and taxonomies increasingly gave hierarchy the appearance of natural fact. They could convert histories of conquest and enslavement into supposedly inherited differences.

## How knowledge circulated

The image of knowledge flowing from colonial periphery to European center is too simple. It moved through chains. A plant might be identified by a healer, obtained by an intermediary, dried by a collector, labeled by a clerk, packed by sailors, damaged at sea, compared in a garden, renamed by a botanist, drawn by an artist, and described by a printer. At each stage information could be added or removed. Living plants required soil, water, and care; seeds lost viability; colors faded; locations were generalized; local names were misspelled. Material survival shaped what Europe could know.

Correspondence networks were social systems, not pipes. Metropolitan patrons depended on colonial collectors who expected payment, books, duplicate specimens, offices, or public acknowledgment. Joseph Dalton Hooker and Kew, for example, relied in the nineteenth century on officials, missionaries, officers, farmers, soldiers, and other correspondents across the British Empire. Kew's own history emphasizes that many were unpaid and that their motives and bargaining positions varied. The central institution accumulated collections and reputation, but it could not command every participant or erase competition among gardens, museums, companies, and national empires.

Intermediaries were often translators in the broadest sense. They matched names across languages, judged whether two specimens were “the same,” negotiated entry, and decided what could be disclosed. Go-betweens might exploit their position, protect restricted knowledge, selectively cooperate, or redirect European questions. A person could be subordinate in colonial hierarchy yet exercise expertise in a particular encounter. Recovering such agency is necessary, but it must not romanticize constraint: the ability to bargain was radically different for a court physician, a free broker, an indentured laborer, and an enslaved field hand.

Print widened some exchanges while narrowing others. Journals, floras, atlases, and catalogues allowed observations to be compared beyond the life of a voyage. Standard forms—tables, specimen labels, questionnaires, instructions for collectors—disciplined what observers recorded. But print favored claims that could fit metropolitan genres. Oral explanation, embodied skill, seasonal memory, and sacred restriction were hard to transport. Publication could turn a negotiated encounter into the apparently solitary observation of a named author.

## Labor, coercion, and extraction

Scientific travel rested on ordinary labor. Expeditions required ships, food, roads, pack animals, camps, interpreters, porters, cooks, taxidermists, draftspeople, and pilots. Colonial states could requisition supplies or compel movement. Plantation and mining regimes supplied information about soils, plants, disease, and minerals while subjecting workers to violence. Enslaved Africans and their descendants carried agricultural and medicinal knowledge across the Atlantic, including expertise in rice cultivation; European planters and writers often claimed the resulting productivity without equal acknowledgment.

Economic botany shows the junction of classification and extraction. Institutions sought plants that could become commodities or support imperial settlement: tea, coffee, sugar, cotton, rubber, timber, dyes, and cinchona among them. Identifying a useful species was only the beginning. Officials and cultivators needed to move it, reproduce it, reorganize land, recruit or coerce labor, and secure markets. Botanical success could therefore mean ecological transformation and harsher labor discipline rather than a simple triumph of discovery.

Kew's Museum of Economic Botany, founded in 1847, made plant materials legible to manufacturers and the public. Its surviving collection is especially rich for territories touched by British trade and colonialism. Kew served as a node among colonial gardens, agricultural and forestry stations, officials, scientists, and commercial interests. This network aided genuine botanical research, yet also helped assess and circulate useful plants. The same specimen could be scientific evidence, a cultural object, and a potential industrial input.

The case of cinchona illustrates both appropriation and contingency. Andean peoples possessed knowledge of medicinal barks; European physicians and naturalists gradually incorporated cinchona into antimalarial practice. In the nineteenth century, European empires sought reliable supplies outside Spanish American control, transferring cinchona to Asian plantations. Classification mattered because different species and barks contained different alkaloid profiles. So did Indigenous expertise, smuggling, gardening, chemistry, plantation labor, and state support. No single heroic “discovery” explains the commodity chain.

Coercion was not identical everywhere. A naval hydrographic survey, a missionary collection, a settler herbarium, and a plantation experiment operated under different institutions. French, British, Dutch, Spanish, Portuguese, Russian, German, and other empires had distinct administrative capacities and scientific cultures that changed over time. Informal empire and commercial pressure could organize knowledge without direct annexation. Regional variation is therefore part of the explanation, not noise around a universal model.

## Maps, surveys, and populations

Cartography was both an intellectual achievement and a practice of rule. Accurate coastal charts could reduce shipwreck and support trade; triangulation could connect measurements across large spaces. The same maps could guide armies, define claims, assign taxes, or facilitate settlement. Surveyors depended on local guides, existing itineraries, place names, and labor. A blank-looking area on a European map was rarely an empty social space.

Cadastral surveys translated land into bounded parcels, owners, values, and revenue obligations. Their categories could clash with overlapping rights to grazing, forests, water, or seasonal cultivation. The survey did not simply reveal a preexisting property order; it could help create one. Yet implementation remained contested. Villagers supplied or withheld information, officials negotiated, boundary markers were moved, and fiscal ambitions exceeded administrative reach.

Censuses and ethnographic classifications similarly made populations visible in selected ways. Administrators fixed fluid identities into boxes of religion, caste, tribe, race, occupation, or language. Such categories could determine taxation, representation, recruitment, policing, and access to land. But colonial enumerations differed by place and decade, and enumerated people interpreted or resisted them. It is presentist to assume that every category worked like a modern racial census, just as it is misleading to treat the resulting statistics as unmediated facts.

## Competing knowledge systems and colonial limits

European science often secured institutional dominance without eliminating other knowledge systems. Farmers continued to judge seeds and weather through accumulated local practice; healers used therapeutic traditions that colonial medicine dismissed, borrowed, or regulated; navigators retained environmental knowledge unavailable in European instruments. Colonial officials themselves moved between systems when expedient. They might condemn a practice as superstition in print while relying on its practitioners in a crisis.

Competition also occurred within European science. Metropolitan museums disputed specimens and priority. Field naturalists challenged armchair authorities. Commercial secrecy obstructed open exchange. Administrators wanted usable answers, while taxonomists might pursue questions with no immediate payoff. Acclimatization schemes failed when climate, pests, soils, or labor conditions did not conform to metropolitan expectation. Empire expanded the reach of scientific institutions, but it never granted complete information or control.

Colonized scholars and political actors did more than preserve a static tradition. They entered colleges, societies, surveys, presses, and museums; combined learned practices; criticized European claims; and used scientific languages to argue for reform, status, or national autonomy. Their work warns against a binary of active Western science and passive Indigenous knowledge. At the same time, celebrating hybridity alone can conceal who controlled appointments, archives, property, and publication.

## Race, evolution, and the problem of retrospective judgment

By the later nineteenth century, anthropology, anatomy, statistics, archaeology, linguistics, and evolutionary thought were mobilized in arguments about human difference. Some practitioners produced rankings that rationalized domination; imperial conquest supplied bodies, artifacts, photographs, and observations. Museums arranged peoples along conjectural sequences from “savagery” to “civilization.” Craniometry and other measurements promised objectivity while sampling, categories, and interpretation embedded social assumptions.

Darwinian evolution did not create racial hierarchy, which long predated 1859, and evolutionary theory did not dictate one politics. Abolitionists, socialists, nationalists, feminists, and imperialists drew different conclusions from biology. “Social Darwinism” is therefore a retrospective umbrella, useful only if attached to specific authors, institutions, and policies. Likewise, calling every eighteenth-century taxonomy “scientific racism” can collapse important changes in theology, heredity, anatomy, and political economy. The safer chronology traces how particular classifications traveled and what work they performed.

Avoiding presentism does not require moral neutrality. Enslavement, expropriation, coerced labor, and human display were violent in their own time, and contemporaries contested them. Historical precision asks which actors knew what, which alternatives they articulated, and how institutions rewarded or suppressed disagreement. It also asks whether today's collection metadata reproduces old silences by preserving only the metropolitan collector's name.

## Historiographic limits

Older histories often narrated science as a succession of European discoveries. Later scholarship rightly exposed imperial infrastructures and recovered intermediaries. But a totalizing reversal—“science was empire”—creates new problems. It can flatten differences among disciplines, periods, and empires; imply that colonized actors had no intellectual projects of their own; or treat the validity of a claim as settled by the politics of its production. Knowledge can be empirically robust and still have been acquired through injustice. Conversely, imperial sponsorship did not guarantee accuracy.

The archive imposes unequal visibility. Institutions preserved the letters they received, specimens they accessioned, and names they recognized. Coerced workers often appear as numbers or not at all. Historians can read labels, field notebooks, payrolls, shipping records, oral histories, and material traces against the grain, but they should distinguish recovery from speculation. An unnamed guide should not be transformed into a fully knowable voice merely to repair the record.

The most useful unit of analysis is therefore the chain of knowledge. Who posed the question? Who identified, collected, measured, carried, drew, translated, classified, financed, and published? What was lost between stages? Who could contest the result? What new capacities did the classification give to a museum, company, state, or local community? Those questions preserve scientific content while making its social production visible.

## Why it matters

This history explains why European intellectual change cannot be contained within Europe. Collections and theories associated with the Enlightenment, natural history, geography, anthropology, and industrial development emerged through global circulation. Empire supplied access and resources, but its networks were populated by intermediaries whose expertise was indispensable and whose credit was unequal.

It also clarifies why classification is politically consequential without being reducible to politics. Shared names and measurements can enable cumulative inquiry. They can also erase context, harden identities, and turn life or land into administrable resources. Modern debates over museum provenance, Indigenous data sovereignty, taxonomy, and restitution inherit these layered histories. The goal is not to discard knowledge because of its origins, but to understand the conditions of its making, repair attribution where evidence permits, and recognize that different systems may encode different but valuable relations to the world.
