“Feudalism” bundles several distinct medieval relationships, so it is safer to name the institution, place, and period being explained.

Up: [[European history (MOC)]]

## Why the umbrella leaks

The word is post-medieval, not a shared medieval name for a Europe-wide system. Historians have used it for fiefs and vassalage among elites, peasant dependence, private justice, cavalry, fragmented authority, and hereditary privilege. Those mechanisms could coexist, but they did not necessarily arise together or operate uniformly across Europe.

Susan Reynolds's challenge was not that lords, fiefs, homage, or dependent peasants were imaginary. It was that later legal and historical models made their relations look earlier, more coherent, and more universal than the evidence permits. Subsequent work preserves disagreement: some historians abandon the category; others retain narrow, regionally specified “feudal” relations.

## A better reading protocol

Replace “feudal society caused X” with a testable claim: who held what right, from whom, under which vocabulary, in what region, and in which century? Separate elite land tenure, personal lordship, jurisdiction, military service, and peasant labor before asking how they interacted.

## Place in the map

This is a vocabulary prerequisite for [[Medieval state formation]], [[Carolingian Empire]], and later comparisons of lordship. It also guards against treating western French or English evidence as a template for Byzantium, Iberia, Scandinavia, or eastern Europe.
