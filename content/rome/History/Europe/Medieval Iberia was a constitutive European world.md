Between 500 and 1000, Iberia joined Visigothic, Byzantine, Islamic, Christian, and Jewish histories rather than sitting outside a supposedly northern European core.

Up: [[European history (MOC)]]

## A changing political field

Visigothic rule, a Byzantine foothold, the eighth-century conquest, the Umayyad emirate and caliphate, and northern Christian polities produced overlapping sovereignties. Córdoba became a major political and cultural center with Mediterranean and Middle Eastern connections, while Christian, Jewish, and Muslim populations participated in exchange under unequal and changing conditions.

This does not justify a timeless story of either harmony or inevitable religious war. Legal status, violence, patronage, migration, and intellectual exchange varied by ruler, locality, and century. The methodological gain is to ask how particular regimes organized difference rather than treating “Europe” and “Islam” as sealed civilizations.

## Place in the map

This breadth-first note complicates [[Christianization of Europe]] and provides a regional case that [[Feudalism debate]] cannot absorb. Next come bounded studies of Visigothic institutions, Umayyad state formation, and the evidence behind later *convivencia* debates.
