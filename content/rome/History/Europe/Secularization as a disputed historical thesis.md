Between 1750 and 1914, Europeans did not simply cease to be religious: religious authority, institutions, practices, beliefs, and identities changed at different speeds, in different directions, and through political conflict.

## The thesis is a question, not a result

The classic secularization thesis joins several propositions into one story. As societies became more urban, industrial, educated, scientifically minded, and politically liberal, religion supposedly lost plausibility for individuals, churches lost members, clergy lost authority, and states separated public life from sacred institutions. In its strongest form, modernization made religious decline likely or inevitable. Europe appeared to be the exemplary case: by the twentieth century its churches were weaker than they had been under the confessional regimes of the seventeenth century, and intellectuals could narrate this outcome as emancipation from “superstition.”

That synthesis identifies real changes, but it becomes misleading when treated as a single measurable process. “Religion” was never one variable. At least four dimensions must be distinguished:

- **Belief:** assent to doctrines, confidence in providence, experiences of the sacred, and ideas about death or salvation.
- **Practice:** worship attendance, communion, confession, prayer, fasting, pilgrimage, life-cycle rites, and household devotion.
- **Authority:** the capacity of clergy or religious norms to define truth, morality, education, marriage, and legitimate government.
- **Institutions:** churches’ property, personnel, legal privileges, schools, welfare agencies, associations, and relations with states.

A fifth dimension, **identity**, often outlasted or diverged from all four. Someone might rarely attend worship yet call himself Catholic against a Protestant state, Jewish in a Christian society, or Orthodox within an empire. Conversely, regular attendance might express village conformity, employer pressure, or access to poor relief rather than doctrinal conviction. State disestablishment could reduce ecclesiastical privilege while voluntary churches recruited more adherents. A fall in Easter communion could coexist with a boom in pilgrimage. No single statistic can settle all these questions.

The safest historical claim is therefore limited: parts of Europe experienced secularization in particular dimensions between the eighteenth century and 1914, but the timing, mechanisms, and extent were disputed even within each country. The period also produced **religious modernization**—centralized churches, mass missions, denominational newspapers, voluntary societies, new devotions, and political parties—that could make religion more organized and publicly combative than before.

## The old confessional order did erode

Around 1750, most European rulers still governed through legally privileged confessions. Churches registered births, marriages, and deaths; controlled much schooling and poor relief; owned land; censored print; collected tithes or taxes; and supplied the moral language of monarchy. Religious minorities faced disabilities, exile, or restricted worship. This was not a uniformly devout “age of faith.” Clergy complained continually about ignorance, irregular attendance, anticlerical humor, magic, and popular customs they considered heterodox. Yet institutions made Christianity unavoidable even for the indifferent.

The [[Enlightenments in plural|Enlightenment]] weakened that settlement without simply abolishing faith. Deists, materialists, and critics of priestcraft attacked revelation or ecclesiastical power, but many reformers sought a rational Christianity. “Enlightened” Catholic and Protestant governments subordinated churches, dissolved contemplative houses, reorganized dioceses, and promoted useful education. This was secularization of jurisdiction more clearly than mass unbelief: the state appropriated functions formerly treated as ecclesiastical while still presenting itself as Christian.

The [[French Revolution]] made the rupture violent and visible. The nationalization of church property, abolition of tithes, Civil Constitution of the Clergy, revolutionary calendar, and dechristianizing campaigns transferred resources and authority from church to nation. But the Revolution also showed the limits of coercive secularization. The clerical oath split communities; resistance became a language of counterrevolution; and Napoleon’s Concordat restored public Catholic worship under state supervision. The long-term result was not religion’s disappearance but a durable political cleavage between clerical and anticlerical France.

Revolutionary and Napoleonic conquest carried civil equality, confiscation, and administrative reform across Europe. Jewish emancipation dismantled corporate restrictions in uneven stages. Monasteries and prince-bishoprics were secularized. Civil law increasingly treated marriage, property, and citizenship as state concerns. Restoration after 1815 could not fully reconstruct the old order, because both states and populations had learned that religious institutions were historically alterable.

## Modernization changed the religious market more than it dictated one outcome

Industrialization and urbanization are central to the classic thesis because they disrupted parish communities. Migrants arrived in expanding cities before churches could build enough seats or train enough clergy. Factory time competed with Sunday observance. Anonymous neighborhoods weakened clerical inspection, while newspapers, clubs, unions, taverns, theaters, and political meetings offered alternative communities. In districts of Berlin, Paris, London, and other large cities, working-class men were often conspicuously absent from formal worship.

But urbanization did not mechanically produce unbelief. Cities also concentrated audiences, printers, money, and organizers. Methodists, Baptists, Salvationists, Catholic religious orders, Jewish congregations, and Orthodox brotherhoods built missions, chapels, schools, hospitals, choirs, temperance groups, and mutual-aid societies. Competition could mobilize believers. A migrant might leave a territorial parish yet join a voluntary congregation whose discipline was more demanding. The relevant causal chain was mediated by housing patterns, church finance, denominational competition, gender relations, political repression, and the speed of institutional adaptation.

Education was equally ambiguous. Literacy widened access to skeptical and scientific writing, biblical criticism, socialist journalism, and anticlerical newspapers. State school systems reduced clerical control in several countries. Yet literacy also expanded Bible reading, devotional publishing, catechisms, hymnals, and religious newspapers. Churches helped create mass literacy and then used it. Scientific developments—from geology and evolutionary theory to historical criticism of scripture—undermined some inherited chronologies, but “science versus religion” was never a clean social boundary. Clergymen practiced science; believers reconciled evolution and providence; and conflicts depended on institutions and politics as much as ideas.

Class mattered. European elites had long included skeptics who still conformed publicly. During the nineteenth century, sections of the educated bourgeoisie adopted liberal Protestantism, anticlerical republicanism, positivism, spiritualism, or ethical culture. Skilled workers in some industrial districts built socialist and freethinking subcultures, especially where a church was identified with landlords or conservative governments. Elsewhere chapels gave artisans literacy, leadership, credit, and collective dignity. Rural populations were not uniformly devout: landholding, clerical quality, inheritance, seasonal work, and memories of revolution created sharply different religious landscapes.

Gender also complicates aggregate decline. Women generally predominated in congregations and devotional associations, while men were more visible in freethinking and anticlerical organizations. That pattern reflected unequal access to public life as well as belief. Churches offered women approved forms of association, teaching, philanthropy, and sometimes religious vocations outside marriage; at the same time they defended patriarchal family law and male clerical authority. A “feminized” church could remain socially powerful through households and welfare even while male workers described churchgoing as unmanly or politically submissive.

## Confessions and regions followed different paths

In **Britain**, the established churches retained public roles, but pluralism grew. The 1851 Religious Worship Census—an exceptional attempt to count attendance—revealed both extensive provision and large nonattendance, especially in cities. Its estimates are not transparent measures of belief: people could attend more than once, weather and seating mattered, and the census captured one Sunday. Protestant Nonconformity flourished in Wales and industrial England, while Catholicism expanded through Irish migration and institutional rebuilding. Disestablishment in Ireland (1869) and Wales (after this period, in 1920) reflected political conflict and religious plurality, not an immediate collapse of Christianity. Britain before 1914 was simultaneously more denominationally competitive, less uniformly Anglican, and densely organized through churches.

In **France**, the Revolution created a particularly sharp clerical–anticlerical divide. Practice varied geographically: western and some eastern regions remained observant, while parts of the Paris basin and south showed much lower conformity. Nineteenth-century Catholic revival produced seminaries, teaching orders, Marian apparitions and pilgrimages, missions, and a more Rome-centered piety. Republicans nevertheless associated clerical education and royalism with threats to civic sovereignty. The Third Republic secularized public schooling, restricted congregations, and enacted the 1905 separation of churches and state. This was decisive institutional and political secularization, but it occurred amid Catholic mass mobilization and did not demonstrate the extinction of belief.

In the **German lands**, confessional division prevented a single national pattern. Protestant church bureaucracies were closely tied to states; revivalist and liberal currents competed within them. Catholic ultramontanism strengthened attachments to Rome and built a dense “milieu” of associations, newspapers, schools, and the Centre Party. Bismarck’s [[Kulturkampf]] in the 1870s attempted to subject Catholic education and clergy to state authority. Instead of dissolving Catholic allegiance, persecution often intensified organization and electoral solidarity. Meanwhile the Social Democratic movement offered many workers a comprehensive secular culture, though socialist voting cannot be equated automatically with atheism.

**Italy** combined low observance in some regions, strong local devotion in others, and a national conflict with the papacy. Unification abolished the Papal States in 1870, a profound loss of temporal sovereignty. Yet the papacy responded by centralizing Catholic identity, condemning liberal propositions, promoting devotional movements, and ultimately cultivating Catholic social action. The institutional defeat of a territorial church-state accompanied the spiritual and administrative expansion of ultramontane Catholicism.

In the **Habsburg lands**, a Catholic dynasty governed a confessionally diverse empire. Joseph II’s reforms had subordinated church institutions to state purposes; later governments alternated between concordat and liberal regulation. Catholic, Protestant, Orthodox, Jewish, and Muslim communities were entangled with language and national politics. Census labels could harden confessional identities even when practice varied. Religious affiliation operated as a framework for schools, charities, marriage, and nationality, making “secularization” inseparable from imperial governance.

In **Russia and southeastern Europe**, Orthodox churches were closely connected to empire and emerging nation-states. Russian Orthodoxy possessed legal privilege and administrative reach, while intellectual radicals and revolutionary movements attacked autocracy and religion together. This produced secular elites and countercultures, not necessarily mass rural dechristianization before 1914. In the Balkans, independent or autonomous churches often served national projects after Ottoman rule; institutional differentiation from empire could mean religious nationalization rather than secular decline. Muslim populations, Catholic minorities, Sephardi communities, and other groups further defeat any equation of Europe with Latin Christianity.

**Jewish emancipation** provides another caution. Civil equality weakened the authority of autonomous communal corporations and opened paths to integration, conversion, religious reform, and secular Jewish identities. Yet modern states also classified citizens by confession, while antisemitism racialized Jewishness so that unbelief did not erase imposed identity. Reform, Orthodox renewal, Wissenschaft des Judentums, Zionism, and socialist Jewish politics were alternative responses to modernity. Differentiation from rabbinic authority was real, but it did not have one endpoint.

## Practice, belief, authority, and institutions diverged

Religious practice is the dimension for which historians often possess the most records, but church-generated measures are selective. Easter communion registers, confirmations, marriages, baptisms, burials, and attendance counts reveal behavior under particular rules. A priest’s complaint about empty churches may reflect rising expectations; a rise in communicants may reflect stricter enumeration; a civil marriage may signal anticlerical conviction, poverty, mixed confession, or legal convenience. Local series are valuable precisely when linked to migration, occupation, sex, and age—not when inflated into a continental curve.

Belief is harder to recover. Intellectual history gives abundant evidence of atheism, agnosticism, biblical criticism, materialism, and alternative creeds among literate elites. It gives far less direct access to most Europeans. Wills, diaries, letters, folklore collections, oral testimony, and devotional objects disclose mixed cosmologies rather than a binary switch. People could doubt hell, consult a healer, baptize children, distrust priests, pray during illness, and vote anticlerically. The category “believing” must therefore be plural and graded.

Authority declined in important domains. By 1914, many states claimed control over census, education, marriage, welfare, and law. Political legitimacy increasingly rested on nation, constitution, race, class, or popular sovereignty rather than divine right alone. Clergy faced rival experts: teachers, physicians, journalists, scientists, lawyers, and party organizers. Yet transfer of authority was not always separation. States could bureaucratize churches, pay clergy, teach religion, or use confessional institutions to govern populations. A church subordinate to the state might lose autonomy while retaining everyday reach.

Institutions could grow even when authority narrowed. Nineteenth-century Catholicism expanded female orders, schools, hospitals, missions, associations, and print. Protestant denominations constructed voluntary networks at home and overseas. Religious organizations pioneered welfare later absorbed by states. The same century that confiscated monasteries generated new congregations. Institutional counts therefore measure capacity and mobilization, not necessarily doctrinal consensus.

## Religious revival belongs inside the explanation

The nineteenth century was an age of revival as well as criticism. Evangelical awakenings, Catholic missions, Marian devotion, ultramontanism, Orthodox renewal, Jewish reform and Orthodoxy, and new religious movements all responded to revolutionary upheaval. Revival was not simply medieval survival. It used railways, cheap print, bureaucratic fundraising, electoral parties, mass rallies, and standardized schooling—the machinery of modernity.

This matters causally. Confessional conflict sometimes created the very identities later mistaken for ancient continuity. When states challenged schools or clergy, Catholics organized politically; when established churches excluded dissenters, Nonconformists made voluntary religion a vehicle of liberal citizenship; when nationalists fused church and people, religious affiliation acquired new political force. Modernization did not act upon a passive sacred residue. Religious actors selected, resisted, and appropriated modern institutions.

Nor did secular alternatives simply remove religion. Liberal nationalism, socialism, republicanism, and positivism offered calendars, martyrs, rituals, moral narratives, and visions of collective redemption. Calling these “political religions” can blur important differences, but their form shows that declining clerical authority did not eliminate the demand for meaning or ceremony. The question is not whether Europeans moved from belief to reason, but how rival institutions claimed truth, loyalty, time, and the life cycle.

## What historians actually dispute

Defenders of a revised secularization thesis argue that differentiation and long-run decline remain visible once inevitability is discarded. Churches lost monopolies; Christian doctrine became less taken for granted; observance weakened in important urban and working-class settings; and autonomous spheres of science, politics, and law expanded. Unevenness does not disprove a process, and revival may be a defensive response to a contracting cultural position.

Revisionists answer that the thesis often projects post-1960 western European outcomes backward. Medieval and early modern populations were never uniformly disciplined or orthodox; nineteenth-century churches sometimes recruited more effectively than their predecessors; and national or local trajectories diverged too much for “modernization” to explain them. Measurements of belonging, behavior, belief, and institutional strength do not move together. Some therefore prefer **dechristianization** for a specific decline in Christian practice, **differentiation** for institutional separation, or **laicization** for deliberate removal of clerical control.

The strongest synthesis treats secularization as a family of contingent processes. Mechanisms included state building, confiscation, religious competition, migration, class conflict, changing gender norms, mass education, scientific authority, consumer culture, and generational transmission. Each mechanism requires evidence; none should be smuggled in under “modernity.” Periodization is likewise regional. The French revolutionary rupture, the mid-nineteenth-century urban problem, the Kulturkampf, and the pre-1914 socialist milieu were not versions of one event.

By 1914, Europe was less confessionally monopolized than in 1750, and religious authority faced far more organized rivals. That is a meaningful transformation. But most Europeans still encountered religion through identity, rites, education, welfare, moral controversy, or the state. The continent had not completed a scheduled passage from sacred to secular. It had generated a new and unstable field in which churches, states, parties, experts, families, and individuals renegotiated what religion was allowed to govern.

## Why it matters

Secularization is a test case for historical explanation. If belief, practice, identity, authority, and institutions are collapsed, evidence that one changed becomes proof that all declined. If Europe is treated as a single civilization, French anticlerical legislation can stand in for Polish Catholic nationalism, British denominational competition, or Balkan Orthodox nation-building. If 1914 is read through later experience, the much sharper religious changes of the late twentieth century appear inevitable.

Disaggregation produces a better account of [[Nation-state formation in nineteenth-century Europe]], class politics, gender, welfare, education, empire, and minority emancipation. It also prevents two mirror-image myths: that modernity naturally destroys religion, or that every revival disproves secularization. Both mistakes substitute a verdict for causal history.

## Open questions

- Which local series before 1914 permit genuinely comparable measures of attendance, communion, and life-cycle rites across confessions?
- How far did women’s disproportionate participation preserve household transmission while masking male detachment in aggregate institutional records?
- When did religious identity become national or ethnic enough to survive the decline of doctrine and practice, especially among Jews, Catholics, Orthodox Christians, and Muslims?
- Can differentiation be measured without assuming that transfer from church to state necessarily reduced the religious character of public power?
- How should colonial missions and Europe’s imperial encounters be incorporated into an account framed here mainly around change within Europe?
