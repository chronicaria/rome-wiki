“Byzantium” is a modern label for a medieval state whose inhabitants called themselves Romans, making continuity with Rome a historical claim rather than a semantic curiosity.

Up: [[European history (MOC)]]

## Continuity through transformation

Constantinople remained an imperial capital after western emperorship ended, and its rulers sustained Roman law, taxation, diplomacy, armies, and universal claims. Greek increasingly dominated administration and culture, Christianity structured imperial legitimacy, and seventh-century territorial losses changed the state's scale. None of those transformations made its people stop claiming Roman identity.

This case warns against defining medieval Europe as Latin western Europe. Between roughly 700 and 900, Byzantium and Frankish realms shared late-Roman and Christian inheritances while exchanging people, objects, diplomatic claims, and religious practices. Similarity can reflect common inheritance as well as fresh “influence,” so each borrowing claim needs a mechanism and date.
