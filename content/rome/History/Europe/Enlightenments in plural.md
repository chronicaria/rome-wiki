The European Enlightenment was not a single Parisian doctrine but a family of overlapping projects that used criticism, useful knowledge, sociability, and reform in locally different confessional, commercial, imperial, and provincial settings.

Up: [[European history 500-1914]]

## A category, not a command center

“The Enlightenment” is useful if it names a historical problem rather than a unified party. From the later seventeenth century through the French Revolution—and, in some regions, into the early nineteenth century—writers, clergy, officials, merchants, physicians, teachers, and readers asked how inherited authority might answer to reason, experience, utility, and a widening public. They did not agree on what reason required. Voltaire’s anticlerical wit, Immanuel Kant’s defense of public reasoning, Adam Smith’s political economy, Cesare Beccaria’s penal reform, and Catherine II’s legislative language belonged to intersecting but nonidentical conversations.

The plural **Enlightenments** corrects three distortions. First, it shifts attention beyond Paris to Edinburgh, Naples, Milan, Göttingen, Halle, Geneva, Warsaw, Vilnius, Saint Petersburg, Moscow, provincial academies, and commercial ports. Second, it prevents “reason” from being equated automatically with unbelief: many reformers pursued a Christian, Jewish, Catholic, Protestant, Orthodox, or natural-theological enlightenment. Third, it places ideas inside institutions—churches, universities, bureaucracies, publishing markets, salons, lodges, learned societies, courts, and households—that determined who could speak and what reform could do.

Plural does not mean that every eighteenth-century reform was an Enlightenment or that no common pattern existed. A defensible family resemblance joins recurrent commitments: criticism of arbitrary authority; comparison across societies; empirical or historical inquiry; improvement through education and law; circulation of knowledge; and an appeal to a public capable of judgment. These practices traveled through translations, correspondence, reviews, encyclopedias, diplomatic service, migration, and commerce. Their mobility created a European field, but reception changed them at every stop.

## Public reason had material infrastructure

Kant’s 1784 answer to “What is Enlightenment?” supplied the memorable injunction to dare to know. Yet his distinction between the public use of reason by a scholar and the private duties of an officeholder reveals that enlightenment could coexist with hierarchy and obedience. It was not simply free thought escaping the state. It depended on licensed printers, postal routes, booksellers, periodicals, universities, patrons, and rulers willing to tolerate some criticism. Censors could suppress a work while courts recruited its author; clandestine books crossed the same borders that states tried to police.

Print enlarged audiences, but the “public sphere” was neither universally open nor confined to print. Coffeehouses, taverns, salons, Masonic lodges, reading societies, theaters, academies, churches, and lecture halls made judgment social. A newspaper reader in a provincial town could participate in disputes originating elsewhere; a salon hostess could shape reputations despite exclusion from universities; a preacher could translate philosophical ethics into a confessional vocabulary. Oral conversation, manuscript circulation, and sermons remained important beside printed books.

The [[The printing press and uneven information change|printing revolution]] therefore had an eighteenth-century sequel rather than an automatic culmination. Literacy, purchasing power, language, censorship, and transport remained uneven. French served many cosmopolitan elites, but German, Italian, Polish, Russian, Dutch, English, Latin, and other languages supported distinct publics. Translation was creative selection: editors abridged, domesticated, refuted, or Christianized foreign texts. “Diffusion” from a western center misses these acts of local production.

Commercial expansion helped make knowledge a commodity. Publishers calculated markets; authors cultivated readers; periodicals accelerated controversy; subscription libraries and commercial lectures sold access. Political economy turned population, agriculture, taxation, trade, luxury, and poverty into objects of systematic debate. Smith’s *Wealth of Nations* criticized mercantilist restrictions, yet “commercial society” was not a consensus ideal. Thinkers disputed whether commerce softened manners and promoted peace or instead produced dependency, corruption, luxury, empire, and war. Enlightened criticism grew with commercial capitalism while also furnishing languages for criticizing it.

## Religious enlightenments

The sharp French conflict between philosophes and a privileged Catholic Church made anticlericalism highly visible, but it is a poor template for all Europe. Even in France, arguments ranged from atheistic materialism through deism to reforming Catholicism. Elsewhere clergy were often authors, teachers, scientists, administrators, and conduits of new knowledge. Natural theology treated the ordered universe as evidence of divine reason. Biblical criticism applied philology and history to sacred texts. Sermons praised usefulness, sociability, toleration, and moral improvement.

In Protestant lands, Pietism and enlightenment could oppose each other, but they also shared investments in literacy, pedagogy, disciplined conduct, and practical reform. Halle’s institutions linked devotional renewal to education and global missionary knowledge. German **Aufklärung** included disputes over revelation, conscience, ecclesiastical authority, and the limits of public criticism; it cannot be reduced to French irreligion. In Scotland, ministers and university professors made moral philosophy, history, political economy, and natural religion parts of a connected inquiry into sociability and improvement.

Catholic Enlightenments likewise developed within religious institutions. Italian and Habsburg reformers attacked superstition, excessive clerical privilege, or papal jurisdiction without necessarily rejecting doctrine. Some promoted a disciplined, useful clergy and stronger episcopal or state control. The Bourbon and Habsburg monarchies reorganized education, religious orders, poor relief, and church-state relations. These projects could expand schooling or rationalize administration while coercing communities and subordinating the church to dynastic power.

Jewish Enlightenment, or **Haskalah**, emerged within this wider field but posed distinct questions about communal authority, language, education, civil status, and participation in European society. Moses Mendelssohn defended religious liberty and distinguished persuasion from coercive ecclesiastical power. Maskilim promoted new curricula and engagement with surrounding cultures, though their projects varied by city and later moved eastward. Haskalah should not be treated as a mere Jewish copy of a Christian movement: it negotiated both exclusion by states and disagreements within Jewish communities.

Orthodox environments also generated selective enlightenments. In Russia and southeastern Europe, clerics, academies, translators, and administrators mediated western European natural law, science, pedagogy, and classical learning. Reform did not require a secular public identical to London or Paris. It could be framed as strengthening an Orthodox monarchy, educating clergy, or improving subjects. The relevant comparison is not presence versus absence, but which institutions authorized inquiry and which boundaries they defended.

## Provincial and eastern-European variants

The map changes when “provincial” is treated as a position in networks rather than a synonym for backwardness. Edinburgh was provincial relative to London but became a major center through universities, clubs, publishing, law, and medicine. Naples and Milan formed reform cultures around jurisprudence, administration, academies, and political economy. Beccaria’s 1764 *On Crimes and Punishments*, produced in the Milanese circle around the journal *Il Caffè*, condemned torture and capital punishment through arguments about utility, proportionality, and legitimate sovereignty. Its rapid translation shows a provincial center changing the European agenda.

German-speaking Europe was politically fragmented, and that fragmentation multiplied universities, courts, journals, and competing patrons. Göttingen’s scholarship, Halle’s religious and pedagogical institutions, Berlin’s academy and press, and smaller courts formed connected but distinct ecologies. No single German capital monopolized enlightenment. Territorial rulers sometimes protected intellectual competition; they also censored it. The same bureaucracy that collected statistics or reformed schooling could intensify surveillance and conscription.

The [[The Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth as negotiated state|Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth]] produced enlightenment through noble republican debate, ecclesiastical reform, schools, periodicals, theater, and projects of constitutional repair. The Commission of National Education, established in 1773 after the suppression of the Jesuits, is often described as Europe’s first national education ministry. The Constitution of 3 May 1791 sought to strengthen government while preserving a political nation structured by estate. These reforms were not belated copies of France. They answered the Commonwealth’s own problems of elective monarchy, liberum veto, confessional plurality, magnate power, and intervention by neighboring empires. Their setting also exposes the violence of geopolitics: Russia, Prussia, and Austria partitioned the polity even as their rulers used languages of enlightened government.

Russia’s court Enlightenment was similarly real and bounded. Catherine II corresponded with philosophes and adapted Montesquieu and Beccaria in her 1767 *Nakaz* (Instruction) to the Legislative Commission. The text rejected torture and spoke the language of law, but Catherine also affirmed autocracy, and the commission did not produce a new code. Provincial administration, schooling, publishing, and learned institutions expanded unevenly; serfdom remained fundamental. The Pugachev rebellion and imperial security narrowed reform. Russia illustrates “enlightened absolutism” less as a stable regime type than as a tension: monarchs sought useful knowledge and regular government without conceding sovereignty to an independent public.

East-central and southeastern Europe contained multiple language zones and imperial jurisdictions. Habsburg reform under Maria Theresa and Joseph II standardized administration, schooling, toleration policy, and ecclesiastical governance across heterogeneous lands. Joseph’s 1781 Patent of Toleration eased restrictions on certain non-Catholic Christians, but toleration remained an administrative grant, not equal citizenship. Greek-speaking intellectuals circulated texts through merchant and diaspora networks linking Ottoman cities, Venice, Vienna, and the Danubian principalities. Such routes complicate a map whose light radiates only west to east.

## Reform, rule, and contradiction

Enlightenment reform usually worked through existing power. Monarchs wanted taxable, healthy, educated, and governable populations. Officials mapped land, counted people, codified law, reorganized poor relief, and promoted agriculture. The language of utility could restrain cruelty, as in campaigns against judicial torture, while also rendering subjects more legible to the state. [[The fiscal-military state and its limits|Fiscal-military competition]] funded academies and surveys as well as armies. Improvement was emancipatory for some and disciplinary for others.

The encyclopedic impulse captured both promise and hierarchy. Diderot and d’Alembert’s *Encyclopédie* organized arts, sciences, and trades as connected human knowledge, challenged inherited intellectual rankings, and depended on skilled craft information. Yet artisans became objects of representation more readily than equal authors in the republic of letters. Academies solicited provincial observations while metropolitan institutions assigned prestige. Claims to universality rested on unequal divisions of intellectual labor.

Revolution was neither the necessary outcome nor an external betrayal. Enlightenment supplied languages—natural rights, public opinion, sovereignty, utility, constitutionalism—that revolutionaries could radicalize. It also supplied arguments for gradual reform, enlightened monarchy, commercial order, and elite tutelage. The American, French, Haitian, Polish, and other revolutionary crises altered how Europeans interpreted the earlier movement. After 1789, defenders and enemies retrospectively made “the Enlightenment” more coherent than it had been.

## Who was outside the enlightened public?

Universal language did not create universal standing. Most formal institutions privileged educated men with money, rank, or professional credentials. Women participated as authors, translators, readers, salon organizers, patrons, and political actors, but universities, academies, offices, and citizenship commonly excluded them. Mary Wollstonecraft’s insistence that women were rational beings exposed the contradiction between universal reason and gendered education. Salon influence should not be romanticized into equality.

Class mattered just as sharply. Commercial venues might admit anyone who could pay, which was not the same as openness. Peasants, domestic servants, urban laborers, soldiers, and the poor often appeared in reform writing as populations to educate, discipline, employ, or count rather than as coauthors of policy. Popular reading and political speech existed, but elite observers frequently labeled autonomous popular action ignorance, fanaticism, or disorder.

Religious toleration was selective. A thinker could oppose persecution while retaining civil disabilities for Jews, dissenters, atheists, or other confessions. States often tolerated worship to attract industrious subjects, not because they recognized an unconditional right. European Jews faced residence restrictions, occupational limits, special taxes, and corporate regulation even while philosophers debated tolerance in abstract terms.

Race, slavery, and empire were constitutive, not peripheral, contradictions. European consumer and publishing cultures were entangled with Atlantic commodities, colonial trade, slavery, and conquest. Classification of humanity could support criticism of slavery and cultural relativism, but natural history also helped systematize racial hierarchy. Colonized and enslaved people were commonly denied membership in the supposedly universal public. The Haitian Revolution would force the most radical practical test of proclaimed universal rights.

The plural approach must therefore avoid becoming an alibi. Expanding the cast beyond famous French men is valuable only if it also maps unequal access and coercion. There were connected Enlightenments because actors translated shared questions into different settings; there were exclusions because institutions defined the “public,” “humanity,” and “improvement” unevenly. Both propositions are necessary.

## A comparative model

Instead of asking whether a region possessed the Enlightenment, compare five variables:

1. **Institutions:** Were universities, churches, courts, academies, lodges, markets, or bureaucracies the principal carriers?
2. **Confessional problem:** Was reform directed against a dominant church, through it, among competing confessions, or within a minority community?
3. **Political relation:** Did writers address a parliament, republican estate, composite monarchy, imperial court, city government, or reading public?
4. **Political economy:** Which commercial, agrarian, colonial, and fiscal structures made “improvement” plausible, and who bore its costs?
5. **Boundaries of membership:** Who could publish, assemble, study, hold office, own property, or count as a rights-bearing person?

This model preserves comparison without installing western capitals as the norm. It also connects intellectual history to [[Serfdom's divergent paths in eastern and western Europe|agrarian coercion]], confession, commerce, state-building, and empire. Enlightenment becomes neither a set of timeless ideals nor a cynical mask, but a contested European practice whose emancipatory claims and governing ambitions developed together.

## Open questions

- How did vernacular translation networks alter specific concepts such as “public,” “civilization,” and “toleration” between French, German, Polish, Russian, Greek, and other languages?
- Which provincial reading societies included artisans, women, or religious minorities in practice, rather than only claiming an abstract public?
- How differently did Haskalah develop in Berlin, Galicia, Lithuania, and the Russian Empire, and where does one periodize its eastward shift?
- Can the Ottoman and Greek-speaking reform worlds be compared with Russian and Habsburg enlightenments without treating Christian Europe as a sealed civilizational unit?
- Which reforms measurably reduced coercion, and which mainly increased the administrative reach of dynastic states?
