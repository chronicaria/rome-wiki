The centuries around 500 were an uneven regional transformation of Roman institutions, economies, religions, and identities—not one synchronized “fall” into a uniform medieval order.

Up: [[European history (MOC)]]

## What changed, and where

Imperial rule disappeared in the western provinces during the fifth century, but successor kings reused Roman cities, law, taxation, titles, Christianity, and elite culture in different combinations. Material contraction and political fragmentation were severe in some western regions without erasing exchange or every institution. In the eastern Mediterranean, the polity centered on Constantinople remained Roman in its own self-understanding and preserved an imperial fiscal and administrative scale, even as seventh-century war and territorial loss transformed it.

The useful question is therefore not whether “Rome fell” once, but which capacities survived, who controlled them, and how conquest, Christian institutions, rural production, settlement, and long-distance exchange changed region by region. This also prevents [[Feudalism debate]] from becoming a universal explanation for the whole post-Roman world.

## Place in the map

Compare [[Byzantium as a continuing Roman polity]] with [[Carolingian rule as negotiated empire]] before attempting a Europe-wide synthesis. [[Medieval Iberia was a constitutive European world]] supplies a third case in which Roman, Christian, Islamic, and local inheritances recombined.
