[[Gwanghaegun]] was neither a heroic reform king nor a mere tyrant: he was a capable crisis ruler whose foreign-policy realism was stronger than his domestic politics.

The best case for Gwanghaegun is strategic. After the [[Imjin War]], [[Joseon]] was exhausted, [[Ming dynasty|Ming]] was still morally prestigious but weakening, and the rising [[Later Jin]]/Manchu state was becoming too strong to ignore. Gwanghaegun tried to keep Joseon from being dragged into a Ming-Manchu war it could not win. A National Institute of Korean History source on his 1618 diplomacy shows him arguing that Joseon lacked the military capacity for rash offensive action and should strengthen frontier defense instead of committing deeply to Ming demands.

That policy looks better because [[Injo]] and the Westerners reversed it after the [[Injo Banjeong]] of 1623. Their anti-Gwanghae legitimacy depended heavily on "pro-Ming, anti-Later Jin" politics. Our History Net's explanation of the coup says this posture became a core foreign-policy line after the coup, partly because Injo's regime needed Ming recognition and wanted to distinguish itself from Gwanghaegun. This helps explain why Injo's reign stumbled into the 1627 Later Jin invasion and the 1636 Qing invasion.

But Injo's incompetence does not make Gwanghaegun innocent. Gwanghaegun's domestic rule gave his enemies real material to work with:

- He ruled from weak legitimacy: a concubine-born king with rival royal males around him.
- His court became entangled with the Greater Northerners and repeated purge politics.
- Grand Prince Yeongchang was killed, Queen Inmok was confined, and the rhetoric of "deposing the mother and killing the brother" became politically devastating.
- Large palace and construction projects placed real strain on a postwar society.
- His own court management seems to have isolated him badly enough that the 1623 coup succeeded with shockingly small force.

So the right judgment is asymmetric. On foreign policy, Gwanghaegun was probably the better reader of Joseon's actual power position. On domestic politics, he failed to convert that realism into durable legitimacy. He understood the external balance better than his opponents, but he did not manage the internal balance well enough to survive.

The exile to [[Jeju-do]] was therefore not simply the punishment of a monster. It was the political disposal of a defeated king whose existence remained dangerous to the new regime. From Injo's perspective, keeping him alive but isolated avoided the ugliness and risk of royal execution while preventing him from becoming an active center of restoration. From a historical-ethical perspective, the exile feels excessive because many of the charges against him were written by the victors, and later events made his foreign policy look prescient.

See also [[Gwanghaegun's exile and Sado's death]] for why Gwanghaegun's exile did not create the same succession problem as [[Crown Prince Sado]]'s survival.

My verdict: Gwanghaegun deserves partial rehabilitation, not sainthood. He should be remembered as a hard, insecure, often ruthless king who nevertheless grasped the geopolitical problem better than the regime that overthrew him.

- How much of Injo's disastrous foreign policy was personal incompetence versus the structural need to legitimize the coup?
