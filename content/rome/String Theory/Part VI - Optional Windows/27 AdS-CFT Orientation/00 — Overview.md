AdS/CFT says a theory of gravity living in a curved 5D "bulk" is *the same theory* as an ordinary quantum field theory (no gravity) living on that bulk's 4D "boundary" — a hologram, where the extra dimension is stored as information on the edge.

## The hologram picture

Picture a solid glass paperweight with a 3D image floating inside it. All the information for that 3D image is actually etched on the 2D surface — that is a **hologram**: a lower-dimensional surface encoding one dimension more. AdS/CFT is that statement made exact for a piece of physics. The floating 3D image is *gravity* (curved spacetime, strings, black holes) in a 5D world called the **bulk**. The etched surface is a *quantum field theory with no gravity at all* living on the 4D **boundary** of that world. Every question you can ask on one side has an exact translation to the other.

Why anyone would believe two such different-looking theories are identical: they turn out to be **two descriptions of the same physical object** — a stack of $N$ D3-branes. Look at the branes one way and you see a gauge theory; look at the same branes another way and you see gravity in a curved throat. Same object, two languages. The rest of this module makes each phrase precise.

The slogan is *"gravity in the bulk is dual to a field theory on the boundary."* By the end of the module you should be able to say exactly what sits on each side, what "dual" means, and — just as important — what this does **not** claim.

## The two sides, named precisely

$$
\underbrace{\text{type IIB closed strings (gravity) on } \mathrm{AdS}_5\times S^5}_{\text{BULK, 10-dimensional}}
\;\;=\;\;
\underbrace{\mathcal{N}=4 \text{ supersymmetric } SU(N) \text{ Yang–Mills}}_{\text{BOUNDARY, 4-dimensional, no gravity}}
$$

- **Bulk.** Closed superstrings — which contain gravitons, hence gravity — moving on the ten-dimensional geometry $\mathrm{AdS}_5\times S^5$. Here $\mathrm{AdS}_5$ is a five-dimensional space of constant negative curvature (27.1 [[Anti-de Sitter geometry]]) and $S^5$ is a five-sphere of the same radius $R$.
- **Boundary.** A four-dimensional gauge theory — $\mathcal{N}=4$ super-Yang–Mills with gauge group $SU(N)$ — living on the 4D flat conformal boundary of $\mathrm{AdS}_5$ (27.2 [[Conformal boundary]]). It is a **conformal** field theory: it has no preferred length scale, because its coupling does not run. Critically, **it contains no gravity.**

The dimension count is the holography. Set the compact $S^5$ aside — it supplies the internal R-symmetry, not the holographic direction — and compare $\mathrm{AdS}_5$ with its boundary: five bulk dimensions against four. The one extra is the radial direction of $\mathrm{AdS}_5$, and on the boundary it reappears not as space but as **energy scale** (27.1 [[Anti-de Sitter geometry]]).

## Why believe it — the two pillars

This is a **conjecture**, not a theorem. It has never been proven; it rests on two independent pieces of evidence.

**1. Symmetry match.** The conformal symmetry of the 4D boundary theory has $15$ generators — $6$ Lorentz transformations ($3$ rotations $+\,3$ boosts) $+\,4$ translations $+\,1$ scale (dilatation) $+\,4$ special conformal — closing into the algebra $\mathfrak{so}(4,2)$. The **isometries** (distance-preserving maps) of the 5D bulk $\mathrm{AdS}_5$ are generated by $15$ operators satisfying *the same algebra*. Independently, the isometries of the $S^5$ form $SO(6)$, which shows up on the boundary as the R-symmetry rotating $\mathcal{N}=4$ SYM's scalars and fermions. The bulk *geometry* already encodes the boundary theory's *symmetry* — developed in 27.2 [[Conformal boundary]].

**2. D3-brane decoupling — one object, two descriptions.** Take $N$ coincident D3-branes and a low-energy limit $E \ll 1/\sqrt{\alpha'}$ two ways:

- *As a gauge theory:* open strings ending on the branes give $SU(N)$ Yang–Mills on the 4D worldvolume, and gravity switches off because the 10D Newton constant $G^{(10)}\sim g_s^2(\alpha')^4 \to 0$.
- *As curved spacetime:* the branes carry energy and charge, so they bend spacetime into an infinite throat; low-energy physics survives only deep in the throat (the near-horizon region), whose geometry is exactly $\mathrm{AdS}_5\times S^5$.

Both descriptions leave behind the same free closed strings in flat space. Equating what remains gives $SU(N)$ Yang–Mills $=$ IIB strings on $\mathrm{AdS}_5\times S^5$. The full argument is 27.3 [[Gauge-gravity duality]].

## The dictionary and why the duality is useful but hard to test

Each side has two dimensionless parameters, related by

$$
\boxed{\;g_s = g_{YM}^2 \qquad\text{and}\qquad \frac{R^4}{\alpha'^2} = g_{YM}^2\,N \equiv \lambda\;}\tag{1}
$$

where $g_s$ is the closed-string coupling, $g_{YM}$ the gauge coupling, and $\lambda = g_{YM}^2 N$ is the **'t Hooft coupling**. From (1), $R/\sqrt{\alpha'} = \lambda^{1/4}$: large $\lambda$ means a large, gently curved AdS.

This dictionary is exactly what makes the duality *powerful* and *hard to check* at once:

- **Gravity is easy when $\lambda$ is large** — the geometry is weakly curved, so classical supergravity approximates the strings well.
- **Gauge theory is easy when $\lambda$ is small** — you can expand in perturbation theory.

The two easy regimes **never overlap**. So the duality is a machine for computing *strongly*-coupled gauge theory (hopeless directly) by doing *classical gravity* — but for the same reason a head-to-head check is rarely possible. Supersymmetry rescues testability by supplying **protected observables**, quantities independent of $\lambda$ that can be computed at weak coupling and matched against the gravity prediction. Several such matches have succeeded. The 't Hooft limit and its genus expansion are in 27.4 [[Large-N gauge theory]]; the observable-by-observable map is in 27.5 [[Bulk versus boundary observables]].

## What you can and cannot claim

**Do claim, cleanly:**
- Both sides by name, and that the boundary has *no gravity*.
- "Dual" $=$ the same physics in two languages, computable in *opposite* regimes.
- The dictionary (1), and that large $\lambda \Leftrightarrow$ weakly curved geometry $\Leftrightarrow$ supergravity reliable.
- It is holographic: 5D bulk gravity is stored on a 4D non-gravitational theory, echoing black-hole entropy scaling with **area, not volume** ([[Entropy-area relation]]). The extra radial dimension reads on the boundary as an energy scale (RG flow).

**Do not overclaim:**
- It is **not proven** — it is a well-tested conjecture.
- The sharp case has **maximal supersymmetry** and is conformal, so it is **not QCD**: no string description of the real strong force follows from it yet.
- Anything quantitative (actual correlators) needs the GKP–Witten dictionary and QFT/GR machinery this module does not build.

## Exercise

Using only the dictionary (1), verify the module's central tractability claim: *the regime where classical gravity is a good description is the regime where the gauge theory is strongly coupled.* Concretely, (a) express $R/\sqrt{\alpha'}$ in terms of $\lambda$, (b) argue which range of $\lambda$ makes the AdS geometry weakly curved, and (c) conclude that "gravity easy" and "gauge theory easy" cannot hold at once. Then (d) show that at fixed $\lambda$, taking $N\to\infty$ sends $g_s\to 0$, so the bulk becomes classical in the string-loop sense.

> [!success]- Solution
> **(a)** The second relation in (1) is $R^4/\alpha'^2 = \lambda$. Take the fourth root:
> $$\left(\frac{R^2}{\alpha'}\right)^{2}=\lambda \;\Rightarrow\; \frac{R^2}{\alpha'}=\sqrt{\lambda}\;\Rightarrow\; \frac{R}{\sqrt{\alpha'}}=\lambda^{1/4}.$$
> **(b)** Curvature scales are set by $R$ measured in string units $\sqrt{\alpha'}$. The geometry is *weakly curved* (radius of curvature $\gg$ string length) when $R/\sqrt{\alpha'}\gg 1$, i.e. $\lambda^{1/4}\gg 1$, i.e. $\lambda \gg 1$. In that regime the massive string states are heavy compared to the curvature scale and classical **supergravity** approximates the full string theory — gravity is the *easy* description. (Equivalently, the IIB string expansion parameter is $\alpha'^2/R^4 = 1/\lambda \to 0$.)
> **(c)** The gauge theory is tractable by perturbation theory when its effective coupling is small, i.e. $\lambda = g_{YM}^2 N \ll 1$. But (b) needs $\lambda \gg 1$. A single value of $\lambda$ cannot be both $\gg 1$ and $\ll 1$, so the two "easy" regimes are disjoint — you can almost never compute the same quantity easily on both sides. This is exactly why the duality is a *tool* (do hard gauge theory via easy gravity) rather than something checkable head-to-head, and why supersymmetry-protected, $\lambda$-independent observables are needed to test it.
> **(d)** Hold $\lambda$ fixed and use the first relation $g_s = g_{YM}^2 = \lambda/N$. As $N\to\infty$ with $\lambda$ fixed, $g_s = \lambda/N \to 0$. Since $g_s$ is the string-loop (genus) expansion parameter, the bulk becomes classical: at leading order it is *classical* supergravity, and $1/N$ corrections are bulk quantum/loop corrections. So the cleanest AdS/CFT statement — classical gravity $\leftrightarrow$ planar, strongly-coupled $\mathcal{N}=4$ SYM — lives at $N\to\infty$, $\lambda\gg 1$, both simplifications flowing from the same limit.
