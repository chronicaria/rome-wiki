A conserved charge, promoted to a quantum operator, generates by commutation the very symmetry it came from: momenta $p^\mu$ generate translations, and the Lorentz charges $M^{\mu\nu}=x^\mu p^\nu-x^\nu p^\mu$ generate rotations and boosts and close into the Lorentz algebra. Running this test for the particle rehearses the one calculation that later kills or saves the string.

## Charge = generator

Quantum mechanics has already shown you one instance: angular momentum $L_z$ generates rotations — sandwich a state with $e^{-i\theta L_z}$ and it comes out rotated. This is universal. Every continuous symmetry has a Noether charge, and once that charge is an operator, commuting with it applies an infinitesimal version of the symmetry:

$$ \delta(\text{anything}) = [\,iG,\;\text{anything}\,]. \tag{1}$$

The charge and the generator are one object. Momentum generates translations; the Lorentz charge generates Lorentz transformations. So "check that $p^\mu$ generates translations" is not busywork — it verifies that the operator built from the quantum theory still does the geometric job its classical ancestor did.

Why bother for a single point particle? Because the whole question funnels into a Lie-algebra closure test, and the particle passes it trivially. Watching it pass — seeing exactly which commutator carries the danger — is what lets you recognize, in Modules 14 and 16, the moment the string version fails unless spacetime is 26-dimensional. This note is the control experiment.

In applied-math terms, the charges give a representation of the Poincaré Lie algebra on the operator algebra. Verifying brackets like $[M^{\mu\nu},M^{\rho\sigma}]$ checks that the alleged symmetry operators satisfy the defining relations of the group; if they don't, the "symmetry" was a fiction.

One convention in (1) needs pinning down. For a translation with parameter $\epsilon^\mu$ the generator is $G=\epsilon_\rho p^\rho$. For a Lorentz transformation the parameter $\epsilon^{\mu\nu}$ is antisymmetric, so the generator is $G=-\tfrac12\epsilon_{\rho\sigma}M^{\rho\sigma}$ and (1) reads $\delta x^\mu=[-\tfrac{i}{2}\epsilon_{\rho\sigma}M^{\rho\sigma},x^\mu]$. The factor $\tfrac12$ avoids double-counting the antisymmetric pair.

## Momentum generates translations

The Lagrangian $L=-m\sqrt{-\dot x^2}$ (mostly-plus signature, $\hbar=c=1$) sees only $\dot x^\mu$, never $x^\mu$, so it is invariant under a constant shift
$$ \delta x^\mu(\tau)=\epsilon^\mu. \tag{2}$$
Noether hands us the momentum $p_\mu$ as the charge. Now test that $iG=i\epsilon_\rho p^\rho$ regenerates (2), using $[x^\mu,p^\rho]=i\eta^{\mu\rho}$:
$$ \delta x^\mu=\big[\,i\epsilon_\rho p^\rho,\,x^\mu\,\big]=i\epsilon_\rho\,[p^\rho,x^\mu]=i\epsilon_\rho\,(-i\eta^{\rho\mu})=\epsilon^\mu.\ \checkmark $$
The commutator returns exactly the shift $\epsilon^\mu$: the charge is the generator. This used the covariant commutator; in light-cone gauge one component misbehaves, which is the next order of business.

### The light-cone twist: $p^-$ drags the gauge with it

In light-cone gauge the mass shell has already been solved, so $p^-=\tfrac{1}{2p^+}(p^Ip^I+m^2)$ is no longer independent — it is a function of $p^+$ and the transverse $p^I$. Split the generator into light-cone pieces:
$$ i\epsilon_\rho p^\rho=-i\epsilon^- p^+ - i\epsilon^+ p^- + i\epsilon^I p^I. $$
The transverse shifts ($\epsilon^I$) and the $\epsilon^-$ shift behave as expected. The dangerous piece is the one generated by $p^-$ (set $\epsilon^+\neq0$). It should translate $x^+$; instead
$$ \delta x^+=0,\qquad \delta x^I=-\epsilon^+\frac{p^I}{p^+},\qquad \delta x^-=-\epsilon^+\frac{p^-}{p^+}. $$
The coordinate that should move sits still, and coordinates that should sit still move. What happened?

The resolution is that $p^-$ generates a translation *and* a reparameterization of the worldline. A reparameterization $\tau\to\tau+\lambda(\tau)$ shifts every coordinate by $\delta x^\mu=\lambda\,\partial_\tau x^\mu$, and this is a symmetry of the point particle — the action changes only by a total derivative. The physical logic: a bare shift $\delta x^+=\epsilon^+$ would ruin the gauge condition $x^+=p^+\tau/m^2$ of [[Light-cone Hamiltonian]], which pins $x^+$ to $\tau$. So $p^-$ must supply a compensating reparameterization that restores the gauge. Demand that translation plus reparameterization leave $x^+$ in its gauge form:
$$ 0=\epsilon^+ + \lambda\,\partial_\tau x^+=\epsilon^+ + \lambda\frac{p^+}{m^2}\quad\Rightarrow\quad \lambda=-\frac{m^2}{p^+}\,\epsilon^+. $$
Feed this constant $\lambda$ into $\delta x^I=\lambda\,\partial_\tau x^I$ and $\delta x^-=\lambda\,\partial_\tau x^-$ and the anomalous motions above come back exactly.

> Fixing a gauge gives the generators extra structure: $p^-$ cannot just translate, it must translate and re-glue the gauge, and the extra motion is the receipt for the gauge choice. The string does the same thing on a larger scale — gauge-fixed generators acquire oscillator tails, and Lorentz invariance stops being automatic.

The only new input is the commutator $[x_0^-,p^-]$, nonzero purely because $p^-$ depends on $p^+$. From $[x_0^-,p^+]=-i$, i.e. $[p^+,x_0^-]=+i$,
$$ \Big[x_0^-,\frac{1}{p^+}\Big]=\frac{1}{p^+}\,[p^+,x_0^-]\,\frac{1}{p^+}=\frac{1}{p^+}\,(+i)\,\frac{1}{p^+}=\frac{i}{p^{+2}}, $$
$$ [\,x_0^-,\,p^-\,]=\Big[x_0^-,\tfrac{1}{2p^+}(p^Ip^I+m^2)\Big]=\tfrac12(p^Ip^I+m^2)\,\frac{i}{p^{+2}}=i\,\frac{p^-}{p^+}. $$
Nonzero, and proportional to $p^-/p^+$ — a direct consequence of the longitudinal sign $[x_0^-,p^+]=-i$ flagged in [[Canonical commutators]]. Track that sign; it propagates.

## Lorentz charges and their algebra

The particle action is Lorentz invariant: under $\delta x^\mu=\epsilon^{\mu\nu}x_\nu$ with $\epsilon^{\mu\nu}=-\epsilon^{\nu\mu}$, Noether gives the charges
$$ \boxed{\;M^{\mu\nu}=x^\mu p^\nu - x^\nu p^\mu\;}\tag{3}$$
This is orbital angular momentum, relativistically completed: the spatial $M^{ij}$ are ordinary angular momenta, the mixed $M^{0i}$ are boosts. It is Hermitian — rearranging $xp$ back to $px$ produces two c-number pieces that cancel — as any generator of a unitary symmetry must be.

These charges generate Lorentz transformations. The one commutator needed is
$$ [M^{\mu\nu},x^\rho]=x^\mu[p^\nu,x^\rho]-x^\nu[p^\mu,x^\rho]=i\eta^{\mu\rho}x^\nu-i\eta^{\nu\rho}x^\mu, \tag{4}$$
which says the generator rotates/boosts $x^\rho$ into the two coordinates whose labels sit on $M^{\mu\nu}$. Then
$$ \delta x^\rho=\Big[-\tfrac{i}{2}\epsilon_{\mu\nu}M^{\mu\nu},\,x^\rho\Big]=\tfrac12\epsilon_{\mu\nu}\big(\eta^{\mu\rho}x^\nu-\eta^{\nu\rho}x^\mu\big)=\epsilon^{\rho\nu}x_\nu,\ \checkmark $$
recovering the transformation we started from. The identical computation on momentum gives
$$ [M^{\mu\nu},p^\rho]=i\eta^{\mu\rho}p^\nu-i\eta^{\nu\rho}p^\mu, \tag{5}$$
so $p^\rho$ is a Lorentz vector too — the missing ingredient for the algebra.

### The Lorentz algebra — the payoff

Now compute the bracket of two Lorentz generators. Expand $[M^{\mu\nu},x^\rho p^\sigma-x^\sigma p^\rho]$ and feed in (4)–(5):
$$
\begin{aligned}
[M^{\mu\nu},M^{\rho\sigma}]
&=[M^{\mu\nu},x^\rho]\,p^\sigma+x^\rho[M^{\mu\nu},p^\sigma]-[M^{\mu\nu},x^\sigma]\,p^\rho-x^\sigma[M^{\mu\nu},p^\rho].
\end{aligned}
$$
Each of the four terms produces a piece of the form $\eta\,(x^\kappa p^\lambda-x^\lambda p^\kappa)=\eta\,M^{\kappa\lambda}$; collecting them gives the closed relation
$$ \boxed{\;[M^{\mu\nu},M^{\rho\sigma}]=i\eta^{\mu\rho}M^{\nu\sigma}-i\eta^{\nu\rho}M^{\mu\sigma}+i\eta^{\mu\sigma}M^{\rho\nu}-i\eta^{\nu\sigma}M^{\rho\mu}\;}\tag{6}$$

This is the defining relation of the Lorentz Lie algebra, and it is the headline of the whole module:

> Any Lorentz-invariant quantum theory must possess operators $M^{\mu\nu}$ obeying (6); if you cannot build them, the theory is not Lorentz invariant. For the point particle this comes free — $M^{\mu\nu}$ is a simple bilinear and $m^2$ is just a number. For the light-cone string, closure of one specific bracket, $[M^{-I},M^{-J}]=0$, fails unless the spacetime dimension is $D=26$ and the normal-ordering constant is $a=-1$. That is where the number 26 is born.

To read off (6): each $\eta$ carries one index from each generator, and antisymmetry in $\mu\!\leftrightarrow\!\nu$ and $\rho\!\leftrightarrow\!\sigma$ then fixes all four terms and their signs. Two consequences in light-cone coordinates:
$$ [M^{+-},M^{+I}]=iM^{+I},\qquad [M^{-I},M^{-J}]=0. $$
The first uses $\eta^{-+}=-1$, the only nonzero $\eta$ available. The second is automatic for the covariant charges: the only $\eta$ on offer would force an $M^{--}$, which vanishes by antisymmetry. It is the light-cone-*gauge* version of this bracket that bites the string, so we watch the particle pass it.

## Light-cone gauge generators and the closure test

The covariant formula (3) still guides the construction, but now $x^+$, $x^-$, $p^-$ are dependent operators. Substitute the gauge solution $x^+=\tfrac{p^+}{m^2}\tau$, $x^-=x_0^-+\tfrac{p^-}{m^2}\tau$, $x^I=x_0^I+\tfrac{p^I}{m^2}\tau$. The explicit $\tau$ terms cancel (as they must for conserved charges), leaving the easy pieces
$$ M^{IJ}=x_0^Ip^J-x_0^Jp^I,\qquad M^{+I}=-x_0^Ip^+. $$

For $M^{+-}$ the naive answer $-x_0^-p^+$ is not Hermitian, because $x_0^-$ and $p^+$ do not commute. Gauge fixing has made Hermiticity non-automatic; the fix is to symmetrize:
$$ M^{+-}=-\tfrac12\big(x_0^-p^+ + p^+x_0^-\big). $$
This is not cosmetic — a Lorentz generator is an observable, so it must be Hermitian.

The dangerous generator is $M^{-I}$, the light-cone analog of the string's deadly boost. Symmetrization is again needed, since $[x_0^I,p^-]=i\,p^I/p^+\neq0$:
$$ \boxed{\;M^{-I}=x_0^-p^I-\tfrac12\big(x_0^Ip^-+p^-x_0^I\big)\;}\tag{7}$$
The symmetrized piece is cleanest in momentum space, where $x_0^I=i\,\partial/\partial p^I$. For any operator built as $\tfrac12(x_0^IK+Kx_0^I)$ with $K$ a function of momenta,
$$ \tfrac12\big(x_0^IK+Kx_0^I\big)=iK\frac{\partial}{\partial p^I}+\frac{i}{2}\frac{\partial K}{\partial p^I},\qquad K=p^-=\frac{p^Jp^J+m^2}{2p^+}. $$
Since $\partial K/\partial p^I=p^I/p^+$, the symmetrization adds exactly $\frac{i}{2}\,\frac{p^I}{p^+}$; equivalently, $M^{-I}=(x_0^-p^I-x_0^Ip^-)+\frac{i}{2}\frac{p^I}{p^+}$. That extra term is what Hermiticity demands, and the exercise below shows that closure never sees it.

Now the test: does $[M^{-I},M^{-J}]=0$? Work in the momentum representation, where $x_0^-=-i\,\partial/\partial p^+$ and $x_0^I=i\,\partial/\partial p^I$. The generator (7) acts as
$$ M^{-I}=-i\Big(\underbrace{p^I\frac{\partial}{\partial p^+}+K\frac{\partial}{\partial p^I}}_{A_I}+\underbrace{\frac{p^I}{2p^+}}_{b_I}\Big). $$
Split it into a first-order differential operator $A_I$ and a plain multiplication $b_I$. The vector fields commute because $A_I$ annihilates $K$:
$$ A_IK=p^I\Big(-\frac{K}{p^+}\Big)+K\Big(\frac{p^I}{p^+}\Big)=0\quad\Rightarrow\quad [A_I,A_J]=0. $$
Geometrically, $A_I$ is the boost's flow on momentum space, and $K=p^-$ is invariant along it — the covariant algebra promised as much, since (5) gives $[M^{-I},p^-]=0$. The mixed and multiplication terms also cancel, because
$$ A_Ib_J=\frac{K\,\delta^{IJ}}{2p^+}-\frac{p^Ip^J}{2p^{+2}} $$
is symmetric in $I,J$, so it drops out of the antisymmetric commutator, while $[b_I,b_J]=0$ trivially. Therefore
$$ \boxed{\;[M^{-I},M^{-J}]=0\;} $$
for the light-cone-gauge point particle.

> The particle passes the Lorentz test. After the correct Hermitian ordering, the reduced light-cone operators — even the awkward $M^{-I}$ — still realize the full Lorentz algebra, with no constraint on the dimension. The string runs this same calculation with $M^{-I}$ now an infinite oscillator sum: the boost no longer annihilates the operator-valued mass, a normal-ordering anomaly appears, and the zero on the right survives only for $D=26$, $a=-1$. One catastrophic new term separates the two. Remember the shape here and the string result will feel inevitable rather than magical.

## What to carry forward

- **Charge = generator:** $\delta(\text{anything})=[\,iG,\,\text{anything}\,]$.
- $p^\mu\to$ translations (mutually commuting); $M^{\mu\nu}\to$ Lorentz, closing into (6).
- Gauge fixing makes $p^-$ generate translation + reparameterization, and forces Hermitian symmetrization of $M^{+-}$ and $M^{-I}$.
- The particle's light-cone Lorentz algebra closes unconditionally. The string's $[M^{-I},M^{-J}]=0$ is the same bracket — and it is where $D=26$ enters.

## Open questions

- Treating $x_0^-=-i\partial_{p^+}$ as a self-adjoint operator on the half-line $p^+>0$ needs a domain analysis this note skips. The commutators are algebraically correct; rigor belongs with covariant quantization or a dedicated light-cone Hilbert-space note.
- In the string, $M^{-I}$ carries oscillator bilinears and its commutator develops the normal-ordering anomaly. Modules 14 and 16 show why the anomaly cancels only at $D=26$, $a=-1$ — see [[Light-cone Lorentz anomaly]] for the sequel this note is the prequel to.

## Exercise

The closure $[M^{-I},M^{-J}]=0$ rested on two facts about $M^{-I}=-i(A_I+b_I)$: the boost vector field annihilates the energy, $A_IK=0$ (so $[A_I,A_J]=0$), and the multiplication term $A_Ib_J=\frac{K\delta^{IJ}}{2p^+}-\frac{p^Ip^J}{2p^{+2}}$ is symmetric in $I,J$ (so it drops from the antisymmetric bracket). Now suppose a careless student **forgets to symmetrize** and uses the non-Hermitian generator $\widetilde M^{-I}=x_0^-p^I-x_0^Ip^-$ (operators in that order, no $\tfrac12$).

(a) Show that in momentum space $\widetilde M^{-I}=-i(A_I+\tilde b_I)$ with $\tilde b_I=p^I/p^+$ — twice the symmetrized value. (b) Deduce whether $[\widetilde M^{-I},\widetilde M^{-J}]$ still vanishes. (c) In one sentence, if closure survives, what did the student break, and how do we know?

> [!success]- Solution
> **(a)** In momentum space $x_0^-=-i\partial_{p^+}$, $x_0^I=i\partial_{p^I}$, and $p^-=K$ acts by multiplication. Acting on a test function,
> $$ \widetilde M^{-I}f=x_0^-(p^If)-x_0^I(Kf)=-ip^I\partial_{p^+}f-i(\partial_{p^I}K)f-iK\partial_{p^I}f. $$
> With $\partial_{p^I}K=p^I/p^+$ this is $-i\big(A_I+\tfrac{p^I}{p^+}\big)f$, so $\tilde b_I=p^I/p^+=2b_I$. (The extra derivative-of-$K$ term is what symmetrization halves.)
>
> **(b)** Yes, it still vanishes. Write $[\widetilde M^{-I},\widetilde M^{-J}]=-[A_I+\tilde b_I,\,A_J+\tilde b_J]$. The three pieces: $[A_I,A_J]=0$ (still, since $A_IK=0$ is unchanged); $[\tilde b_I,\tilde b_J]=0$ (both are multiplications); and the cross term $A_I\tilde b_J-A_J\tilde b_I$. But $A_I\tilde b_J=2A_Ib_J$ is just twice the *symmetric* matrix above, so its antisymmetric part is zero. All three vanish: $[\widetilde M^{-I},\widetilde M^{-J}]=0$.
>
> **(c)** Closure alone cannot see the error: $\widetilde M^{-I}$ and the correct $M^{-I}$ differ only by the multiplication operator $\tfrac{i}{2}\,p^I/p^+$, and (b) shows that any shift of the multiplication piece proportional to $p^I/p^+$ enters the bracket only through the cross term $A_Ib_J-A_Jb_I$, which the symmetry of $A_Ib_J$ kills. What the student broke is **Hermiticity**: $(\widetilde M^{-I})^\dagger-\widetilde M^{-I}=[x_0^I,p^-]=i\,p^I/p^+\neq0$, so $\widetilde M^{-I}$ is not an observable. For the particle, symmetrization is thus a matter of Hermiticity rather than closure — but it is exactly this ordering choice that turns anomaly-sensitive when $M^{-I}$ becomes an oscillator sum for the string.
