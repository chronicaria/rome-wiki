Gold often becomes more valuable when US real yields or the dollar fall, but neither relationship is a law: gold prices jointly reflect the opportunity cost of holding a non-remunerated asset, currency translation, inflation and tail-risk demand, official-sector purchases, physical supply, and changing risk premia.

## One price, several economic comparisons

Gold is quoted globally in US dollars, but the quoted price is not a single-factor reading of inflation, monetary policy, fear, or dollar strength. It is the clearing price between heterogeneous holders and users: private investors, exchange-traded products, jewellery buyers, manufacturers, miners, recyclers, central banks, and other official institutions. These participants compare gold with different alternatives and operate at different horizons.

The familiar shorthand is that gold rises when US real yields fall. Gold produces no contractual coupon or dividend. A higher return on a default-risk-free inflation-indexed Treasury therefore raises the apparent opportunity cost of holding it. A second shorthand says that gold rises when the dollar falls. If the dollar price of gold is unchanged, dollar depreciation makes gold cheaper in another currency; foreign demand may then rise, or the dollar price may adjust upward to preserve a broadly similar foreign-currency price.

Both mechanisms are sensible comparative statics: they describe the direction of one pressure while holding other things fixed. Markets rarely hold everything else fixed. The shock that moves a real yield can also change the dollar, inflation uncertainty, recession risk, leverage, reserve policy, and desired hedges. A large official buyer may purchase for institutional reasons that are only weakly sensitive to a quarterly change in US yields. Mine supply responds slowly, while recycling and investment flows can move much faster. The observed gold price is the result of all these margins, not a thermometer for one of them.

This also makes causal language demanding. A negative correlation between gold and real yields does not prove that rates caused the gold move. Both may have responded to the same macroeconomic news. A correlation that disappears does not prove the opportunity-cost mechanism ceased to exist; another demand shift may simply have outweighed it.

## The real-yield opportunity-cost channel

Consider an investor choosing between physical gold and a Treasury inflation-protected security. The TIPS offers contractual payments indexed to the US consumer price index, subject to its specific indexation and deflation-floor rules. Gold offers no promised cash flow but may appreciate, diversify bad states, supply collateral or liquidity services in some settings, and avoid being another institution's liability when held outright.

A schematic investor-specific net-benefit comparison is

$$
E_t(B^{gold}_{t,t+h})-y^R_{t,h}
=E_t(\Delta p^{gold}_{t,t+h})-y^R_{t,h}-c_t+v_t,
$$

where $B^{gold}$ is the holder's utility-adjusted net benefit rather than a financial return, $y^R$ is a horizon-matched real yield, $c_t$ represents storage, insurance, financing, trading, and custody costs, and $v_t$ collects non-cash services such as diversification or protection against selected tail risks. This is not a pricing identity. It is a checklist. A rise in $y^R$, with expected gold appreciation and the other terms unchanged, makes gold relatively less attractive. A rise in the perceived hedge service $v_t$ can offset that change.

“Real yield” must be specified. The US Treasury publishes par real-yield curves derived from TIPS prices. The Federal Reserve also publishes fitted zero-coupon real curves. An individual TIPS yield, an official par yield, a fitted zero-coupon yield, a real-rate swap, and an ex ante real policy-rate estimate are related but different objects. As [[Real rates inflation compensation and term premium]] explains, a TIPS yield contains expected real short rates, a real term premium, liquidity and technical effects, and instrument-specific features. It is not a pure observation of the economy's natural real rate.

Maturity matching matters too. Gold has no maturity, so researchers often compare it with a 10-year TIPS yield as a convenient long-horizon benchmark. That convention is useful, not theoretically unique. Shorter real yields may better capture near-term monetary-policy opportunity cost. Longer yields may better match a durable reserve asset, but they carry more term-premium exposure. Selecting the maturity that produces the cleanest negative chart after seeing the data invites specification mining.

The nominal-versus-real distinction is essential. A nominal yield can rise because expected inflation rises, because expected real rates rise, or because risk and liquidity premia change. Gold could rise alongside nominal yields if the yield increase is dominated by inflation compensation or inflation-risk demand. Conversely, a fall in nominal yields during disinflation need not help gold if real yields rise. Analysts should compare matched nominal and TIPS curves rather than label any Treasury-yield move a change in gold's real opportunity cost.

The sign of the reaction also depends on why real yields moved. Stronger productivity or growth news can raise real yields, support the dollar, reduce demand for defensive assets, and pressure gold through several aligned channels. A disorderly fiscal or inflation-risk shock can raise long real yields through term premium while simultaneously increasing demand for gold as a hedge against policy or institutional uncertainty. A TIPS liquidity disturbance can mechanically lift measured real yields without delivering a comparable increase in the safe real return available to every investor. The same observed yield change can therefore accompany different gold outcomes.

## The dollar channel is relative and currency-specific

Let $P_t^{USD}$ be dollars per ounce of gold and let $E_t^{LC/USD}$ be units of local currency per US dollar. Ignoring market frictions, the local-currency gold price is

$$
P_t^{LC}=E_t^{LC/USD}P_t^{USD}.
$$

In log changes,

$$
\Delta \log P_t^{LC}=\Delta \log E_t^{LC/USD}+\Delta \log P_t^{USD}.
$$

If the dollar appreciates against the local currency, the first term is positive: gold becomes more expensive for that buyer even if its dollar quote is unchanged. Quantity demanded may weaken, inventory holders may sell, or the dollar price may fall. This is the clean currency-translation mechanism behind the often-negative gold-dollar relationship.

But “the dollar” is not one observation. A bilateral exchange rate, the Federal Reserve's broad trade-weighted index, its advanced- and emerging-economy subindexes, and a BIS effective exchange-rate index use different partners and weights. A euro-specific dollar move need not have the same demand consequences as a broad appreciation against major Asian jewellery-consuming economies. As [[Trade-weighted US dollar indexes]] emphasizes, a trade-weighted index is a measurement model, not the price of a tradable universal dollar.

The relevant comparison can also be real rather than nominal. A nominal currency depreciation paired with high local inflation may affect household purchasing power differently from the same exchange-rate move under stable prices. Taxes, import restrictions, capital controls, local premia, fabrication costs, and exchange access can drive a wedge between the converted international quote and the price households or firms actually pay.

The gold and dollar legs can move together because they sometimes provide different forms of safety. During a global funding scramble, investors may demand dollars to meet liabilities while also bidding for gold against broader financial or geopolitical risk. During the most acute phase, forced liquidation can instead push investors to sell liquid gold to obtain dollars, producing a stronger inverse relationship. Sequence and funding structure matter: the first-day response need not match the later portfolio response after central-bank liquidity facilities or margin pressures change.

Interest rates connect the two shorthands but do not collapse them into one. An unexpected rise in the US expected policy path relative to foreign paths may lift US real yields and support the dollar, giving two reasons for dollar gold to weaken. Yet the relative foreign-rate move matters for the exchange rate, whereas the real opportunity-cost comparison may be domestic or global. Foreign yields can rise as much as US yields, leaving the dollar little changed. Or the dollar can appreciate on safe-haven funding demand while US real yields fall. A regression that includes one US TIPS yield and one dollar index does not automatically identify two independent causal shocks.

## Why the correlations change across regimes

Correlations are reduced-form summaries of the shocks that dominated a sample. They change when the mixture of shocks changes.

**Conventional disinflation or policy repricing.** If softer inflation or activity data lowers the expected real policy path, weakens the dollar, and reduces the opportunity cost of gold, the familiar pattern is likely: real yields and the dollar down, gold up. The reverse can follow unexpectedly restrictive policy news. This is the regime in which the two-factor shorthand is most informative.

**Inflation shock.** An inflation surprise can raise nominal yields and inflation compensation while real yields move less or even fall. Gold may rise if investors seek protection against persistent inflation or policy error. It may fall if the surprise produces an even larger anticipated real-policy tightening and dollar appreciation. Calling gold an “inflation hedge” without specifying horizon and policy response misses this conditionality.

**Growth or productivity shock.** Stronger real growth can raise real yields and the dollar and reduce defensive demand, aligning the negative correlations. It can also raise jewellery and industrial demand through income, or increase fiscal confidence, so the net response need not be stable. Gold's industrial use is economically smaller than its jewellery and investment roles, but it is not zero.

**Deflationary financial stress.** Safe Treasury demand can lower real yields, which by itself should support gold. At the same time, margin calls, dollar liabilities, dealer constraints, and demand for cash can force gold sales. Gold and real yields may then fall together temporarily. Once acute liquidation passes, hedge demand can re-emerge. Daily or monthly data can mix these stages and obscure the mechanism.

**Fiscal, institutional, or geopolitical shock.** A shock that raises concern about sanctions, reserve access, sovereign credit, war, or the durability of monetary arrangements can increase the desired hedge service of gold. It can also raise long real term premia or support the dollar as a liquid funding currency. Gold may rise with both the dollar and measured real yields. The European Central Bank documented that the previously negative gold-real-yield relationship broke down after Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine, while official gold demand remained unusually strong. That evidence is consistent with a larger geopolitical and reserve-demand factor; it does not by itself identify the exact amount of price change caused by that factor.

**Supply and physical-market shock.** Mine disruption, sanctions on producers, refining or transport constraints, import-policy changes, and recycling responses can move gold independently of US macro factors. Mine production is slow to adjust because exploration, permitting, financing, and construction take years. Recycling is more price-elastic but depends on household conditions and local market access. A macro correlation can fail simply because the commodity-specific shock is larger.

**Official-sector accumulation.** Central banks can purchase over long horizons for diversification, balance-sheet resilience, domestic confidence, or geopolitical reasons. Their decisions may be less sensitive than leveraged private positions to the current TIPS yield. Sustained official demand can therefore allow gold to appreciate even while real yields remain positive or rise.

These regimes can overlap. A geopolitical event may worsen inflation, change fiscal issuance, tighten dollar funding, and alter official reserve preferences simultaneously. The disciplined conclusion is not that gold has no relationship to real yields or the dollar. It is that the coefficients are state-dependent and summarize a shifting joint system.

## Official-sector demand changes the marginal buyer

Gold has unusual properties for a reserve manager. Physical monetary gold is not the liability of a foreign government or commercial issuer. It can diversify credit and currency exposures and may remain valuable when confidence in particular settlement arrangements falls. It is globally recognized and has a deep market. Those benefits can become more salient under geopolitical fragmentation or concern that reserve assets could be immobilized.

The trade-offs are equally real. Gold pays no interest, its market price is volatile, physical custody costs money, and mobilizing it can require swaps, deposits, transport, assaying, or market intermediation. Its supply cannot expand elastically to meet a sudden global demand for reserve liquidity. Reserve managers therefore compare more than expected return: they consider safety, liquidity, legal control, currency intervention needs, operational capacity, and the liability structure of the state.

IMF research on reserve diversification finds that active shifts toward gold have been concentrated among emerging-market central banks and are associated with sanctions exposure as well as the appeal of gold during economic, financial, and geopolitical volatility. This is evidence of systematic relationships, not proof that sanctions explain every purchase. Countries differ in starting allocations, reserve adequacy, domestic gold programs, reporting practices, and valuation conventions.

The ECB reported that central banks accounted for more than one-fifth of global gold demand in 2024 and that gold's negative relationship with real yields, visible over 2008 to early 2022, subsequently broke down. Its 2026 international-currency report says official purchases slowed in 2025 from the exceptional 2022–24 pace but remained elevated. This illustrates a crucial stock-flow distinction. A central bank can report no purchase while the market value of its gold reserves rises sharply because gold appreciated. Conversely, tonnes can rise while gold's portfolio share changes little if total reserves grow or other assets appreciate.

Official statistics require definitional care. The IMF's International Reserves and Foreign Currency Liquidity template reports monetary gold within official reserve assets and distinguishes reserve assets from other foreign-currency positions and drains. Gold may be held physically, deposited, or involved in swaps, and national reporting practices can complicate comparisons. “Central-bank buying” estimates compiled from reported changes may also be revised or supplemented by estimates where public reporting is incomplete. A serious study should record whether it uses tonnes, fine troy ounces, market value, share of reserves, gross holdings, or estimated net purchases.

Valuation can create misleading narratives. If gold's dollar price rises 30% while the physical stock is unchanged, gold's share of a reserve portfolio rises mechanically. That does not show a policy decision to sell dollars and buy gold. Likewise, COFER measures the currency composition of allocated foreign-exchange reserves; monetary gold is outside that foreign-exchange currency breakdown. A higher gold share and a stable dollar share in COFER are not arithmetically contradictory because their denominators and asset universes differ.

Official demand can matter disproportionately at the margin, but an annual tonnage total is not a causal price estimate. Purchase timing may be unknown; buying can respond to the price; private sellers absorb flows; expected purchases can be priced before reporting; and official demand may correlate with the same geopolitical shock affecting private demand. Establishing price impact requires higher-frequency or plausibly exogenous variation, not a chart of annual gold prices and annual central-bank purchases.

## Measuring the relationships without overclaiming

A useful empirical design begins by declaring the object. Specify the gold price—spot fixing, futures settlement, exchange-traded product price, or local physical quote—along with currency, time zone, and timestamp. Specify the real-yield measure, maturity, curve construction, and observation time. Specify the dollar index and its basket. Daily Treasury par yields are official closing-derived curve observations, not synchronized intraday transaction prices.

Returns and yield changes should normally be studied rather than price levels and yield levels. Trending levels can generate impressive but spurious correlations. Rolling correlations can describe instability but do not explain it; window length, overlap, and outliers matter. Because gold volatility and rate volatility vary over time, regression residuals are unlikely to be identically distributed. Inference should allow heteroskedasticity and serial dependence where the sampling design warrants it.

A baseline time-series specification might relate gold returns to changes in a chosen real yield and dollar-index returns, then test whether coefficients change across declared regimes. But interpretation remains reduced form. Real yields and the dollar are endogenous asset prices. Contemporaneous coefficients mix direct channels, common news, risk premia, and feedback. Adding controls does not guarantee identification, especially when controls are themselves jointly determined.

Event studies can narrow the information set. Around a precisely timed Federal Reserve announcement or macro release, one can compare synchronized changes in gold, nominal and real yields, inflation compensation, and the dollar. The surprise must be measured relative to what was priced immediately before the event. Even then, a policy announcement can contain several shocks—current-rate action, forward guidance, balance-sheet news, and information about the economy. High-frequency co-movement strengthens timing, not necessarily structural interpretation.

For official purchases, monthly or quarterly reserve data are usually too coarse for a clean price-impact estimate. Country-level policy announcements, predetermined allocation rules, or plausibly external changes to reserve access might offer sharper designs, but anticipation and selection remain serious. Cross-country panels should distinguish quantity changes from valuation effects and should not treat missing or delayed reports as zero purchases.

Robust work reports alternatives: several TIPS maturities, nominal and real yields, bilateral and broad dollar indexes, local-currency gold returns, crisis exclusions, and samples that separate pre- and post-2022 periods. If the result exists only for one maturity, index, window, or endpoint, it may be a specification artifact rather than a durable mechanism.

## A disciplined reading sequence

When gold moves sharply, start with the observation rather than the story.

1. Record the exact gold instrument, currency, return window, and market timestamp.
2. Decompose matched nominal Treasury and TIPS changes into real yield and inflation compensation, while retaining the liquidity and term-premium caveats.
3. Check the expected near-term policy path and whether the move was US-specific relative to foreign rates.
4. Measure the dollar with a declared bilateral or effective index; inspect whether the move was broad or concentrated.
5. Check funding stress, volatility, credit, and liquidity evidence for forced-sale or safe-haven channels.
6. Look for dated official-sector announcements, reserve data, exchange-traded-product flows, futures positioning, and physical-market disruptions without assuming any flow caused the price move.
7. State a leading scenario, at least one alternative, and what evidence would distinguish them.

A defensible conclusion might say: “Gold rose while the 10-year TIPS par yield increased and the broad dollar was stable; the move is inconsistent with a simple one-factor real-yield account, while contemporaneous official-demand and geopolitical evidence makes a reserve-demand channel plausible.” It should not say that official buying caused the entire move unless the research design supports that magnitude.

## Why it matters

Gold sits at the intersection of commodity markets, monetary systems, global funding, and official reserve management. Treating it as merely an inverse real-yield trade hides why it may diversify some risks and fail against others. Treating it as mechanically anti-dollar ignores periods when both assets benefit from different kinds of safety demand. Treating it as an automatic inflation hedge ignores the real-policy response, horizon, starting valuation, and currency of the holder.

The practical value of the framework is diagnostic rather than predictive. Real yields organize the foregone return on an inflation-linked safe asset. Exchange rates translate purchasing power across buyers. Official demand can change the marginal holder. Physical balances and market structure determine how flows clear. Tail-risk preferences change the non-cash service investors assign to gold. Keeping those channels separate makes historical explanations more credible and scenario analysis more useful.

This page is explanatory research, not investment advice. The relationships described here do not establish a trading rule, expected return, or appropriate allocation for any person.

## Open questions

- How stable are gold's real-yield and dollar sensitivities when estimated with synchronized intraday observations rather than daily closes?
- Which component of TIPS real yields—expected real short rates, real term premium, or liquidity effects—has the most stable relationship with gold across regimes?
- How much of the post-2022 correlation break remains after separating reported official purchases, valuation effects, exchange-traded-product flows, and geopolitical event windows?
- Which country-level reserve-policy changes provide plausibly exogenous variation for estimating the price impact of official gold demand?
- Do local-currency gold prices and physical premia show materially different dollar sensitivity in the largest jewellery-consuming and bar-investment markets?
