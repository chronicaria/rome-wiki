U.S. political prediction markets sit within a federal derivatives regime whose statute, agency orders, no-action letters, and court decisions do not reduce to a general rule that “election betting is legal” or “prediction markets are banned.”

> [!warning] Scope
> This is a research docket, not legal advice. It distinguishes operative holdings and orders from proposals, enforcement discretion, and unresolved questions. Venue access, a contract's legality, and a user's legal obligations can depend on facts and jurisdictions not resolved here.

## The shortest accurate account

The Commodity Exchange Act (CEA) gives the Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC) federal authority over commodity futures, options, and swaps, including many event contracts. A contract offered on a registered designated contract market (DCM) travels through a different legal channel from an off-exchange binary option offered to U.S. persons. Section 5c(c)(5)(C), codified at 7 U.S.C. § 7a-2(c)(5)(C), supplies a special review rule for event contracts involving specified activities: unlawful activity, terrorism, assassination, war, gaming, or a Commission-designated similar activity. If such a contract is contrary to the public interest, the Commission may prohibit its listing or trading on a registered entity. CFTC Regulation 40.11 implements that provision.

Three qualifications prevent overstatement. First, the special rule is not the entire CEA: registration, core principles, anti-fraud and anti-manipulation rules, product submissions, clearing, and recordkeeping can matter independently. Second, “the CFTC did not prohibit this contract” is not equivalent to an affirmative agency finding that it is lawful in every respect. Third, no-action relief is a staff statement about enforcement posture under stated facts and conditions, not a statute, rule, license, or judicial declaration of legality.

The regulatory history has moved in both restrictive and permissive directions. The CFTC prohibited Nadex's proposed political contracts in 2012 and Kalshi's congressional-control contracts in 2023. A federal district court vacated the Kalshi order in 2024 because, in the court's construction, contracts about elections did not “involve” gaming or unlawful activity merely because trading them resembles wagering or state law may prohibit election wagering. The D.C. Circuit denied a stay pending appeal without deciding the merits, and the parties dismissed the appeal in May 2025. The district court judgment therefore remained in place, but it did not erase the statutory special rule or decide every possible political contract.

Meanwhile, PredictIt operated under a conditional 2014 CFTC staff no-action letter, became the subject of litigation after staff attempted to withdraw that relief, and received an amended letter in July 2025. Polymarket's operator settled a 2022 CFTC enforcement action concerning off-exchange event-based binary options and agreed to wind down noncompliant markets, pay a civil monetary penalty, and cease and desist from the charged violations. These paths are legally distinct even if all three venues are casually called prediction markets.

As of July 10, 2026, the CFTC has a June 2026 notice of proposed rulemaking (NPRM) pending after withdrawing a different 2024 proposal and soliciting information in a March 2026 advance notice. The June proposal would amend Regulation 40.11 and add a structured, contract-by-contract framework. It is not a final rule. The operative legal baseline remains the statute, existing regulations, final orders and relief still in force, and binding judicial judgments—not the text of the proposal as though already adopted.

## Governing architecture

### Statute and product review

The CEA permits a DCM to submit a product for prior approval or self-certify that it complies with the Act and Commission regulations. Self-certification is not immunity from review. Section 5c(c)(5)(C) authorizes the Commission to examine certain event contracts and, after determining both that the contract involves an enumerated activity and that it is contrary to the public interest, prevent it from being listed or traded on a registered entity. Regulation 40.11 sets out the special-rule process, including a review period.

The two-stage structure matters. An analyst should ask separately:

1. Does the contract “involve” one of the statutory categories?
2. If so, has the Commission determined it is contrary to the public interest?

The Kalshi litigation turned principally on the first gateway. The district court held that the congressional-control contracts involved elections, politics, Congress, and party control—not gaming or unlawful activity as those terms operate in the special rule. Because the gateway was not met, the court did not decide whether election contracts were good policy or whether the Commission's asserted election-integrity concerns were empirically sound.

Registration is a separate axis. The 2022 Polymarket order alleged that the operator offered event-based binary options that constituted swaps off exchange without DCM designation or swap execution facility registration. Thus a product may avoid—or never reach—a political-contract public-interest ruling and still violate the CEA through the manner or venue in which it is offered. Conversely, DCM status does not mean each listed product has received individualized affirmative approval; product filing and review procedures retain their own legal significance.

### Enforcement discretion is not permission in gross

CFTC no-action letters ordinarily state that staff will not recommend enforcement if specified requesters act within described facts and conditions. The 2014 Victoria University letter allowed a small-scale, not-for-profit academic market, operating as PredictIt, under limits concerning traders, investment, contracts, and research purpose. Its form was deliberately narrower than DCM registration.

That distinction became outcome-determinative in the PredictIt litigation. When the CFTC's Division of Market Oversight attempted in August 2022 to withdraw the letter and require remaining contracts and open interest to close by February 15, 2023, market participants and associated plaintiffs sued. In July 2023, the Fifth Circuit held that the controversy was not moot and that the withdrawal was reviewable final agency action for purposes of the preliminary-injunction appeal. The court reasoned, at that procedural stage, that the withdrawal had immediate legal consequences for parties who had structured conduct around the letter. It reversed and remanded with instructions to enter a preliminary injunction while the case proceeded. That ruling did not convert the no-action letter into a DCM license or finally adjudicate all merits claims.

The agency's July 14, 2025 Letter No. 25-20 amended Letter No. 14-130 and states that all unamended terms remain in effect. Accordingly, the current PredictIt reference point is the original letter as modified, not a generic proposition that all academic or small-stakes political markets are exempt. A researcher should read both letters and verify continuing compliance before using PredictIt as precedent for another venue.

## Chronological docket

### 2012 — Nadex political-event prohibition

Nadex self-certified binary options tied to the 2012 federal elections in December 2011. The CFTC opened a 90-day review in January 2012 and requested suspension during review. On April 2, it prohibited the contracts, finding that they involved gaming and were contrary to the public interest. The order is historically important because it demonstrates the Commission's early restrictive interpretation after the Dodd-Frank-era special rule.

Its precedential weight should be described carefully after *Kalshi*. The Nadex order was an agency application, not a federal appellate holding. The 2024 district court's statutory construction rejected the critical logic used in the later Kalshi order—that an election contract can be brought inside “gaming” because trading it is betting or wagering. The Nadex action remains part of the administrative record and legal history, but one should not cite it alone as a conclusive statement of current judicially accepted law.

### 2014, 2022–2025 — PredictIt relief and withdrawal litigation

CFTC staff issued Letter No. 14-130 to Victoria University of Wellington on October 29, 2014. It provided conditional no-action relief for a not-for-profit event-contract market offered to U.S. persons. Staff attempted to rescind that letter in 2022. In *Clarke v. CFTC*, the Fifth Circuit's July 21, 2023 opinion treated the operative withdrawal controversy as reviewable and not moot despite the agency replacing its August 2022 action with a March 2023 letter framed as a preliminary determination.

This litigation clarifies administrative-law consequences more than substantive election-contract legality. Its central lesson is that even formally discretionary relief can generate reliance and legal effects sufficient for judicial review when the agency withdraws it in a way that forces a market wind-down. Letter No. 25-20, issued July 14, 2025, subsequently amended the original relief. The exact amended conditions, rather than the litigation headline, define the staff posture as of this docket's date.

### 2022 — Polymarket enforcement order

On January 3, 2022, the CFTC entered a consent order against Blockratize, Inc. d/b/a Polymarket. The order found that event-based binary options offered on the platform were swaps and that Polymarket operated an unregistered facility for their trading. The settlement imposed a $1.4 million civil monetary penalty, required the wind-down of markets that did not comply with the CEA and regulations, and ordered cessation of the charged violations.

The order supports a bounded proposition: technological architecture and labels such as “decentralized” do not, by themselves, remove event derivatives offered to U.S. persons from federal commodities law. It does not by itself adjudicate every later Polymarket product, every non-U.S. transaction, or the legality of political subject matter as such. Those broader claims require current facts and separate legal analysis.

### 2023–2025 — Kalshi congressional-control litigation

Kalshi self-certified cash-settled contracts asking which party would control each chamber of Congress. In September 2023, after review, the CFTC prohibited them. The Commission concluded that the contracts involved both gaming and unlawful activity under state law and were contrary to the public interest. Kalshi sought Administrative Procedure Act review in the U.S. District Court for the District of Columbia.

On September 12, 2024, Judge Jia M. Cobb granted Kalshi summary judgment. The opinion construed “gaming” to require a game and held that the relevant contracts concerned elections rather than a game. It also held that the contracts did not “involve” unlawful activity merely because staking money on elections might violate some state gambling laws; the event defining settlement was congressional control, not the act of wagering. The court vacated the Commission's order.

The CFTC appealed and sought a stay. On October 2, 2024, the D.C. Circuit denied the stay because the Commission had not shown irreparable harm. Judge Millett's opinion emphasized the absence, in that emergency record, of concrete evidence that allowing the contracts during appeal would likely cause the asserted election-integrity harms. A stay ruling predicts neither final merits disposition nor universal safety. The panel expressly dealt with the extraordinary-remedy standard, and its discussion should not be misreported as a final appellate endorsement of all election markets.

The parties stipulated to dismissal of the appeal in May 2025. Consequently, there is no D.C. Circuit merits opinion adopting or reversing the district court's statutory construction. The district judgment resolved the challenged congressional-control order and is the central judicial authority in that dispute. **Legal uncertainty:** its reasoning is persuasive and operative in that case, but questions involving differently worded political contracts, other enumerated activities, later rules, or other jurisdictions may not be controlled by the same analysis.

### 2024 proposal withdrawn; 2026 rulemaking renewed

The Commission proposed a broader event-contract rule in June 2024. It withdrew that proposed regulatory action in February 2026. Withdrawal means the 2024 text did not become final law.

On March 16, 2026, the Commission published an advance notice of proposed rulemaking on prediction markets, asking about core principles, prohibited activities, cost-benefit questions, and other issues. After comments, the Commission issued a narrower NPRM on June 10, 2026. The current proposal would amend Regulation 40.11, define terms including “involve” and “gaming,” provide a 90-day process with procedural protections, and articulate public-interest factors for contract-specific review. The release says the March inquiry could still support further rulemaking.

**Current status as of July 10, 2026:** the June text is proposed. It supplies evidence of the Commission's intended approach, not an operative amendment. Any statement that a contract “is prohibited under the 2026 rule” is premature unless and until a final rule takes effect and actually covers that contract. ## What the docket does and does not establish

### Historical facts supported by primary records

- The CFTC prohibited Nadex's 2012 federal-election contracts under the special rule.
- Staff granted conditional no-action relief to Victoria University in 2014; the withdrawal generated judicial review; an amended letter was issued in 2025.
- The CFTC settled an unregistered-facility and off-exchange-swaps case with Polymarket's operator in 2022.
- The CFTC prohibited Kalshi's congressional-control contracts in 2023; the district court vacated that order in 2024; the appellate court denied a stay; the merits appeal was later dismissed.
- The Commission withdrew its 2024 event-contract proposal and initiated a new 2026 rulemaking sequence.

### Reasonable inference, labeled as such

The regime is moving away from categorical slogans and toward disputes over gateway definitions, venue status, product-specific terms, and administrable review factors. That inference follows from the Kalshi opinion's textual analysis and the 2026 proposal's contract-by-contract structure. It is not a forecast that the final rule will be permissive, survive judicial review, or treat political and sports contracts alike.

Another inference is that researchers should treat regulatory status as part of [[Prediction-market resolution risk]]. A stay, prohibition, enforcement order, amended no-action condition, or review request can affect listing, liquidity, closeout, and available counterparties even without changing beliefs about the underlying election. That is an analytic consequence, not a legal conclusion about any trader's rights.

### Unresolved legal questions

The district court's Kalshi opinion narrows the Commission's 2023 interpretation, but Congress has not enacted a bespoke national election-contract statute, and the D.C. Circuit never decided the appeal's merits. The scope of CEA preemption over state gambling regulation is being litigated in event-contract cases involving sports; those disputes can inform general market structure but do not automatically decide political contracts. The legal effect of a future final Regulation 40.11 will depend on its text, administrative record, effective date, and any challenge. Federal registration also does not settle tax, sanctions, election-law, consumer-protection, or user-jurisdiction questions.

## Why it matters

Regulatory category is part of contract design. A “Yes” share is not merely an opinion about an election; it is a claim offered through a particular legal entity under rules governing listing, surveillance, settlement, customer eligibility, and emergency action. Ignoring the docket can make two prices appear comparable when one is a DCM contract, another exists under conditional staff relief, and a third is offered offshore or through a venue whose U.S. access is restricted.

The docket also disciplines political argument. The 2024 district court did not hold that election markets improve democracy. The 2023 CFTC order did not prove that they corrupt elections. The D.C. Circuit's stay denial said the Commission had not demonstrated likely irreparable harm on the record before it; it did not decide that manipulation is impossible. Keeping procedural posture attached to each claim is essential for both legal accuracy and empirical research.

For forecasting work, archive the applicable contract rules, venue status, product filing, regulatory action, and timestamp alongside price and liquidity data. A later legal change can create selection bias or truncation: markets that close, migrate, exclude users, or change limits are not missing at random. The legal docket therefore belongs beside [[Backtesting political forecasts without leakage]], not only in a compliance appendix.
