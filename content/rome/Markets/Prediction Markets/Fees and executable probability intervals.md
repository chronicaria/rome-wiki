Fees turn a prediction-market bid and ask into a wider no-trade probability interval whose boundaries depend on side, size, maker–taker role, rounding, and the contract’s fee schedule at that moment.

Up: [[Political prediction markets]]

A binary contract that pays $1 if an event occurs and $0 otherwise has a seductively simple price scale. A Yes ask of 62 cents is often reported as a 62% probability. That number is only the gross price of acquiring one contingent dollar, however. The actual trade may also incur an execution fee; a sale may deliver less than the displayed bid; a large order may walk through several price levels; and a venue may use a different fee rule for a particular series. The market therefore does not usually reveal one directly executable probability. It reveals a range within which a trader with a given belief would decline to trade after costs.

This note isolates fees from the other frictions in [[From event-contract prices to probabilities]]. Its object is a **fee-aware executable probability interval**: the range of subjective event probabilities for which neither immediately buying Yes nor immediately selling Yes (or equivalently buying No, when that equivalence is supported by the venue) has positive expected value for a risk-neutral trader. It is not a confidence interval, a credible interval, or an estimate of population uncertainty. It is a no-trade band generated by observable quotes and specified transaction costs.

## The frictionless benchmark

Let $q$ be a trader's subjective probability that a Yes contract pays $1. Let $a$ be the best executable Yes ask and $b$ the best executable Yes bid, both expressed in dollars per contract. Ignore discounting, taxes, collateral costs, resolution risk, and fees for the moment.

Buying Yes at $a$ has expected profit

$$
\mathbb E[\pi_{\text{buy}}]=q-a.
$$

It is attractive when $q>a$. Selling a Yes claim at $b$ has expected profit $b-q$ when the trader can create or deliver that claim under the venue's collateral rules, so it is attractive when $q<b$. A trader with $q\in[b,a]$ does neither. The executable interval is therefore $[b,a]$.

This interval already differs from the midpoint $m=(a+b)/2$. The midpoint is an analyst's summary, not a price at which either side promises to transact. A 58-cent bid and 64-cent ask produce a 61-cent midpoint but a six-point frictionless no-trade interval. [[Bid-ask spreads as probability uncertainty]] explains what that spread does and does not reveal. Fees widen the economic interval further.

## Adding side-specific transaction fees

Let $f_B(a,C)$ be the total fee for immediately buying $C$ Yes contracts at ask $a$, and let $f_S(b,C)$ be the total fee for immediately selling $C$ Yes contracts at bid $b$. Define per-contract fees $\bar f_B=f_B/C$ and $\bar f_S=f_S/C$. For a risk-neutral trader who holds cash and can take either side without other constraints, the expected profits per contract are

$$
\mathbb E[\pi_{\text{buy}}]=q-a-\bar f_B
$$

and

$$
\mathbb E[\pi_{\text{sell}}]=b-\bar f_S-q.
$$

The fee-aware no-trade interval is consequently

$$
I_C=[b-\bar f_S(b,C),\;a+\bar f_B(a,C)].
$$

The lower endpoint is the seller's net receipt, while the upper endpoint is the buyer's all-in cost. This is the central mechanism. Subtracting a fee from both quotes, adding it to both quotes, or “correcting” only the midpoint gets at least one side wrong.

The formula is also size-specific. If the order consumes several levels, replace $a$ and $b$ with side-specific volume-weighted execution prices and calculate fees on the actual fills under the operative rule. A one-contract interval need not describe the threshold for 100 or 10,000 contracts. [[Order-book microstructure of event contracts]] gives the book-level extraction procedure.

There is another way to construct the lower boundary. If the trader cannot directly sell a newly created Yes claim but can buy No at executable ask $a_N$, and one No share pays $1$ exactly when Yes pays $0$, then buying No has expected profit

$$
(1-q)-a_N-\bar f_N.
$$

It is attractive when $q<1-a_N-\bar f_N$. The lower endpoint is then $1-a_N-\bar f_N$, not a mechanically assumed Yes bid. On a venue whose matching engine makes a Yes bid at $b$ economically equivalent to a No ask at $1-b$, the two constructions agree before fees. They may diverge if fee assessment, order role, token creation, collateral, or execution path differs. Contract complementarity must be verified rather than presumed.

## A worked generic example

Suppose a political Yes contract has a best bid of $0.58 and best ask of $0.62. A trader wants 100 contracts. Assume, only for this example, that the entire quantity executes at the best quotes and that both marketable sides pay a fee

$$
f(P,C)=0.07CP(1-P)
$$

without rounding. Buying 100 Yes at $0.62 costs $62. The fee is

$$
0.07\times100\times0.62\times0.38=1.6492,
$$

or about $0.016492 per contract. The buyer needs $q>0.636492$ to have positive expected profit. Selling 100 Yes at $0.58 produces $58 gross and incurs the same $1.7052 fee because $0.58(1-0.58)=0.2436$. Net proceeds are $56.2948, or $0.562948 per contract. The fee-aware interval is approximately

$$
[0.5629,0.6365].
$$

The raw quote interval was $[0.58,0.62]$ and its midpoint was 0.60. Fees did not transform 60% into another unique probability. They expanded the band of beliefs for which immediate trading is unattractive from four percentage points to about 7.35 points.

This example is deliberately generic. Applying it to a live venue requires the exact schedule, multiplier, rounding rule, side, order role, quantity, and series. It also assumes linear expected utility and no carrying cost. Those assumptions make the arithmetic transparent; they do not describe every participant.

## Kalshi's mutable schedule as of July 10, 2026

Kalshi's official fee schedule accessed July 10, 2026 says it was last updated and effective July 7, 2026. Its general immediately matched trading-fee formula is

$$
f_T=\operatorname{round\ up}\left(M\times0.07\times C\times P(1-P)\right),
$$

where $P$ is the contract price in dollars, $C$ is the contract count, and $M$ is a series multiplier whose default for taker fees is 1. The document defines the relevant “round up” so that fee plus position cost is rounded to a centicent. The schedule's general maker formula is

$$
f_M=\operatorname{round\ up}\left(M\times0.0175\times C\times P(1-P)\right),
$$

but the default maker multiplier is 0 unless the listed series indicates otherwise. Resting orders incur a maker fee only when executed; canceling a resting order does not itself incur that fee. The same schedule lists non-standard series with explicit maker and taker multipliers.

Three consequences matter for probability extraction.

First, the fee is nonlinear in price. The $P(1-P)$ term is largest at 50 cents and symmetric around it. Under the general $0.07$ taker coefficient and before rounding, the fee for 100 contracts is $1.75 at 50 cents, about $1.47 at 30 or 70 cents, and $0.63 at 10 or 90 cents. Describing the fee as a constant percentage of notional is therefore wrong.

Second, rounding makes the per-contract burden depend on batch size. The official table shows a one-contract general trading fee of one cent at a 10-cent price but a 100-contract fee of 63 cents, not one dollar. An analyst cannot safely multiply the displayed one-contract fee by quantity. The formula and rounding convention must be applied to the whole matched amount as specified.

Third, role and series identity matter. An immediately matched order normally uses the trading-fee rule. An order that rests and later executes may use the maker rule only when its series has a nonzero maker multiplier. Political-sounding series in the July schedule do not share one universal treatment: the table must be joined by exact series ticker. A historical observation should therefore store the schedule's effective date, series ticker, maker/taker role, multiplier, price, quantity, and computed fee.

The July 7 schedule also states that Kalshi has no settlement fee or membership fee and no Kalshi fee for ACH deposits or withdrawals. Other rails may impose costs: debit deposits can carry up to a 2% fee, third-party processors may charge for crypto transfers, and futures commission merchant customers may face separately disclosed intermediary fees. These account-level costs should not automatically be allocated to one trade, but they should not be silently described as exchange trading fees either. The research convention should report them separately.

## Polymarket's mutable schedule as of July 10, 2026

Polymarket's official documentation accessed July 10, 2026 says fees are set per market and applied at match time. Markets with fees enabled expose that state in market data, and the documentation directs clients to query the market's fee parameters. Its documented formula is

$$
f=C\times r\times p(1-p),
$$

where $r$ is the category fee rate. The page says makers are not charged and only takers pay. Its table lists a politics taker rate of $0.04$, with a 25% maker rebate funded by fees; geopolitics and world-events markets are described as fee-free. It also says Polymarket itself charges no deposit or withdrawal fee for USDC, while intermediaries may charge their own fees.

The category label is not enough for a durable calculation. The official page says fee status and parameters are determined per market at match time. A market that appears political to a reader might be classified as politics, geopolitics, finance, or another category under the platform's data. The correct input is the fee configuration returned for the exact condition identifier, archived with a retrieval timestamp.

For 100 politics shares at 60 cents, the documented formula gives

$$
100\times0.04\times0.60\times0.40=0.96\ \text{USDC},
$$

or 0.96 cents per share. A taker buying Yes at $0.60 would therefore need a subjective probability above 0.6096 to clear the immediate fee, before other frictions. A maker at the same price is not charged according to the accessed schedule, but “maker” does not mean the trader was guaranteed an execution at that price. The order must rest, may never fill, and faces adverse-selection and queue risk. A maker rebate is also not a reason to subtract an assumed amount from every maker quote: the official program distributes rebates under its own eligibility and calculation rules, and the realized rebate is not identical to an unconditional execution-price improvement.

Polymarket's page specifies fee precision of five decimal places and a minimum charged fee of 0.00001 USDC, with smaller calculated amounts rounded to zero. That is a different rounding regime from Kalshi's July schedule. Even when two venues use the same mathematical shape $Cp(1-p)$, their rate, role treatment, precision, market classification, and ancillary costs can differ. “Same formula” does not imply the same executable interval.

## Maker quotes require a different interpretation

The simple no-trade interval describes immediate execution against displayed liquidity. A trader can try to improve the threshold by posting a limit order inside the spread and becoming a maker. If it fills, the price may be better and the fee may be lower or zero. But a posted order is not an executable opportunity in the same sense as an existing ask or bid. It is a request to trade if someone later chooses to interact with it.

That distinction creates selection risk. A resting Yes buy at 60 cents may fill precisely when new information makes the event worth 55 cents. The ex ante value of posting cannot be calculated from the quoted price and fee schedule alone; it depends on fill probability, queue position, cancellations, latency, and the relationship between fills and information arrival. The fee-aware **taker interval** can be calculated from a snapshot. A maker strategy needs a model of execution and adverse selection.

Rebates deepen the distinction. A venue may use taker fees to reward eligible makers, but the expected rebate depends on program terms and the trader's qualifying activity. Unless a rebate is deterministic for the exact fill and known at the observation time, archive it as a separate contingent cash flow. Do not embed a headline rebate percentage into the probability endpoint.

## From one level to an executable interval at size

For quantity $C$ that sweeps asks $p_i$ for quantities $c_i$, where $\sum_i c_i=C$, define the gross acquisition cost

$$
K_B(C)=\sum_i c_ip_i.
$$

Let $F_B(C)$ be fees computed according to the venue's actual fill-level or aggregate rule. The upper probability boundary is

$$
U(C)=\frac{K_B(C)+F_B(C)}{C}.
$$

For a sale consuming bids $r_j$ in quantities $d_j$, define gross proceeds $K_S(C)=\sum_jd_jr_j$ and fees $F_S(C)$. The lower boundary is

$$
L(C)=\frac{K_S(C)-F_S(C)}{C}.
$$

Thus $I_C=[L(C),U(C)]$. As size increases, $U(C)$ usually rises and $L(C)$ usually falls because the order walks outward through the book, though new fee rounding can make the path stepwise. Reporting $I_1$ without quantity is incomplete; reporting it as the market's probability is misleading.

A reproducible record should preserve raw levels and fill simulation, not merely the resulting endpoints. Fee schedules change, data corrections occur, and researchers may later choose a different standard size. With the raw book, schedule version, and computation code, the interval can be rebuilt. With only “fee-adjusted probability: 61%,” it cannot.

## What fees do not measure

The interval is a set of private beliefs that do not justify an immediate risk-neutral trade under the modeled costs. It does not prove that the true probability lies inside the band. Traders may be risk-averse, capital-constrained, prohibited from participating, or exposed to correlated political risks. Their beliefs may be wrong. The contract may resolve under language that differs from the analyst's natural-language event, as [[Prediction-market resolution risk]] emphasizes.

The formula also omits time value and collateral. A contract paying $1 months later ties up capital differently from one settling tomorrow. Taxes depend on participant and jurisdiction. Deposit, withdrawal, conversion, gas, and intermediary costs may be fixed, variable, or shared across trades. Expected resolution disputes and counterparty or platform risk can change the value of the promised dollar. These can be modeled, but they should be separate named adjustments rather than hidden in a generic “fee correction.”

Finally, fees are not the bid–ask spread. The spread is embedded in the difference between available buying and selling prices; the fee is an additional cash flow. Adding “the spread” again after using bid and ask double-counts it. Conversely, taking a midpoint and subtracting one estimated fee loses the side asymmetry. Build endpoints from executable sides.

## Why it matters

Political-market claims often differ by only one or two percentage points. That is the same scale as ordinary spreads and trading fees, especially around 50 cents or for small rounded orders. A cross-venue difference can disappear when both books are compared at equal size with exact fees. A backtest can rank a market too confidently if it scores a midpoint that no trader could obtain. A chart can create false precision by labeling a last trade “63%” while the economically relevant interval spans several points.

Fee-aware intervals supply a conservative common language. They let an analyst say, for example, “At time $T$, a trader could immediately sell 100 Yes down to a net probability-equivalent of $L$ or buy 100 Yes up to an all-in equivalent of $U$, under schedule version $S$.” That statement is auditable. It separates market evidence from the analyst's choice of a midpoint and connects directly to [[Cross-market arbitrage and contract basis risk]], [[Prediction-market calibration]], and [[Backtesting political forecasts without leakage]].

The practical rule is simple: archive the quote, size, depth, role, exact fee parameters, rounding rule, schedule effective date, and computation. Present the raw bid–ask interval beside the fee-aware interval. If any required field is unavailable, label the quote “before fees” rather than inventing a corrected probability.
