An honest political-forecast backtest reconstructs what each forecaster could have known at a fixed historical cutoff, scores a prospectively defined event universe, and treats repeated forecasts about the same political contest as dependent observations.

## The backtest is a historical information experiment

A backtest asks a counterfactual question: if a fixed evaluation procedure had been running at time $t$, what forecast would it have recorded, and how would that forecast have scored after resolution? It does not ask how well an analyst can reconstruct a plausible probability today. The distinction is decisive in politics because contract wording changes, polls are revised or reweighted, model pages are overwritten, markets open after news, and analysts learn which races ultimately became interesting.

The experimental unit should therefore be an immutable forecast record, not a present-day chart. At minimum, each record needs an event identifier, forecast source, forecast probability or executable interval, observation timestamp and timezone, forecast horizon, exact proposition, resolution rule, information cutoff, later outcome, and a provenance pointer to the archived artifact. For a market, add venue, contract identifier and version, bid, ask, last, depth at a stated size, fees, and settlement convention. [[From event-contract prices to probabilities]] explains why a displayed price is not automatically an executable probability.

This design is *prequential*: predict first, observe the outcome later, then score. Historical reconstruction can approximate it only when artifacts prove what was public at the cutoff. A current webpage bearing an old chart is insufficient if the underlying series, definitions, or methodology could have been revised. When the archive cannot establish a field, mark it missing rather than infer it from the outcome.

## Write the estimand before touching results

“Which forecaster is best?” is not an estimand. A defensible protocol specifies all of the following before scores are inspected:

1. **Target universe.** For example, all major-party US Senate general-election winner contracts opened by a named date, rather than “important races” selected afterward.
2. **Forecast origin.** A named venue and contract field, poll-to-probability model version, published expert forecast, or archived model output.
3. **Cutoff rule.** A fixed horizon such as 30 days before the scheduled election, with a prospective fallback when no admissible quote exists.
4. **Outcome rule.** The designated official result and a policy for recounts, cancellations, substitutions, postponements, and unresolved or void contracts.
5. **Primary score.** Binary Brier loss, log loss with prospectively fixed clipping, or another justified proper rule. See [[Proper scoring rules and market forecasts]].
6. **Comparator.** A contemporaneously available baseline with the same target semantics and cutoff.
7. **Dependence unit.** The contest, election cycle, jurisdiction, or larger political shock at which uncertainty will be clustered.
8. **Missingness policy.** Whether absent, stale, illiquid, or semantically unmatched forecasts count as coverage failures, exclusions, or prespecified imputations.

This specification prevents the analyst from changing horizons, samples, scores, and liquidity filters until a preferred winner appears. A dated protocol or code commit—or a cryptographic hash anchored to a trusted timestamp—is stronger evidence than a prose claim that choices were made “in advance.” A fully prospective study should publish the protocol before outcomes. A retrospective study cannot truly preregister history, but it can blind outcome labels while the universe and transformations are frozen, then timestamp the frozen manifest before scoring.

## Build a point-in-time event manifest

Start with a manifest of eligible events generated by a rule that did not depend on later excitement or resolution ease. Preserve every candidate event and an exclusion reason. Political markets list salient questions selectively; a sample of listed contracts is not a random sample of political events. Likewise, a model may publish only races it judges competitive. Coverage is part of performance, not housekeeping.

Give each proposition a semantic version. The proposition should include actor, office or action, jurisdiction, deadline, resolution authority, tie and recount treatment, candidate-withdrawal treatment, and void conditions. Hash the normalized terms or retain a dated rules artifact. If wording changes materially, terminate the old series and begin a new version; do not splice prices across different claims.

Map related contracts to a common *event family*. A presidential popular-vote contract, Electoral College winner contract, party-to-win contract, and conditional nomination contract do not share an outcome merely because they mention the same candidate. Conversely, fifty state winner contracts and the national winner are not fifty-one independent political experiments. The manifest needs both a contract-level identity and a higher-level contest or cycle cluster.

Outcome collection comes last conceptually even if it is convenient technically. Record the official resolution authority named by the protocol, the outcome timestamp, and whether the event resolved yes, no, void, disputed, postponed, or remained unresolved at the analysis freeze. Do not silently delete nonbinary terminal states. Ordinary binary scores may be undefined for them, but their frequency and concentration reveal whether a forecast source systematically chooses fragile questions.

## Enforce the as-of join

Every input must satisfy an availability test: could a user following the written procedure have obtained this exact value before the cutoff? This requires at least three times:

- **event time**, when the underlying political or economic fact occurred;
- **publication or exchange time**, when the source made it public;
- **ingestion time**, when the research system captured it.

The admissible value is the latest version whose publication time is no later than the cutoff, not the latest value stored today. This is an as-of join, commonly written as “take the record with maximum available-at time subject to available-at $\le t$.” Retain the raw source timestamp and the normalized UTC timestamp so daylight-saving and midnight-boundary errors are auditable.

Polls require field dates, release time, sample definition, question wording, and the version of any poll-average transformation available then. A poll completed before the cutoff but first published afterward is future information. A historical poll average rebuilt with pollster ratings or house effects estimated using later elections is also contaminated unless those parameters are refit within each training window.

Economic predictors require release vintages. The Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis's ALFRED system and FRED vintage API explicitly distinguish dates on which observations were released or revised. Using the latest unemployment or income series in an old election model can let later revisions enter an apparently historical forecast. The same rule applies to redistricting files, candidate rosters, incumbency status, fundraising filings, and expert ratings that were corrected or overwritten.

Market data add microstructure problems. A last trade may precede the cutoff by days; a midpoint requires live two-sided quotes; an interface “probability” may be rounded; and an empty side cannot be converted to zero or one. Fix an admissibility rule prospectively—maximum quote age, minimum displayed depth at a specified size, maximum spread, and fee treatment. If both sides are executable, preserve bid and ask as an interval and state how a point forecast is derived. Never use the eventual settlement payout, closing quote, or post-cutoff trade to fill a missing historical observation.

## Synchronize comparators rather than handicapping them

A market, poll, and statistical model must forecast the same proposition using information available at the same cutoff. Erikson and Wlezien show why comparing an election market's election-day expectation with an unadjusted contemporaneous trial-heat poll is conceptually uneven: the poll measures current preference, while the market targets the future result. A fair poll comparator needs a prospectively specified transformation from current polling to election-day outcome, estimated without the test election.

Useful baselines become progressively harder to beat:

- the unconditional event base rate, estimated only from prior eligible events;
- a simple constant such as $0.5$ for balanced binary questions, when substantively defensible;
- an incumbency or fundamentals model trained on earlier cycles;
- a poll-based probability model using only polls available by the cutoff;
- the other forecasting system observed at the identical horizon and semantic target.

Hyperparameters, calibration mappings, clipping thresholds, liquidity filters, and forecast-combination weights must be learned inside the training period. For election-cycle evaluation, use expanding-origin validation: train on cycles before year $T$, choose all transformations within those earlier cycles, predict cycle $T$, then advance. Randomly splitting daily rows places later observations from the same race in training and earlier observations in testing, an especially direct form of leakage.

If a comparator was not historically published, label it a reconstructed baseline. Its point-in-time inputs and code may still make it valuable, but it does not become a contemporaneous public forecast. Conversely, an archived published forecast may be scored even when its internal model cannot be reproduced, provided its timestamp and target are clear.

## Score events, horizons, and forecast paths separately

For a binary event with probability $p_i$ and outcome $y_i\in\{0,1\}$, mean Brier loss is

$$
\overline L_B=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}(p_i-y_i)^2.
$$

Log loss is

$$
\overline L_{\log}=-\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\left[y_i\log p_i+(1-y_i)\log(1-p_i)\right].
$$

Lower is better. Log loss strongly penalizes failed near-certainties, so any clipping away from zero and one must be fixed before outcomes and reported as a sensitivity analysis. [[Prediction-market calibration]] explains why an average score alone does not establish reliability, discrimination, or sharpness.

One fixed-horizon forecast per event gives each event equal weight. Scoring every hourly or daily tick instead answers a different question: how accurate was the *forecast path* across time? It also gives long-running or frequently sampled contracts much more weight. Both estimands can be useful, but they should appear in separate tables. For path scores, first average within event and horizon band, then average across events, or publish explicit weights. Do not allow API sampling frequency to determine scientific importance.

Report performance by prospectively meaningful horizon bands, such as 180–91, 90–31, 30–8, and 7–1 days. Exact bands depend on the application. Avoid searching dozens of cutoffs and reporting only the one where a venue wins. If horizon is the research question, display the whole prespecified curve with uncertainty.

Calibration plots should use bins defined before outcome inspection or smoothing whose tuning is isolated from the test set. Report bin counts and outcome counts; a smooth-looking line from a handful of correlated elections is not strong evidence. Decompose performance only with care: reliability and resolution estimates can be noisy and depend on grouping choices.

## Dependence is not a cosmetic standard-error choice

Political forecasts share shocks. Candidate contracts within one primary are mutually exclusive. State outcomes within one presidential election respond to the same national environment. Repeated quotes for one race share the same final outcome. Senate and House markets can react to the same macroeconomic release or candidate event. Treating all rows as independent makes uncertainty intervals far too narrow and turns one election wave into hundreds of apparent replications.

Choose the inferential population explicitly. If the intended claim is about future US presidential cycles, the effective number of independent cycles may be tiny even when the dataset contains millions of ticks. Cluster paired score differences at least by contest; for national generalization, cluster or resample by election cycle. A hierarchical bootstrap can sample cycles, then contests within cycles, while preserving all linked contracts and forecast dates inside a sampled contest. With few clusters, emphasize the distribution of cycle-level effects and leave-one-cycle-out sensitivity rather than relying on asymptotic significance.

Compare systems on matched event-cutoff pairs. For pair $i$, define $d_i=L(p_{A,i},y_i)-L(p_{B,i},y_i)$. Summarize the mean or median paired difference, its cluster-aware interval, and how many distinct cycles and contests support it. A lower unpaired average can merely reflect that system A forecast easier events. When coverage differs materially, publish both matched-pair skill and a coverage table; neither substitutes for the other.

Nested or mutually exclusive contracts invite additional checks. Probabilities for exhaustive candidates should sum coherently after accounting for spreads and fees, while conditional and unconditional questions must obey probability bounds. These constraints can diagnose data errors, but do not retroactively “repair” forecasts using outcomes. Any coherence projection must be fixed prospectively and scored as a distinct derived forecast.

## Leakage audit

Before opening the outcome column, audit each transformation against common failure modes:

- **Outcome leakage:** resolution status, official results, certification dates, or post-election labels enter features or filters.
- **Timestamp leakage:** a release is assigned its field date rather than its public release time; a daily observation accidentally contains the closing value.
- **Revision leakage:** latest macro data, poll weights, candidate metadata, or model parameters replace historical vintages.
- **Semantic leakage:** today’s contract wording is imposed on an earlier version, or unmatched propositions are treated as identical.
- **Selection leakage:** only resolved, liquid, competitive, famous, or correctly forecast events survive the sample.
- **Training leakage:** calibration and hyperparameters use the test cycle, directly or through random row splits.
- **Horizon leakage:** the analyst chooses a favorable snapshot after observing results or substitutes a later quote when the scheduled one is missing.
- **Comparator leakage:** a “baseline” incorporates polls, ratings, or model code unavailable to the market at the comparison cutoff.
- **Dependence failure (pseudoreplication):** multiple timestamps or correlated subcontracts are presented as independent evidence, reusing the same outcome and overstating the effective sample size.

Automated tests should assert `available_at <= cutoff`, one semantic version per series segment, no outcome fields in feature tables, no test-cycle records in fitted transformations, and deterministic inclusion from the frozen manifest. Manually inspect a stratified sample of winners, losers, voids, missing forecasts, wording changes, and extreme probabilities. Data checks can prove internal consistency; only archived provenance can establish historical availability.

## A reproducible reporting package

A credible backtest should ship, or at least describe, five artifacts:

1. a frozen event manifest with inclusion and exclusion reasons;
2. a raw provenance table and immutable archived references;
3. a transformation log that maps raw records to forecast probabilities without outcomes;
4. a scoring specification with weights, clusters, missingness, voids, and sensitivity analyses; and
5. a results table reporting coverage, matched pairs, event and cycle counts, primary and complementary scores, cluster-aware uncertainty, and all prespecified horizons.

The headline should match the evidence. “Lower mean Brier loss on 86 matched Senate races at a 30-day cutoff” is supportable if the protocol and uncertainty agree. “Markets beat polls” is usually not: it suppresses target construction, horizons, venue selection, political domain, coverage, and dependence. A single election can be a case study, not a general performance estimate.

Negative results remain informative. If semantics cannot be aligned, the correct output is a basis-risk finding rather than a score comparison. If historical quotes lack depth or timestamps, report that the market forecast is not recoverable. If only three independent cycles exist, show them without converting rows into false precision. The purpose of leakage control is not to make a forecasting system look weak; it is to ensure that any apparent skill could actually have been achieved in real time.

## Open questions

- What minimum archival evidence should make a reconstructed market quote admissible when a full order-book snapshot is unavailable?
- Should Rome's primary political backtest weight each event equally, each election cycle equally, or report both as separate estimands?
- How should an executable bid–ask interval be scored without inventing a midpoint probability?
- Which political event families are sufficiently stable across cycles to justify claims beyond a single historical episode?
- Can contract coverage and forecast skill be combined in one proper evaluation without rewarding indiscriminate listing or hiding missingness?
