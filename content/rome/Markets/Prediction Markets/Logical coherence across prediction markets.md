Logical coherence asks whether prices on related event contracts can all arise from one probability distribution over the states their rules actually define, after spreads, fees, and execution constraints are respected.

Up: [[Political prediction markets]]

A prediction market can look sensible one contract at a time and still contradict itself as a system. A venue might quote 55% for a party to win the House, 48% for it to win the Senate, and 30% for it to win both. Those numbers satisfy one necessary restriction—“both” is no more likely than either component—but many larger contract families fail subtler tests. Candidate outcomes may sum above 100%; a narrower event may cost more than an event that logically contains it; a conditional contract may conflict with its joint and marginal counterparts; or contracts on different venues may appear to price the same payoff differently.

Logical coherence is the discipline for separating three possibilities. The first is a genuine inconsistency in the quoted contingent payoffs. The second is an apparent inconsistency created by using midpoints or last trades rather than executable prices. The third is a false comparison between contracts whose wording, resolution source, deadline, or exceptional-case rules make them different events. Only the first is a probability contradiction, and even then it is not automatically a realizable arbitrage.

This distinction matters because “the market probability” is often constructed from several securities. Election dashboards sum candidates, infer conditionals, compare chamber-control contracts, or combine markets into scenarios. If the inputs cannot belong to one joint distribution, the dashboard is not merely noisy. It is mathematically inconsistent. But forcing every visible number to sum to one can be equally misleading when the numbers came from wide books or semantically mismatched contracts. Coherence is a diagnostic applied after [[From event-contract prices to probabilities]] and [[Prediction-market resolution risk]], not a substitute for either.

## Coherence begins with state-contingent payoffs

Let the possible terminal states be $\omega \in \Omega$. A binary contract on event $A$ pays $1_A(\omega)$: one dollar when $A$ occurs and zero otherwise. If a set of prices behaves like probabilities, there must exist some probability distribution $q(\omega) \ge 0$, with $\sum_{\omega}q(\omega)=1$, such that each contract price equals the expected payoff under that same distribution:

$$
p(A)=\sum_{\omega \in \Omega}q(\omega)1_A(\omega).
$$

This single representation produces the familiar probability laws. The sure event costs one and the impossible event costs zero. A complement satisfies $p(A)+p(A^c)=1$. Mutually exclusive and exhaustive outcomes $A_1,\ldots,A_n$ satisfy $\sum_i p(A_i)=1$. If $A\subseteq B$, then $p(A)\le p(B)$. For any two events,

$$
\max(0,p(A)+p(B)-1)\le p(A\cap B)\le \min(p(A),p(B)).
$$

These Fréchet bounds require no independence assumption. They say only that “both” cannot happen more often than either event and cannot be so rare that the two marginals have nowhere to overlap. If a House win is priced at 0.55 and a Senate win at 0.48, a coherent price for winning both lies between 0.03 and 0.48. A 0.30 joint price is possible. A 0.60 price is not.

The classical Dutch-book interpretation makes the connection to markets operational. Treat the assessor as willing to buy or sell arbitrary finite quantities of each unit-payoff bet at one quoted price. The assessment is coherent if no counterparty can choose a portfolio whose net payoff is strictly positive in every possible state—equivalently, one that forces a sure loss on the assessor. Modern finite-event statements of de Finetti's theorem express this geometrically: such a precise price assessment is coherent exactly when it extends to a probability distribution on the algebra generated by the events. Probability axioms are therefore not cosmetic formatting rules. The equivalence depends on the idealized symmetric price and unrestricted-stake setup; a real limit-order book with different bids and asks requires the interval and portfolio tests below.

Real prediction markets differ from that ideal. A displayed price is rarely an unlimited two-sided commitment by one bookmaker. It may be a midpoint, a stale last trade, or a bid available for only a few contracts. Short sales, conversions, collateral, fees, and access can be asymmetric. The theorem supplies the logical benchmark; the order book determines whether anyone can implement the benchmark's portfolio.

## The basic families of constraints

The easiest coherence test is complementarity. If “Yes” and “No” are claims on the same binary condition and together redeem for exactly one dollar, their frictionless values sum to one. Kalshi's official order-book documentation encodes this directly: a Yes bid at price $x$ is equivalent to a No ask at $1-x$, so its API can return bids on both sides without separate asks. An analyst who adds the displayed Yes and No *asks*, however, may obtain more than one simply because both include the spread. That is not a probability failure; it is the cost of demanding immediate execution on both legs.

The next family is partition coherence. Suppose a presidential-nomination event has mutually exclusive, exhaustive outcomes for candidates $C_1,\ldots,C_n$. Their fair probabilities sum to one. At executable prices there are two distinct portfolio tests:

- If the sum of the asks is below one by more than all costs, buying one unit of every exhaustive outcome locks in a one-dollar terminal payout for less than one dollar.
- If the sum of the bids is above one and the venue permits the necessary selling, minting, or offsetting portfolio, selling the complete set can lock in proceeds above its liability.

The words “exhaustive,” “permits,” and “all costs” do most of the work. A candidate list without an “Other” outcome may not cover every state. A platform may cap positions or require separate collateral for each leg. Available size is the minimum depth across the portfolio, not the largest order on any one candidate. Quotes may move while a multi-leg order is being assembled.

Polymarket's official negative-risk documentation illustrates how venue design can make partition relationships economically real. In a negative-risk event, one No share in an outcome can be converted into one Yes share in every other outcome; the mechanism assumes only one outcome wins. Standard negative risk requires the full outcome set to be known at creation. Augmented negative risk allows placeholders and an explicit Other outcome, but the documentation warns that Other changes meaning as placeholders are named. That warning is a semantic coherence issue: the payoff partition itself can evolve, so a time series must preserve which outcome universe applied at each observation.

Monotonicity supplies another strong test. If event $A$ logically implies event $B$, then the payoff of $A$ is never greater than the payoff of $B$ in any state, so $p(A)\le p(B)$. “Candidate wins the presidency” should not exceed “candidate becomes the party nominee” when the first contract truly requires the second under matched rules. Yet ordinary-language implication can fail at the contract level. A replacement nominee, death, disqualification, faithless electors, a date cutoff, or different definitions of “wins” can create a state in which one contract pays and the other does not. Before flagging a monotonicity violation, construct an explicit state table from both rulebooks.

Joint and conditional contracts create the most tempting derived quantities. The identity

$$
P(A\mid B)=\frac{P(A\cap B)}{P(B)}
$$

holds when $P(B)>0$ and all terms describe the same event algebra. It does not license dividing any contract labeled “A if B” by a separate contract labeled “B.” Some conditional markets void or refund if $B$ does not occur; others resolve from a counterfactual rule; still others are ordinary joint events. Their prices can encode different collateral timing and payouts. A ratio of two midpoints also compounds two non-executable estimates and can become unstable when $P(B)$ is small. [[Conditional political probabilities]] therefore requires contract-level payout algebra, not just labels.

## Coherence is a feasibility problem

For a small family, inequalities can be checked by hand. For a large political market, the clean formulation is linear feasibility. Enumerate every logically possible terminal state, place a nonnegative probability $q_\omega$ on each, require the probabilities to sum to one, and constrain every contract's expected payoff to its observed value. If the linear system has a solution, the point-price vector is coherent. If it has none, no joint distribution can generate all the inputs.

Bid-ask data turn equalities into intervals. If contract $i$ has an executable bid $b_i$ and ask $a_i$, the analyst can ask whether there exists a state distribution with

$$
b_i\le \sum_{\omega}q_\omega x_i(\omega)\le a_i
$$

for every $i$. A feasible distribution means the books admit at least one common set of state prices. It does not establish that any particular midpoint is correct. An infeasible system suggests a possible executable inconsistency, but a portfolio optimizer must still include direction-specific prices, depth, fees, and trading constraints.

The geometry is useful. Every terminal state produces a vector of contract payoffs. Coherent point prices lie in the convex hull of those payoff vectors. Checking coherence is checking membership in that polytope. Finding a separating hyperplane when a price vector lies outside it is equivalent to finding a portfolio whose payoff reveals the inconsistency. This is the bridge between probability logic and no-arbitrage.

The computational problem can become hard because $n$ binary questions can generate as many as $2^n$ assignments, and political clauses introduce dependencies. Work on combinatorial prediction markets addresses exactly this scale problem. Dudík, Lahaie, and Pennock propose a constraint-generation market maker that begins with a tractable subset of constraints and adds violated logical constraints as they are discovered. Their application to large sets of election predictions shows why independently pricing related securities wastes information: a coherent mechanism can propagate restrictions across the family without listing every joint state in advance.

More general work on arbitrage-free combinatorial market making represents feasible outcomes with integer programs. The intellectual lesson for Rome is modest but important: a coherence scanner needs a formal event model. Text similarity between market titles is not enough. The system must know which outcome assignments are possible, which claims imply others, and how each security pays in each assignment.

## Apparent incoherence is not executable arbitrage

An arbitrage claim should specify a portfolio, prices, sizes, cash flows, and terminal payoff in every rule-consistent state. Saying that probabilities “sum to 103%” does not meet that standard. Several frictions can consume or invalidate the apparent edge.

First, use the correct side of the book. To buy an exhaustive set, sum asks at the intended size. To sell it, sum bids and verify that shorting or complete-set conversion exists. Midpoints cannot be executed, and last trades may have occurred at different times. A family can have midpoint prices summing to 0.97 while the executable asks sum to 1.08.

Second, size every leg simultaneously. A ten-cent discrepancy visible for one share is not available for a thousand shares if one outcome has only one share at its best quote. Walking the book raises acquisition cost, and partial execution leaves directional exposure. A reproducible test stores the full depth used, a common timestamp, and a policy for quote changes during execution.

Third, subtract all costs. Trading fees, conversion or redemption costs, blockchain transaction costs where relevant, withdrawal costs, collateral opportunity cost, taxes, and settlement delay can exceed a small gross gap. Fees can also depend on price, order type, or series. A correct scanner treats these as portfolio-level cash flows rather than applying one generic percentage.

Fourth, respect access and balance-sheet constraints. A price difference between venues is not a single-venue Dutch book unless the same participant can legally and operationally trade both, move or pre-position collateral, and bear platform and settlement risk. Capital locked until certification has a cost. An apparent cross-venue arbitrage can be a basis trade with residual risk.

Fifth, distinguish deterministic payout from adjudication risk. If two contracts name different resolution authorities, dates, fallbacks, or cancellation rules, there may be states in which they settle differently. The payoff difference is then real even if both titles say “wins the House.” [[Prediction-market resolution risk]] is not a nuisance adjustment to a logical test; it defines the propositions being tested.

Research on prediction-market misalignment reinforces this separation. Rothschild and Pennock's field work argues that published books can understate available price support and that logical contradictions can persist, while also emphasizing design changes that move the burden of finding inconsistencies from traders to the exchange. That evidence supports studying incoherence as a market-design and information-aggregation problem. It does not authorize treating every static screen discrepancy as free money.

## Contract semantics are the event algebra

The hardest part of a political coherence audit is not optimization. It is deciding what each event means. Consider three superficially nested claims: a party wins the most House seats, the party controls the House, and a member of the party is elected Speaker. Those events usually align, but vacancies, ties, independents, delayed races, coalitions, and organizing votes can separate them. A monotonicity constraint is valid only if the actual resolution rules establish implication in every admissible state.

A useful audit begins with a semantic matrix:

| Field | Coherence relevance |
| --- | --- |
| Exact contract identifier and version | Prevents joining a current rule to an old price. |
| Resolution proposition | Defines the contract's payoff indicator. |
| Source hierarchy and adjudicator | Determines which evidence creates settlement. |
| Deadline and effective time | Separates announcement, certification, seating, and control. |
| Void, refund, and cancellation rule | Adds terminal payoff states beyond Yes and No. |
| Exhaustiveness and mutual exclusivity | Determines whether a candidate set is a partition. |
| Other or placeholder behavior | Determines whether the outcome universe can change. |
| Payout and conversion rights | Determines which portfolios replicate one dollar. |

Void states are especially important. If every leg of a family refunds in the same void state, a complete-set identity may survive. If some legs void and others resolve, it may not. A binary model that forces every contract to zero or one can invent an arbitrage by deleting the venue's actual third settlement state.

Semantics should be versioned. When a venue clarifies a placeholder, edits a rule, changes a source, or adds an outcome, the old and new instruments should not be spliced into one coherent time series without a break. The same title can denote a different payoff vector after the change.

## From detection to coherent summaries

An analyst often wants one clean probability table even when raw quotes are inconsistent. Projection can help: find the coherent probability vector closest to the observed vector under a chosen loss function. Squared-error projection, likelihood-based methods, or spread-weighted optimization can produce a coherent summary. But projection is an analytical transformation, not a recovered market belief.

Every projection makes choices. Equal weighting treats a deep one-cent market like a stale twenty-cent market. Liquidity weighting can over-reward volume that does not represent current depth. Midpoint projection discards direction-specific execution. A Euclidean objective spreads correction across contracts differently from a log-loss objective. If the events are not a genuine partition, normalization manufactures a relationship that the contracts do not possess.

The best output is often an interval or a flagged contradiction rather than one repaired number. Report the raw bid and ask, the semantic constraints, whether the interval system is feasible, the smallest identified conflicting subset, and any projected table under an explicit method. Preserve raw observations so later work on [[Prediction-market calibration]] can score what the venue actually showed rather than a silently repaired series.

Coherence and calibration answer different questions. A forecast can be coherent but badly calibrated: probabilities obey all logical laws yet systematically overstate favorites. A set can be individually calibrated in coarse bins but jointly incoherent because related questions were generated independently. Coherence is internal consistency at a timestamp; calibration is empirical agreement with outcomes over repeated observations. Serious forecast aggregation needs both.

## A research-grade coherence workflow

For Rome's political desk, a defensible audit proceeds in this order:

1. Freeze a synchronized snapshot of bids, asks, sizes, fee rules, and timestamps; do not start from rounded display probabilities.
2. Retrieve and version the complete rules for every contract.
3. Define terminal states, including Other, void, cancellation, postponement, replacement, tie, and source-unavailability states.
4. Encode each contract's payoff in every state and document every claimed implication or partition.
5. Test whether one state-price distribution fits all executable intervals.
6. If infeasible, identify the smallest conflicting family and solve for a direction-specific portfolio using available depth.
7. Apply fees, collateral, settlement timing, conversion constraints, access limits, and platform risk.
8. Classify the result as coherent, point-estimate incoherent but not executable, executable within one venue, cross-venue basis with residual risk, or unresolved because semantics are ambiguous.

This workflow produces useful findings even when no arbitrage remains. A wide but coherent family shows how little the books jointly identify. A semantic mismatch explains why two dashboards disagree. A persistent non-executable violation can reveal fragmented liquidity or weak market-maker incentives. A true executable contradiction can reveal a design failure, but public research should document it without placing trades or encouraging account activity.

## Place in the map

Logical coherence sits between contract interpretation and forecast evaluation. It depends on [[From event-contract prices to probabilities]], [[Prediction-market resolution risk]], and [[Bid-ask spreads as probability uncertainty]]. It supplies the formal foundation for [[Conditional political probabilities]], [[Cross-market arbitrage and contract basis risk]], and any candidate, chamber-control, or policy dashboard that combines several markets. Its central rule is simple: do not ask whether numbers add up until the contracts' payoffs add up; do not call the remaining gap arbitrage until a complete portfolio is executable.
