Atmospheric retrieval is an inverse problem: spectra constrain families of atmospheres, not a unique literal atmosphere, because several physical and instrumental changes can produce nearly the same photons.

Up: [[Exometeorology research frontier 2026]]

## Why it matters

Claims about an exoplanet's molecules, temperature, clouds, and even weather usually pass through an atmospheric retrieval. A retrieval is more than curve fitting, but it is not a direct assay of the atmosphere. It is a probabilistic comparison between calibrated data and spectra generated by a deliberately simplified model. Its answer is conditional on the model's parameterization, opacity data, priors, treatment of the star and detector, and wavelength coverage.

The central discipline is therefore to separate evidence levels. A transit-depth spectrum or eclipse flux is **measured**. A water mixing ratio or pressure–temperature profile is **retrieved** under assumptions. A circulation pattern is usually **simulated**. Calling that pattern the explanation of the spectrum is **interpreted**. Predicting that another band or orbital phase will distinguish it is a **forecast**. Degeneracy lives mainly in the gap between the first two levels, but careless prose can propagate it through all five.

## From photons to a posterior

### 1. Photons become a data product

A detector records counts, not abundance. Calibration removes or estimates bias, dark current, flat-field structure, wavelength drift, cosmic rays, pointing-dependent throughput, background, and time-correlated detector behavior. A transit or eclipse analysis then fits an astrophysical light curve jointly or sequentially with an instrument model. The result may be a vector of transit depths, eclipse depths, or planet-to-star flux ratios,

$$
\mathbf d=(d_1,\ldots,d_N),
$$

with uncertainties and, ideally, a covariance matrix $\mathbf C$. Spectral bins can be correlated by common-mode corrections, stellar variability, or the reduction itself. Treating $\mathbf C$ as diagonal when it is not can make a posterior look tighter than the information warrants.

This first transformation is already inferential. Different defensible reductions can produce slightly different spectra. Gibson (2014) showed why marginalizing across plausible systematics models can be safer than selecting one deterministic correction and proceeding as though it were known. A retrieval cannot repair uncertainty silently discarded upstream.

### 2. A forward model predicts what the instrument would see

Let $\boldsymbol\theta$ collect atmospheric and nuisance parameters: molecular mixing ratios, a pressure–temperature profile, cloud pressure and opacity, reference radius, gravity, stellar-contamination terms, and calibration offsets. The forward model

$$
\mathbf m=F(\boldsymbol\theta,M)
$$

uses hydrostatic structure, molecular and atomic opacity, collision-induced absorption, scattering, cloud prescriptions, and radiative transfer to calculate a spectrum for model family $M$. It must then integrate that spectrum through the instrument response and binning used for $\mathbf d$.

In transmission, the wavelength-dependent occulting area is set by where the slant optical depth becomes order unity. An approximate scale height,

$$
H=\frac{k_B T}{\mu g},
$$

sets the vertical distance over which pressure changes appreciably. Stronger opacity moves the effective transit radius upward; larger $H$ amplifies spectral modulation. In emission, the emergent intensity is an integral of the source function weighted by optical depth, so different wavelengths sample broad and overlapping pressure ranges. Temperature gradients and opacity jointly decide whether a band appears in absorption, emission, or nearly flat.

No forward model is the atmosphere. One-dimensional columns average over limbs or hemispheres; chemical equilibrium suppresses degrees of freedom that disequilibrium chemistry permits; grey clouds compress particle microphysics into a few numbers. Greater physical detail can reduce one bias while adding weakly identifiable parameters. Retrieval design is a choice about which approximations are adequate for the data.

### 3. Likelihood and prior define the inference

For Gaussian residuals with known covariance, a common likelihood is

$$
\ln p(\mathbf d\mid\boldsymbol\theta,M)
=-\frac12(\mathbf d-\mathbf m)^T\mathbf C^{-1}(\mathbf d-\mathbf m)
-\frac12\ln|\mathbf C|+\text{constant}.
$$

The likelihood asks how probable the data would be if a parameter point and model were true. It is not the probability that the parameter point is true. Bayes' theorem supplies that different object:

$$
p(\boldsymbol\theta\mid\mathbf d,M)
=\frac{p(\mathbf d\mid\boldsymbol\theta,M)p(\boldsymbol\theta\mid M)}
{p(\mathbf d\mid M)}.
$$

The prior $p(\boldsymbol\theta\mid M)$ specifies parameter values admitted before these data. The denominator

$$
Z_M=p(\mathbf d\mid M)=\int p(\mathbf d\mid\boldsymbol\theta,M)p(\boldsymbol\theta\mid M)\,d\boldsymbol\theta
$$

is the Bayesian evidence. Sampling algorithms such as Markov-chain Monte Carlo or nested sampling approximate the posterior; nested sampling is also commonly used to estimate $Z_M$.

There is no parameterization-free “uninformative” prior. A distribution uniform in abundance differs from one uniform in log abundance; the latter allocates equal mass per decade. A prior uniform in temperature is not uniform in scale height. Bounds matter when the likelihood is broad. Fisher and Heng demonstrated that chemically unconstrained retrievals can be prior-sensitive and that abundance ratios or metallicity claims demand particular care. Prior sensitivity is not automatically a flaw: it is a diagnostic that the data contribute limited information along that direction.

### 4. The posterior is a landscape, not a best-fit atmosphere

The posterior can contain curved ridges, multiple modes, long tails, and hard boundaries. A median spectrum may not equal the spectrum generated by median parameters, because $F$ is nonlinear. One-dimensional credible intervals hide correlations. A complete reading therefore inspects joint posterior plots, posterior predictive spectra, residuals, and sensitivity to model and prior choices.

Identifiability is the key distinction. **Structural non-identifiability** occurs when different parameters produce exactly the same observable within the model. **Practical non-identifiability** occurs when their differences are smaller than the data's noise, resolution, or coverage can reveal. More samples from the same narrow band may shrink random error without breaking a structural similarity; a strategically different wavelength can be much more valuable.

## The major degeneracy families

### Abundance, temperature, gravity, and mean molecular weight

Transmission-feature amplitudes scale roughly with a few scale heights. Raising $T$ increases $H$; raising mean molecular weight $\mu$ or gravity $g$ decreases it. Changing an absorber's abundance shifts where optical depth unity occurs. Thus a deeper molecular feature can be produced by more absorber, a puffier atmosphere, or some combination. If mass and radius are uncertain, uncertainty in $g$ enters the atmospheric inference.

This mnemonic is useful but not universal. Real molecular bands have shapes, weak and strong lines, pressure broadening, and collision-induced continua that supply additional leverage. Welbanks and Madhusudhan found that broad optical-to-infrared data, flexible cloud coverage, alkali opacity, and H$_2$–H$_2$/H$_2$–He collision-induced absorption can constrain abundances more strongly than simplified analytic arguments imply. “A degeneracy exists” does not mean “nothing can be learned”; it means the constraint must be demonstrated for the actual data and model.

Emission adds a different abundance–temperature ambiguity. Spectral contrast depends on both the absorber opacity and the temperature difference between the pressures sampled inside and outside a band. A high abundance with a shallow temperature gradient can resemble a lower abundance with a steeper gradient. An inversion can turn absorption into emission. Sparse photometry is especially vulnerable because a flexible pressure–temperature profile can route a small number of points through many chemically different atmospheres.

### Clouds, haze, abundance, and reference level

An opaque high cloud truncates the atmospheric path and mutes features. Lower abundance also mutes features. Small-particle haze can impose a wavelength-dependent optical slope, but temperature, mean molecular weight, stellar heterogeneity, and uncertain scattering physics can change the same slope. Patchy limbs complicate the problem further: a one-dimensional homogeneous retrieval may fit a mixture of clear and cloudy chords by biasing abundance or temperature.

Cloud absence is not established merely because a cloud-free model fits. Clouds may be spectrally grey across the observed band or lie below the pressures probed. Conversely, a flat spectrum does not uniquely establish clouds; high mean molecular weight, weak absorbers, stellar contamination, or low signal-to-noise can also flatten it.

Transmission spectra measure radius relative to a reference pressure that is generally not observed directly. Shifting the chosen reference radius and pressure changes the atmospheric column assigned above it. This produces a radius–reference-pressure correlation and can couple to abundance. Detailed numerical retrievals show that its practical impact depends on wavelength coverage, continuum opacity, and model completeness; it should not be promoted into a blanket claim that transmission abundances are unknowable.

### Planetary radius and stellar radius

The leading transit depth is approximately

$$
D(\lambda)\simeq\left[\frac{R_p(\lambda)}{R_\star}\right]^2.
$$

An uncertain $R_\star$ therefore correlates strongly with the inferred $R_p$. Novais and collaborators' retrievals of 38 Hubble/WFC3 spectra recovered this expected correlation, while finding that allowing stellar radius and gravity to vary did not generally drive water abundance in their sample. This is a useful warning against translating a visible parameter correlation into an assumed bias in every downstream quantity.

### Stellar contamination and planetary opacity

Transmission spectroscopy compares light from an occulted chord with the disk-integrated star. If unocculted spots or faculae have spectra different from the transit chord, the ratio acquires wavelength structure even if the planet's effective radius were constant. Rackham, Apai, and Giampapa formalized this “transit light source effect” and showed that heterogeneous M-dwarf photospheres can imprint signals comparable to planetary atmospheric features. Limb darkening, spot crossings, stellar rotation, and epoch-to-epoch active-region evolution add related uncertainties.

A planetary-only retrieval may explain stellar structure by changing molecular abundance, haze slope, or cloud coverage. Adding stellar parameters can reveal the ambiguity, but those parameters may themselves be weakly constrained. Simultaneous or contemporaneous stellar monitoring, repeated transits, and broad spectral coverage help; they do not make the correction exact.

### Instrument and reduction systematics

Detector ramps, slit losses, pointing drift, saturation behavior, spectral trace motion, background subtraction, and inter-instrument offsets can mimic low-order spectral structure or alter individual bins. When a nuisance model is fitted simultaneously, astrophysical and instrumental parameters can exchange explanatory power. When it is fixed beforehand, its uncertainty may disappear from the reported atmospheric posterior.

Combining instruments extends wavelength coverage but introduces offsets and different calibration histories. Agreement from one pipeline is not replication. Useful robustness checks include independent reductions, injection–recovery tests, alternate baseline and noise models, residual autocorrelation, leave-one-visit or leave-one-instrument-out analyses, and retrievals that propagate spectral covariance.

### Chemistry and opacity-model degeneracies

Molecules often absorb in overlapping bands. Incomplete line lists, inaccurate line wings, collision-induced absorption, or omitted species can force included absorbers to compensate. Equilibrium-chemistry retrievals reduce dimension by tying abundances to metallicity, elemental ratios, temperature, and pressure; free-chemistry retrievals permit independent mixing ratios. Agreement between them can be reassuring, but disagreement does not say which is correct unless the equilibrium, quenching, condensation, and photochemical assumptions are tested.

Barstow and colleagues' retrieval-code comparison found broad agreement under controlled setups while also exposing differences caused by forward-model choices and parameterizations. Code agreement is most informative when inputs, opacities, and assumptions are harmonized; otherwise it can conflate numerical implementation with scientific model uncertainty.

## What constitutes evidence?

A retrieval supports **parameter estimation** when the data meaningfully update a justified prior, the result is stable to reasonable prior changes, and the inference procedure is validated on synthetic or injected signals. Updating need not mean only a narrower marginal: the posterior may shift, become multimodal, or constrain a joint parameter combination while leaving one-dimensional widths broad. It supports **molecular attribution** more strongly when removing the molecule worsens predictive adequacy across its multiple bands and plausible clouds, stellar contamination, other absorbers, and systematics have been compared. A visually recognizable bump is not by itself a detection.

Report evidence in layers:

1. the calibrated observable and covariance;
2. the posterior conditional on a named model, opacity set, and priors;
3. sensitivity across plausible reductions and model families;
4. posterior predictive performance, including held-out data when possible;
5. the physical interpretation, explicitly distinguished from the retrieval.

Credible intervals answer a within-model probability question. They do not include unknown unknowns or automatically cover model misspecification. A narrow posterior can be precisely wrong if the atmosphere, star, or detector lies outside the model family.

## Diagnostics that expose degeneracy

Corner plots are a beginning, not a verdict. Useful diagnostics include:

- **Prior-to-posterior comparison:** parameters whose marginal posterior reproduces the prior are not data-constrained, even if a median is printable.
- **Joint and conditional posteriors:** curved or multimodal relationships reveal compensating parameters hidden by marginals.
- **Posterior predictive checks:** simulate spectra or light curves from posterior draws and compare residual structure, not only the maximum-likelihood curve.
- **Retrieval on synthetic data:** inject a known atmosphere through the actual cadence, noise, binning, and reduction when feasible; test coverage across many truths, not one favorable case.
- **Prior and parameterization sweeps:** repeat with log versus linear abundance priors, alternative pressure–temperature profiles, free versus equilibrium chemistry, and multiple cloud prescriptions.
- **Data ablation:** remove a band, visit, instrument, or suspicious bin. A claim that disappears when unrelated leverage is removed needs explanation.
- **Independent pipelines and codes:** convergence across separately implemented reductions and retrievals is stronger than repeated sampling of the same processed spectrum.
- **Information-content measures:** quantify how much the posterior departs from the prior, while remembering that the result remains parameterization- and model-dependent.
- **Residual and noise tests:** inspect time correlation, wavelength correlation, outliers, and whether nuisance terms absorb injected astrophysical signals.

Sampling convergence is necessary but different from scientific identifiability. Thousands of effective samples from a wrong or incomplete model produce a well-sampled wrong posterior.

## Model comparison—and its limits

Bayes factors compare evidences, $B_{12}=Z_1/Z_2$. Evidence rewards likelihood fit but averages it over the prior volume, imposing an Occam penalty on flexible models whose extra parameter space does not predict the data well. This can compare, for example, a model with and without a molecule or two cloud prescriptions.

But a Bayes factor is conditional on the exact model pair, likelihood, parameterization, and normalized priors. Broadening an abundance prior can lower a molecule model's evidence by diluting its high-likelihood region. An omitted alternative—stellar contamination, correlated noise, or another absorber—is not vindicated or rejected by a comparison it never entered. Evidence estimates also have numerical uncertainty. Converting a Bayes factor mechanically into “sigma” can import assumptions that do not hold, especially near prior boundaries or for non-nested models.

Information criteria are faster approximations, not interchangeable substitutes for evidence. Cross-validation asks a different and often valuable question: whether a model predicts held-out data. No scalar ranking certifies that the winning model is physically complete. Model averaging can propagate uncertainty across an enumerated set, but cannot average over models nobody considered.

## Observations that can discriminate

The best follow-up is not always “more precision.” It targets a direction where leading explanations diverge.

- **Wider wavelength coverage** samples several bands of the same molecule, optical scattering and alkalis, infrared continua, and cloud-sensitive regions. It often breaks abundance–cloud–temperature tradeoffs better than denser sampling of one band.
- **Higher spectral resolution** resolves line forests and Doppler shifts. Combining high resolution with low-resolution absolute spectral shape can constrain complementary aspects of abundance, temperature, and velocity, as Brogi and Line demonstrated methodologically.
- **Multiple geometries**—transmission, eclipse, phase curves, and direct emission—probe different limbs, hemispheres, and pressure-weighting functions. Agreement should not be assumed because the atmosphere is spatially variable.
- **Repeated epochs** test whether a feature follows the planet, star, detector state, or time-variable weather. Repetition across instruments is stronger than repetition inside one calibration family.
- **Stellar characterization** through rotational monitoring, activity indicators, spot-crossing information, and contemporaneous spectra constrains—but rarely eliminates—the transit light source effect.
- **Independent mass and radius constraints** reduce gravity and scale-height uncertainty; improved ephemerides and orbital geometry prevent avoidable light-curve correlations.
- **Polarization, optical slopes, and mid-infrared features** can constrain particle size and cloud composition when models predict distinct signatures.
- **Designed retrieval experiments** should forecast expected information gain under competing models, then choose bands and cadence that maximize separation rather than merely signal-to-noise.

The conclusion to carry into [[Exoplanet phase curves]], [[Transmission spectroscopy]], or any object dossier is simple: an atmospheric story is robust only to the extent that alternative routes from atmosphere, star, instrument, and model to the same data have been actively tested.

## Open questions

- Which retrieval conclusions remain stable when stellar, detector, cloud, chemistry, and temperature-model uncertainty are varied jointly rather than one family at a time?
- How should retrieval studies report uncertainty from opacity databases and line-shape theory alongside statistical posterior uncertainty?
- When does a spatially resolved or time-dependent retrieval add identifiable information rather than merely redistribute a one-dimensional degeneracy?
- Which observation-design metric best balances expected information gain against the risk that every proposed model is misspecified?
- Can community blind challenges establish empirical coverage rates for molecular detections and abundance intervals across instruments and atmosphere classes?
