JWST transmission spectra measure a strong limb asymmetry on WASP-94A b; a cloudy, cooler morning limb and clearer, hotter evening limb are the authors' retrieval-based interpretation.

Up: [[Exometeorology research frontier 2026]]

## Evidence ladder

- **Measured:** the team reports a $6\sigma$ difference between the two limb-resolved transmission spectra.
- **Retrieved:** the morning limb is cloud-covered at reported $11\sigma$ significance, while the evening limb is hotter and has strong water absorption, reported at $10\sigma$.
- **Interpreted:** condensates form on the cooler morning side and evaporate as circulation carries them toward the hotter evening side.

The last step is a physical explanation of the spectra, not direct tracking of individual clouds. The result also shows why a one-dimensional, limb-averaged retrieval can bias inferred abundances when the terminators differ.

## Why it matters

The paper turns spatially resolved transit spectroscopy into a test of cloud microphysics and circulation. It is stronger than a generic “weather detected” claim because the measured asymmetry and the inferred cloud cycle remain explicitly separate.

## Open questions

- How sensitive are the limb temperatures and cloud evidence to opacity choices and retrieval priors?
- Which condensate species and particle-size distributions remain compatible with the spectra?
- Can repeated transits distinguish persistent longitudinal structure from time-variable weather?
