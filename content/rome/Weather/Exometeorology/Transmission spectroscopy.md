Transmission spectroscopy measures the wavelength-dependent fraction of starlight blocked during transit; atmospheric composition is a model-dependent inference from the effective occulting area of the planet’s limb.

Up: [[Exometeorology research frontier 2026]]

## Why this method matters

When a planet transits, its opaque body blocks part of the stellar disk while a thin annulus of atmosphere filters starlight along long, grazing paths. At wavelengths where gases, aerosols, or atoms are more opaque, the atmosphere becomes opaque at a higher altitude and the apparent planet is slightly larger. Repeating a transit measurement across wavelength produces a **transmission spectrum**.

The technique has exceptional leverage because the slant path through the limb is much longer than a vertical path. It can reveal spectral modulation from trace absorbers even when the atmosphere contributes essentially none of the system’s total luminosity. But the detector does not observe a molecule, pressure, temperature, cloud, or wind directly. It observes time- and wavelength-dependent counts. Calibration and light-curve fitting yield transit depths. A forward model then connects those depths to an effective radius, and a retrieval estimates which combinations of atmospheric, stellar, orbital, and instrumental parameters are compatible with the data.

That evidence ladder is essential:

1. **Measured:** calibrated relative fluxes and fitted wavelength-dependent transit depths, with uncertainties and covariance.
2. **Retrieved:** abundances, temperature, cloud properties, or reference radius inferred under a stated forward model and priors.
3. **Simulated:** limb structure, circulation, chemistry, or clouds generated by a physical model.
4. **Interpreted:** a proposed atmospheric story connecting measurements, retrievals, and simulations.
5. **Forecast:** an unmeasured prediction for another wavelength, epoch, or instrument.

“Water detected” is shorthand for evidence favoring opacity shaped like water within a model comparison. It should not erase this chain.

## Transit-depth geometry

For a uniformly bright star and an opaque circular planet, the transit depth is approximately

$$
D(\lambda)\simeq \left[\frac{R_{\rm eff}(\lambda)}{R_*}\right]^2,
$$

where $R_*$ is stellar radius and $R_{\rm eff}(\lambda)$ is the planet’s wavelength-dependent effective occulting radius. Real transit fitting includes orbital geometry and stellar limb darkening: the planet blocks different surface brightnesses as it crosses the disk. The measured spectrum is often a vector of radius ratios or transit depths after jointly or sequentially fitting a transit model and an instrument/systematics model.

If atmospheric opacity shifts the effective altitude by $\Delta z\ll R_p$, the spectral modulation is

$$
\Delta D\simeq \frac{2R_p\Delta z}{R_*^2}.
$$

The geometry explains target selection. A large planet around a small star gives a larger signal. A low-gravity, hot, hydrogen-rich atmosphere has a large pressure scale height,

$$
H=\frac{k_B T}{\mu m_H g},
$$

where $T$ is temperature, $\mu$ is mean molecular weight in hydrogen masses, and $g$ is gravity. Strong molecular bands can span several scale heights in an ideal clear atmosphere, giving a rough feature amplitude of $2R_p N H/R_*^2$ for $N$ effective scale heights. This is an observing heuristic, not an abundance measurement: $N$ depends logarithmically on opacity and is changed by clouds, refraction, composition gradients, and limb structure.

## Why the slant path is powerful

Consider a ray with impact parameter $b$ passing through a spherical atmosphere. Its optical depth is

$$
\tau_\lambda(b)=\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}\sum_i n_i(s)\,\sigma_{i,\lambda}[T(s),p(s)]\,ds,
$$

with number density $n_i$, cross section $\sigma_{i,\lambda}$, and distance $s$ along the ray. The occulted area is not simply the disk below the altitude where $\tau=1$. Rays are partially attenuated, so the effective area is an integral over impact parameter,

$$
\pi R_{\rm eff}^2(\lambda)=\pi R_0^2+2\pi\int_{R_0}^{\infty}\left[1-e^{-\tau_\lambda(b)}\right]b\,db.
$$

This expression assumes the disk below $R_0$ is fully opaque; $R_0$ must therefore be chosen deep enough to serve as the lower boundary of the calculation. In an isothermal, hydrostatic atmosphere with an exponential density profile, a grazing ray near altitude $z$ samples a slant column enhanced over the vertical column by roughly $\sqrt{2\pi R_p/H}$. For a hot Jupiter this factor can be tens. Effective transit altitude therefore occurs where the *slant* optical depth is of order unity, often at much lower pressure than a vertical optical-depth estimate suggests.

Analytic treatments show that effective altitude varies approximately as the logarithm of opacity. For opacity $\sigma(\lambda)$ and otherwise fixed conditions,

$$
z(\lambda)\sim H\left[C+\ln\frac{\sigma(\lambda)}{\sigma_{\rm ref}}+\ln\frac{X}{X_{\rm ref}}+\ln\frac{P_0}{P_{\rm ref}}+\ldots\right],
$$

where $X$ is mixing ratio, $P_0$ is the pressure assigned to the reference radius, the reference quantities make the logarithms dimensionless, and $C$ groups those reference choices with geometry and thermodynamic factors. Only changes and parameter combinations have physical meaning; taking a logarithm of a dimensional cross section or pressure by itself would not. A power-law scattering cross section $\sigma\propto\lambda^\alpha$ then gives the idealized slope

$$
\frac{dz}{d\ln\lambda}=\alpha H.
$$

The relation explains why a resolved Rayleigh-like slope can constrain the product of temperature and inverse mean molecular weight if gravity and the opacity law are known. In practice, stellar heterogeneity, aerosols, non-isothermal structure, unknown particle physics, and calibration offsets can generate or alter slopes.

## The reference-radius problem

A broadband transit light curve measures an apparent radius, but it does not label the pressure at that radius. Forward models therefore specify a pair $(R_0,P_0)$. Hydrostatic integration maps altitude to pressure above that boundary. Changing $R_0$ and $P_0$ shifts the atmospheric column encountered by grazing rays, and abundance changes opacity through the absorber number density. In restricted wavelength coverage these effects can compensate one another.

This produces a reference-radius–reference-pressure–abundance degeneracy. In the simplest isothermal expression, the spectrum depends partly on products such as $X P_0$, so the absolute mixing ratio cannot be separated from the boundary pressure without additional information. Clouds add another boundary: an optically thick cloud deck truncates the chord column below its top and can mute all bands. High-altitude haze adds a wavelength-dependent continuum. Increasing mean molecular weight or gravity shrinks $H$ and also suppresses modulation. A flat spectrum can consequently mean no atmosphere, a compact high-$\mu$ atmosphere, high clouds or haze, refraction, weak absorbers over the bandpass, or insufficient precision.

The degeneracy is real but not absolute. Broad wavelength coverage can constrain multiple opacity sources with different shapes. Collision-induced absorption, alkali wings, Rayleigh-like scattering, resolved line structure, and continuum windows can help anchor pressure or composition. Welbanks and Madhusudhan showed with detailed retrievals of hydrogen-rich atmospheres that the radius–pressure correlation can be well characterized and need not destroy abundance constraints when optical and infrared data and appropriate opacity/cloud models are combined. Heng and Kitzmann showed why restricted WFC3-like coverage and semi-analytic assumptions leave a more severe normalization ambiguity. The apparent disagreement is a lesson about model and data scope, not permission to claim either that abundances are always precise or fundamentally unknowable.

Retrievals must therefore report the parameterization, priors, opacity database, cloud prescription, reference boundary, stellar model, and sensitivity to alternative models. Posterior precision within one forward model is not the same as robustness to model misspecification. See [[Atmospheric retrieval degeneracies]] for the broader inverse-problem framework.

## The star is part of the spectrum

Transmission spectroscopy is a differential comparison between light from the portion of the stellar surface crossed by the planet and light from the entire unresolved stellar disk. If those sources have different spectra, the inferred transit depth acquires wavelength structure that is not planetary.

Suppose the transit chord has specific intensity $I_{\rm chord}(\lambda)$ while the disk average is $I_{\rm disk}(\lambda)$. Schematically,

$$
D_{\rm obs}(\lambda)\simeq D_{\rm planet}(\lambda)\frac{I_{\rm chord}(\lambda)}{I_{\rm disk}(\lambda)}.
$$

Unocculted cool spots can make the star dimmer and redder than the transit chord, increasing and tilting apparent depths. Bright faculae can act in the opposite direction or imprint line-dependent structure. Occulted spots produce anomalies in the transit time series; masking or fitting them does not automatically solve the effect of unocculted regions. The distribution evolves with stellar rotation and activity.

Rackham, Apai, and Giampapa termed this the **transit light source effect**. Their modeling shows that contamination is target-dependent: active cool stars are especially challenging because spots and faculae can cover substantial fractions of the disk and possess molecular features, while inactive FGK stars generally present weaker contamination. Stellar limb darkening is also wavelength-dependent and imperfect stellar-atmosphere models can bias fitted depths. Repeated transits, rotational monitoring, spot-crossing analysis, multiwavelength stellar constraints, and retrievals that include heterogeneity parameters can reduce but not universally eliminate the problem.

A claimed planetary slope or band should therefore be tested against plausible stellar spectra, particularly for M-dwarf hosts and active stars. The measured depth belongs to the star–planet system until that test is made.

## Instrument and reduction systematics

An ideal transmission spectrum is much smaller than the raw changes affecting many detectors. A pipeline must correct or model bias and dark signals, nonlinearity, saturation, cosmic rays, flat-field variations, background, wavelength calibration, pointing drift, detector persistence, ramp-like behavior, scan-direction offsets, and time-correlated noise. The exact list depends on instrument and observing mode. Ground-based spectra additionally face Earth’s variable telluric absorption, seeing, slit losses, and changing airmass.

Analysis choices can change the result: extraction aperture, background region, spectral binning, bad-pixel treatment, limb-darkening priors, orbital parameters, baseline length, common-mode correction, and functional form for systematics. Fitting white and spectroscopic light curves sequentially can transmit uncertainty differently than a joint fit. Combining instruments or visits introduces offsets because each bandpass has distinct calibration, epochs, and stellar states.

Robust work examines residuals for autocorrelation, compares plausible systematics models, performs injection–recovery tests where appropriate, propagates covariance, and checks whether a feature survives independent reductions. Error bars based only on photon noise are incomplete if nuisance-model uncertainty dominates. A spectral point is **measured** only with reference to its reduction and light-curve model; it is not a raw property of the atmosphere.

JWST has transformed wavelength coverage and precision, but it has not removed these principles. Detector behavior, saturation strategy, trace motion, background, order overlap, and pipeline versions remain relevant. Cross-instrument agreement is powerful only when offsets and stellar variability are modeled rather than silently normalized away.

## Where measurement ends and retrieval begins

The direct result of a conventional low-resolution analysis is a set of wavelength bins with fitted transit depths and a likelihood or covariance description. An absorption-shaped modulation is still a measurement of relative occulting area. Assigning it to H$_2$O, CO$_2$, Na, aerosols, or another opacity source is a model comparison. Turning that identification into an abundance requires assumptions about temperature, gravity, mean molecular weight, other absorbers, clouds, reference pressure, and often chemical uniformity.

Retrieval is valuable precisely because it makes this conditional inference explicit. A forward model calculates $R_{\rm eff}(\lambda)$, convolves it with the instrument response, and evaluates a likelihood against the depths. Bayesian or other algorithms explore parameter combinations. Evidence ratios, posterior predictive checks, leave-one-instrument-out tests, and alternative opacity or cloud models ask different robustness questions. None converts an underdetermined spectrum into a direct atmospheric sample.

High-resolution transmission spectroscopy uses time-dependent Doppler shifts to separate planet lines from stellar and telluric spectra. Cross-correlation can detect a resolved line pattern even when individual lines are too weak to detect separately. Its amplitude and velocity likewise depend on templates, line lists, temperature, abundance, clouds, rotation, limb geometry, and removal of the spectral continuum during processing. The measured velocity can mix orbital ephemeris error, rotation, morning–evening weighting, and winds. A cross-correlation peak is therefore not automatically a volume mixing ratio or a global wind speed.

## What spatial claims are supported

Transmission primarily probes the narrow annulus near the planet’s **terminator**, not the dayside, nightside, or global mean. Even “the terminator” is a mixture. During most of transit, rays traverse both morning and evening limbs; ingress and egress weight them differently. The chord spans a range of latitudes and pressures. Refraction, clouds, scale height, and opacity determine which parts contribute. A one-dimensional retrieved profile is therefore an effective representation of a three-dimensional limb.

Horizontal inhomogeneity creates nonlinear averaging. A hot clear limb with a large scale height can dominate spectral modulation over a cool cloudy limb even if both cover equal azimuth. Molecular dissociation or condensation may make composition different on morning and evening sides. Combining their spectra is not equivalent to evaluating one column at the mean temperature and abundance. Three-dimensional forward models have demonstrated biases when such spectra are fitted with one-dimensional retrievals.

Spatial information is possible, but it demands specific data and models. Ingress–egress differences can in principle separate limbs; high-resolution line shifts and broadening can constrain combinations of orbital motion, winds, and rotation; time-resolved spectra can weight different chord regions. These signals are small and degenerate with ephemeris, stellar line distortions, limb darkening, instrumental drift, and asymmetric clouds. “Wind detected” should specify the measured Doppler quantity and the atmospheric model connecting it to circulation.

Transmission spectra do not make a weather map. A single transit is a spatially integrated limb spectrum. It cannot establish temporal variability without a comparison epoch and a repeatable difference that survives instrument and stellar tests.

## What temporal claims are supported

Repeated transits can test whether the system’s transmission spectrum changes. A credible atmospheric-variability claim requires comparable reductions, control of visit-dependent systematics, and evidence that stellar surface evolution is insufficient to explain the difference. Instrument upgrades or pipeline changes can masquerade as temporal evolution. Multiple comparisons across many wavelength bins inflate false-positive risk unless accounted for.

If repeatable variability remains, transmission data may constrain changing cloud opacity, escaping gas, chemistry, or terminator structure. The causal label is still interpreted or simulated rather than measured. Atmospheric advection and cloud-evolution timescales can guide cadence, but the transit samples once per orbit and may alias faster processes. Nonrepeatability may also be informative: it can bound large changes in the effective limb over the pressures and wavelengths observed. It does not prove meteorological steadiness at unprobed levels.

The conservative hierarchy is: measure epoch-specific depths; demonstrate a statistically and reduction-robust difference; test stellar and instrumental alternatives; then compare atmospheric models that predict variability. Only after these steps does the result contribute to [[What counts as weather on an exoplanet]].

## Practical interpretation checklist

Before treating a transmission spectrum as atmospheric evidence, ask:

- Which instrument, mode, wavelength range, visits, and reduction produced the depths?
- Are uncertainties correlated, and do independent pipelines recover the feature?
- Which pressures and limb regions contribute at each wavelength?
- Which opacity source is favored over explicit alternatives, at what model-comparison strength?
- How do the abundance posterior and detection significance change with clouds, reference radius, temperature structure, and opacity database?
- Were stellar spots, faculae, limb darkening, and epoch variability modeled?
- Is the claim measured, retrieved, simulated, interpreted, or forecast?
- What new wavelength, resolution, or epoch would distinguish the leading explanations?

This discipline preserves what makes transmission spectroscopy powerful: not a direct photograph of an atmosphere, but a precise, repeatable geometric experiment whose inference chain can be tested.

## Open questions

- Which retrieval diagnostics most reliably expose bias from a two-dimensional morning–evening terminator when the data do not support a full three-dimensional model?
- Can coordinated stellar monitoring constrain the unocculted photosphere tightly enough for temperate M-dwarf planets, or must stellar heterogeneity remain a dominant latent variable?
- What minimum number and cadence of repeat transits can distinguish atmospheric variability from evolving stellar contamination for a given host activity level?
- Which pressure anchors—collision-induced absorption, alkali wings, or complementary emission data—most improve absolute abundance constraints for different planet classes?
- How reproducible are current high-precision spectra across independent reductions and pipeline versions when covariance is propagated consistently?
