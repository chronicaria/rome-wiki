The House settlement gives Duke new ways to pay and support football players, but the public record does not reveal Duke football's pool, player-by-player payments, or final allocation formula.

Up: [[Duke football]]

## Summary

The shorthand “revenue sharing” hides three connected changes. Participating Division I schools may provide direct payments and other new benefits to athletes up to an annual institution-wide cap. Sport-specific scholarship ceilings have been replaced by roster limits, so a school may scholarship every athlete on a declared roster if it chooses and can afford to do so. Third-party NIL remains possible, but settlement-related systems impose reporting and review rules intended to distinguish real commercial NIL activity from disguised recruiting inducements.

For Duke football, the immediate consequence is not that a known percentage of football revenue automatically flows to the team. Duke has discretion within an institution-wide benefits cap. Its leaders must decide how much of that optional capacity to use, how to divide it among sports and athletes, how many new scholarships to fund, and how to pay for the package. Duke's own public governance records are unusually useful here: they say football, men's basketball, and women's basketball were priorities for revenue sharing; that faculty advisers generally preferred scholarships over direct revenue-share payments; that leaders expected philanthropy rather than base operating funds to support new spending; and that Duke did not expect to reach the national maximum immediately. Those statements describe direction, not a published football budget.

## What the national framework actually does

The settlement resolved three related antitrust cases—House, Hubbard, and Carter—and received final court approval in June 2025. The damages side provides about $2.8 billion in back payments to eligible former and current athletes. The forward-looking side changed what schools may provide going forward. Duke football's competitive planning is mainly affected by the second part, while Duke and NCAA distributions also bear costs associated with the first.

For institutions in the defendant conferences, including the ACC, the new framework permits direct financial payments and other benefits to athletes. NCAA implementation materials estimated the first 2025–26 benefits pool at roughly $20.5 million per school. The pool is not a football cap and it is not a mandatory payroll. It is an institution-wide ceiling based on a settlement formula tied to 22 percent of specified average revenues for the defendant-conference schools. NCAA guidance says the amount is recalculated in three-year periods and rises four percent in the second and third year of each period. The compliance year runs July 1 through June 30.

What counts against that ceiling is broader than a check labeled “revenue share.” Institutional payments for use of an athlete's NIL count. So can other new payments, benefits, and expenses that were not already permissible under the pre-settlement rules. NCAA legislation requires participating institutions to report covered benefits and upload agreements through the cap-management system. Previously permissible benefits generally remain outside the new cap unless the rules specify otherwise. An institution that exceeds the cap carries the overage into the next available cap year under NCAA legislation.

That distinction matters when reading any claim about Duke's spending. A reported institution-wide number could combine cash payments, institutional NIL agreements, and other cap-counting benefits. It would not necessarily equal the amount available to football, and it would not reveal how much any player received. Conversely, a football player's total compensation could include a Duke agreement plus legitimate third-party NIL activity outside the institutional cap.

The new rules also do not turn athletes into salaried university employees by themselves. They authorize and regulate defined payments and benefits under the settlement and NCAA legislation. Employment status remains a separate legal and policy question. Calling every payment a salary or assuming a collective-bargained payroll would go beyond the public framework.

## Scholarships and the 105-player roster

The roster change is as important to football as direct cash. Before the settlement, FBS football operated with an 85-equivalency scholarship limit and often carried a larger roster that included walk-ons. Under the implemented settlement rules, a participating school has a football roster limit of 105. Sport-specific scholarship limits are gone for participating institutions, which means a school may provide scholarships to every player on that roster. It does not have to do so.

For Duke, this creates a choice rather than a command. At one extreme, it could approach 105 football scholarships, adding as many as 20 above the old FBS ceiling. At another, it could operate below the 105-person roster maximum or leave some roster positions without athletics aid. Every added scholarship has a real university cost, and football is only one of Duke's varsity programs competing for resources. Duke's University Priorities Committee estimated in late 2024 that fully exploiting added scholarship flexibility across athletics could cost as much as $37 million annually, while clearly saying the university was not required to make that maximum investment.

The NCAA also adopted protections for athletes caught in the transition. “Designated student-athletes” who were on a 2024–25 roster or had been recruited or assured a 2025–26 place before the specified cutoff could be exempted from roster limits for the remainder of their eligibility. That exception prevented the 105 limit from functioning as an immediate hard cut for every incumbent. It is transitional, however, not a permanent expansion of the normal football limit.

Roster economics therefore change recruiting and development. A scholarship offer can now be extended deeper into the roster, potentially reducing the old distinction between scholarship players and preferred walk-ons. But a 105-player hard roster also makes each place more deliberate. Players receiving athletics aid or settlement-related benefits must be included on the submitted roster, and the rules restrict participation outside it. Coaches must balance high-school recruiting, transfer additions, developmental prospects, specialists, injury contingencies, and retention within one finite list.

For [[Duke football recruiting and development]], expanded aid could be a genuine advantage if Duke chooses to fund it. The university can pair education, athlete support, and direct benefits more comprehensively. Yet the same national rule gives richer competitors the option to fund 105 scholarships too. The new ceiling creates competitive capacity; it does not itself close Duke's resource gap.

## What Duke has said publicly

Duke's governance record supports several concrete conclusions, though most statements were made while the settlement was pending or in early implementation.

First, Duke viewed the change as a major incremental cost. A December 2024 athletics financial overview reported that roughly 75 percent of athletics costs were covered by external sources such as media-rights revenue, gifts, ticket sales, and conference distributions, with about 25 percent supported by the university. The same report said university support had risen amid football investment and athletics operating deficits beginning in fiscal 2021. Against that backdrop, the House framework added possible direct-benefit spending, scholarship costs, and an expected reduction in NCAA distributions.

Second, Duke identified football as one of three revenue-sharing priorities. Athletic director Nina King told the University Priorities Committee that football, men's basketball, and women's basketball would be the top priorities for revenue sharing. That is the strongest public basis for saying football will receive a meaningful share. It does not establish an amount or percentage.

Third, Duke's faculty governance discussion favored scholarships as the general priority. In May 2025 Academic Council minutes, then-president Vincent Price described the joint faculty group's view: prioritize scholarships over revenue share as a general matter and fund the additional commitment through philanthropy rather than base operating dollars. Price also said Duke did not plan to run up to the full national cap immediately and that most teams expected to operate below their maximum roster limits. He described the situation as actively evolving.

Those statements are not contradictory. Duke can prioritize football for direct payments while prioritizing scholarships across athletics overall. It can also phase spending, fund fewer than the maximum 105 football scholarships, or allocate different mixes of cash and aid by sport. The public materials indicate a portfolio approach, not an all-or-nothing choice.

Fourth, Duke expected outside NIL arrangements to persist but hoped more donor money would flow through the institution. The settlement does not ban collectives or genuine third-party NIL. It subjects many associated-entity deals to disclosure and review for valid business purpose and reasonable compensation. Price said he expected charitable-giving incentives and Duke's institutional culture to shift much activity from independent collectives into university-administered structures. That was a forecast, not evidence that all football collective activity ended.

## ACC implications

Duke does not make these decisions in isolation. The ACC is one of the defendant conferences, so its members operate inside the settlement's participation and enforcement structure. Conference distributions are an important external revenue source for Duke athletics. The ACC's media-rights position, College Football Playoff receipts, bowl revenue, and NCAA basketball distributions therefore influence the resources from which Duke can support athletics even when they do not mechanically determine a football payment pool.

Duke's University Priorities Committee also noted the ACC's “success initiative,” which distributes additional conference revenue based on performance in football, men's basketball, and women's basketball. The incentive reinforces a cycle: football success can increase distributions; larger distributions can make competitive investment easier; and investment may improve the odds of future success. It also means Duke faces an opportunity cost when peers use the full benefits cap or aggressively add scholarships.

The ACC connection should not be overstated. No public ACC rule found for this note requires Duke to give football a fixed share of its institutional benefits pool. The national cap is not a conference revenue-sharing formula, and Duke's internal allocation remains a university decision subject to applicable law, settlement enforcement, NCAA rules, donor restrictions, and university governance.

## Likely football consequences

The most direct competitive effect is a new recruiting offer stack. A prospect or transfer can evaluate an athletics scholarship, institutional payments, third-party NIL opportunities, playing time, coaching, education, and support services together. Duke's academic value remains a differentiator, but it no longer substitutes for a credible benefits structure in a market where comparable ACC programs can offer both education and cash.

Retention becomes a recurring budget decision. Multiyear agreements are allowed, but payments count against the cap in the year provided. Duke must reserve enough flexibility for returning contributors, new recruits, transfers, and unexpected roster needs. Spending the entire football allocation early could constrain later retention; leaving too much unused could weaken recruiting. The optimal plan is therefore dynamic rather than a single preseason number.

The framework may also increase internal differentiation. High-impact quarterbacks, pass rushers, tackles, and proven skill players have scarce market value. Developmental and special-teams players remain essential to operating a 105-person program but may command less cash. Scholarship expansion can help Duke distribute meaningful value deeper into the roster even when direct payments are concentrated. That is an inference about incentives, not a claim about Duke's actual player contracts.

Finally, the rules increase compliance and forecasting work. Agreements must be documented, covered benefits tracked, roster status reconciled, and third-party NIL reviewed through the relevant systems. Football personnel decisions now affect both a finite roster and an institution-wide cap shared with every Duke sport. A transfer is not merely a roster addition; it can be a multiyear benefits commitment with consequences for later cap periods.

## Facts, inferences, and unknowns

### Established facts

- The court-approved settlement permits participating schools to provide direct payments and other new benefits subject to an institution-wide annual cap.
- The 2025–26 pool was estimated at about $20.5 million; it was not a football-only pool or a mandatory spend.
- Participating FBS programs operate with a 105-player football roster limit and may scholarship every rostered player.
- Duke publicly identified football, men's basketball, and women's basketball as priorities for direct revenue-sharing investment.
- Duke leaders publicly said they did not expect to reach the national maximum immediately and expressed a general preference for scholarships funded by philanthropy.
- Duke has not published in the sources reviewed here a football-specific benefits budget, player-payment schedule, or complete scholarship count under the new framework.

### Reasonable inferences

- Duke football probably receives a material portion of Duke's direct-benefits spending because university leadership named it a priority.
- Duke's allocation is likely phased and may combine direct payments with additional scholarships rather than maximizing either immediately.
- Scholarship flexibility can strengthen recruiting depth, while the 105-person limit raises the opportunity cost of every roster spot.
- ACC peers' spending choices create pressure on Duke even though the national cap does not require equal spending.

### Unknown or nonpublic

- Duke football's exact annual allocation and what portion of Duke's overall cap is used.
- The number of football players receiving institutional payments, their amounts, contract terms, and distribution by position or class.
- Whether Duke currently funds all 105 possible football scholarships or intends to do so.
- The exact balance among athletics operating revenue, ACC distributions, restricted gifts, unrestricted philanthropy, and other lawful sources supporting the program.
- How Duke applies gender-equity analysis to its final institution-wide payment and scholarship portfolio.
- How much third-party football NIL remains outside Duke-administered agreements and how many deals pass or fail external review.

## Why it matters

Revenue sharing changes the unit of competition. Duke football is no longer competing only through coaches, facilities, scholarships, and outside NIL opportunities. It is also competing through an internally designed package of direct benefits, scholarships, roster access, and institutional support. That package must fit within a university-wide financial strategy and a finite football roster.

For readers assessing [[Manny Diaz's Duke program]], the right question is not “Does Duke have $20 million for football?” It does not: the referenced figure was an institution-wide cap. The useful questions are whether Duke's football offer is competitive with comparable ACC programs, whether expanded aid improves roster depth, whether payment commitments support retention without sacrificing future flexibility, and whether the funding model is sustainable alongside Duke's broader academic and athletics obligations.

The public record establishes Duke's priorities but stops before the most consequential operational details. Any precise claim about a football pool or an individual player's compensation needs a Duke document, athlete agreement, or attributable reporting beyond the sources available here.
