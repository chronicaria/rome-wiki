Duke's 2026 offensive-line problem is not finding five plausible names; it is preserving an experienced interior communication core while converting several different kinds of tackle experience into one stable protection and run-blocking unit.

## The direct answer

As of July 10, Duke has two officially identified returning starters: center Matt Craycraft and left guard Jordan Larsen. Craycraft started all 14 games at center in 2025 and is entering a third season as Duke's starting center. Larsen started all 14 at left guard. Those two give the Blue Devils continuity in the part of the line most responsible for sorting interior fronts, passing off movement, and making combination blocks.

The other three jobs are not publicly settled. Duke has imported or recovered unusually credible candidates rather than relying only on projection. Nick Del Grande started 35 games at Coastal Carolina. Braden Miller started seven games for California in 2025, six at left tackle and one at right tackle. Micah Sahakian has 21 career starts, mostly at tackle, but missed the 2025 season. The official roster also includes returners who have practiced in Duke's system and younger linemen whose value cannot be measured from their roster listing alone.

That creates a promising inventory but not yet a line. Development in 2026 means answering four connected questions: who plays beside Craycraft and Larsen; whether experienced tackles can translate their prior work into Duke's calls; how the line coordinates with an unresolved quarterback; and whether a sixth or seventh lineman can enter without forcing a multi-position reshuffle. Any preseason five-man combination is therefore a hypothesis, not a reported depth chart.

## What Duke is replacing

The 2025 line was not merely adequate by Duke's own account. It was a Joe Moore Award semifinalist and supported an offense that set school records for total touchdowns, points, and pass efficiency. Duke's late-December game notes list the regular group as Bruno Fina at left tackle, Larsen at left guard, Craycraft at center, Justin Pickett at right guard, and Brian Parker II at right tackle. That group paired four returners with Larsen, a transfer from South Dakota, and accumulated extensive starting experience before the season.

The 2026 transition removes Fina, Pickett, and Parker from that arrangement. The loss is therefore not just three individual blockers. Duke is replacing both tackles, one guard, and the adjacent relationships they built with Larsen and Craycraft. On the left, Larsen must learn the timing and body language of a new tackle. On the right, Craycraft must work with a new guard while that guard builds the same relationship with a new tackle. A protection can fail even if every newcomer is physically capable because two blockers interpret a twist, late linebacker, or defensive-line slant differently.

Craycraft is the most important continuity mechanism. Duke credits him with 36 career games, 28 starts, and 2,057 snaps entering 2026. His 14 starts and 970 snaps in 2025 are reported on his current biography; an earlier Sun Bowl game-note packet, published before the bowl, credited 886 2025 snaps. The difference is chronological rather than contradictory: the biography incorporates the completed season. More important than the raw total, Craycraft has already operated in Duke's offensive language and has made protection calls against ACC defenses.

Larsen supplies the second anchor. He arrived only in summer 2025, then started all 14 games at left guard. That rapid integration is evidence that an experienced transfer can become functional quickly in Duke's system. It does not guarantee the same path for every newcomer. Larsen's spring comments emphasized that younger linemen were receiving repetitions and that he would be playing beside new teammates. That is useful firsthand evidence about the developmental task, although it does not identify the leaders in the competition.

## The candidate pool is experienced in different ways

The phrase “experienced line” can hide several distinct things. Craycraft and Larsen possess recent experience in Duke's system. Del Grande possesses extensive starting experience elsewhere. Miller has recent Power Four tackle starts but a smaller starting sample. Sahakian has substantial career experience and some Duke game work, but his most recent season contained no game action. Returners such as Caleb Dorris, Reagan McCranie, Bradley Smith, and the younger cohort possess Duke practice knowledge without the same verified starting record.

Del Grande has the largest external starting résumé. Duke's January announcement says he played and started 35 games at Coastal Carolina and was a team captain in 2025. Duke also repeats Pro Football Focus charting that credited him with no sacks allowed, three quarterback hits, and ten pressures in 2025, plus an 88.2 pass-blocking grade. Those figures are useful evidence that he handled a substantial tackle workload. They are vendor-derived metrics quoted by Duke, not official NCAA statistics, and their definitions and competition context matter. They should support his candidacy, not settle an ACC role before Duke publishes one.

Miller's official biography supplies a different profile. He played in 22 games across Michigan State and California and started seven times for Cal in 2025—six at left tackle and one at right tackle. The cross-side start matters because tackle flexibility can reduce the cost of an injury or allow coaches to place their best two tackles without treating left and right as fixed identities. Yet seven starts remain a smaller sample than Del Grande's 35. Miller must still demonstrate that his technique and communication transfer to Duke's protections.

Sahakian is both experienced and difficult to forecast. Duke credits him with 34 career games, 21 starts, and 1,849 snaps, mostly at tackle. At Duke in 2024, he appeared in four games and made two starts at right guard after returning late in the season. He then missed all of 2025. His résumé therefore establishes position range and prior functional play, not his present place in the lineup. No public source reviewed for this article establishes his fall health, workload, or best 2026 position. It would be wrong to turn the cause of his 2025 absence into a medical forecast.

The internally developed group matters because five starters are not enough for a season. Duke's 2025 Sun Bowl notes credited Dorris with appearances in all 13 games then played and 96 snaps, McCranie with three appearances, and Smith with 298 snaps. Those are dated totals before the bowl and should not be mistaken for 2026 roles. They do show that “returner” covers a range from extensive reserve work to little or no game action. A player with fewer outside starts may nevertheless understand Duke's calls more quickly than a transfer, while a transfer may own a higher demonstrated physical floor. The staff's task is to compare the whole assignment rather than résumés in isolation.

Freshman Sean Stover is the lone offensive lineman from Duke's December 2025 signing group. His roster size and recruiting evaluations make him a developmental candidate, not an obligation to play. Offensive-line readiness requires strength, technique, assignment processing, and the ability to communicate against presentations that are more varied than high-school fronts. If Stover earns an early role, that will be meaningful evidence. If he does not, it will not by itself mean the recruitment failed.

## Development mechanism one: keep the interior as the reference point

The most defensible way to build the unit is to treat Craycraft and Larsen as a communication reference point without assuming every other position. A returning center can identify the front, communicate the line's responsibility, and help a new quarterback distinguish blocked threats from the offense's immediate answers. A returning guard beside him reduces one interior variable. This does not make the left side automatically strong; it makes the learning problem smaller.

Center continuity is especially important because [[Duke football 2026 quarterback room]] remains unresolved. Protection is shared work. Depending on the play and system, the quarterback may declare a linebacker, reset the protection, alert a pressure, or understand that a defender is intentionally unblocked and must be defeated by the throw. The center must see a compatible picture. If the quarterback and center count the front differently, a tackle can execute his assigned block perfectly while a rusher comes free elsewhere.

The useful fall evidence will therefore be procedural. Does Duke reach the snap without visible protection confusion? Can the line pass off tackles and ends on games inside? Do the quarterback and center respond coherently when a defense adds a safety late? Do false starts and delay problems rise in road noise? These questions diagnose development more directly than a preseason ranking.

## Development mechanism two: choose combinations, not five isolated winners

An offensive-line competition is often described as five independent battles. In practice, coaches must select a combination. The best left tackle in isolation may not produce the best five if another candidate's ability to play both sides allows a stronger guard-tackle pairing. Sahakian's tackle history and two Duke starts at guard increase the number of plausible combinations. Miller's starts on both sides do the same. Del Grande's extended tackle record may allow another flexible player to be placed where the unit needs him most.

That flexibility has a cost. Moving players repeatedly can delay the shared repetitions that make twists and combination blocks feel automatic. The correct preseason balance is exploration followed by convergence: test enough combinations to identify the best five and the best contingencies, then give the leading group enough work together to stabilize. Public spring reports do not reveal whether Duke reached that convergence. Indeed, Duke's spring notebook listed Craycraft and Del Grande among unavailable players, which reduced the opportunity for the likely competition to become a complete five-man rehearsal.

The absence also complicates interpretation of spring praise. Larsen said the period gave younger players valuable reps and helped build chemistry. That is credible evidence that developmental players received work. It is not evidence that the eventual first unit played together enough to solve every communication problem. Fall camp must integrate players who missed spring work while preserving the gains of those who took the repetitions.

## Development mechanism three: make run and pass solutions reinforce each other

The line's job depends on [[Duke offensive identity 2026]]. Duke returns running back Nate Sheppard but is changing quarterbacks and much of its receiving production. A credible run game can keep the new passer out of obvious dropback situations, slow aggressive rush plans, and create cleaner play-action pictures. But “run more” is not itself a solution. The line must create repeatable movement and prevent penetration across several run families.

On zone concepts, development appears in coordinated first steps, secure combination blocks, and timely releases to linebackers. On gap concepts, it appears in down blocks, pulls, and the ability to account for edge movement without destroying timing. Short yardage poses a separate test: can the unit create immediate displacement when the defense expects the run? A season rushing average blends these mechanisms and can be inflated by quarterback scrambles or long runs after a back escapes the structure.

Pass protection should likewise be separated by situation. Quick-game pressures allowed tell a different story from long-developing third-down dropbacks. A tackle who receives tight-end help is operating under a different burden from one left alone against the opponent's best rusher. A sack may belong partly to coverage, quarterback timing, or an intentionally unblocked defender rather than a clean offensive-line loss. Conversely, a quick completion can hide immediate leakage. Responsible evaluation needs play context.

The best version of Duke's line would make these phases mutually supportive. Efficient early-down runs prevent defenses from specializing in pressure. Reliable pass protection punishes crowded boxes. Screens and running-back releases can exploit aggressive rushers, but only if the offense communicates which defender is being invited and which is a genuine breakdown. Sheppard's receiving ability becomes more valuable when he can release rather than remain a mandatory sixth protector on every difficult down.

## Development mechanism four: define playable depth before it is needed

The roster lists many offensive linemen, but Duke needs contingency plans at positions, not an ordered list of backups. The most important contingency is center. If Craycraft leaves a series, who can snap accurately, set the protection, and maintain quarterback communication? If the answer requires moving a starting guard to center, who then replaces the guard? One absence could trigger one substitution or three position changes; those are very different forms of depth.

Tackle depth poses the same structural question. Miller's two-side experience, Sahakian's tackle history, and Del Grande's starting record create options, but Duke has not publicly assigned them. The sixth lineman should be understood as the player—or combination of players—who preserves the most functionality, not merely the next most experienced name. A reserve who can cover tackle and guard may be more valuable on game day than a slightly better specialist because travel and substitution constraints reward flexibility.

Playable depth also includes trust under stress. A reserve can collect late-game snaps without demonstrating that the staff will use him on third-and-long, near its own goal line, or against an elite edge rusher. The meaningful evidence will be when and why Duke rotates, not just whether a player appears in a box score.

## A verification framework for the season

The line should be evaluated across at least three games before declaring a stable trend, while individual games can establish a role change or a specific failure mechanism. Track the five-player combinations and the quarterback paired with them. Separate sacks, hits, and pressures by protection type, time to throw, and whether help was assigned. Record free rushers independently from ordinary one-on-one losses. On runs, distinguish penetration from failure to sustain, and distinguish line-created space from yards a back creates after contact.

Opponent and game state must remain visible. Duke opens at home against Tulane, travels to Illinois in the second week, and later faces an ACC schedule with different pressure structures. The Illinois trip is an early communication test because road noise affects the operation, but one noisy-road performance cannot establish the season norm. Likewise, success against a four-man rush does not prove the unit can sort simulated pressure, and success while leading late does not describe third-and-long protection.

Penalties deserve diagnosis rather than aggregation. A false start may indicate cadence trouble, crowd noise, or a tackle anticipating speed. Holding can arise from a beaten block, a quarterback escaping outside the protection, or a run that changes direction. The count matters, but the mechanism determines whether the problem is likely correctable through communication, technique, or play design.

## Why it matters

Duke's headline uncertainty sits at quarterback, but the line determines how fairly that competition can be judged. Clean structure lets the winner work through reads; repeated free rushers make decisiveness look like panic. Protection also determines whether Duke can send Sheppard and tight end Jeremiah Hasley into routes instead of using them as permanent repair tools.

The development question reaches beyond 2026. Del Grande is a post-baccalaureate transfer, Miller is also listed as post-baccalaureate, Sahakian is a graduate student, and Craycraft and Larsen are redshirt seniors. A veteran solution could give Duke a strong season while leaving another reconstruction ahead. The program-level success case is therefore twofold: experienced players must form an effective 2026 unit, and younger linemen must acquire enough meaningful work to create future continuity. [[Duke football recruiting and development]] should judge the room by that succession chain, not simply by the number signed.

The current evidence supports cautious optimism. Duke retains the center and left guard from a decorated 2025 group and has added or recovered multiple players with verified starting experience. It does not support naming a five, guaranteeing health, or assuming prior-school performance transfers unchanged into ACC play. The line's ceiling will be set by communication, combination stability, and playable depth—the things a roster page cannot show.
