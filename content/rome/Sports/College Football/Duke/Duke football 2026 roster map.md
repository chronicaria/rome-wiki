Duke's 2026 roster has proven returning anchors at running back, tight end, linebacker, and punter, but its most consequential positions—quarterback, receiver, offensive line, and much of the secondary—are built around new arrivals or players moving into larger roles.

## How to read this map

This is a membership and continuity map, not a projected depth chart. The official Duke roster establishes who is listed, each player's position and class, and, where supplied, a previous school. It does not establish an August role, health, scholarship status, snap order, or even that every listed player will remain through opening day. Spring-game participation supplies a dated clue, not a promise. The useful question is therefore not “Who are the starters?” but “Where does Duke possess demonstrated production, where has it imported college experience, and where must projection substitute for evidence?”

That distinction matters after an unusual 2025. Duke went 9–5, won the ACC championship, and beat Arizona State in the Sun Bowl. The official statistics show an offense that averaged 34.6 points and 423.5 yards, including 285.4 passing yards per game. But quarterback Darian Mensah accounted for 3,973 of Duke's 3,995 passing yards and 34 of its 34 passing touchdowns. The roster also no longer lists the receivers who led the championship offense. Continuity in team identity therefore cannot be inferred from continuity in team name or coaching staff.

The same caution applies on defense. Duke allowed 424.2 yards per game and a 45.4 percent third-down conversion rate in 2025 even while producing 14 interceptions and finishing the season with two trophies. Returning defenders can improve with age and repetition, but “returning” does not automatically mean that last season's strengths and weaknesses will recur. This map identifies the places where those questions should be tested once fall camp and game evidence arrive.

Continuity is uneven, so position counts require context. **Returning production** means a verified Duke statistical or starting record directly bears on 2026; Sheppard's 1,132 rushing yards and Hasley's 40 receptions qualify. **Returning development** means a player was already in Duke's program without comparable public game volume. **Imported experience** means a transfer has verified college games elsewhere. **Projection** means the case rests mostly on recruiting biography, physical profile, spring snippets, or expected opportunity. Projection is unavoidable in July; it becomes misleading only when presented as fact.

Class year is a weak shortcut. “Graduate” can describe a newcomer learning a new system, while “redshirt freshman” can describe someone with a full year in Duke's meetings. Internal familiarity still does not create game-speed evidence. Each room should be judged on proven college performance, Duke-system continuity, and plausible role fit because no roster field captures all three.

## Quarterback: experience was imported, not inherited

The roster lists six quarterbacks: post-baccalaureate transfers Walker Eget, Ari Patu, and Blaine Hipa; freshman Terry Walker III; and redshirt freshmen Dan Mahan and Lawrence Gardner. That is a large room by head count, but head count disguises how concentrated the proven passing experience is.

Eget brings the clearest recent starting résumé. Duke's February announcement says he started 11 games for San José State in 2025 and completed 232 of 393 passes for 3,051 yards, 17 touchdowns, and nine interceptions. Those numbers establish substantial FBS volume, not automatic superiority in Duke's offense. Patu brings a different experience profile: Duke reported 20 career games across North Alabama and Stanford, four starts, 1,202 passing yards, and 260 rushing yards. Hipa arrived from Princeton; his presence on the official roster confirms membership, while his fit and place in the competition require later evidence.

Mahan produced the most visible spring evidence among the younger quarterbacks. In the April 18 Blue & White game, he threw a 15-yard touchdown to Jaivon Solomon and led a late two-minute drive that ended with a nine-yard touchdown to Brody Keefe. Yet the event contained only two 15-minute quarters, used special defensive scoring, and included walk-through series designed to rotate more players. It cannot be treated as a normal game sample, much less proof that Mahan leads the room.

The central roster fact is turnover: Mensah handled essentially the entire 2025 passing workload, and Duke must replace that production. The central evaluation question is broader than who wins the first snap. Jonathan Brewer, the returning offensive coordinator and quarterbacks coach listed on the 2026 staff, must decide how much of the previous passing structure transfers to a quarterback with different strengths. Eget's recent volume, Patu's mixed passing-and-running history, Hipa's arrival, Mahan's developmental continuity, and Walker's freshman status create options. They do not yet create an answer. A separate [[Duke football 2026 quarterback room]] should compare decision-making, pressure response, mobility, and explosive-pass creation once representative evidence exists.

## Backfield: the strongest known offensive anchor

Running back is the least speculative major offensive group because Nate Sheppard has already carried a championship-season workload. Duke's current biography credits him with 1,132 rushing yards and 11 rushing touchdowns as a freshman, plus 37 receptions for 286 yards; it also records 611 snaps. His 170 rushing yards in the Sun Bowl and 97 in the ACC championship are particularly useful because they came in the games that defined the season, although two postseason performances should not be mistaken for a complete forecast.

Sheppard is joined by several different roster types. Post-baccalaureate CJ Campbell Jr. arrives after stops including Rutgers, Florida Atlantic, and Florida State. Wilhelm Daal II is listed after Yale, and Paul Holyfield Jr. after Cal Poly. Redshirt sophomore Cole Denson returns, while freshmen Jayvian Tanelus and CJ Givers enter from high school. The signing announcement describes Givers as a four-year productive high-school runner and Tanelus as another three-star back, but recruiting biographies measure past competition and traits rather than immediate ACC readiness.

The practical issue is workload distribution. Sheppard supplies known rushing and receiving value, but a full season requires pass protection, short-yardage answers, and credible relief. The roster's older transfers could provide experience, while the freshmen could eventually supply upside. None should be assigned a role from class year alone. A useful fall test is whether Duke can preserve Sheppard's explosiveness and receiving contribution without forcing him to inherit every departed backfield responsibility.

## Receivers and tight ends: one room must be rebuilt while another has an anchor

The receiving roster is an instructive blend of transfers, developmental returners, and freshmen. Post-baccalaureate newcomers include Javen Nicholas from Charlotte/LSU, Jonah Burton from Idaho State/Baylor, Jared Richardson from Penn, and Blake Rohrer from Wisconsin–River Falls. The roster also lists redshirt sophomore Jayden Moore; redshirt freshmen Jamien Little, Jaivon Solomon, Kavon Simmons, and Qeanu Johnson; redshirt sophomore Paxon Kettering; and freshmen Kavon Conciauro, Brody Keefe, and Jackson Maggiore.

That list conveys options but not proven Duke production. The spring game offered two useful, narrow observations: Solomon caught one touchdown from Mahan, and Keefe caught another during the two-minute period. It did not establish separation ability across a season, blocking dependability, or a target hierarchy. Burton and Rohrer joined late enough to appear in Duke's April transaction announcements, further reinforcing that the room was being actively reconstructed rather than simply promoted intact.

Tight end is more stable at the top. Jeremiah Hasley returns as a redshirt senior after recording 40 catches for 454 yards and six touchdowns in 2025. His selection as one of Duke's three player representatives for ACC Kickoff is an official signal of program prominence, though not a statistical projection. The room also lists redshirt senior Jake Taylor, post-baccalaureate Louisville transfer Nate Kurisky, redshirt junior Tony Boggs, redshirt sophomore Brett Elliott, and freshmen Benjamin Moseley and Jack Gaskins.

Hasley's receiving record gives the next quarterback a familiar middle-of-field and situational target. The presence of several older tight ends may also give Brewer flexibility to use heavier personnel while the receiver rotation settles. That is a hypothesis, not a declared scheme: roster size and body type alone cannot prove formation frequency. The measurable fall questions are whether a receiver can command defensive attention outside, whether multiple tight ends can stay on the field without making intentions obvious, and whether the passing game can replace departed explosive production rather than merely redistribute short targets.

## Offensive line: many bodies, few safe assumptions

The official roster lists 17 offensive linemen, including several older transfers. Jordan Larsen returns after starting all 14 games at left guard for Duke in 2025 following his move from South Dakota. Nick Del Grande arrives from Coastal Carolina, while Micah Sahakian returns for a third Duke season after originally transferring from Cornell, and Braden Miller arrives after California and Michigan State. Returning upperclassmen also include redshirt senior Matt Craycraft and redshirt juniors Caleb Dorris and Reagan McCranie. The younger group includes Ryan Daly, Bradley Smith, Gemyel Allen, Daniel Boyd, Cole Allen, Kai Jacobowitz, Jamin Brown, Nathan Kutufaris, Roman Fina, and freshman Sean Stover.

This is the position where a roster count is most likely to mislead. Five functional starters require a coordinated unit, and nominal depth is not interchangeable with game-ready depth. Previous-school listings show that Duke has sought external experience, but they do not specify positions within the line, health, chemistry, or performance in Duke's protection system. Stover was the lone offensive lineman in Duke's 14-member December signing class and was described by Duke as a four-star prospect by two services. That provides recruiting context, not a requirement that he play immediately.

The line's importance is magnified by quarterback turnover. A veteran quarterback can still struggle if protections are unstable; an inexperienced quarterback can be helped by consistent identification and a credible run game. Duke allowed 26 sacks in 2025 while attempting 506 passes, but raw sack totals cannot isolate line play from quarterback decisions, route timing, opponent pressure, and game state. The next version of this map should add returning snap and start data by exact position before labeling continuity. Until then, the honest conclusion is that Duke has assembled age and competition, not that it has already assembled a line.

## Defensive front: developmental returners plus targeted additions

Duke separates defensive ends and defensive tackles on the roster. At end, sophomore Bryce Davis is the most prominent young returner by class-year signal, joined by redshirt junior Kevin O'Connor; redshirt sophomores Tyshon Reed, Kobe Smith, Semaj Turner, and Jaylen Carl; redshirt freshmen Tommy Rupley and Sampson Onuoha; and freshman Obinna Umeh. Trent Harris is the listed defensive ends coach.

At tackle, the roster lists redshirt junior David Anderson, junior Preston Watson, redshirt sophomore Owen Wafle, redshirt freshmen Julius Columbus and Bariate Kara, post-baccalaureate Dartmouth transfer Dakota Quiñonez, and freshmen Ayden Cain and Chadwicke Cannon Jr. Alex Devine coaches the tackles, while assistant head coach and special-teams coordinator Gabe Infante also carries a defensive-line title. That division of titles is a staff-structure fact; it does not by itself reveal how practice responsibilities or game planning are divided.

The freshman additions show a deliberate investment in interior depth: Cain and Cannon were two of the 14 December signees, while Umeh was the class's defensive end. But young defensive linemen often face a strength and technique transition, and Duke's own listings should not be converted into immediate-rotation claims. The near-term evaluation should focus on who can hold up against the run, who can create pressure without blitz assistance, and whether the second unit can preserve performance. Those mechanisms matter directly to [[Duke defense under Manny Diaz]], because pressure design works differently when the front can win with four.

## Linebacker: the clearest returning defensive center

Luke Mergott provides the strongest verified continuity signal on defense. Duke's current biography credits him with 90 tackles, 7.0 tackles for loss, one sack, two interceptions, and two fumble recoveries across all 14 games in 2025; he started the final ten. The program also selected him for the 2026 ACC Kickoff delegation. He is listed as a redshirt junior in a room coached by defensive coordinator Jonathan Patke.

The room also includes graduate Nick Morris Jr.; redshirt juniors Kendall Johnson; redshirt sophomores Derrick Brown Jr. and Connecticut transfer Jon Morris; sophomores Bradley Gompers and Elliott Schaper; redshirt freshmen Will Felder Jr. and Asher Wasserman; and freshmen Tony Grimmer and Colsen Gatten. The spring game supplied a single concrete newcomer moment when Jon Morris recovered a fumble. Again, that is evidence of one play, not a depth ruling.

Mergott's return can stabilize communication, but linebacker performance depends on the front keeping blockers from climbing and on the coverage assignments created by Diaz and Patke. The room has multiple classes and some external experience, which is healthier than a thin name count. The unresolved issue is role clarity: who plays beside Mergott, who handles passing downs, and whether Duke can improve a defense that allowed opponents to convert 45.4 percent of third downs in 2025. That team rate cannot be assigned to linebackers alone, but it supplies a concrete problem for the entire defense.

## Secondary: imported experience around a small returning core

The cornerback list is transfer-heavy at the top of the class distribution. Post-baccalaureate Dylan Flowers arrives after multiple schools, Evan Smith after Northwestern, and Ché Ojarikre after Stanford; redshirt sophomore Kyon Loud is listed after Montana. Returning Duke players include redshirt junior Kimari Robinson, redshirt sophomore Landan Callahan, and redshirt freshmen Kolbe Harmon and Kaleb Lanier. Freshman Tyson Sanford completes the listed group. V'Angelo Bentley remains the cornerbacks coach.

Spring evidence gave Flowers and Lanier an interception apiece. Those plays confirm ball production in a controlled scrimmage, but not an established pairing. At safety, post-baccalaureate Patrick Smith-Young arrives after North Texas and Old Dominion. Redshirt junior Leon Griffin III and DaShawn Stone, sophomore Andrew Pellicciotta, redshirt freshman Brady Winter, and freshmen KD Cotton and Lyrik Pettis form the rest of the listed room. Cotton intercepted a pass in the spring game; Rick Lyster is the listed safeties coach.

This construction suggests Duke wanted experienced options rather than relying exclusively on a young returning secondary. “Suggests” is the proper word: the official roster documents prior schools, while the reasons for each addition and the eventual roles remain interpretive. The 2025 defense conceded 8.37 yards per opponent pass attempt, so preventing explosive completions is an obvious evaluation priority. Coverage performance, however, is inseparable from pressure timing and opponent quality. The fall map should track combinations and responsibilities rather than grade a corner from one isolated target or a safety from one interception.

## Specialists: unusual continuity at punter, open questions elsewhere

Punter Kade Reynoldson is the most established specialist. Duke's 2025 team statistics report a 42.0-yard net punting average, and the program named him a preseason All-America selection in June 2026. Preseason recognition is an external evaluation, not an outcome, but the underlying 2025 role is real. The roster also lists kickers Cosme Salas, Ashton Zamani, and Howard transfer Mika Levy; long snappers Jack Yates, Jack Ronilo, and post-baccalaureate Jake Fitzgerald.

The spring game records a 27-yard field goal by Salas and extra points by Salas and Zamani. That is too small a sample to settle the kicking job. Special teams should also be understood beyond kicking: Duke's 2025 statistics show a 29.6-yard kickoff-return average and 3.8 yards allowed per punt return, while Gabe Infante returns as coordinator. Some players responsible for those numbers are no longer on the roster, so unit continuity must be checked by assignment and coverage participation rather than coordinator continuity alone.

## Staff and roster-building structure

Manny Diaz remains head coach after Duke announced a contract extension in April. The official staff page lists Brewer as offensive coordinator/quarterbacks coach, Patke as defensive coordinator/linebackers coach, and Infante as assistant head coach, special-teams coordinator, and defensive-line coach. Position coaches include Bentley, Zohn Burden at receiver, Devine, Rodney Freeman II at running back, Harris, Lyster, Jeff Norrid at offensive line, and Justin Watts at tight end.

Two organizational details deserve attention. First, Binuk Kodituwakku is now listed as general manager after serving as chief strategy officer. Duke's biography explicitly connects his work to analytics, roster construction, recruiting, NIL strategy, and revenue-sharing arrangements. That does not reveal private valuations or contracts, and none should be inferred. It does show that roster management has an identified executive owner alongside the coaching staff. Second, the staff includes analysts and an associate offensive-line coach, evidence of a larger support structure than the on-field assistant list alone.

The roster itself reflects a three-part construction model: retain a small number of proven central players, import older players at high-uncertainty positions, and develop a 14-member high-school class. Quarterback, receiver, offensive line, cornerback, and safety all feature post-baccalaureate or prior-school additions. Running back, tight end, linebacker, and punter retain the clearest identifiable 2025 anchors. Whether that mix becomes coherent depends on role fit and development, not simply talent acquisition.

## Why it matters

Duke enters 2026 with the credibility of an ACC championship but without the luxury of running back the same offense. The quarterback who produced virtually all of the passing yards is gone, the receiving hierarchy is being rebuilt, and the line includes multiple newcomers. Sheppard and Hasley give the new quarterback proven skill-position support, but they cannot by themselves recreate a 34.6-point offense.

On defense, Mergott and several young returners provide a spine, while transfers offer possible answers in the secondary. The correct preseason thesis is therefore conditional: Duke has enough verified continuity to avoid calling this a total reset, and enough turnover at leverage positions to reject the idea of simple continuation. Fall camp should be used to narrow roles. The opening nonconference block described in [[Duke football 2026 schedule map]] should then test whether the assembled roster has become functional depth.

## A fall verification plan

The next update should change labels only when the evidence changes. A preseason depth chart identifies Duke's intended ordering for a date, not guaranteed snap distribution. Camp availability answers whether players are practicing, not whether they are fully effective. Stronger evidence will be stable first-team repetitions, a declared depth chart near the opener, and game participation.

For the offense, verify quarterback availability and first-unit share; exact line positions and combinations; receiver target hierarchy; and the workload behind Sheppard. For the defense, verify four-man pressure capacity; the linebacker pairing around Mergott; and the corner, nickel, and safety assignments. Specialists need separate confirmation of placekicking, kickoff, punting, and return duties because one roster label can hide four competitions.

Two quantitative additions would improve this map once Duke publishes game notes: returning 2025 snaps by group and returning starts by exact position. They would not prove future quality, but they would distinguish operational experience from a room returning mostly names. Until those data exist, narrow claims are more honest than precision manufactured from class years and head count.
