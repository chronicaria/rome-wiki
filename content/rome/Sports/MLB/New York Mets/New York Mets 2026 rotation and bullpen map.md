As of the end of July 9, the Mets’ pitching staff is best understood as four conventional starters, a flexible fifth-game lane, and a bullpen whose strongest late-inning options coexist with acute length and workload problems.

This is a dated role map, not a permanent depth chart. It freezes the official active-roster and transaction evidence through the Mets’ 7–3 win over Kansas City on **July 9, 2026**. “Starter,” “bridge,” and “leverage” below describe demonstrated recent use or a clearly labeled inference; they do not claim that the club has announced those roles.

The active staff after the July 9 moves comprised 13 pitchers: **Huascar Brazobán, Xzavion Curry, Dan Hammer, Sean Manaea, Nolan McLean, A.J. Minter, Freddy Peralta, Cionel Pérez, Brooks Raley, Christian Scott, Kodai Senga, Luke Weaver, and Devin Williams**. That list is reconstructed from MLB’s official July 8–9 transactions and the post-move roster. Myers belongs in the July 8 workload history but not the July 9 closing snapshot because the Mets optioned him before selecting Hammer.

## How the rotation arrived here

The shape of this staff cannot be read from reputation alone. The Mets opened 2026 with more conventional starting names available, but the July map reflects attrition and a major personnel change. Associated Press reporting on June 26 records that the Mets had traded David Peterson to the Cubs the previous day and identifies Clay Holmes among the injured players. Peterson had been the club’s longest-tenured player, so his exit removed both a rotation turn and an innings source. Holmes was not part of the active July 9 staff.

The eight games from July 1 through July 9 show the practical response:

| July 3 | at Atlanta | Christian Scott | 4 / 82 | Senga supplied 2⅔, then Minter and Gerber |

| July 5 | at Atlanta | Nolan McLean | 6 / 96 | Four relievers covered the final three innings of a 10–9 win |

| July 7 | vs. Kansas City | Cionel Pérez | 1 / 14 | Senga, Minter, Warren, Seelinger, Brazobán covered eight innings in a 16–12 loss |
| July 8 | vs. Kansas City | Christian Scott | 5 / 90 | Raley, Myers, Curry covered four innings in a 6–2 win |

This sequence establishes four recurring starter lanes. Peralta, Scott, Manaea, and McLean all took ordinary turns. It does **not** establish five. The fifth slot became a staff game on July 7, when Pérez opened and Senga carried the next three innings.

That distinction matters because a rotation list can conceal the actual burden it places on the bullpen. Peralta’s two turns in the window totaled 8⅔ innings and 194 pitches. Scott’s totaled nine innings and 172 pitches. Manaea’s two starts totaled 12 innings and 205 pitches, but seven of those innings came on July 9. McLean provided six innings in his one turn. Before Manaea’s July 9 outing, the staff had received only three starts longer than four innings in the previous seven games in this window. The bullpen was not merely protecting leads; it was routinely being asked to finish nearly half the game.

Manaea’s seven innings on July 9 therefore had value beyond the two earned runs in the box score. It allowed the club to win using only Brazobán and Williams after the July 7 bullpen game forced six pitchers into service and the July 8 win still required four relief innings. One long start temporarily restored options. It did not, by itself, solve the fifth-turn problem.

## The Senga question: role is not identity

Kodai Senga is the easiest pitcher to misclassify if this map is built from name recognition. In this snapshot, the relevant fact is his use, not his career identity: he did not start between July 1 and July 9. He entered after Scott on July 3 and threw 2⅔ innings and 44 pitches; he entered after Pérez on July 7 and threw three innings and 61 pitches.

Those outings make Senga a multi-inning follower in the observed period. They do not prove a permanent conversion to relief. Several explanations remain possible: the club could be managing health or workload, searching for a better matchup path through a lineup, easing him toward a future start, or testing a durable bulk-relief role. The public evidence cited here does not choose among them.

The tactical consequence is clearer than the motive. A Pérez–Senga arrangement lets the manager avoid presenting the same pitcher to the top of the order three times. It can also force the opponent to set a lineup for a left-handed opener before facing a right-handed bulk pitcher. But this structure spends two roster pieces to approximate one start, and it transfers the final five innings to the rest of the bullpen when the follower covers only three. On July 7, the plan became especially costly because the Royals scored 16 runs and Austin Warren was injured without recording an out.

Calling Senga “the fifth starter” would therefore overstate what happened. Calling him “just a reliever” would erase the length and sequencing purpose of his appearances. **Bulk follower / flexible multi-inning arm** is the most accurate dated label.

## The bullpen is a set of jobs, not a ladder

The late-inning group contains several pitchers with closer or setup histories, but recent usage is better represented as a portfolio of jobs.

### Devin Williams: highest-leverage candidate, not an automatic ninth-inning claim

Williams appeared on July 5, July 6, and July 9. He threw the final inning of the July 9 win with a four-run margin, allowing no runs on 17 pitches. He also worked in tighter, more complicated games earlier in the road trip. That pattern supports treating him as a primary leverage arm. It does not support declaring that every save situation belongs to him, especially because this short window includes no clean series of conventional save chances.

The distinction is practical. A manager can use the best available swing-and-miss option against the opponent’s most dangerous pocket in the eighth, then allow Weaver, Minter, or Raley to handle the ninth. The ninth inning remains important, but lineup quality and inherited-run danger can make an earlier inning the actual leverage peak.

### A.J. Minter and Brooks Raley: left-handed leverage with different availability patterns

Minter worked on July 1, July 3, July 6, and July 7. The schedule shows both his importance and the need to track accumulated appearances: four outings in seven calendar days can remove a pitcher from the next night’s plan even when no injury is involved. He generally entered for one inning or a little more, making him a matchup or leverage bridge rather than a bulk solution in this sample.

Raley appeared July 5, July 6, and July 8, recording scoreless work in each outing. His assignments ranged from one batter on July 6 to a full inning on the surrounding dates. That makes him useful for a difficult left-handed pocket without requiring the manager to reserve him for a named inning. Because both Minter and Raley throw left-handed, the bullpen can cover multiple left-heavy pockets; because their usage clusters, day-to-day rest can still leave only one available.

### Luke Weaver: a short right-handed bridge

Weaver threw one inning on both July 5 and July 6. The first was scoreless; the second included an unearned run. In this map he belongs with the primary short relievers: an arm who can connect a starter or bulk pitcher to the final lane and whose value rises when Williams or a left-hander is unavailable. There is not enough evidence in this window to assign him exclusively to the seventh, eighth, or ninth.

### Brazobán and Pérez: elasticity in the middle

Brazobán’s assignments show why “middle relief” is not synonymous with low value. He recorded only one out while allowing five runs July 5, then supplied two scoreless innings in the July 7 loss and a scoreless eighth on July 9. That range—from damage control to protecting a lead—makes him an elasticity piece. His role depends on what the game and the previous two days have consumed.

Pérez’s week was similarly fluid. He allowed four runs in 1⅓ innings July 1, recorded four outs without a run July 6, and opened July 7 with a scoreless inning. As the only other active left-hander in this map besides Minter and Raley, he offers handedness coverage, but the opener assignment also demonstrates that his roster function can include sequencing rather than only batter-to-batter matchups.

## July 7–9 exposed the depth mechanism

The July 7 loss was not merely a bad bullpen line. It triggered roster consequences. MLB’s transaction log shows that the Mets placed **Austin Warren on the 15-day injured list with a right forearm strain** on July 8 and designated **Matt Seelinger** for assignment one day after selecting his contract. The club simultaneously recalled **Tobias Myers** and selected the contract of **Xzavion Curry**.

The next night explains the operational purpose. Scott supplied five scoreless innings, but the Mets still needed four more. Raley handled the sixth, Myers covered the seventh and eighth, and Curry finished the ninth. Myers and Curry were not abstract depth-chart names; both absorbed innings immediately after the staff’s most destructive game of the stretch. On July 9, the Mets optioned Myers and selected Hammer, superseding the one-day Myers roster state even though Hammer did not pitch in that night's game.

This churn creates two cautions. First, active-roster lists captured on July 7 and July 8 were already wrong by the following day. Second, a pitcher’s presence does not disclose his hierarchy. Myers’s two innings and Curry’s one inning show why fresh length was added, but Myers's next-day option also shows how quickly that job can turn over. Curry and Hammer are best classified as **fresh middle/length coverage pending further use**; Myers is a completed one-day shuttle entry in this frozen map.

## Game-state decision tree

The following is an analytical model of how this staff can be deployed, not a report of a published club policy.

1. **A regular starter reaches six or seven innings.** Use two from Williams, Minter, Weaver, Raley, or Brazobán according to opponent pocket and rest. July 9 is the clean example: Manaea went seven, Brazobán took the eighth, Williams the ninth.
2. **A regular starter exits after four or five.** Choose a bridge based on the next inning’s hitters, but preserve at least one multi-inning option. The danger is cascading one-inning relievers across four or five frames, as occurred repeatedly early in the window.
3. **The fifth lane comes due.** An opener–follower design with Pérez and Senga is the only arrangement demonstrated here. It demands that the club either get more length from Senga than it received July 7 or carry fresh Myers/Curry-type coverage behind him.
4. **The game is close on consecutive days.** Availability matters more than static hierarchy. Minter’s four appearances in seven days and the July 5–6 back-to-back use of several relievers show why the “next best” option changes daily.
5. **The game breaks open early.** Protect leverage arms and use length, but the July 7 experience shows how a blowout can still consume the entire staff when the bulk plan fails and a pitcher is hurt. The Myers-to-Hammer shuttle illustrates the next-day roster response.

## What this map says about the staff

The rotation’s central issue is not simply run prevention. It is **outs allocation**. Four ordinary starters are visible, but only Manaea and McLean completed six innings in this nine-day sample. Peralta and Scott were asked to carry normal turns yet combined for four starts of five innings or fewer. The fifth lane required an opener and a follower. Every missing starter out therefore had to be purchased from the bullpen.

The bullpen’s strength is optionality. Williams, Minter, Weaver, and Raley offer multiple late-inning combinations. Pérez supplies another left-handed shape, Brazobán can work across leverage levels, and Senga offers length that a one-inning group cannot. The bullpen’s weakness is that these options are interdependent. When starters leave early, the club spends short relievers before the highest-leverage innings arrive. When an opener game fails, the following day’s manager inherits a reduced menu. When one pitcher is injured midgame, the transaction wire becomes part of the pitching plan.

That is why a good bullpen ERA or a list of famous names would be an incomplete evaluation. The more revealing questions are: How many outs must relief supply? Which arms are rested? Can the follower get through the middle innings? Does the club reach Williams/Minter/Weaver/Raley with a lead, or spend them merely to remain close? Those questions connect this map to [[Why Mets reliever performance is volatile]].
