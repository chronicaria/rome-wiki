Service time does more than measure a player's path toward arbitration and free agency: at defined thresholds, it gives him consent rights that can block or reshape a trade, option, or outright assignment the Mets might otherwise appear free to make.

“Under club control” is an incomplete description of a baseball asset. A club may hold a player's contract for future seasons yet lack unilateral power to send him to the minors, outright him off the roster, or trade him to every destination. The 2022–26 Basic Agreement and MLB's official transaction glossaries place player consent at several different gates. Some rights arise from Major League service alone. Others arise from negotiated contract language. They should never be collapsed into a generic “no-trade clause.”

For the Mets, the distinction matters on both sides of a deadline deal. A veteran they hope to move may be able to reject the assignment. A player they acquire may arrive with restrictions that reduce roster flexibility. A younger player with several seasons before free agency may nevertheless have enough service to refuse an optional assignment. And a player who clears outright waivers may have a right to reject the minor-league destination or elect free agency. A valid trade analysis must therefore ask not only *how long can the Mets retain him?* but also *which transactions can the Mets compel during that time?*

This is a rules framework, not a claim about any current Met's private terms. Public age, debut date, salary, reputation, or a third-party contract summary cannot establish an exact service total or reproduce an executed contract. Any player-specific conclusion requires the official service record and applicable contract documents available to the parties.

## The first boundary: what Major League service time measures

MLB defines Major League service as days credited while a player is on a Major League active roster or Major League injured list. Under the current framework, 172 credited days constitute one full year of service even though a championship season ordinarily contains more calendar days. Service is written in years and days: for example, `5.000` means exactly five years, while `5.001` means five years and one day.

That arithmetic affects salary arbitration, free agency, pensions, and several assignment rights, but those consequences do not all begin at the same threshold. A player generally needs six full years of Major League service to become a free agent after his contract expires, absent an earlier release or other route. The right to refuse an option arrives earlier: the Basic Agreement and MLB option glossary say a player with five or more years of Major League service may not be optionally assigned without his consent. The well-known trade-consent right arrives through a different combination: ten years of Major League service, including the last five with his current club.

These are separate tests. A player can have enough service to refuse an option but not enough to block a trade under the ten-and-five rule. A player can be years from free agency because he signed a long extension yet still acquire ten-and-five trade protection during that contract. Conversely, a player can be approaching free agency without ten years of service and have no automatic ten-and-five protection, though his negotiated contract may supply different protection.

Service is credited under detailed rules, so a public analyst should not estimate a threshold by subtracting a debut year from the current year. Minor-league time on optional assignment ordinarily does not count as Major League service. Injured-list time ordinarily does. Partial seasons, late callups, earlier options, suspensions, and collectively bargained service adjustments can all change the ledger. When a right turns on a day, “about five years” is not adequate evidence.

## Ten-and-five rights can turn a trade into a request

Article XIX of the 2022–26 Basic Agreement provides the core assignment-consent rule commonly called **ten-and-five rights**. A player with ten or more years of Major League service, the last five of them with one club, may not be assigned to another Major League club without his written consent. In practical terms, a qualifying player can veto a trade.

The second condition is as important as the first. Ten years across several teams do not create ten-and-five protection unless the immediately preceding five satisfy the same-club requirement. The right is also tied to an assignment away from the current club; it is not a general power to dictate lineups, roles, or acquisitions.

Consent changes bargaining without making a deal impossible. A qualifying player may approve one destination, reject another, or condition approval on a negotiated benefit. The clubs and player may discuss financial treatment, option decisions, bonuses, an extension, or changes to existing trade protection, subject to the Agreement and the contract. But no public observer should infer either a demand or a willingness to waive rights from silence, rumors, family circumstances, or a player's demeanor. Until consent is documented through authoritative reporting or the completed transaction, the safe statement is only that consent would be required if the verified service conditions are met.

For a selling Mets club, ten-and-five protection can reduce the effective bidder pool. Even if many contenders value the player, the realizable market consists only of destinations he accepts. That can weaken New York's leverage because an acquiring club knows a veto exists, especially if only one destination remains. It can also stop a salary-clearing transaction that would otherwise be permissible between clubs.

The effect is not mechanically negative for the Mets. A player may approve several destinations, leaving meaningful competition. A club can negotiate early enough to understand the feasible market. And the right protects the player's settled expectations after a long career and extended service to one club—the purpose of requiring consent, not a procedural nuisance to be wished away.

## Negotiated no-trade protection is a different source of authority

An individual Uniform Player's Contract can include negotiated assignment restrictions even when a player has not earned ten-and-five rights. These provisions vary. A full no-trade clause may require consent for every destination. A limited clause may let a player identify a specified number of blocked clubs, sometimes through a list updated on a schedule. Other language may restrict assignment for part of a contract, change after an option is exercised, or contain conditions known only from the executed agreement.

The legal source matters. Ten-and-five protection follows from collectively bargained service conditions. A negotiated restriction follows from the player's contract. One player may have both. Another may have neither. A public database's icon or summary can be a research lead, but the Mets and the other club must work from the actual documents filed with MLB, not from an abbreviated public label.

That distinction also prevents a common analytical error: treating a list of blocked clubs as a complete veto. If the Mets are not on the list, acquisition may be possible without a waiver of that clause; if they are, the player may still consent. The precise list, update date, and contract language control. Similarly, a player consenting to one trade does not establish that he permanently surrendered every protection for later assignments. The surviving rights depend on the agreement and any written waiver or amendment.

## Five-year service consent limits the option shuttle

Minor-league options are usually described as club flexibility. An optioned player leaves the 26-man roster but remains on the 40-man roster and can later be recalled, subject to option-year, annual-assignment, and minimum-stay rules. Yet Article XIX and MLB's official option glossary add a player-rights boundary: a player with five or more years of Major League service may not be optionally assigned without prior consent.

This means an unused option year does not necessarily give the Mets power to send the player to Syracuse. Two questions must be answered independently:

1. Does a technical option year remain, and is another optional assignment allowed under the annual limit?
2. Has the player reached the service threshold at which the assignment also requires consent?

The distinction can materially change a trade's value. Suppose the Mets consider acquiring a veteran as flexible depth. If he has an option remaining but sufficient service to refuse assignment, the front office cannot price him as a freely shuttleable 27th player. He may consent, but the plan depends on his choice. If he does not, the Mets must keep him active, place him on an appropriate list only when the facts permit, trade him, release him, or pursue another lawful roster route.

Consent to one option should not be presumed to cover an indefinite series. The governing documents and the actual written consent control. Nor should a refusal be portrayed as misconduct; the right is part of the compensation and employment system negotiated between MLB and the Players Association.

The Mets therefore need a consent column beside every deadline candidate's option inventory. [[How MLB option assignments shape Mets roster decisions]] explains the other counters: the normal three option years and possible fourth, the 20-day seasonal threshold, the five optional-assignment limit, and ordinary 10-day or 15-day minimum stays. None displaces the service-time consent rule.

## Outright assignment creates another decision point

An outright assignment is not an option. When a club removes a player from the 40-man roster and seeks to retain him in the minors, it must place him on outright waivers. Every other club has an opportunity to claim the contract under the waiver process. If the player clears, his service and prior outright history may give him the right to reject the minor-league assignment.

MLB's official outright-waivers glossary identifies two important categories. A player with at least three years of Major League service, or a player who has previously been outrighted, may reject an outright assignment and elect free agency. The Basic Agreement supplies more detailed treatment, including consequences attached to the election and salary rights. Those details must be checked for the actual player and contract; the general point is that “cleared waivers” does not always mean “can be stored in Triple-A.”

This boundary matters when evaluating an out-of-options acquisition. If the Mets later need his 40-man place, the fallback may be DFA and waivers. Claim risk is the first loss channel. If he clears but can reject the outright, free agency is the second. His theoretical minor-league depth value may therefore be much lower than that of an optionable, consent-free player.

The same issue affects outgoing Mets. A designation for assignment is only an interim status. MLB's glossary says the club has seven days to trade the player or place him on outright or unconditional release waivers. The eventual transaction—not the initial DFA headline—reveals whether he was traded, claimed, released, accepted an outright, or exercised an available right. Analysts should not write “sent to Syracuse” until the official log establishes that result.

## Deadline legality and consent are separate gates

MLB's trade-deadline rule sets the league-wide calendar for ordinary Major League trades. The 2022–26 Basic Agreement fixed a window in which the Commissioner selects a date between July 28 and August 3, with the exact annual deadline announced by the league. After that deadline, Major League contracts generally cannot be traded for the rest of the season. Waiver and release mechanisms continue to exist, but the former August revocable trade-waiver route no longer provides a second general trading season.

A trade being timely does not satisfy assignment consent. Before the deadline, the clubs still need any consent required by ten-and-five rights or the individual contract. Likewise, player consent cannot authorize the clubs to ignore the deadline. The calendar gate and the consent gate both must pass.

Postseason eligibility is yet another issue. MLB's glossary generally ties eligibility to being in the organization by the end of August 31, with replacement provisions for certain injured players. That rule should not be confused with the trade deadline or with roster control. A player might be legally acquired before the deadline but later fail to make a postseason roster on baseball grounds. A player already in the organization may be postseason-eligible through a permitted route even though he was not on the active roster on August 31. Each proposition needs its own rule.

## How consent changes trade price and structure

Consent rights affect a Mets trade in at least five ways.

**They narrow feasible counterparties.** A full veto or limited blocked list may remove clubs that otherwise offer the best return. The public “market” can therefore be larger than the executable market.

**They change timing.** The Mets need enough time to secure consent and complete any related paperwork before the league deadline. Waiting until the final minutes increases execution risk even if the baseball terms are settled.

**They can change economics.** A player may agree to alter an assignment restriction only as part of a lawful negotiated amendment. Cash considerations between clubs, retained salary, option treatment, and player compensation can change the clubs' valuation. Public analysis should label reported terms and avoid inventing what induced consent.

**They reduce roster flexibility after acquisition.** Five-year option consent and outright-rejection rights can make a nominal depth player difficult to move off the active or 40-man roster without losing him. That shadow cost belongs in the acquisition price alongside salary and prospect capital.

**They can outlast the deadline headline.** A trade solves today's assignment question, not necessarily tomorrow's. The acquiring Mets must verify which clauses survive, whether new rights vest during the contract, when service thresholds will be crossed, and how options interact with future consent.

This is why “years of control” is not a sufficient valuation unit. Two players controlled through the same season can offer different bundles of authority. One may be optionable without consent and retain outright flexibility. Another may control every minor assignment and block many trade destinations. The latter may still be the better player and better acquisition, but New York should pay for the bundle it actually receives.

## An evidence-safe Mets workflow

For every possible outgoing or incoming player, the Mets decision board should separate verified facts into distinct fields:

- official Major League service in years and days, with the as-of date;
- years of service with the current club and whether they are consecutive for ten-and-five purposes;
- free-agent eligibility and guaranteed contract term;
- option years remaining, assignments already used, and whether five-year consent applies;
- prior outright history and the service basis for rejecting an outright;
- full or limited contractual assignment protection, based on the executed agreement;
- annual trade deadline and any timing needed to obtain written consent;
- salary, bonuses, options, buyouts, retained money, and tax treatment relevant to the deal.

The public version should be more conservative than the club version because the public lacks the complete file. It can explain the rule and cite an official completed transaction. It should not claim that a named player will waive protection, accepted a payment, placed a club on a blocked list, or has a specific private family preference unless authoritative on-record evidence establishes it.

When evidence conflicts, the transaction should stay conditional. A media contract summary can alert a researcher to a clause; it cannot overrule the executed contract. A roster site can estimate service; it cannot replace the official record when a consent threshold is decisive. A player's past waiver of protection can show precedent; it does not prove consent to the next destination.

## Why it matters

The 2026 Mets deadline framework emphasizes converting short-term value into future options and avoiding purchases that solve only the final weeks of a distant race. Consent rights sharpen that strategy. An outgoing veteran's market may be smaller than his performance suggests. An incoming controllable player may offer fewer roster options than his contract length suggests. A supposedly reversible depth acquisition may become an active-roster commitment because he can refuse an option, then become difficult to retain in Triple-A because he can reject an outright.

None of those rights makes a player undesirable. They make precision necessary. Player consent is part of the collectively bargained allocation of power, earned through service or negotiated in exchange for contract terms. The Mets' task is not to evade it but to understand it early, value it correctly, and avoid building a trade around authority the club does not possess.

The most reliable public conclusion is therefore procedural: verify service and contract rights before naming a player tradeable, optionable, or stashable. Until that verification exists, use conditional language. “The Mets can trade him” and “the Mets can send him down” are legal and contractual claims, not synonyms for “the move would make baseball sense.”

## Evidence boundaries

This article states the generally applicable framework in the 2022–26 Basic Agreement and MLB's official glossaries as of July 10, 2026. It does not identify any current Met as holding or lacking ten-and-five rights, five-year option consent, outright rejection rights, or negotiated trade protection. It does not infer an executed clause from a salary website, rumor, age, debut date, or past transaction.

The Basic Agreement expires at 11:59 p.m. ET on December 1, 2026. Rules extending into a later agreement must be rechecked. Individual rights also depend on precise service credit, prior transaction history, the Uniform Player's Contract, and written amendments unavailable in the public record.
