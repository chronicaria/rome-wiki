## How to read this ledger

The “sample” column reproduces the official MLB/MiLB display checked at the cutoff. It does not estimate true talent. Ages come from MLB Pipeline’s 2026 Mets prospect table where a player appears there; otherwise the player’s official date of birth is the underlying reference. “Role” means the position or roster state shown by the official page, not an independent scouting judgment. A level describes the competition and assignment, not a guarantee of proximity or readiness.

The main questions are deliberately task-shaped. A strong line matters when it narrows uncertainty about a job the major-league club could actually offer. A weak line can be equally informative when it identifies the next test. Neither outcome should be translated directly from OPS, ERA, ranking, or a few games.

## Level and roster interpretation

The cards separate three different kinds of proximity. **Competition proximity** is highest at Syracuse, where players face experienced Triple-A opponents. **Transaction proximity** is highest for Morabito, Mauricio, and Tong because the official Syracuse roster marked them as members of the MLB 40-man roster. **Role proximity** cannot be read directly from either fact. A player may be on the 40-man without having a suitable active-roster job; a non-roster player may be the better baseball fit but cost a designation or other 40-man move.

That distinction matters for the [[New York Mets 2026 roster and transaction ledger]]. Mauricio’s July 7 option formally created an active-roster change while preserving his 40-man relationship. Morabito and Tong are easier to access transactionally than Clifford, but that does not make them better players or prove that their present skills answer New York’s needs. Clifford’s non-roster status creates an additional cost only if the club wants to select him.

Age must likewise be paired with level. Peña’s Single-A line carries unusual developmental context because he is 18, while Morabito’s Triple-A sample is more directly relevant to a near-term MLB role. Neither comparison says who will have the better career. It says the uncertainty takes a different shape: Peña has much more developmental distance; Morabito faces a sharper test of whether current skills fit a specific major-league job.

## Data-quality limits

1. **The official pages are live snapshots, not a synchronized database extract.** MLB Pipeline, affiliate roster pages, and affiliate stats pages were checked in the same research pass but displayed different cumulative samples for several players. This ledger records the provider and number rather than silently choosing one as “correct.”
2. **The affiliate table’s labels were internally imperfect.** Binghamton’s page showed “International League” despite Binghamton being the Mets’ Double-A affiliate. The player rows and affiliate identity remain useful, but the anomalous league label is a warning against treating page chrome as validated metadata.
3. **Standard batting tables omit essential translation evidence.** They do not provide park or league adjustment, reliable defensive value, position innings, swing decisions, batted-ball quality, platoon splits, opponent quality, or injury/workload context.
4. **Pitchers require a separate verified pull.** The default affiliate pages opened on hitting, and this pass does not infer pitching performance from roster placement, reports, or an ERA fragment. Tong is therefore retained as an identity/access card with a defined next query rather than fabricated statistical precision.
5. **Role labels are provisional.** A single listed position can reflect the page’s primary classification rather than every position played or the organization’s intended MLB role. Catcher and center-field translation especially require defensive evidence not present here.
6. **No rank is treated as a forecast.** Pipeline rank identifies the population surfaced by the official prospect tool. It does not settle probability, ceiling, readiness, or fit.
