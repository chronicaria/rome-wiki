## Return-to-play activity is a separate ledger

### Dedniel Núñez

MLB’s transaction log placed Núñez on the 60-day injured list on February 15 with the description “Tommy John surgery recovery.” On July 8, the same official system recorded that the Mets sent him on a rehabilitation assignment to Triple-A Syracuse. Those two facts can coexist: he was still labeled IL-60 on the July 9 roster while appearing in the rehab-assignment channel.

The correct workload conclusion is therefore limited. Núñez had progressed far enough for an official minor-league rehab assignment, but the transaction record did not announce a target number of appearances, a maximum pitch count, back-to-back availability, or a major-league role. A later box score could document pitches actually thrown; it could not by itself reveal an undisclosed medical limit. Until activation appears as a formal transaction, he remains an IL player rather than an available member of the Mets bullpen.

### Luis Robert Jr.

The official sequence for Robert is longer. MLB.com reported on April 30 that the Mets placed him on the 10-day injured list with lumbar spine disc herniation. The transaction log then recorded a transfer to the 60-day IL on May 26 with the same condition. That transfer changed the roster mechanism and minimum absence, but it did not create a new diagnosis or establish a recovery forecast.

MLB’s log recorded a rehab assignment to Triple-A Syracuse on June 30. It then recorded assignments to Double-A Binghamton on July 7 and again July 8. The repeated Binghamton entries should not be inflated into multiple distinct recoveries; they establish that Binghamton was the official rehab destination around the cutoff. They also do not erase his IL-60 status, which remained visible on the July 9 roster page.

For workload purposes, the defensible claim is that Robert had reached minor-league game activity and that the club moved the level of that activity from Syracuse to Binghamton. The checked official sources did not promise an activation date, state that he could play every day, or define a post-activation center-field load. His earlier rest pattern is historical context, not proof of a future restriction.

### Marcus Semien

The Mets placed Semien on the 10-day injured list on June 25 with a left hip flexor strain. MLB.com’s same-day report said manager Carlos Mendoza described the player as week to week and said rest and treatment were the immediate recovery mode. A June 29 MLB.com follow-up emphasized that Mets officials were not publicly providing a return timeline while relaying a separate reported four-to-six-week estimate.

The ledger therefore retains the formal injury label and IL status but does not convert the reported estimate into a club guarantee. No official rehab assignment appeared in the checked transaction evidence through July 9. His July 9 state is “IL-10, no verified rehab assignment in this review,” not “due back” on a calculated date. The distinction matters because a 10-day IL label is a minimum roster period, not a prognosis.

### Austin Warren

Warren’s state changed most recently. The official July 8 transaction placed him on the 15-day injured list with a right forearm strain. At the cutoff, that was the full medically relevant disclosure supported by the transaction record. The phrase should be copied exactly and not expanded into a ligament diagnosis, imaging result, procedure, or recovery calendar.

Nor should the 15-day label be read as a promise that he will miss precisely 15 days. It establishes the applicable roster list for a pitcher and the minimum absence framework. No official rehab assignment, throwing plan, or workload restriction was located after the placement and before the July 9 cutoff.

## The four IL-60 cases without a verified July rehab assignment

Garrett, Hagenman, Holmes, and Megill were all labeled IL-60 on the July 9 roster, but the checked official July transaction evidence did not show a current minor-league rehab assignment for any of them. That negative finding is deliberately scoped: it means none was located in the official sources reviewed through the cutoff, not that a player performed no private throwing, treatment, conditioning, or baseball activity.

Garrett was placed on the 60-day IL February 10. His official MLB player page carries an October 2025 news item saying he would miss the 2026 season after Tommy John surgery, while the 2026 placement line itself does not append a condition. The ledger keeps those claims separate rather than silently adding a diagnosis to a transaction that omitted one.

Hagenman’s March 14 transaction explicitly disclosed a rib fracture. The official roster still labeled him IL-60 on July 9. Without a later official activity update, no claim about throwing readiness or a target month is warranted.

Holmes was placed on the 15-day IL May 16 with a right fibula fracture, according to the official Mets transaction page. By July 9, the roster labeled him IL-60. The status change is observable, but the roster page alone does not explain rehabilitation milestones or forecast an activation.

Megill was placed on the 60-day IL February 12 with a right elbow sprain. He remained IL-60 on July 9. “Elbow sprain” is the disclosed term; the sources checked here do not justify grading it, identifying a particular structure, or inferring a treatment course.

## Workload controls for the active roster

This ledger does not invent health flags for active players. The official active roster on July 9 included Sean Manaea, Nolan McLean, A.J. Minter, Freddy Peralta, Brooks Raley, Christian Scott, Kodai Senga, and the rest of the listed staff without an IL marker. Active status means roster availability; it does not prove the absence of soreness, fatigue, innings planning, or private restrictions.

The same evidentiary rule applies to workload. A pitch count in an official box score establishes how many pitches were thrown in that appearance. It does not establish why a pitcher was removed or what limit the club intended unless the team or manager says so. Consecutive-day relief usage can describe recent load but not certify availability for the next game. A skipped start or extra rest can be recorded as schedule fact without being labeled an injury.

For future updates, preserve four different fields rather than compressing them into “healthy” or “hurt”:

1. **Formal status:** active, IL-10, IL-15, IL-60, or rehab assignment.
2. **Official disclosure:** the club or MLB’s exact body-area or condition language.
3. **Observed workload:** pitches, innings, plate appearances, defensive innings, and days between appearances from official game records.
4. **Stated plan:** only a plan attributed to a named team official or an official transaction, with its date and uncertainty intact.

This structure prevents three common errors: treating eligibility as readiness, treating rehab results as medical clearance, and treating a reporter’s estimate as a guaranteed activation date.

## Recently resolved status: Jorge Polanco

Polanco belongs at the boundary of this ledger rather than in the July 9 IL snapshot. The July 7 official transaction activated him from the 60-day injured list, and the July 9 active roster listed him as the designated hitter. Those records establish a completed roster transition: he was no longer on the IL at the cutoff and was eligible to play.

They do not establish that his earlier condition had disappeared in every medical sense, that he would start every game, or that the club had abandoned any rest plan. Future entries should record his plate appearances and defensive usage as observed workload. A day out of the lineup is not, without an official statement, evidence of a setback. Keeping Polanco outside the current IL count while retaining him as a recently activated monitoring case prevents both an outdated nine-player IL total and an unsupported declaration of unrestricted health.
