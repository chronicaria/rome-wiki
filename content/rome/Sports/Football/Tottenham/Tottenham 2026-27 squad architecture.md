Tottenham's 2026 summer has created a deep, technically ambitious squad for Roberto De Zerbi, but the abundance of senior centre-backs, goalkeepers, and central midfielders makes role clarity and registration management as important as further recruitment.

Up: [[Tottenham Hotspur 2026-27]]

## What is actually confirmed?

This is a roster-state analysis, not a transfer forecast. As of 9 July 2026, Tottenham have officially announced Andy Robertson, Marcos Senesi, Jan Paul van Hecke, Martin Dúbravka, Mateus Fernandes, and Sandro Tonali as incoming men's first-team players. The earlier June announcements specified 1 July joining dates where applicable; Tonali's announcement remains expressly **subject to a work permit**, while Senesi's was subject to international clearance. The club also confirmed Alejo Véliz's permanent move to Bahia and Yves Bissouma's departure at the end of his contract. Rumoured targets and reported negotiations are excluded.

The club's public first-team squad page is useful but not yet a complete summer register: at the research date it listed the inherited group but had not incorporated the six announced arrivals. It therefore cannot be read as the final 2026–27 Premier League submission. Nor has the Premier League published Tottenham's final post-window list. The correct picture is a working squad assembled from the club page and dated official transaction announcements, with registration status still provisional.

### Working senior group

The inherited club-page group comprises:

- **Goalkeepers:** Guglielmo Vicario, Antonín Kinský, Brandon Austin.
- **Defenders:** Kevin Danso, Destiny Udogie, Cristian Romero, Pedro Porro, Djed Spence, Kota Takai, Ben Davies, Ashley Phillips, Micky van de Ven, Souza, plus Luka Vušković; Radu Drăgușin is listed as out on loan.
- **Midfielders:** Xavi Simons, Conor Gallagher, James Maddison, Archie Gray, Lucas Bergvall, Pape Matar Sarr, Rodrigo Bentancur, Wilson Odobert, Yang Min-hyeok.
- **Forwards:** Richarlison, Mathys Tel, Dominic Solanke, Mohammed Kudus, Dejan Kulusevski, Manor Solomon, Dane Scarlett.

The six official additions expand that working group with Dúbravka in goal; Robertson, Senesi, and Van Hecke in defence; and Fernandes and Tonali in midfield, subject to the stated administrative conditions. Positions on the club page are broad labels, not a tactical depth chart. Gray can play in defence or midfield, Kulusevski can operate inside rather than as a touchline forward, Odobert can play wide, and Davies has long covered both left-back and centre-back. Those overlaps are central to the architecture.

## A squad built around the ball

The recruitment pattern is unusually coherent. De Zerbi's teams ask the goalkeeper and first line to invite pressure, preserve short options, and find a free player beyond an opponent's first jump. The summer additions concentrate on footballers with experience and composure in those phases.

Dúbravka described the modern goalkeeper as an extra outfield player who must be comfortable with the ball and hold a high starting position. The club explicitly connected that profile to De Zerbi's use of goalkeepers in build-up. Yet his arrival should not automatically be interpreted as a new first choice. Kinský played every minute of De Zerbi's final seven matches of 2025–26 after Vicario's surgery, and the club rewarded him with a new contract on 30 June. De Zerbi praised both his performances and his development potential. With Vicario still on the squad page, Tottenham have three experienced senior goalkeepers plus homegrown Austin. That is valuable insurance but a crowded allocation of minutes and, potentially, senior-list places.

The central-defender recruitment is even more striking. Van Hecke already knows De Zerbi's methods from Brighton. Senesi called himself a defender who wants to break lines and play forward, and the club highlighted both his passing volume and defensive actions at Bournemouth. Add them to Romero, Van de Ven, Danso, Davies, Takai, Phillips, Vušković, Souza, and the loaned Drăgușin, and Spurs possess more centre-back candidates than a conventional two-centre-back structure can regularly satisfy.

This surplus has three plausible explanations, none yet proven. First, it can be a deliberate attempt to protect the build-up model against injury: losing one press-resistant passer should not force a different game. Second, it gives De Zerbi specialist choices—left-footed distribution, recovery pace, aerial strength, or prior system knowledge—against different opponents. Third, some inherited defenders may be development loans or later departures. Until official moves occur, the third explanation must remain a roster possibility rather than a claim.

At full-back, Robertson changes the left side's age and experience profile. Udogie remains a high-upside long-term option, while Davies can cover the same flank and central defence. Robertson adds a veteran who has repeatedly played in aggressive possession and pressing sides. Porro's new long-term contract stabilizes the right side; the club framed him as a technical and tactical leader, while Spence supplies two-sided cover. The question is not raw numbers but how the full-backs divide width, midfield occupation, and rest-defence responsibility. A traditional overlapping Robertson and an inverting Porro do not produce the same shape as Udogie driving inside or Spence carrying from deeper positions.

## Midfield: quality, congestion, and role differentiation

Bissouma's exit removed one senior midfielder, but Fernandes and Tonali more than replace the numerical slot. The working pool now includes Tonali, Fernandes, Bentancur, Gallagher, Sarr, Gray, Bergvall, Maddison, and Simons, with Kulusevski also capable of playing as an interior creator. It is a formidable range of passing, pressing, running, and final-third ability. It is also a group in which several players may reasonably expect major minutes.

The most important distinction is between roster labels and functional jobs. De Zerbi's midfield needs can include a deepest organizer who offers under pressure, a runner who attacks the space created by the first pass, and a player between the lines who can turn possession into chances. Tonali arrives as an established Premier League midfielder, but official confirmation of his role must wait for matches. Fernandes adds another midfield option, but a welcome interview is not tactical evidence. Gallagher and Sarr can raise pressing intensity; Bentancur can progress possession; Gray and Bergvall remain young enough to grow across roles; Maddison and Simons offer creation higher up.

The danger is redundancy without hierarchy. If too many midfielders require the same receiving zones, depth becomes congestion rather than resilience. If De Zerbi can differentiate their functions—some starting the move, some accelerating it, some finishing it—the group can absorb injuries and support rotation without losing the central idea. Early pre-season line-ups should therefore be read less as a fixed first XI than as tests of partnerships: who drops beside or between the centre-backs, who holds width, who runs beyond the striker, and who protects the ball after an attack breaks down.

## The forward line is deep but not perfectly symmetrical

Solanke and Richarlison provide senior centre-forward options, with Scarlett a club-developed alternative and Tel able to play through the middle or from the left. Kudus, Kulusevski, Odobert, Solomon, and Yang widen the range of ball-carrying and creative profiles. Maddison and Simons further increase the supply of players who prefer central or half-space possession.

That is depth in aggregate, but it does not guarantee balance. Several attackers are most comfortable coming toward the ball or moving inside. A De Zerbi side also needs players who threaten behind the last line, preserve width when required, press with coordinated timing, and attack the box when possession develops on the opposite flank. The architecture will be judged by complementary movement, not the number of recognizable names.

There is also a pathway issue. Scarlett and Yang need meaningful competitive minutes to develop; being nominal members of a large first-team group is not the same as having a role. Tel, Odobert, Bergvall, Gray, Takai, Phillips, Vušković, and Souza sit at different points on the development curve. Tottenham must decide which young players improve through first-team rotation and which require loans. A squad with European-level ambitions can use more players than a one-game-a-week side, but it cannot give every prospect enough starts merely by retaining them.

## Age curve: a deliberate two-speed build

The roster does not follow a single age strategy. It combines an unusually large young cohort with targeted veterans. Robertson, Dúbravka, and Davies bring experience and leadership; Romero, Van de Ven, Porro, Solanke, Gallagher, Tonali, Bentancur, Kudus, Kulusevski, and other prime-age players should carry the competitive load; Gray, Bergvall, Tel, Odobert, Takai, Phillips, Vušković, Souza, Yang, Scarlett, and Kinský retain developmental value.

This two-speed structure can work if the veterans lower the learning cost for the younger players rather than blocking them. Dúbravka's mentorship has value, for example, but four senior-list goalkeeper candidates would be inefficient if all remain. Robertson can teach and compete while Udogie develops, but three left-back-capable seniors plus Spence's flexibility require explicit rotation. Davies' renewal preserves leadership and versatility, yet the addition of three centre-backs makes his playing route narrower.

The age curve also concentrates transition risk. Several senior players may have limited resale value, while a large group of prospects need minutes to realize theirs. The club should not be judged simply for signing older free agents or talented youngsters. The meaningful test is whether each veteran solves an immediate problem and whether each prospect has a credible development route.

## Premier League registration is a constraint, not yet a crisis

The latest published Premier League rules and squad-list guidance establish the relevant framework: a club can name up to 25 senior players, no more than 17 of whom may be non-homegrown. Homegrown status is based on registration with an FA- or FAW-affiliated club for three seasons or 36 months before age 21, not nationality. Eligible under-21 players sit outside the 25-man limit. For 2025–26, the under-21 cutoff was birth on or after 1 January 2004; the league's final 2026–27 list and cutoff must be confirmed when published rather than assumed here.

Tottenham have a useful homegrown core. The Premier League's February 2026 list explicitly classified Austin, Davies, Spence, Maddison, Gallagher, Solanke, and Danso as homegrown. Younger players such as Phillips, Gray, and Scarlett were outside that senior-list classification exercise and may have strong cases based on their development histories, but their 2026–27 treatment must be confirmed from the new list. Robertson should not be assumed homegrown merely because of his long later career in England: the rule concerns the defined training period before 21. Nationality alone does not decide the question.

The more immediate pressure lies in the number of overseas-trained senior candidates. Vicario, Dúbravka, Kinský, Romero, Van de Ven, Senesi, Van Hecke, Porro, Udogie, Robertson, Bentancur, Sarr, Tonali, Fernandes, Kudus, Kulusevski, Richarlison, Solomon, and others may compete for the 17 non-homegrown slots depending on age eligibility and confirmed training history. Even if under-21 exemptions keep several prospects outside the 25, the club could not simply register every senior overseas-trained player in an inflated working group.

This does not mean Spurs have already breached a limit. The summer window remains open, loans and sales can change the population, and the official squad submission comes later. It does mean every additional senior non-homegrown arrival has an opportunity cost. The centre-back and goalkeeper surpluses are therefore not only sporting questions; they affect which players can be registered at all.

Tottenham have no European competition in 2026–27, so UEFA registration is not an active constraint this season. UEFA's locally trained and club-trained categories remain relevant only as a future comparison: a player who helps the domestic list may not fill a club-trained European place if Spurs qualify again.

## Where the architecture is strongest

The first strength is central build-up depth. There are multiple ball-playing centre-backs, several midfielders comfortable receiving under pressure, and three senior goalkeepers whom the club can plausibly ask to participate with the ball. A single injury should not force Spurs to abandon the intended structure.

The second is tactical variety. Van de Ven's recovery speed, Romero's aggression, Senesi's left-footed progression, Van Hecke's familiarity with De Zerbi, and Robertson's overlapping experience offer different solutions. Gallagher, Sarr, Tonali, Bentancur, Gray, and Fernandes can form midfield combinations with different balances of control and intensity. The attack can field a fixed reference point, multiple dribblers, or additional creators.

The third is succession planning. Porro and Kinský have renewed, while the club has retained a broad group of young players. There is no obvious single-season cliff in the core. The free-agent additions of experienced players also reduce the need to ask every prospect to mature at once.

## Where decisions remain

The central defensive queue is the clearest unresolved problem. A healthy squad cannot provide regular minutes to every current candidate. Loans for young defenders may be developmentally sensible, while a permanent departure could rebalance senior registration, but only official transactions should change the ledger.

Goalkeeper hierarchy is similarly opaque. Kinský's run-in and renewal suggest genuine backing; Vicario remains an established senior; Dúbravka arrives with extensive Premier League experience; Austin contributes a homegrown place. Competition is healthy, but four-way retention would consume training attention and potentially roster space.

The attack must prove it has enough off-ball depth and width. The midfield must demonstrate complementary rather than duplicated roles. Finally, Tottenham need a transparent route for the young cohort: first-team rotation, domestic cups, or loans chosen for actual playing opportunity.

## Why it matters

Squad construction is the material basis of the [[Roberto De Zerbi at Tottenham|De Zerbi project]]. A coach cannot reliably build through pressure, counterpress after losses, or rotate through a congested calendar if the roster contains only one player for each critical function. Tottenham have addressed that fragility aggressively. The next stage is subtraction and hierarchy: turning a large collection of plausible fits into a registerable group with clear roles.

The summer should therefore be evaluated through three linked tests. First, does the final list fit Premier League constraints without excluding useful seniors? Second, do the young players receive credible development paths? Third, do early matches show that the apparent technical depth produces repeatable structures rather than merely selection dilemmas? The answers will determine whether this is coherent redundancy or expensive congestion.
