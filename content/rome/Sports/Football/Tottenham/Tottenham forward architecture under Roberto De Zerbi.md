Tottenham's forward group contains several plausible ways to receive, carry, run and finish, but Roberto De Zerbi's attack will work only if those players divide five connected jobs—press manipulation, depth, width, box occupation and counterpressure—rather than competing for the same spaces.

## Scope and evidence boundary

This note is a functional map, not a predicted lineup. Tottenham's official men's squad page, read on 10 July 2026, lists Richarlison, Mathys Tel, Dominic Solanke, Mohammed Kudus, Dejan Kulusevski, Manor Solomon and Dane Scarlett as forwards. It lists Wilson Odobert and Yang Min-hyeok among the midfielders, although both can be relevant to wide attacking occupation. James Maddison and Xavi Simons can also influence the spaces immediately behind or around a centre-forward. Those club labels establish a working population, not positions in a formation or a selection order.

The distinction matters because a forward architecture is a set of relationships. “Left wing,” “number nine” and “number ten” can describe starting coordinates without explaining who holds the touchline, who pins a centre-back, who drops to connect play, who attacks the far post, or who reacts first after a turnover. A balanced attack may ask one player to perform several of those actions in one possession. It may also redistribute them when the opponent changes its press.

The confirmed facts here are deliberately narrow: the players appear on Tottenham's official working squad page, Solanke and Tel have official club joining profiles that describe their prior positional experience, and Kudus's club profile records his addition to Spurs. The tactical account comes from documented patterns in De Zerbi's earlier teams, principally Premier League analysis and Coaches' Voice. It is a prior for what to test, not proof of how he will use Tottenham's players after a full preseason. No hierarchy, transfer rumor or claim about an unannounced role is included.

## Why the forwards begin the build-up

De Zerbi's best-documented possession idea is to attract an opponent toward the ball before playing through or beyond the resulting pressure. Premier League analysis of his Brighton describes defenders delaying in deep areas to induce a press. Coaches' Voice describes the goalkeeper and centre-backs supported by a narrow double pivot, with higher attackers occupying the opponent's last line. The forwards therefore affect the deep build-up even when they do not touch the ball.

Their first job is to stretch the opponent vertically. If every attacker comes short because the ball is near Tottenham's box, the opponent can compress the pitch, send more players toward the first pass and still protect the space behind. If at least one forward threatens beyond the last line, the deepest defenders must decide whether to hold their ground or follow the compression. That decision helps create the room in which the free player can receive after Tottenham escape the first pressure.

The second job is to provide a usable target beyond the press. “Going long” can be part of the same press-manipulation logic as short passing. Once opponents commit several players near Tottenham's goal, a clipped or driven pass may find a forward with fewer defenders around him. The receiving action must do more than register a completed pass: it should secure the ball, connect a third player, draw a foul, or release a runner into the space the press has vacated. A centre-forward who drops can become that wall-pass option; a wide forward can hold outside a narrow full-back; a runner can attack the channel behind an advancing defender.

These are complementary choices, not simultaneous instructions for everyone. If the centre-forward drops, another attacker generally needs to occupy the depth he has left. If a winger comes inside to receive, a full-back or another forward may need to preserve width. If both wide attackers move toward the ball while the striker also checks short, Tottenham can possess securely yet remove the very outlet the deep build-up is designed to release.

## Five jobs that must remain covered

### 1. Pin and threaten the last line

Depth is both an attacking weapon and a spacing service. A runner behind can receive directly, but even an unrewarded run can stop a defender from stepping into midfield. Against an aggressive press, that restraint enlarges the space between the opponent's midfield and defence. Against a settled block, repeated depth runs can move the back line and create room for a teammate arriving between the lines.

Tottenham's roster provides several *candidates* for this function, but the official evidence does not justify ranking them. Solanke's signing profile identifies him as a centre-forward with extensive Premier League experience. Tel's announcement describes a forward able to operate centrally and from the left. Richarlison and Scarlett are listed as forwards by the club. Those facts establish possible starting locations and roster coverage. They do not prove which player will time runs best in De Zerbi's patterns, which combinations will be selected, or whether one will be treated as the principal depth threat.

The key test is coordinated timing. A run too early becomes offside or disconnects the runner from the passer. A run too late allows the opponent to recover after Tottenham break the press. A useful review should record the moment the first line is escaped, the runner's starting lane, the defender moved by the run, and whether the passer could realistically find it. Counting sprints without their relationship to the ball would miss the point.

### 2. Connect play without emptying the box

De Zerbi's earlier structures have used forwards dropping into pockets created by a pressing midfielder or defender. A forward receiving to feet can turn, set the ball to a facing teammate, or draw a centre-back away from the last line. Tottenham have several players whose broad profiles make such receptions conceivable, including Kulusevski, Kudus, Maddison, Simons and the centre-forwards. But “can receive inside” is not a role allocation.

The architecture must answer what happens around the receiver. If a striker checks toward midfield, a wide forward can run diagonally into the vacated central lane. If a wide creator moves into the half-space, the striker can pin the near centre-back and the opposite winger can attack the far post. If an advanced midfielder becomes the connector, the centre-forward may remain high and offer depth rather than duplicate the short option.

This is where apparent abundance can become congestion. Kulusevski, Kudus, Maddison and Simons can all plausibly value central or half-space touches; Odobert, Solomon, Tel and Yang expand the wide-player pool; the centre-forwards may also want service into feet. If three players enter the same pocket, they can make short combinations but leave the touchline, back line or far post unoccupied. A successful rotation is not movement for its own sake. It ends with different lanes occupied and at least one forward prepared to attack the next action.

### 3. Preserve or create width

Width forces the opponent's last line to defend more of the pitch. Coaches' Voice's account of De Zerbi's earlier teams describes rotations in which wingers and full-backs alternate between wide and inside positions. The principle does not demand a permanently touchline-bound winger. It demands that someone remains able to receive outside the opponent's compact block while another player occupies the inside channel.

Tottenham's personnel allow multiple theoretical arrangements. A wide forward could hold the line while the full-back supports underneath. A forward could move inside while an overlapping full-back becomes the widest player. A full-back could stay deeper, leaving the forward to isolate an opponent one against one. Those are tactical inferences from role types, not claims about De Zerbi's preferred pairings at Spurs.

The functional question is what the width achieves. Receiving wide is useful if it draws a defender, creates an inside passing lane, permits a cross with credible box occupation, or gives a dribbler room to advance. It is less useful if the receiver is isolated with no underlap, overlap, backward support or far-post target. Match analysis should therefore evaluate the triangle around the ball and the occupation on the opposite side, not only the winger's average position.

### 4. Occupy the penalty area in layers

Breaking pressure is not the same as creating a chance. Once Tottenham advance, someone must attack the goal. A robust box structure usually needs more than one line: a near-side option, a central or cutback target, and an opposite-side runner, with supporting players positioned for rebounds and defensive security. The exact number must vary with opponent, score and the location of the ball, but emptying the penalty area to facilitate possession would make progression sterile.

Centre-forward presence can anchor this structure, yet the burden cannot rest on one player. When the ball reaches one flank, the far-side attacker must decide whether to hold width for a switch or narrow toward the far post. An advanced midfielder can arrive late rather than stand beside the striker from the beginning. A dropping nine may become the cutback receiver while another forward runs beyond. These layered arrivals are harder to mark than a flat row waiting for a cross.

Official squad data cannot confirm who performs each action most effectively. Nor should goals from a prior club be treated as proof of a Tottenham role. The evidence required is sequence-level: who is in the box when the crossing or cutback window opens, whether their runs occupy different defenders, and whether enough protection remains behind the attack. Box occupation and rest defence are a tradeoff to manage, not two separate phases.

### 5. Lead the press and counterpress

The forwards also establish the direction of Tottenham's defending. Coaches' Voice describes De Zerbi's earlier centre-forward curving his press to guide play toward one side, with the next attacker screening a pivot and wide players narrowing or jumping according to the ball. The Premier League's tactical work likewise treats coordinated triggers as more important than generic intensity. One sprint can be energetic and still damage the press if teammates cannot lock the next options.

The forward line therefore needs shared reference points: when to approach the goalkeeper or centre-back, which pass to leave apparently open, who blocks the return route, and when to stop chasing and recover compactness. After Tottenham lose the ball in attack, the nearest forward may need to press the receiver while another blocks the first escape. The objective is either an immediate regain or enough delay for the rest defence to organize.

This responsibility changes how attacking selections should be judged. A player may create with the ball but leave the side structurally weak if his reaction after loss is late or disconnected. Conversely, pressing volume alone does not establish value if it opens a lane behind him. The best evidence combines the trigger, the whole unit's movement, the opponent's available outlet and the consequence when the first pressure fails.

## What the confirmed group can and cannot tell us

The working group is numerically deep. Solanke, Richarlison and Scarlett are listed as forwards and can provide central roster coverage; Tel's club profile confirms central and left-sided experience. Kudus, Kulusevski, Solomon and the wider group increase the number of players who could operate outside or between lines. Odobert and Yang appear under a different official positional heading, while Maddison and Simons add advanced creative options from the midfield list.

That population supports four cautious conclusions. First, Tottenham are not dependent on a single nominal centre-forward being available to field an attack. Second, the squad contains several plausible carriers and receivers between the lines. Third, several players can be considered for more than one starting zone. Fourth, the large pool makes minutes and role clarity a real management problem.

It does **not** support a depth chart. It does not establish who De Zerbi trusts to start, who presses best within his cues, who should be loaned or sold, or which player is “natural” in a role without current match evidence. It also does not prove balance. Ten players who all want the ball to feet would not supply the same architecture as a smaller group with clearly differentiated depth, width and box-running functions.

Rather than attach a player permanently to each job, the early-season analysis should study combinations. A central connector needs a runner beyond him. A touchline carrier needs an inside target and security behind. A high striker needs creators close enough to find him. A far-side attacker needs to read when to become a finisher. Any front four should be assessed by whether it covers the complete set of functions during the same possession.

## Four provisional attacking configurations

These are testable configurations, not lineup predictions.

**Reference forward plus two differentiated wide players.** One central player pins and connects; one wide player threatens behind; the other can receive and create. A midfielder arrives between the lines. The strength is clear occupation. The risk is predictability if the reference forward becomes isolated or both wide players move inward simultaneously.

**Dropping centre-forward with a diagonal runner.** The central forward leaves the last line to receive after Tottenham escape pressure, and a wide player attacks the vacated channel. This can turn a vertical pass into a third-player release. It fails if the runner moves before the receiver controls the ball, or if nobody replaces the runner's width.

**Narrow creators with full-back width.** Two advanced players occupy the half-spaces around a centre-forward while full-backs stretch the pitch. This can increase central combination play. It also commits more players ahead of the ball and makes counterpressure, far-side coverage and full-back recovery especially important.

**Wide isolations around a stable central box presence.** Tottenham circulate until a wide player receives against one defender, while the centre-forward and opposite attacker occupy the area. The value depends on support and crossing choices, not simply dribble attempts. If the opponent doubles the winger, the free teammate must be found quickly.

De Zerbi may use none of these as a stable base, or combine them within matches. Their purpose is to discipline observation: ask which functions a shape supplies, which it sacrifices, and what adjustment restores balance.

## How to verify the architecture in matches

A four-to-six-match competitive sample should be the minimum for provisional claims, with preseason kept separate. Tag possessions by opponent behavior: organized high press, mid-block, low block, Tottenham transition and opponent transition. For each successful escape from deep build-up, record which forward pinned the last line, which player offered to feet, who ran beyond, and whether width remained available. Then record whether the sequence reached a forward-facing receiver, the penalty area or a shot.

Against low blocks, record lane occupation at the moment the ball reaches the final third. Are two teammates asking for the same pass? Is the far-side attacker positioned to finish or switch play? Does the centre-forward move a defender before the delivery? After every turnover, note the nearest presser, the blocked outlet, the number of Tottenham players behind the ball and whether the opponent reaches an exposed defender.

The evidence should remain relational and opponent-aware. Goals and assists are outcomes, not complete explanations. Heat maps can reveal broad territory but conceal timing. Pressing counts can conceal whether the press was coordinated. One successful run or one bad turnover should create a clip for review, not a durable conclusion. The architecture becomes established only when the same relationships recur across different opponents and game states.

## Why it matters

The forward line is where [[Roberto De Zerbi at Tottenham|De Zerbi's press-provoking build-up]] either gains purpose or becomes risk without reward. Deep possession can move opponents, but forwards must exploit the new space. Final-third possession can pin a defence, but attackers must maintain width, depth and box presence. Aggressive occupation can support an immediate counterpress, but only if the unit reacts together and the players behind it retain a viable [[Rest defence]].

Tottenham's confirmed group offers options, not answers. The central construction task is complementarity: ensure that receiving, running, carrying, finishing and pressing are present in the same unit without assigning every player to the same attractive pocket. The first competitive sample should therefore be judged less by a preferred front three and more by whether the chosen players cover the full sequence from inviting pressure to protecting the next turnover.
