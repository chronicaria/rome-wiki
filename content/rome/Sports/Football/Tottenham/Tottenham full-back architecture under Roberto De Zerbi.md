Up: [[Tottenham Hotspur 2026-27]]

## The question is geometry, not a depth chart

As of 10 July 2026, Tottenham's official evidence identifies five senior players with credible full-back availability: Pedro Porro, Destiny Udogie, Djed Spence, Andy Robertson, and Ben Davies. The club's current squad page lists Porro, Udogie, Spence, and Davies as defenders. Robertson's signing announcement states that he joined on 1 July after his Liverpool contract expired. Davies has played both left-back and centre-back for Spurs, while Spence has said he is comfortable on either the left or right. This is the confirmed pool; it is not a predicted hierarchy.

The distinction matters because “full-back” is only a starting label. Under [[Roberto De Zerbi at Tottenham|Roberto De Zerbi]], the players on the outside of the back line can be asked to perform four different jobs: provide a safe route around the first press, occupy the touchline to stretch an opponent, move inside to connect midfield, and defend the spaces exposed when possession is lost. No one player can maximize every job at the same moment. If a full-back advances high and wide, another player must protect below the ball. If he moves inside, a winger usually has to preserve width. If both full-backs attack, the goalkeeper, centre-backs, and midfield base inherit a larger transition burden.

That makes Tottenham's full-back question an architectural one. The useful issue is not whether Porro is “better” than Spence or Robertson should start ahead of Udogie. It is which combination of occupations makes the whole side coherent against a particular opponent. Until preseason and competitive matches supply recurring evidence, any settled pecking order or fixed shape would be speculation.

## What De Zerbi's earlier teams establish

The best available tactical references describe principles from De Zerbi's previous sides rather than Tottenham instructions. Coaches' Voice explains that his goalkeeper can act as an extra centre-back in deep build-up, enabling the full-backs to push higher. A narrow double pivot stays close to the centre-backs, the team invites pressure with short passes, and the ball can then move around the outside to a full-back. The Premier League's explanation of De Zerbi's 4-2-4 adds that narrow pivots and high attackers create room for full-backs to receive on the move after the press has been drawn inward.

These patterns clarify why the wide defender is not merely an overlap runner. During first-phase construction, he can be the free player created by the opponent's attempt to close the goalkeeper, centre-backs, and pivots. His receiving angle matters: standing too high may remove a short exit, while standing too deep may allow the opposing winger to cover him and a centre-back simultaneously. His first touch can continue the invitation, escape up the line, or reverse play through the centre. The correct action depends on where the press has moved.

Higher up the pitch, De Zerbi's earlier sides used both overlaps and underlaps. When a winger holds the touchline and pulls the opposing full-back outward, the attacking full-back can run through the inside channel. When the winger comes inside, the full-back can overlap into the vacated width. Coaches' Voice also notes that the double pivot supported beneath attacks and covered wide spaces so the full-backs could advance. This is a relational mechanism: the full-back's route is created by the winger's occupation and insured by midfield positioning.

The defensive demand is equally important. The same analysis describes full-backs jumping forward to press wide, requiring athleticism, while identifying the space behind those jumps as a vulnerability in some earlier De Zerbi teams. An opponent that plays around the first pressure can attack the channel before the back line and midfield screen have recovered. Tottenham therefore cannot evaluate their full-backs only by progressive carries, chances created, or crossing. The unit's value includes recognizing when not to advance, delaying a counter, winning an isolated duel, and coordinating with the nearest centre-back and midfielder.

This earlier evidence supplies a framework, not a Tottenham blueprint. De Zerbi has adapted shapes across clubs, and the personnel around each full-back changes the available solution. The responsible use of the evidence is to identify jobs that Spurs must allocate, then test competitive matches for who performs them and in which combinations.

## The four jobs Tottenham must distribute

### Build-up access

The first job is to keep the pitch connected while Tottenham invite pressure. A full-back may begin outside the opposing first line, offer a bounce pass, or receive after the goalkeeper and central defenders have attracted an extra presser. The player must scan before receiving because the same pass can either escape pressure or trap the team beside the touchline.

Porro has the clearest official evidence of recent tactical importance. Tottenham's June 2026 contract announcement says he made 47 appearances in 2025–26, more than any teammate, and was particularly influential during De Zerbi's seven-match closing spell. The club also recalls that in an earlier Spurs system he stepped into midfield from right-back. Those facts establish experience with interior occupation and a short working sample under De Zerbi. They do not establish that he will invert in every 2026–27 match.

Udogie's official contract material similarly documents an earlier inverted role under Ange Postecoglou, along with forward raids and final-third regains. That shows he has performed inside as well as outside from left-back. It is evidence of capability under a different coach, not proof that De Zerbi will copy the role. For either Porro or Udogie, moving inside could create an additional central connector, but it could also crowd a narrow double pivot unless another player changes lane.

Spence supplies two-sided flexibility. In his August 2025 interview, he explicitly said he was comfortable on either flank while still learning both positions. That makes him a credible replacement without automatically preserving the same geometry. A right-footed player receiving on the left may naturally choose different passing and carrying angles from Robertson or Udogie. Versatility reduces the need for a separate reserve in every slot; it does not make roles interchangeable.

### Width and final-third access

The second job is to stretch the opponent and turn progression into threat. Full-back width can prevent a compact defence from protecting the centre with all ten outfield players. It can also release an inside forward to receive closer to goal. The Premier League noted during De Zerbi's Brighton tenure that injuries to natural full-backs reduced width and pushed attacks through the centre, contributing to difficulty against low blocks. That is contextual analysis from a previous club, but it demonstrates the tactical cost of having nominal defenders without the required occupation.

Robertson is the clearest established width provider in the confirmed Tottenham pool. That is an inference from his long playing record, not a role assigned in Tottenham's announcement. The official release verifies nine seasons at Liverpool, left-back status, elite-level experience, and the club's assessment of his technical quality and leadership. His career there offers a substantial historical record of overlapping and delivery from the left. Yet Tottenham's announcement does not say that De Zerbi has prescribed a Liverpool-style role, and prior output cannot settle how often he will advance, underlap, or hold position at Spurs.

Porro also has an established attacking record at Tottenham. His contract announcement labels him a player capable of influencing matches in attacking and defensive situations, while the earlier club profile called him attack-minded. He can plausibly supply right-sided delivery, combination play, or interior creation. The question is which player then occupies the right touchline. If the winger holds width, Porro can appear inside or underlap. If the winger moves into the half-space, Porro may become the outside outlet.

Udogie's forward carrying offers a different route. His official material describes raids forward from an inverted starting role rather than only early crossing. A carry through the inside channel can force a defender to step and open a pass to the winger or striker. Spence's previous Tottenham usage on both sides offers another way to carry into space and confront a winger or full-back physically. These are plausible applications of documented histories, not claims about their future assignments.

### Interior support

The third job is to connect the outside of the pitch to the central midfield and attacking line. “Inverting” is often used too loosely here. A full-back who walks into midfield is not automatically helping. He must arrive in a lane that creates a free player, offer the correct body orientation, and know whether to receive, drag a marker, or make a third-player run. If his movement merely puts another Tottenham player beside the pivots, the opponent can compress the same space more efficiently.

Porro and Udogie have official club evidence of playing inverted roles in previous Tottenham systems. That makes them reasonable candidates for interior support, but the partner structure is decisive. [[Tottenham midfield architecture under Roberto De Zerbi]] identifies a deep central pool with several players capable of offering for the first pass. If two pivots are already narrow and close to the centre-backs, an inverting full-back may be more useful after progression than in the initial line. Alternatively, one full-back inside could allow a midfielder to run beyond the ball.

Davies offers a different kind of interior value. Tottenham's 2026 renewal announcement documents his experience at left-back and on the left of a back three. He could therefore help a back four become a back three in possession without a substitution, leaving the opposite full-back freer to advance. That is a role inference from confirmed positional history, not evidence of De Zerbi's plan. Davies may instead be used primarily as centre-back depth or leadership support.

This is why asymmetry is likely to be more informative than formation labels. A nominal 4-2-3-1 could build as a three when Davies holds and Porro advances, or retain a four while Udogie moves into midfield after the first pass. The visible locations may change within one possession. Analysis should track responsibilities—who forms the first line, who supports the ball, who holds width, and who protects the turnover—rather than freeze the team into a broadcast graphic.

### Transition protection and wide defending

The fourth job is to make the attacking structure survivable. When Tottenham lose possession, the nearest full-back may need to counterpress, block the line pass, recover toward the centre, or delay an isolated winger. The choice depends on teammate distances. Jumping toward the ball can keep an attack pinned if midfield covers behind; the same jump can open the channel if the cover is late.

Spence's club profile provides confirmed evidence of two-sided use and describes strong defensive one-versus-one work during 2025. That makes him a plausible option when Tottenham expect a demanding wide duel. It does not prove he will be the preferred defensive full-back, because a player's isolated duel quality cannot compensate for a disorganized rest defence. Robertson brings extensive high-level experience, while Porro, Udogie, and Davies have all defended Premier League wide areas. The official sources confirm their histories but do not provide a common metric by which to rank them.

The centre-back pairing changes the full-back risk budget. [[Tottenham centre-back architecture under Roberto De Zerbi]] describes a large group with different combinations of recovery speed, front-foot aggression, aerial ability, and progression. A fast covering defender may protect more space behind an advanced full-back, but using recovery pace as the plan rather than emergency insurance remains dangerous. A centre-back who steps out aggressively also needs the full-back to narrow and protect the vacated channel.

The midfield screen is the other half of the bargain. If a pivot shifts wide to cover an advancing full-back, central protection may weaken. If he holds the centre, the full-back may have to delay rather than immediately chase. A functioning [[Rest defence]] is therefore not a list of defenders left behind; it is a coordinated distribution of cover, pressure, and recovery routes.

## Five coherent structures, not five predictions

Tottenham's confirmed options permit several internally coherent arrangements. They should be treated as hypotheses to recognize in matches, not projected lineups.

**One high, one held.** A full-back such as Robertson, Udogie, Porro, or Spence supplies width or an advanced inside run while the opposite defender remains connected to the centre-backs. This produces a natural back-three rest defence and limits simultaneous exposure. Its cost is that the held side may become easier to ignore unless its winger can create alone.

**One inside, one outside.** Porro or Udogie moves into a central lane while the opposite full-back stretches the pitch. The structure can create a midfield overload and two distinct progression routes. It requires the wide forwards to coordinate: the winger beside the inverting full-back probably needs to preserve outside width, while the winger beside the overlapping full-back can move inward.

**Both high with a protected base.** The goalkeeper, two centre-backs, and two pivots form the supporting platform while both full-backs advance. This resembles parts of the De Zerbi reference model and can create wide outlets after the opponent compresses centrally. It commits many players ahead of the ball and demands excellent spacing, counterpressure, and wide coverage from the pivots.

**A full-back forms the back three.** Davies' centre-back history makes him the most obvious theoretical example, though other defenders could perform the job if matches show it. The opposite full-back then has greater attacking freedom, and the team may preserve three players behind the ball. The trade-off is reduced simultaneous width unless the winger on the held side supplies it.

**Conservative double width.** Both full-backs remain available outside during build-up without immediately joining the last line. This can offer safe circulation against an aggressive press and reduce the distance to recover. It may also leave the attack short of bodies near goal. The structure could be useful as a phase rather than a full-match identity.

No arrangement is universally best. An opponent that presses with narrow forwards may concede the outside pass; a passive low block may require more advanced width; a dangerous transition side may justify an extra player beneath the ball. Tottenham's architecture should be judged by whether it solves the opponent's problem without creating a worse one elsewhere.

## How to evaluate the unit without inventing a hierarchy

The first competitive sample should be recorded possession by possession and over several opponents. Four questions would produce better evidence than counting starts.

First, **where does each full-back receive during first-phase build-up?** A touch map alone cannot answer whether the position was useful. The analyst should record whether the player was free, which opponent he attracted, what the next pass achieved, and whether Tottenham escaped pressure.

Second, **who provides width after progression?** If a full-back moves inside, note whether the winger stays outside and whether the rotation creates access to the half-space. If both players occupy the same lane, the nominal overload may actually be congestion.

Third, **who remains below the ball?** Every advanced run should be read alongside the positions of the opposite full-back, pivots, and centre-backs. A successful attack does not prove the rest defence was sound; an unsuccessful attack does not prove it was reckless. The relevant evidence is what options the opponent had at the instant possession changed.

Fourth, **how does the structure vary by opponent and game state?** A full-back may advance more while Tottenham trail, hold more against an elite transition winger, or receive deeper because an opponent refuses to press. These adaptations should not be misread as permanent promotions or demotions.

The sample should cover at least four to six competitive matches with opponent and score-state context before describing a recurring model. Preseason can reveal rehearsed occupations, but substitutions, training loads, and experimental lineups limit its authority. Contract announcements and coach praise establish status and valued qualities; they are not tactical proof.

## Why it matters

Tottenham have not assembled five copies of the same player. They have an experienced left-sided width provider in Robertson, a right-sided attacker and interior-capable connector in Porro, an aggressive forward carrier with prior inverted experience in Udogie, a two-sided and physically strong option in Spence, and a veteran left-back/centre-back hybrid in Davies. Those descriptions are grounded in official career histories, but the roles are deliberately provisional.

The diversity can be an advantage. In principle, De Zerbi can change the route around a press, the source of final-third width, and the number of players retained behind the ball without replacing the entire team; competitive evidence is still needed to show that Tottenham can execute those changes. The same diversity can become a problem if too many senior players need minutes, if both flanks attack without protection, or if versatility disguises the lack of a clearly rehearsed relationship.

The decisive question is therefore not who “wins” left-back or right-back in July. It is whether Tottenham can coordinate full-back, winger, pivot, and centre-back movements so that width creates space, interior movement creates access, and attacking ambition remains protected. The first honest conclusions will come from repeated competitive sequences, not roster arithmetic.
