Indianapolis enters 2026 with Daniel Jones established as its intended starter but recovering from a torn Achilles, Anthony Richardson Sr. and Riley Leonard in a declared competition for the backup job, and Easton Stick as a fourth camp quarterback; that structure is official, while the readiness, ordering, and long-term meaning are not.

## The official state is clearer than the available lineup

The cleanest way to understand the Colts' quarterback room on July 9 is to separate four different questions that are often collapsed into one. Roster membership is one question. The intended Week 1 starter is another. Physical readiness for Week 1 is a third. The order behind the starter is a fourth. Indianapolis has supplied a reasonably firm answer to the first two and only partial answers to the last two.

The club's roster lists four quarterbacks: Daniel Jones, Anthony Richardson Sr., Riley Leonard, and Easton Stick. A roster page is not a depth chart, but Indianapolis has also described Jones as the returning starter. Offensive coordinator Jim Bob Cooter said in May that Richardson and Leonard would compete to be the backup, and head coach Shane Steichen called that contest “50/50” during organized team activities. Stick joined the club on June 1 after Seth Henigan was waived. These transactions make Stick a participant in the room, not an announced co-favorite for QB2.

Jones's recovery prevents the official hierarchy from being the same thing as an available-game hierarchy. He tore his right Achilles in December 2025. At the Colts' May 27 OTA practice, Jones performed individual work but did not participate in team periods. Jones said he expected to be ready for the regular-season opener, while Steichen emphasized the rehabilitation work rather than certifying a return date. The responsible description is therefore “intended starter in rehabilitation,” not “healthy Week 1 starter.” A July depth projection that silently converts optimism into medical certainty would outrun the evidence.

The club has nevertheless made a meaningful commitment. Jones re-signed on a two-year contract after a productive 2025 season. Exact contract accounting should live in a contract ledger rather than this football note, but the term and the club's public description matter to quarterback structure: Indianapolis did not bring Jones back as a temporary camp challenger. The team has organized the room around his return.

That still leaves two competitions. Richardson and Leonard are competing for the formal backup role. All three alternatives are competing, in a broader sense, to demonstrate that Indianapolis can survive a delayed Jones return or an in-season absence. Stick's path is different because the club has not publicly placed him in the 50/50 contest, but a veteran fourth quarterback can earn value through command, preseason function, and emergency reliability. The roster must also contract before the season, so four names in July need not imply four quarterbacks on the initial 53-player roster.

## Daniel Jones: the incumbent, with recovery as the gating variable

Jones gave Indianapolis an efficient 2025 passing baseline over his 13 starts. The club's official statistics credit him with 261 completions on 384 attempts (68.0 percent), 3,101 yards, 19 touchdowns, eight interceptions, 8.1 yards per attempt, and a 100.2 passer rating. He also took 22 sacks. Those are descriptive conventional statistics rather than a complete measure of quarterback value, but they help explain why the Colts chose continuity. Ballard highlighted that Jones finished among the league's top six in completion percentage, yards per attempt, and passer rating.

The context is essential. Jones started 13 games, not a complete season, and the offense's fast start did not survive intact through the year. The AP reported that he played through a hairline fracture in his left leg after the bye before the right-Achilles rupture ended his season. Thus the 2025 record supports a narrower proposition than “Jones solved quarterback”: he operated Steichen's offense efficiently over a substantial sample before injuries broke the continuity. Whether that performance is repeatable after rehabilitation is the central 2026 question.

Jones's fit with Steichen is easiest to describe through operating requirements rather than a rigid scheme label. During the 2025 competition, Steichen repeatedly identified consistency, ball security, decisions, line-of-scrimmage operation, protection checks, ball placement, and moving the offense as his criteria. When he named Jones the starter, he cited those same dimensions. That tells us what the staff values even if the weekly call sheet changes by opponent.

The 2025 results suggest Jones could deliver that version of quarterbacking. His 68 percent completion rate and eight interceptions over 384 attempts are compatible with the accuracy and ball-security criteria Steichen named. Yards per attempt records the outcome of passes, not their air depth or the quarterback's intended distribution, so it cannot by itself establish whether the offense was aggressive or conservative. Personnel nevertheless gave Indianapolis multiple possible answers: Alec Pierce supplied verified vertical production, Tyler Warren could align in several locations, and Jonathan Taylor anchored the run game. How Steichen combines those pieces in 2026 remains a projection until camp and game evidence reveal the actual call distribution.

Jones is also mobile enough that defenses cannot treat every keeper or scramble as a non-event, but his 2026 rushing use should not be inferred from pre-injury tape alone. An Achilles rehabilitation changes the relevant question from “has he run before?” to “what movement can he execute repeatedly and safely now?” Camp may show whether the offense retains designed movement, boot action, and quarterback-run complements. It cannot prove that a reconstructed Achilles will tolerate a full regular season.

The risk profile is therefore concentrated rather than mysterious. Jones must regain the lower-body function required for drops, resets, pocket escape, and throwing from imperfect platforms. He must also show that the 2025 accuracy and decision baseline survives the injury and the league's counter-adjustments. A handful of clean practice completions could confirm participation and timing progress; they could not establish durability or reproduce regular-season pressure.

## Anthony Richardson Sr.: enormous tools, unresolved availability and operation

Richardson remains the room's highest-variance player. Indianapolis selected him fourth overall in 2023 because rare size, arm strength, and rushing ability created a path to an offense that stresses every level of a defense. Those traits have not vanished. Neither have the reasons he lost the 2025 competition.

The club framed consistency and availability as the central development requirements entering that competition. In its April 2025 account of the competition, the Colts reported that Richardson had completed 47.7 percent of his passes in 2024, missed four games because of injury, and was held out of two additional starts after being benched. Jones then won the job because Steichen judged his daily operation, checks, protections, placement, and completion consistency superior. That decision is historical evidence about what Richardson had not yet demonstrated; it is not proof that improvement is impossible.

His 2025 season supplied almost no new passing sample. The official team statistics list two attempts, one completion, and nine passing yards. A freak pregame eye injury eventually sent him to injured reserve, and Indianapolis did not activate him for Week 18. Richardson and his agent later requested permission to seek a trade, according to both the club and independent reporting, but no trade occurred. In May he publicly acknowledged his contractual obligation and participated in the backup competition.

Richardson's best schematic argument begins with conflict creation. A defense accounting for Jonathan Taylor already has difficult run-fit choices. Add a quarterback who can keep the ball, threaten the edge, and throw vertically, and an unblocked defender can be made wrong by design. Run-pass options, zone-read elements, movement passes, and designed quarterback runs can turn Richardson's athleticism into structure rather than asking him to win only as a conventional dropback passer. His arm can also attack space behind safeties who step toward the run.

But “build around the athlete” is not a sufficient NFL offense. The same play can require a correct box count, protection answer, post-snap read, throw location, and self-protection decision. Explosive traits amplify good decisions; they do not replace them. The Colts' own criteria show that Richardson's path to QB2 runs through repeatable operation: getting the offense aligned, identifying pressure, delivering catchable balls on schedule, protecting possession, and finishing practices available for the next one.

Training camp can provide more evidence here than it can for a veteran with a secure role because Richardson's recent competitive sample is so thin. Repeated team periods can show whether his footwork and timing are stable across concept families, whether his short and intermediate placement lets receivers continue after the catch, and whether he handles pressure looks without turning every rep into improvisation. Preseason games can add live pass rush, contact avoidance, and drive-level operation.

Even then, caution is necessary. Practice completion percentages are shaped by personnel, scripted situations, defensive installation, protection rules, and whether quarterbacks are live to contact. A spectacular deep throw does not answer the routine-down question. One interception may be a terrible read, a receiver error, an experiment encouraged by coaches, or a pressure artifact. The useful unit is a repeated pattern with context, not the day's winner declared from a reporter's tally.

## Riley Leonard: a real second-year challenger, not a solved projection

Leonard enters his second season with a small but nonzero NFL sample. The Colts drafted him in 2025, initially placed him third behind Jones and Richardson, and eventually needed him because the room suffered a cascade of injuries. He appeared in five games and started the finale against Houston. Official club statistics credit him with 39 completions on 67 attempts (58.2 percent), 415 yards, two touchdowns, three interceptions, and a 67.7 passer rating. He added six carries for 27 yards and two rushing touchdowns. Sixty-seven attempts are enough to identify questions for camp, not enough to establish a stable professional level.

His Week 18 start was encouraging in specific ways. The club reported 270 passing yards and two touchdowns, and Steichen later praised his huddle command, growing understanding of the offense, and progress through reads. Yet the full season line, including three interceptions on 67 attempts, prevents one finale from being treated as a conclusive arrival. A single start can reveal that a player is capable of executing a plan; it cannot estimate his stable level against varied opponents.

Leonard's college and early professional profile makes the backup competition conceptually interesting. Like Richardson and Jones, he offers functional mobility rather than forcing the offense to abandon every quarterback-run or movement component. He is large enough to be used in short-yardage packages and showed rushing scoring value as a rookie. The fit question, however, is less about whether he can move and more about whether his processing and accuracy advance quickly enough to support the whole call sheet.

A backup who enters for Jones must preserve the offense's connective tissue. He needs to communicate formations and motions, set protections, keep Taylor's run game out of bad looks, distribute to Warren and the receivers on time, and avoid giving away possessions. Leonard's command praise matters because it touches that job description. His 58.2 percent completion rate and small sample remind us that command must become consistent execution.

Camp can establish whether Leonard has made a meaningful second-year leap. Watch the distribution of first- and second-team snaps, performance in third-down and red-zone periods, answers against pressure, and whether coaches trust him with adjustments rather than only simplified calls. Also watch who receives the first relief work in preseason games. Those choices are stronger evidence of organizational trust than an isolated highlight.

Camp cannot fully establish how Leonard responds after opponents build a game plan around his tendencies. Nor can it tell us whether his best 2025 game is the start of a trend or the high end of a wide range. If he wins QB2, the proper conclusion will be that he outperformed Richardson under the club's summer criteria—not that his long-term starting ceiling has been proven.

## Easton Stick: veteran insurance and a different kind of evaluation

Stick joined Indianapolis on June 1 after spending 2025 with Atlanta, primarily on its practice squad. He has seven credited NFL seasons, but his meaningful regular-season passing work is concentrated in 2023 with the Chargers. The Colts' biography lists five appearances, four starts, 111 completions on 174 attempts, 1,129 yards, three touchdowns, one interception, and an 85.6 rating that year. It also records 144 rushing yards and a touchdown on 27 carries.

That history gives Stick more live NFL starting experience than his “fourth quarterback” shorthand might suggest, but it does not put him inside the declared Richardson-Leonard competition. His immediate value is practical: Jones's rehabilitation limits certainty, Richardson and Leonard are competing, and a four-quarterback camp needs enough arms to distribute repetitions. Stick can provide veteran operation in practice and preseason while giving the club another emergency option.

His evaluation should therefore use a realistic standard. Does he learn the terminology quickly? Can he keep reserve-unit practices functional amid line and receiver rotation? Does he protect the ball and handle pressure? Can his mobility preserve some of the offense's movement menu? If he performs well, the club may face a roster decision. But absent an explicit change from Steichen, observers should not manufacture a four-way contest from a four-player room.

## Scheme fit is a spectrum, not four separate playbooks

Steichen has coached offenses around quarterbacks with different strengths, but that history does not prove a fixed 2026 design. A reasonable pre-camp hypothesis is that Indianapolis will begin with Taylor's run game and seek complementary passes to Pierce, Warren, Downs, and the rest of the receiving corps. Shotgun work, play action, run-pass conflicts, and quarterback movement are possibilities supported by Steichen's prior offenses and the quarterbacks' traits, not an announced 2026 scheme spine. Camp and regular-season call distribution must test the hypothesis.

Jones offers the best recent evidence of full-field, down-to-down operation in this system. Richardson offers the largest designed-run and explosive-creation ceiling but must prove routine accuracy, decision-making, and availability. Leonard offers mobility and growing command with a still-small professional passing sample. Stick offers veteran competence and emergency flexibility without an announced claim to the top two roles.

This hypothesis avoids two common errors. The first is assuming the Colts must choose categorically between quarterback movement and passing. Each quarterback has recorded some rushing production, but that does not tell us how often Steichen will call designed runs or movement passes in 2026. The second is treating scheme fit as a trait checklist detached from execution. A quarterback fits only if he can perform the offense's decisions at NFL speed. Arm strength can open a concept; placement and timing complete it.

The supporting cast also changes what should be demanded. Taylor can force safety involvement and create play-action opportunities. Pierce's vertical speed punishes defenses that compress. Warren can give the quarterback a large target and formation flexibility. Josh Downs can win in shorter areas. Those assets create answers, but the quarterback still must diagnose where the defense has conceded space. Camp reports should therefore track decision quality and timing, not merely the number of throws to a favored receiver.

## What July and August evidence can actually establish

The first camp checkpoint is participation. Does Jones progress from individual work into team periods, and at what workload? Does he move without visible protection from the practice design? Participation is meaningful, but a return-to-practice designation is not equivalent to game clearance or full-season durability.

The second checkpoint is role allocation. Richardson and Leonard were split evenly in OTA team work. If that remains even, the competition is still open. If one player receives sustained first relief work, two-minute periods, and the more demanding situational menu, the balance may be shifting. Repetition quality matters alongside count: ten scripted handoffs are not equivalent to ten third-down passing reps.

The third is operational consistency. Track false starts or alignment corrections attributable to the quarterback, protection communication, time to initiate the play, turnover-worthy decisions that can be established from credible observation, and whether completions arrive in rhythm. Public observers rarely have the call, so humility is required when assigning blame. Coaches' explanations and multiple independent reports are stronger than a single sideline angle.

The fourth is translation under contact. Preseason football gives a more realistic pocket and tackling environment than non-live practice, but vanilla defenses, rotating personnel, and small samples limit inference. A backup can strengthen his case by sustaining drives and avoiding negative plays; he cannot prove regular-season quality in two quarters against reserves.

Finally, camp cannot resolve the franchise's long-term quarterback future. Jones is under contract beyond 2026, but recovery and performance will determine the value of that commitment. Richardson's future is clouded by the unsuccessful trade exploration and his development path. Leonard is only entering Year 2. The correct summer conclusion may be as modest as “the Colts have identified their safest available order.” That would still be important. It would not be the same as finding a decade-long answer.
