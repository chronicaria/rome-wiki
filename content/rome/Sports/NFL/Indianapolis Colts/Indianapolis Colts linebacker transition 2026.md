The Colts’ 2026 linebacker change is a transfer of defensive authority from Zaire Franklin’s veteran, every-snap model to a younger committee built around rookie MIKE CJ Allen, scheme-fluent veteran Akeem Davis-Gaither, and a still-unsettled competition for complementary roles.

As of **July 10, 2026**, before training camp and preseason games, Indianapolis has made the direction clear but has not proved the result. The club traded Franklin, its leading 2025 defensive snap-taker and longtime captain, for defensive tackle Colby Wooden. It then signed Davis-Gaither, who already knows coordinator Lou Anarumo’s system, and selected Allen at No. 53 and Bryce Boettcher at No. 135 in the [[2026 NFL Draft]]. The current roster also includes Jaylon Carlies, Austin Ajiake, and rookie free agents Tahj Chambers and West Weeks. That is enough evidence to identify the design: younger, faster, more interchangeable, and less dependent on one established personality. It is not enough evidence to declare the transition successful.

The central question is therefore larger than “Who replaces Franklin?” Indianapolis must replace three things at once: his volume, his communication role, and his cultural authority. Those jobs may not belong to the same player in 2026.

## The break from the Franklin model

Franklin’s departure is the clean dividing line. The Colts traded him to Green Bay on March 11 for Wooden, a 25-year-old defensive tackle. Indianapolis described Franklin’s résumé in unusually weighty terms: a 2018 seventh-round pick who became a core special-teamer, franchise tackle-record holder, Pro Bowler, second-team AP All-Pro, and multi-year captain. His 179 tackles in 2023 set a team record; his 173 in 2024 led the NFL. In 2025, he played 1,112 defensive snaps, more than any other Colt.

Those facts establish the size of the vacancy. They do not establish that all 1,112 snaps were equally valuable or that tackle totals alone measure linebacker quality. High tackle volume can reflect durability and range, but it can also be shaped by defensive structure, opponent possession, and where contact occurs. The more useful conclusion is narrower: Franklin was almost always available, almost always on the field, and central to how the defense organized itself.

The trade also reveals a roster-allocation choice. Indianapolis converted a 29-year-old off-ball linebacker into a younger interior lineman while general manager Chris Ballard spoke openly about getting younger and faster on defense. That move redistributed resources toward the front, where Wooden joins a rotation behind DeForest Buckner and Grover Stewart, and left the linebacker room to be rebuilt through a modest veteran signing and the draft. The Colts did not pay for an external star to reproduce Franklin’s role. They chose to manufacture the replacement internally.

That makes 2026 a test of organizational confidence. If the defensive line keeps the second level clean and the new linebackers process quickly, Indianapolis may gain speed and coverage flexibility without losing control. If the front cannot protect them, or if communication breaks down, the defense may miss Franklin most in the unglamorous moments before the snap.

## CJ Allen is the planned center

Allen is not merely another contender. The Colts’ own post-draft language identifies him as the intended MIKE linebacker and communication hub. After trading down from No. 47 to No. 53, Ballard said Allen would be a “green-dot guy” from the beginning. The green dot marks the defender with the helmet communication link to the sideline; functionally, that player receives the call, communicates it, and helps align teammates before the snap.

Indianapolis accepted some draft risk to get him. The club moved six spots down, gained pick No. 135, swapped seventh-rounders, and then declined another possible trade because it did not want to lose Allen amid a second-round linebacker run. That is meaningful investment even without a first-round pick. The Colts later used the acquired fourth-rounder on Boettcher, turning one original slot into two linebackers who could reshape the room.

Allen’s college record helps explain why the staff believes he can assume responsibility quickly. At Georgia, he became a starter as a true freshman and handled communication duties in a demanding defense. Across three seasons he recorded 205 tackles, 13.5 tackles for loss, 4.5 sacks, two forced fumbles, and 11 passes defended, according to Colts draft coverage. In 2025 he led Georgia with 88 tackles, earned first-team AP All-America and first-team All-SEC honors, and became a finalist for the Butkus Award and Lott IMPACT Trophy.

The projection is straightforward. At 6 feet and 230 pounds on the Colts’ roster, Allen is expected to win through recognition, acceleration, and clean angles rather than through exceptional length or old-style bulk. Anarumo described him as intensely football-focused and said the task was to help him become the best version of himself. Ballard emphasized athleticism, speed, intelligence, and character.

But “planned center” is not the same as “finished NFL signal-caller.” Allen has not yet managed an NFL huddle, adjusted to professional motion and protection packages, or played a meaningful snap against an offense that can repeatedly isolate him. A rookie can know the call and still be late to the solution. Training camp must show whether he can communicate with authority while preserving the speed that made him attractive.

The most important Allen evaluation will not be his raw tackle count. It will be whether the Colts can leave him on the field across early downs, play-action, and passing situations without constantly simplifying the call or substituting around him. If he owns the green dot and remains playable in coverage, Indianapolis has found a foundational piece. If he needs protection from the game’s cognitive or coverage demands, the committee becomes more fragile.

## Davis-Gaither is the bridge

Davis-Gaither offers the kind of continuity a rookie cannot. He played under Anarumo in Cincinnati from 2020 through 2024, appearing in 88 games with 24 starts. After moving to Arizona in 2025, he started 13 of 17 games and set career highs with 117 tackles and five passes defended. The Colts signed him on March 18, and Ballard explicitly identified his prior experience with Anarumo as a key factor.

That history matters because a scheme transition is partly a language problem. Davis-Gaither can understand the coordinator’s vocabulary, landmarks, and adjustment logic before Allen has experienced them at NFL speed. Even if Allen wears the communication helmet, Davis-Gaither can confirm calls, settle formations, and serve as a reference point for younger teammates.

His likely value is versatility rather than dominance. At 6-foot-2 and 238 pounds, he has enough size to work inside while his career history includes coverage production, special-teams work, and varied assignments. The Colts can pair him with Allen in base or nickel looks, use his familiarity to stabilize pressure packages, and avoid asking every young linebacker to learn through error simultaneously.

The uncertainty is whether Davis-Gaither is merely a bridge or one of the defense’s best two linebackers for the full season. His 2025 starting experience makes him the safest projection in the room, but Indianapolis’s double draft investment suggests it wants younger players to claim meaningful work. The strongest version of the plan is not Davis-Gaither blocking Allen or Boettcher; it is Davis-Gaither making their development less chaotic.

## Jaylon Carlies is the upside swing

Carlies may be the variable that changes the room’s ceiling. Indianapolis drafted him in the fifth round in 2024 after his transition from college safety to NFL linebacker. At 6-foot-3 and 227 pounds, he represents the hybrid body type teams seek for space defense: enough length and movement skill to match receiving threats, with the possibility of growing into a more complete second-level defender.

Ballard’s March assessment was both enthusiastic and cautionary. He called Carlies as talented as any linebacker the Colts have had, but said the team still did not know what it had because Carlies had to stay healthy. The evidence supports the caution. Carlies missed time during 2025 organized team activities and training camp, then spent more than half the regular season on injured reserve before returning in December.

That makes durability a football-development issue, not a separate footnote. A converted safety needs live repetitions to improve box reads, take-on technique, run fits, and communication. Missed practices slow that conversion even after the player is medically cleared. Carlies’s athletic profile may suit Anarumo’s preference for disguise and multiplicity, but theoretical flexibility only helps if the staff can trust him to execute assignments repeatedly.

If healthy, Carlies could become the coverage-oriented complement to Allen, allowing Davis-Gaither to rotate by package and preserving specialized roles for Boettcher. He could also be used as a matchup piece rather than a conventional every-down starter. Those are projections, not announced roles. The training-camp evidence to watch is where Carlies aligns, whether he works with the top nickel group, and whether he practices without limitation over consecutive weeks.

## Boettcher raises the floor and the pressure

Boettcher is more than a symbolic second linebacker pick. At Oregon he accumulated 269 tackles, 16 tackles for loss, four sacks, four forced fumbles, and two interceptions across four football seasons. He led Oregon in tackles in 2024 and 2025, served as a 2025 team captain, and recorded 136 tackles over 15 games in his final season. His path—from Oregon baseball player and Houston Astros 13th-round selection to walk-on linebacker and NFL fourth-rounder—also demonstrates unusual competitive development.

His immediate route to Sundays may begin on special teams. That is a common role for a fourth-round linebacker, and it is especially logical for a former center fielder with pursuit experience and a high-volume tackling record. But the Colts did not need to draft a second linebacker at No. 135 simply to fill a coverage unit. Boettcher also gives the defense insurance against Carlies’s health, against Allen’s rookie learning curve, and against Davis-Gaither being only a short-term answer.

Boettcher’s presence creates productive pressure. Allen has a clear inside track to the MIKE job, yet the remaining snaps cannot be allocated on pedigree alone. A fourth-round rookie with captaincy and production can force veterans and developmental players to earn their packages. If Boettcher processes quickly, the Colts could have two rookie linebackers under cheap control who grow together. If he begins as a special-teamer, his selection still makes the room less dependent on one fragile projection.

## The rest of the competition

As of July 10, the official roster lists seven off-ball linebackers: Allen, Boettcher, Carlies, Davis-Gaither, Austin Ajiake, Tahj Chambers, and West Weeks. That list is an offseason roster, not a final depth chart.

Ajiake enters with more professional continuity than the undrafted rookies and can compete through special teams and assignment reliability. Chambers and Weeks joined as undrafted free agents after the draft. Their realistic first contest is for roster and practice-squad value, though injuries or strong camp performance can widen the opportunity. The club waived John Bullock and Joseph Vaughn during the spring, further concentrating the room around the present seven.

The numbers matter because NFL defenses rarely use three traditional linebackers on every snap. Nickel personnel often leaves two off-ball linebackers on the field, and passing situations can reduce the role further or replace a linebacker with a defensive back. Indianapolis therefore does not need seven regular defensive contributors. It needs two or three trusted defenders, multiple core special-teamers, and enough developmental depth to survive injury.

That compression makes role fit decisive. A backup who cannot contribute on special teams is difficult to carry. A linebacker who can cover but cannot fit the run may become package-specific. A sound early-down player who struggles in space may leave the defense vulnerable to tempo because the offense can prevent substitution. Camp competition should be evaluated through those constraints rather than a single one-through-seven ranking.

## What changes inside Anarumo’s defense

The most consequential transition is from concentrated responsibility to distributed support. Franklin’s durability allowed the Colts to build continuity around one constant. The 2026 design asks Allen to become the communicator, Davis-Gaither to supply scheme memory, and the defensive line and secondary to share the burden of keeping the middle organized.

This could produce a faster defense. Allen, Carlies, and Boettcher give Indianapolis younger legs, while Davis-Gaither adds experienced mobility. The broader defensive rebuild also added youth and speed at other levels. In theory, that supports Anarumo’s use of disguised coverages, simulated pressure, and changing post-snap pictures: linebackers must threaten gaps, retreat to zones, carry eligible receivers, and tackle in space without revealing the answer too early.

The risk is that multiplicity becomes hesitation. A linebacker who is processing several possible jobs can lose the half-step that makes athleticism useful. A new communicator can deliver a correct call that teammates receive too late. A young group can also be manipulated by motion, play-action, and route combinations designed to create conflicting keys.

The front therefore has an outsized role in the transition. If Buckner, Stewart, Wooden, and the rest of the tackle rotation control interior gaps, the linebackers can flow cleanly and play fast. If offensive linemen consistently reach the second level, a smaller and younger group will have to defeat blocks while still learning the system. The Franklin-for-Wooden trade links the two rooms directly: Indianapolis effectively wagered that greater front depth plus younger linebacker play can outperform the old allocation.

## A practical training-camp scorecard

The first meaningful evidence arrives when camp opens July 29. Five questions will separate genuine progress from offseason optimism.

1. **Who wears the green dot with the first defense?** Ballard projected Allen for it, but repeated top-unit use will show whether the plan is operational.
2. **Which pair leads nickel periods?** Because nickel is a central NFL personnel grouping, Allen’s partner in those periods may reveal more than a nominal base depth chart.
3. **Can Carlies stack healthy practices?** Availability is the prerequisite for every upside case involving him.
4. **Does Boettcher earn defensive work beyond special teams?** Second-unit MIKE reps, pressure-package assignments, or first-team rotation would signal an accelerated timeline.
5. **How often does Davis-Gaither direct traffic?** His involvement can show whether he is a full-time starter, a stabilizing rotational veteran, or the emergency communication option.

Preseason evaluation should remain modest. Vanilla calls and uneven opposition can make fast linebackers look settled. More reliable indicators include clean alignment against motion, correct leverage in coverage, decisive fits without freelancing, and the absence of repeated communication errors. Regular-season evidence will still be required before judging the rebuild.

## The 2026 standard

The transition succeeds if the Colts replace Franklin’s functional value without requiring one player to copy his statistical profile. Allen needs to become a trustworthy organizer and three-down option. Davis-Gaither needs to translate scheme familiarity into stable execution. One of Carlies or Boettcher needs to become a credible complement, while the remaining depth must sustain special teams and survive injuries.

The transition fails if Indianapolis confuses youth with speed of play. Draft status and athletic traits cannot compensate for late reads, loose run fits, or coverage mismatches. Nor can a deep list of candidates compensate for the absence of two linebackers Anarumo trusts in high-leverage situations.

The Colts have created a coherent pathway: trade an older high-volume linebacker for help up front, sign a veteran who speaks the coordinator’s language, draft a rookie signal-caller, and add another productive rookie to the competition. That coherence is worth recognizing. But as of July 10 it remains a roster thesis. Training camp will decide the hierarchy; the regular season will decide whether the defense actually became younger and faster without becoming less dependable.
