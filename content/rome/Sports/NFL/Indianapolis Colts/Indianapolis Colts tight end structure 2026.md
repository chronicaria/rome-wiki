## A room of functions, not a conventional depth chart

As of July 10, the official Colts roster lists six tight ends: Tyler Warren, Mo Alie-Cox, Drew Ogletree, Will Mallory, Sean McKeon, and Carson Towt. Indianapolis re-signed Alie-Cox and Ogletree in March, while Towt joined as an undrafted rookie. That membership is verified. A final roster count, snap order, and assignment hierarchy are not. Training camp does not open until July 29, and the club has not supplied regular-season usage evidence for 2026.

Calling this a contest for “TE1” would obscure the actual decision. Warren already played 899 offensive snaps, started all 17 games, and caught 76 passes for 817 yards and four touchdowns as a rookie in 2025. He also scored once as a runner. The club says his catches and receiving yards set franchise rookie-tight-end records, and he was added to the 2026 Pro Bowl. Those results make him the established center of the room, barring a future availability change. They do not make the other five players interchangeable reserves.

An NFL tight end may attach to the line, detach into the slot, align outside, move across the formation, enter the backfield, protect, release late, block in space, or cover kicks. Each job tests different traits. A good route runner is not automatically the best point-of-attack blocker; a strong attached blocker may not be the best motion player; a useful offensive specialist may lose a game-day place if he cannot contribute on special teams. Indianapolis therefore has to construct a portfolio rather than merely rank six names.

The central 2026 question is whether the secondary group makes Warren more flexible. If another tight end can credibly block, release, protect, and play on fourth down, Warren can move without advertising the play. If each reserve is a narrow specialist, personnel and alignment may tell the defense what is coming. This is an analytical test, not a claim that any current reserve has already won or failed it.

## Tyler Warren changes what a personnel label means

Warren's rookie alignment record is the foundation. The Colts' official season review, citing Pro Football Focus, reported 466 snaps technically aligned at tight end, 384 aligned as a receiver in the slot or outside, 64 in the backfield, and three in the wildcat. The categories are not a complete assignment chart, but they establish that Indianapolis used him in far more than one location.

That movement can create leverage before the snap. With Warren and another tight end on the field, Indianapolis can present “12 personnel”—one running back and two tight ends—then detach Warren and create a spread passing surface. A defense that answers with a larger run-oriented grouping may have to cover him in space. A defense that stays light may give [[Indianapolis Colts running game 2026|Jonathan Taylor's run game]] a favorable body-type matchup. The offense can also shift Warren from the backfield or slot and change the apparent formation strength without substituting.

Those are possible mechanisms, not guaranteed wins. Alignment does not itself create separation, move a defender, or identify pressure. Warren still must execute the route or block; the quarterback must process the coverage; and the line must protect. Defenses can also match flexible personnel with safeties, hybrid defenders, late rotation, or disciplined zone rules. The useful claim is narrower: Warren gives Shane Steichen more ways to pose a run-pass question with the same group of players.

His second season should therefore not be evaluated only by whether 817 receiving yards becomes a larger number. More targets could reflect improvement, but they could also narrow his role or arise because game states force Indianapolis to throw. A better evaluation tracks receiving efficiency by alignment and situation, blocking responsibility, motion, protection participation, third-down and red-zone use, and whether his movement changes the defense's personnel or coverage. Warren's value lies partly in touches and partly in the choices he forces before other players receive the ball.

Workload is another open variable. His 899 offensive snaps represented a major rookie burden. Indianapolis may keep him on the field extensively because his versatility prevents predictable substitutions. It may also distribute attached blocking or special-teams labor to preserve his receiving and movement value. Neither approach can be inferred from the July roster. Camp and preseason can show experiments; only sustained regular-season usage can establish the 2026 design.

## Why the second tight end matters

Indianapolis used at least two tight ends on 383 offensive snaps in 2025, according to Colts reporting, compared with an average of 245 such snaps across 2023 and 2024. That increase does not prove every multi-tight-end snap was effective. It does establish that the personnel family was a material part of the offense rather than an occasional specialty.

The second tight end determines what that family can do. In the strongest version, he can align attached, move, block either direction, release into a route, and participate in protection without making any one call obvious. Warren can then become the movable piece. Indianapolis can run toward or away from the attached surface, use play action, send Warren across the formation, or release both tight ends. The defense has to diagnose the assignment after seeing the same people.

In a weaker version, the second player's presence identifies a narrow function. If he almost always blocks and seldom threatens a route, a defender can fit the run more aggressively or redirect coverage elsewhere. If he is primarily a detached receiver, the defense may treat the grouping like three-receiver personnel. Specialists can still be valuable, especially in short yardage or the red zone, but their limits create tendencies that coaches must manage.

This makes target totals a poor standalone measure of the secondary tight ends. A player may release infrequently because his protection or point-of-attack assignment enables a deeper concept. He may motion to alter the run fit, block the edge on Taylor's carry, or sell play action before becoming an outlet. Conversely, low targets do not automatically prove good hidden work. Blocking and protection must be checked play by play rather than granted from reputation, listed size, or role labels.

The 383-snap figure also connects the room to [[Indianapolis Colts pass-catcher structure 2026|receiver allocation]]. More two-tight-end personnel usually means one fewer receiver on the field. Colts reporting linked the heavier 2025 usage to Josh Downs's reduced snap opportunity. In 2026, after Michael Pittman Jr.'s trade to Pittsburgh, Indianapolis must decide whether heavier personnel helps redistribute his former work or removes a receiver the offense needs. Warren can soften that tradeoff because he can function as a receiver, but the other tight end must make the package sufficiently credible to justify the personnel choice.

## The five candidates around Warren

### Mo Alie-Cox

Alie-Cox's March re-signing is direct evidence that Indianapolis chose to retain him. His long tenure and listed tight-end role make him a familiar candidate for attached and blocking work, but familiarity is not a current grade. The 2026 evaluation must establish whether he can still execute point-of-attack, movement, protection, and occasional route assignments against the players he faces now.

His best route to value is not to mimic Warren. It is to handle enough physical and low-volume labor that Warren can be deployed where he changes the formation most. Yet “blocking tight end” should not become an exemption from receiving credibility. Even an infrequent route must be timed and threatening enough that defenders cannot abandon it without consequence. Camp observers should record assignment and result, not simply praise a collision or infer a role from body type.

### Drew Ogletree

Ogletree was also re-signed in March, showing that the club wanted him in the competition. He belongs in the same assignment-specific audit: attached blocking, motion, routes, pass protection, and special teams. A retained veteran can supply continuity, but a contract transaction does not guarantee a final-roster place or a particular number of offensive snaps.

The important comparison with Alie-Cox is not a generic ranking. It is whether Ogletree covers a different or broader combination of jobs. If both players occupy nearly identical functions, Indianapolis may face a roster-efficiency decision. If one offers movement or special-teams utility and the other provides a distinct attached role, carrying both may preserve options. That complementarity remains an inference to test once padded work begins.

### Will Mallory

Mallory presents another possible receiving and movement profile within the room. The official roster confirms his membership, not how the current staff grades him. His path depends on whether route value comes with enough blocking, protection, and fourth-down utility to make him active when Warren already owns a major receiving role.

This is where preseason box scores can mislead. Several catches against reserve coverage may be useful evidence of route execution and hands, but they do not answer whether Mallory can align correctly across the menu, protect a quarterback, block an NFL edge, or cover kicks. Conversely, a low receiving total may conceal sound work. His evaluation needs the full sequence of assignments.

### Sean McKeon

McKeon supplies veteran competition and another potential multi-phase option. As with the other reserves, membership and experience do not establish a place. His case should be read through repeatable jobs: which formations he enters, whether he works attached or as a mover, how he executes protection, and which special-teams units use him.

A reserve who can cover several emergency functions can have more roster value than a superior one-task specialist. That is particularly true if Indianapolis carries an unsettled right-tackle situation and wants tight-end help available without removing every route threat. Whether McKeon is that player is a question for evidence, not a conclusion available in July.

### Carson Towt

Towt is the developmental outlier. The Colts signed him after a college career that included basketball, making his transition history relevant background. Basketball experience can suggest movement, spatial awareness, and ball skills, but it does not prove NFL route detail, blocking technique, protection recognition, or special-teams reliability. The conversion should be treated as a reason to investigate traits, not as proof of a familiar success story.

Towt's 2026 progress is best measured in small, concrete gains: correct alignment, legal hand placement, pad level, route depth, assignment retention, and special-teams participation. A developmental player may justify long-term investment without being ready for a regular offensive role. The expanded offseason roster permits that evaluation; the later 53-player and practice-squad decisions force Indianapolis to compare his upside with present game-day coverage.

## Protection and the right-tackle interaction

The tight-end decision cannot be separated from [[Indianapolis Colts offensive line 2026|pass protection]]. Indianapolis enters camp without a settled public answer at right tackle. If the eventual tackle can handle ordinary edge assignments without consistent assistance, Steichen can release more eligible receivers. If that position needs chips or full tight-end help, the room's protection ability becomes more important and the route distribution changes.

Help is not free. A tight end who chips before releasing may arrive late to his route. A tight end kept in protection removes one immediate coverage threat. Sliding or changing the formation to supply help may affect the back, center, and opposite tackle. These can be rational tradeoffs, especially against an elite rusher, but they reduce the offense's available answers elsewhere.

Warren's versatility offers one solution only if another player can absorb the protection job. Indianapolis could keep a secondary tight end attached while moving Warren, or use Warren as the helper and release another player. The better option will depend on opponent, quarterback, route concept, and each player's actual technique. Camp should test recognition and execution against movement and pressure, not merely one-on-one blocking strength.

Quarterback state matters too. Daniel Jones is returning from an Achilles injury, while Anthony Richardson, Riley Leonard, and Easton Stick present different experience and movement profiles. This article does not project the starter. It identifies a shared requirement: tight ends must understand the protection and route adjustments well enough that the offense does not solve one problem by creating another. The cleanest block is wasted if the eligible misses the route conversion the quarterback expects.

## Special teams and the game-day roster

Secondary tight ends often live at the intersection of offense and special teams. The regular-season roster and active list cannot carry a separate specialist for every offensive contingency. A reserve who blocks on return units, covers kicks, and handles an emergency offensive role can justify a uniform even with few targets. A better receiver who contributes on no kicking unit may be inactive behind Warren.

That mechanism should be observed directly. Which players participate in kickoff, kickoff return, punt, and punt return periods? Are they core participants or temporary bodies? Do they block in space without penalties, tackle, maintain lane responsibility, and adjust to different assignments? One spectacular offensive preseason play cannot erase repeated fourth-down mistakes, just as special-teams participation alone cannot prove quality.

The final tight-end count will interact with receiver, running-back, and offensive-line depth. Carrying four tight ends may preserve heavy-personnel options and special-teams labor but remove a place from another group. Carrying three demands broader competence from each reserve. Keeping a developmental fourth player on the practice squad may preserve upside, but only if he clears the relevant roster process and remains available. July membership cannot settle those later choices.

## An evidence plan for camp and preseason

The correct unit chart should record more than first-team repetitions. For every meaningful tight-end snap, track personnel grouping, alignment, motion, down and distance, run or pass, route or blocking assignment, opponent level, quarterback, result, and special-teams phase. Availability context matters: extra work created by another player's absence is an opportunity, not automatically a promotion.

Padded team periods and preseason games will be more informative than unpadded catches because they can test leverage, protection, releases through contact, and full-play consequences. Even then, one rep cannot settle a role. Repeated execution across several practices and games is the stronger evidence. The staff may also deliberately test a player's weakness, so a cluster of difficult assignments can be developmental information rather than a transparent depth-chart vote.

Five practical tests organize the room:

1. **Formation credibility:** Can the offense run and pass from the same grouping without the second tight end revealing the call?
2. **Warren freedom:** Does the secondary configuration let Warren move, or must he repeatedly absorb attached and protection work?
3. **Blocking portability:** Can each candidate execute attached, motion, space, and protection assignments rather than winning only one type?
4. **Receiving consequence:** Do reserve routes punish defenders who ignore them, even if target volume is low?
5. **Game-day labor:** Which candidates provide repeatable special-teams value sufficient to dress?

The likely strategic objective is clear, though the winning personnel are not: preserve Warren's multiplicity while assembling enough complementary competence that two-tight-end personnel remains genuinely ambiguous. Indianapolis has six candidates and a demonstrated history of heavier usage. It does not yet have 2026 evidence assigning the five secondary players to stable jobs. That uncertainty should survive until camp, preseason, roster reduction, and regular-season snaps resolve it.
