As of 2026-07-09, Indianapolis has four credible returning starters and a deliberately unresolved right-tackle succession: the unit's ceiling rests on proven left-side quality and young interior development, while its largest uncertainty is whether Jalen Travis can replace Braden Smith over a full season.

Up: [[Indianapolis Colts 2026]]

## The direct answer

The [[Indianapolis Colts]] enter 2026 with a clearer offensive-line plan than the phrase “one open position” suggests. Bernhard Raimann at left tackle, Quenton Nelson at left guard, Tanor Bortolini at center, and Matt Goncalves at right guard all started in 2025. The official team roster still carries all four. Braden Smith, the fifth member of that group, left for Houston in March 2026 after 105 starts across eight Indianapolis seasons. The Colts did not replace him with a veteran purchase or premium draft pick. Their public reporting instead points toward 2025 fourth-rounder Jalen Travis, who started the final four games of his rookie year—three at right tackle and one at left tackle—and allowed one sack in 165 pass-blocking snaps according to PFF data cited by the club.

That makes the most reasonable *projected* five Raimann–Nelson–Bortolini–Goncalves–Travis. It is a projection, not an official depth chart. Team reporting in May described some combination of Goncalves, Travis, and 2026 fourth-round guard Jalen Farmer as filling the right side. The distinction matters: spring and summer combinations are developmental evidence, while a Week 1 lineup requires health, performance, and coaches' decisions that had not occurred by this article's as-of date.

The larger story is organizational. Indianapolis has built almost the entire room through the draft, developed mid-round selections into starters, and accepted planned turnover rather than paying every incumbent. Ryan Kelly and Will Fries departed after 2024; Bortolini and Goncalves stepped in during 2025. Smith departed after 2025; Travis now has the inside track to succeed him. This is not merely thrift. It is a bet that offensive-line coach Tony Sparano Jr.'s development process can keep converting traits and cross-training into playable depth.

This inventory is not a final 53-man roster. In July, an offseason roster is intentionally larger than the regular-season limit, and position labels are coarse. Bortolini is the clearest warning against reading the roster page as a depth chart: NFL.com lists him as a guard, but Indianapolis identifies him as the player who became its full-time center in 2025. Goncalves likewise demonstrates how the team's development model values position flexibility. He played almost all of his 564 rookie snaps in 2024 at tackle, then moved to right guard for 2025.

The room therefore contains three overlapping layers. Four returning starters form the core. Travis, Farmer, and perhaps Goncalves comprise the live right-side configuration space. The remaining players compete for game-day versatility and reserve jobs. The Colts can carry only a limited number of linemen on a game-day active list, so a reserve who can cover both tackle spots or both center and guard has more practical value than a one-position label implies.

## Why the left side is the stabilizer

Raimann and Nelson give Indianapolis two strong knowns at the positions most capable of insulating change elsewhere.

Raimann signed a contract extension on July 29, 2025. The Colts' announcement did not disclose terms, but it established the important football fact: the club chose continuity at left tackle before his rookie contract expired. Its supporting evidence said Raimann's combined 2023–24 PFF pass-block grade was 85.2 and his run-block grade 82.1, both tenth among qualifying tackles over that span. In 2025, team reporting described him as entrenched. The exact grading methodology is proprietary and should not be treated as objective truth, but multiple-year quality in both phases plus a long-term extension is stronger evidence than a single highlight or camp report.

Nelson remains the line's veteran reference point. He started all 17 games in 2025, earned second-team Associated Press All-Pro recognition, and made an eighth consecutive Pro Bowl. The Colts reported PFF grades of 84.5 overall and 84.3 as a run blocker, both fourth among starting guards. Awards can be reputation-sensitive, and grades can differ across evaluators, but the combination of availability, peer and media recognition, and independent charting supports the modest conclusion that Nelson remained a high-level guard in 2025.

Their adjacency matters. A left tackle and left guard who communicate well can handle games, twists, and overloaded fronts without forcing the center to rescue every exchange. Nelson can also help establish the aiming points and displacement Indianapolis wants in the run game. That does not make the left side invulnerable, nor does it mean protection should always slide right. It means the Colts begin the 2026 installation with fewer unknowns on one side of the formation, allowing coaching attention and tight-end or running-back help to be allocated situationally.

## Bortolini and Goncalves made the succession plan credible

The 2025 transition is the best evidence for Indianapolis's 2026 confidence. After the 2024 season, long-time center Ryan Kelly and right guard Will Fries signed with Minnesota. The Colts did not merely hope their replacements would appear. They had drafted Bortolini in the fourth round and Goncalves in the third round in 2024, then given both real rookie snaps before asking them to take larger roles.

Bortolini made five center starts as a rookie and became the full-time center in 2025. Colts reporting in May 2026 said he finished that season as PFF's third-highest-graded center. A Week 7 PFF snapshot had already shown the trajectory: through that point Bortolini held a 77.7 grade, second among centers in that ranking, and posted an 85.3 single-game grade against Arizona. The season-long result matters more than the one-game high. The inference is that Bortolini did not simply survive the handoff from Kelly; independent charting judged his first full starting season favorably.

Center performance is not reducible to an individual grade. The player must identify fronts, make or relay protection calls within the quarterback's system, snap accurately, reach shaded defenders, and pass off interior movement with both guards. Those responsibilities become especially important when the quarterback situation or cadence changes. Bortolini's successful 2025 increases confidence, but it does not prove that every pressure, run-game error, or protection success belongs to him. Five-man line play is unusually interdependent.

Goncalves is the more complicated evaluation. Indianapolis drafted him after a college career at tackle, used him at both tackle spots as a rookie, and moved him to right guard in 2025. At midseason, PFF graded him 66.2 overall, 24th among guards in its Week 7 table, after an Arizona game in which it charged him with five pressures and four quarterback hits. A later 2026 Colts article nevertheless described him as the 2025 starting right guard on a line that earned PFF's fourth-highest collective grade.

Both statements can be true. A young guard can have a damaging game and still become an adequate or useful season-long starter. Goncalves's tackle background may help him understand the spacing and exchange problems on the right edge, particularly beside another young player. His 6-foot-6, 317-pound listed frame also gives the Colts mass inside. The unresolved question is whether his pass protection becomes consistently clean enough that defenses cannot isolate the right A- or B-gap on known passing downs.

## The right-tackle succession is a real test

Smith's exit removes much more than a name from the depth chart. He started 105 games for Indianapolis and had held the right-tackle job since 2018. Even when injuries interrupted seasons, the Colts knew the veteran's technique, communication habits, and matchup profile. A successor must reproduce the assignment reliability of a long-term starter while working beside Goncalves, who has only one full season at guard.

Travis has the strongest claim because the team already tested him. The Colts selected him 127th overall in 2025 after a college career split between Princeton and Iowa State. He had 26 college starts—18 at left tackle and eight at right tackle—and entered the league listed at 6-foot-8 and 339 pounds. During his rookie season he returned primarily to the right side for the final four games. Team reporting says those four starts included three at right tackle and one at left tackle, with one sack allowed in 165 pass-blocking snaps and a 74.2 PFF overall grade.

That is promising evidence, not a settled verdict. A four-game sample can be distorted by opponent quality, protection design, quarterback time to throw, and the kinds of pass sets called. Defenses also gain an offseason to study a young tackle's vertical set, hand timing, response to inside counters, and ability to redirect against speed-to-power. Travis's exceptional height and length can widen the arc for edge rushers, but taller tackles must consistently control pad level and prevent rushers from winning underneath them. Those technique claims are general positional mechanisms, not a film verdict on Travis's 2025 snaps.

The Colts' choice to let Smith leave without a major external replacement implies confidence in Travis and their developmental pipeline. That is an inference from roster behavior and public comments, not proof that Travis has been promised the job. Farmer complicates the picture constructively. Indianapolis drafted the Kentucky guard 113th overall in 2026, and Sparano publicly praised his size, length, power, quickness, and improvement. Farmer did not allow a sack in his final college season according to NFL.com analysis relayed by the Colts. Yet he remains a rookie guard, and no college statistic establishes immediate NFL readiness.

One plausible alternative is Farmer winning right guard and Goncalves shifting back toward tackle, where he already has NFL experience. Another is Travis starting at tackle while Farmer becomes the sixth interior lineman. The first would create two position changes instead of one; the second preserves four-fifths continuity. For that reason, and because the club explicitly identified Travis as Smith's likely replacement in its free-agency coverage, Travis at tackle with Goncalves at guard is the cleaner baseline forecast.

## What the construction model is buying

Indianapolis's approach spreads investment differently from a line assembled through free agency. Nelson is the sole first-rounder. Raimann was a third-rounder; Goncalves a third; Bortolini and Travis fourth-rounders. All were Colts draft picks. The club's May 2026 analysis said Indianapolis and Philadelphia were the only teams among PFF's ten highest-graded 2025 lines whose five starters were all home-grown. That comparison came from the team and uses PFF's grading as a rough boundary, but the underlying roster fact is meaningful.

Developmental success creates surplus value during rookie contracts. Bortolini, Goncalves, and Travis can occupy starting or near-starting roles without veteran-market salaries, leaving resources for other positions. It also creates continuity of terminology because players learn within the same system. The cost is concentrated coaching risk. If the organization misjudges a developmental player, the replacement market may be thin by late summer. If multiple young linemen struggle together, the offense cannot hide every matchup with chips, slides, and quick throws without constraining route distribution and play design.

Cross-training is the hedge. Goncalves has played guard and tackle. Travis has played both tackle sides. Bortolini entered the league with college experience across the interior, although his verified current role is center. Freeland provides additional tackle experience, while Tucker, Sills, Morrissey, Farmer, Kreutz, and VanDeMark create interior combinations. The value is not that all are interchangeable. It is that the coaching staff can survive a single injury without teaching an entirely new position from zero.

## How the line shapes the offense

The Colts' offensive line should be evaluated through the jobs it enables, not just sacks allowed.

First, the run game needs coordinated movement. [[Jonathan Taylor]] benefits when the front can create a clear first read and prevent penetration from forcing a premature cut. Nelson's power, Raimann's movement skills, and Bortolini's athletic profile give Indianapolis tools for both gap and zone concepts. On the right, the question is whether Goncalves and Travis can generate displacement together and sort edge movement without losing the backside cutoff.

Second, pass protection must fit quarterback health and style. Daniel Jones re-signed in March after a 2025 Achilles tear; his availability and mobility at the start of 2026 were still uncertain in spring reporting. Anthony Richardson and Riley Leonard present different movement and timing profiles. Sack totals therefore cannot be assigned solely to the line: quarterbacks influence pressure through depth, timing, pocket movement, and decisions. Still, stable interior protection is particularly valuable because immediate A-gap pressure can erase the quarterback's ability to step up regardless of which player starts.

Third, the fifth lineman affects formation freedom. If Travis can handle ordinary edge matchups without constant help, Shane Steichen can release tight ends and backs into routes more often. If he needs frequent chips, the offense can still function, but each helper reduces the number or timing of receiving options. This is why the right-tackle competition has consequences beyond a one-on-one grade.

## A disciplined 2026 forecast

The Colts should begin 2026 with an above-average chance of fielding a good offensive line because four starters return from a group the available independent grading rated highly, and because Raimann and Nelson are established high-level players. That conclusion is evidence-backed but still probabilistic.

The upside case is straightforward: Bortolini consolidates his strong first season as a full-time center; Goncalves improves his consistency at guard; Travis translates his four-start audition across a full schedule; and the left side remains healthy. In that case, Indianapolis preserves a top-tier unit despite losing Smith and validates a second consecutive internal succession.

The downside case is also coherent. Travis's small sample fails to hold against a full menu of NFL rush plans, Goncalves's uneven pass-protection moments recur, or an injury forces multiple players to change positions simultaneously. Right-side communication problems would then compound rather than remain isolated. A line can contain several individually talented players and still fail when exchanges are late.

The best current label is therefore **strong core, credible plan, unresolved edge**. Calling the unit elite before 2026 games would overstate the evidence. Calling right tackle an empty hole would ignore Travis's rookie starts, the club's explicit succession language, and the recent success of the same development process with Bortolini and Goncalves.

## What to watch in camp and September

- **First-team right-side combinations:** Repeated Travis–Goncalves work is stronger evidence of the baseline plan than isolated rotations. Farmer taking sustained first-team guard snaps would reopen the alignment question.
- **Unassisted third-down sets:** Travis's ability to survive obvious passing situations without routine chip help is more informative than raw preseason sack totals.
- **Interior games and blitz pickup:** Bortolini and both guards must pass off movement cleanly. Communication failures often reveal themselves through two blockers taking one defender while another runs free.
- **Position response after injury:** Which reserve enters, and whether one injury causes one substitution or several position changes, will expose the staff's true versatility hierarchy.
- **Penalty profile:** False starts and holding calls can be symptoms of a tackle losing the timing battle even when a pressure is prevented.
- **Run-game menu:** The concepts Indianapolis trusts behind the right side will show whether coaches view it as an attack surface or merely something to protect.

None of these observations alone certifies a depth chart. Camp repetitions are evidence of current usage, and preseason opponents do not deploy full regular-season plans. The first meaningful answer arrives only when the Colts protect against starting-caliber rushers over complete games.
