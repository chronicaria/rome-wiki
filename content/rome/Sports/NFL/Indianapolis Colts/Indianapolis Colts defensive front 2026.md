The 2026 Colts are trying to turn a veteran interior and Laiatu Latu's ascending edge game into a flexible, sustainable front; the central question is whether a rebuilt rotation can create pressure and close run lanes without asking DeForest Buckner and Grover Stewart to carry the unit every week.

## Why it matters

The defensive front is the hinge of Lou Anarumo's defense. Indianapolis invested heavily in the secondary by acquiring Sauce Gardner during the 2025 season, but coverage talent alone cannot rescue a defense if the quarterback has time to reset or if an offense can stay on schedule by running into light boxes. Conversely, a four-man rush that wins quickly lets Anarumo disguise coverage, rotate safeties late and save his pressure calls for moments when the offense cannot easily diagnose them.

That makes the 2026 front more than a list of linemen. It is the mechanism that connects coverage, down-and-distance and turnover creation. The Colts need it to do three things at once: generate credible pressure with four rushers, control the interior run game, and survive a full season through a rotation that does not collapse when a core veteran misses time.

As of July 10, 2026, the first two jobs still center on familiar names. Latu, Buckner and Stewart are the most proven Colts in their respective roles. The third job is where the roster changed dramatically. Kwity Paye, Samson Ebukam, Neville Gallimore and other 2025 contributors are gone. Indianapolis replaced them with a large group that includes Arden Key, Micheal Clemons, Colby Wooden, Derrick Nnadi and Jerry Tillery, while also adding rookie edges George Gumbs Jr. and Caden Curry and undrafted defensive tackle Cameron Ball. The resulting front has more possible combinations than established answers.

## The foundation: three different kinds of stress

Latu is the primary edge threat. The Colts' official offseason review credits him with 8.5 sacks in 2025, up from four as a rookie. His rookie underlying production had already suggested room to grow: the club cited 38 pressures, 12 quarterback hits and a 14 percent pass-rush win rate in 2024. In Week 4 of 2025, Pro Football Focus charting cited by Colts.com gave him nine pressures, the most by an Indianapolis defender in a game in four years. Those numbers do not prove future stardom, but they do establish a trajectory: Latu has moved from technically polished prospect to the front's clearest individual pressure source.

His value is not simply speed around the corner. A rusher with multiple counters changes the protection before the snap. If a tackle sets too aggressively outside, Latu can threaten inside; if the offense slides help toward him, another rusher receives a one-on-one. The Colts can also move him through games with an interior penetrator. In an official 2025 example against Arizona, Buckner drove upfield while Latu looped behind him; on a later fourth down, Buckner absorbed a double team and the same stunt created a free run for Latu. That is a compact illustration of the front's desired geometry: one player changes two blockers' paths so another attacks the vacated lane.

Buckner supplies a different problem. At 6-foot-7 and 295 pounds on the current roster, he can rush from the interior, push the pocket and obstruct throwing lanes. Interior pressure is especially valuable because a quarterback cannot always escape it by stepping forward. Buckner also gives the Colts a movable piece for tackle-end exchanges and overloaded fronts. The club says he averaged 8.1 sacks and 13.1 tackles for loss per 17 games across 2024 and 2025, although injuries interrupted both seasons. His 2026 recovery from a neck injury is therefore the largest health variable in the group. In May he said he was aiming to return for training camp; that goal is not the same as medical clearance or a guarantee of a normal workload.

Stewart is the mass and leverage component. Listed at 314 pounds, he is the defender most naturally suited to anchoring against double teams, denting combination blocks and keeping the linebackers clean. His work can disappear from the box score because the successful play may be forcing a runner to hesitate or making two linemen remain attached to him. Yet he also affected the passing game in 2025. Colts.com documented his role in three early-season interceptions: two arose from pocket pressure and another from a tipped pass; he later intercepted a pass himself against the Chargers. The lesson is not that Stewart is a coverage playmaker. It is that an interior defender can change a pass without recording a sack.

Together, the three create complementary stress. Latu threatens the edge and wins with sequencing. Buckner can penetrate or occupy protection from inside. Stewart can compress the run fit and push the center of the pocket. The ideal is multiplicative: the offense cannot devote extra resources to every threat, and the Colts can manufacture advantageous paths without sending a fifth rusher.

## The rebuilt edge rotation

The depth chart opposite Latu is open enough to be a genuine competition. General manager Chris Ballard identified second-year end Jaylahn "J.T." Tuimoloau as a player the Colts expect to take a step in Year 2. Tuimoloau, a 2025 second-round pick, has the size to play a sturdier edge role and could complement Latu by handling early downs or reducing inside in selected fronts. That projected step is organizational belief, not established 2026 production.

Arden Key is the most experienced new pass-rush option. He spent 2023–25 with Tennessee after earlier stops with the Raiders, 49ers and Jaguars. At a listed 240 pounds, he profiles more naturally as a pressure specialist than as the front's primary edge setter. The likely appeal is known NFL rush ability and the freedom to deploy him in passing situations without forcing Latu to take every high-value snap. Whether Key starts, rotates or becomes part of a designated third-down package remains unsettled.

Micheal Clemons adds a different frame at 263 pounds. A bigger end can set a firmer edge, reduce the space available on outside zone and power, and give the coordinator a way to avoid telegraphing pass rush through personnel. The Colts also drafted two developmental edges: Florida's George Gumbs Jr. in the fifth round and Ohio State's Caden Curry in the sixth. Gumbs converted from wide receiver to defense during college, which makes his athletic upside and limited developmental runway inseparable. Curry arrives with more recent production: the Colts' draft page says he led Ohio State in 2025 with 16.5 tackles for loss and recorded 11 sacks.

Those rookies widen the menu but should not be treated as finished solutions. Day-three edge defenders often need time to refine hand usage, rush plans and run-defense discipline. The current roster also lists Mitchell Melton and Durell Nchami. Their path depends heavily on special teams, preseason reliability and whether Indianapolis keeps a larger-than-usual defensive-line group.

The practical aim is a wave rather than a fixed pair. Latu should receive the most consequential rush opportunities, but Key and the younger ends can preserve his legs, create matchup-specific packages and reduce the damage of an injury. A credible third and fourth edge also makes stunts more dangerous because offenses cannot identify the loop player or penetrator solely from star personnel.

## The interior rotation and its two timelines

Indianapolis is managing two timelines at defensive tackle. Buckner and Stewart remain the best-known players, but both are 32. Ballard explicitly framed the offseason task as building depth behind them. The club added Colby Wooden, Derrick Nnadi and Jerry Tillery while retaining Adetomiwa Adebawore and 2025 sixth-round pick Tim Smith.

Wooden may be the most important bridge piece. The Colts acquired him from Green Bay in the trade that sent linebacker Zaire Franklin to the Packers. Wooden said Green Bay used him mainly at nose tackle in 2025, while Indianapolis discussed playing him from three- to five-technique — aligned from an offensive guard's outside shoulder toward the tackle. At a listed 273 pounds, that role better fits a mobile interior defender than repeated nose work. He can rotate behind Buckner, reduce outside in heavier fronts and potentially become part of pressure packages built on movement.

Nnadi is the cleaner Stewart analogue. The Colts list him at 317 pounds, and his eight seasons in Kansas City included 98 starts in 130 regular-season games. His career totals — five sacks but 249 tackles — describe a player hired primarily for interior stability rather than explosive pass-rush production. The three Super Bowl rings noted by the club do not themselves guarantee performance, but his durability and experience make him a plausible early-down reserve who can absorb nose snaps without forcing Stewart into an unsustainable share.

Tillery provides length and alignment flexibility. He is listed at 6-foot-6 and 295 pounds, a body type closer to Buckner's than Nnadi's. That does not make their games interchangeable. It does give Anarumo another possible interior rusher and a lineman who can align across several techniques. Adebawore, a 282-pound athlete whom Ballard said flashed late in 2025, remains a developmental penetration option. Smith offers a heavier body at 306 pounds. Cameron Ball, an undrafted rookie listed at 310, is a roster-development candidate rather than a player to pencil into the rotation before camp.

The rotation can therefore be organized by function: Stewart and Nnadi for anchor snaps; Buckner, Wooden, Tillery and Adebawore for varying blends of penetration and movement; Smith as a young interior option. Real roles will overlap. The purpose of the categories is to show how the Colts might avoid asking every tackle to do the same job.

## How the pressure plan can work

Anarumo's reputation is rooted in multiplicity, but multiplicity does not mean constant blitzing. Before he arrived in Indianapolis, Colts.com reported that his Cincinnati defenses blitzed on 28.6 percent of opponent dropbacks from 2019–24, 18th in the NFL over that span. The important contrast was with the prior Indianapolis system, which blitzed at the league's lowest rate from 2022–24. Anarumo can send extra rushers, but he can also present pressure, drop a potential rusher and force the protection to account for threats that never come.

For the front, four mechanisms matter.

First is isolated winning. Latu must force tackles to turn their shoulders, while Buckner must create vertical interior displacement. Scheme can improve their odds, but a defense eventually needs someone to defeat a block.

Second is the stunt game. A tackle or end attacks a blocker to open a lane for a looping teammate. The Buckner–Latu Arizona sequence is the clearest documented Colts example. Wooden's mobility and Tillery's length could create additional pairings, while a sturdier end such as Tuimoloau or Clemons can act as the penetrator.

Third is alignment ambiguity. By placing several players near the line and changing which four rush, Indianapolis can make the offensive line declare its protection incorrectly. A linebacker or defensive back may replace a dropping end, creating a simulated pressure without reducing the coverage to an all-out blitz. This is a schematic inference based on Anarumo's established approach; the Colts had not published their 2026 package as of the as-of date.

Fourth is coverage-created pressure. Gardner and the secondary can take away the first read long enough for the rush to arrive. The relationship also runs in reverse: early pressure can force a hurried throw into late-rotating coverage. The front and back end should therefore be judged together, not as independent units.

## How the run-defense plan can work

Run defense begins with gap integrity. Against zone runs, interior linemen must prevent clean combination blocks from climbing to the linebackers. Against gap schemes, the front must wrong-arm, spill or box pulling blockers according to the call. A spectacular upfield rush that opens a cutback lane can be a losing play even if it looks disruptive.

Stewart's ability to occupy space is the starting point. If he controls the center or guard and keeps the interior from widening, the runner's track compresses. Buckner can then penetrate an adjacent gap, and the edge defender can set a boundary that sends the ball back toward pursuit. Nnadi exists partly to preserve that structure when Stewart rests. Clemons and Tuimoloau may be especially useful when the Colts value edge firmness over maximum pass-rush speed.

The linebacker transition raises the stakes. Franklin, the defense's leading snap player in 2025, was traded for Wooden, and rookie CJ Allen is expected to compete for the MIKE role. A sound front can simplify a young linebacker's reads by preventing free offensive linemen from reaching the second level. An unsound front does the opposite: it forces the linebacker to sort traffic while moving backward. This is why adding Wooden is not merely swapping an off-ball defender for another athlete. The trade redistributed responsibility toward the line and toward a younger linebacker group.

Indianapolis must also avoid equating heavy personnel with automatic run success. Modern offenses can spread a defense and run into favorable boxes, use motion to alter force responsibilities, or read an unblocked edge defender. The useful advantage of this roster is that several linemen can move across alignments. If Wooden, Tillery or Tuimoloau can defend both run and pass credibly, the Colts can change fronts without substituting and revealing the call.

## The fault lines

The first risk is age and health inside. Colts.com reported that Indianapolis went 1–6 in games Buckner missed in 2025. That record is not a clean measure of his individual value — quarterback injuries, opponent quality and other variables matter — but it underscores how poorly the season held together during his absence. His neck recovery and workload deserve more attention than optimistic spring language.

The second risk is projection opposite Latu. Paye's 721 defensive snaps in 2025 left with him, and the replacements are a mix of a second-year player, veterans with defined but incomplete skill sets and day-three rookies. The Colts may have more options without yet having a proven every-down answer.

The third risk is role congestion. A deep group only helps if the staff identifies useful combinations and preserves enough special-teams value to dress the needed linemen. Tillery, Wooden and Adebawore may compete for similar interior-rush snaps. Key and the rookie edges may compete for a limited number of pass-down opportunities behind Latu. Camp must turn theoretical flexibility into a coherent rotation.

The fourth risk is dependence on game state. Pass rush flourishes when the offense creates leads and forces opponents into obvious throwing situations. If the run defense yields efficient early-down gains, Anarumo loses access to his broadest pressure menu. The front's most revealing statistic may therefore be how often it creates third-and-long, not simply its sack total.

## What success would look like

A successful 2026 front would not require every newcomer to break out. It would require Latu to remain the lead edge threat, Buckner to return healthy enough to influence protections, and Stewart to continue anchoring the run. Around them, two or three additions must settle into repeatable jobs: perhaps Key as a passing-down rusher, Wooden as a movable three-to-five-technique and Nnadi as Stewart's relief.

The best version creates pressure without predictable blitzing, maintains its run fits when the starters rotate out and enters December with its core players fresh. The mediocre version produces sacks in favorable games but cannot control early downs or function without Buckner. The poor version discovers that nominal depth is not functional depth.

That distinction will not be resolved by the offseason roster. Training-camp alignment, preseason snap distribution and the first month of regular-season third-down packages will provide the evidence. For now, the Colts have built a front with a clear top end and many plausible supporting pieces. Its ceiling comes from how those pieces interact; its floor depends on whether the veterans remain available while the new rotation becomes real.
