The Colts' three 2026 preseason games should be read as controlled but imperfect tests of assignments, combinations, and roster utility—not as miniature regular-season forecasts or a scoreboard referendum.

Up: [[Indianapolis Colts 2026]]

## The evaluation problem

Indianapolis has three preseason games before the roster reduction: at New England on Thursday, August 13; home against Atlanta on Saturday, August 22; and home against Detroit on Saturday, August 29. The NFL's calendar requires clubs to reduce to a maximum of 53 players on the Active/Inactive List by 6 p.m. New York time on August 30. The last game therefore ends only about 29 hours before the transaction deadline, making the series both an evaluation window and a compressed decision process.

The scoreboard is real, but it is a poor organizing variable for this work. Coaches can restrict a scheme, protect established starters, rotate combinations, emphasize a particular installation, or leave a player in the game because they need more evidence. An opponent can make the opposite choices. A backup quarterback succeeding against a defense made largely of developmental players is not automatically ready for regular-season first-team work; a reserve tackle losing one rep to an established rusher is not automatically out of the competition. The useful unit of evidence is the **assignment in context**.

This framework connects preseason observations to the unresolved questions in the [[Indianapolis Colts 2026 roster map]], [[Indianapolis Colts quarterback structure 2026]], and [[Indianapolis Colts 2026 roster cut mechanics]]. It does not project the final 53 or prescribe how many snaps a medically managed player should receive. Those are club decisions informed by private medical, practice, and meeting-room evidence that an outside observer does not possess.

## The known calendar

| Checkpoint | Date | Evidence it can add | Important limit |
|---|---|---|---|

| Preseason Week 1 at New England | August 13, 7:30 p.m. ET | First live operation against an unfamiliar opponent | Early personnel plans and scheme menus may be intentionally narrow |

| Preseason Week 3 vs. Detroit | August 29, 1 p.m. ET | Final live roster-edge and contingency evidence | One late performance cannot erase the larger body of work |
| Roster reduction | August 30, before 6 p.m. ET | Official initial 53-player decisions | The initial 53 is not the permanent Week 1 roster |

The two Atlanta practices distinguish the middle week. Repetition against the same opponent can create an unusually useful correction loop: identify a protection, leverage, release, or communication failure; adjust it; then see whether the correction survives another practice and the game. That is stronger process evidence than one highlight. It still does not make Atlanta a regular-season simulation. Teams control access, tempo, contact, personnel, and the portion of the playbook exposed.

The Detroit game presents the opposite problem. Its proximity to the deadline raises the salience of every snap but does not necessarily raise its informational quality. Decision-makers will already possess weeks of practice film, meeting performance, medical information, and two earlier games. A late explosive play can be relevant without becoming dispositive. An analyst should ask what the rep adds to the accumulated file, not whether it supplies a dramatic final verdict.

## A claim ladder

Every observation should be labeled at the strongest level the evidence permits:

1. **Event:** what visibly happened. A player aligned at right tackle, handled the first punt return, communicated a protection change, missed a tackle, or played on two coverage units.
2. **Assignment reading:** the most defensible account of the player's apparent responsibility, with uncertainty when the call is unavailable.
3. **Repeated tendency:** the same role or execution quality appearing across multiple snaps, periods, or games.
4. **Roster inference:** what the repeated tendency may mean for functional value, stated as an inference rather than club fact.
5. **Official outcome:** a transaction, designation, published roster, or actual regular-season assignment.

This ladder prevents common overclaims. “Anthony Gould took the first punt return” is an event. “Gould is the primary punt returner” requires repeated ordering and relevant availability context. “Gould won the job” requires an official or operational outcome. Likewise, a quarterback entering second does not alone identify the regular-season backup: the order may reflect a planned snap distribution, different protection groups, or the need to evaluate another quarterback later in the game.

Negative claims require special restraint. Television does not always show the secondary, line calls, route conversions, or the point where responsibility passed between defenders. When the assignment is unknowable, chart the outcome and the uncertainty. Do not assign blame to the nearest player merely because he appears on screen.

## Quarterbacks: evaluate decisions before totals

The quarterback questions are structurally different. Daniel Jones is the intended starter while returning from a torn Achilles; Anthony Richardson Sr. and Riley Leonard enter the club's stated backup competition; Easton Stick supplies a fourth rostered quarterback. Preseason playing time should therefore be interpreted through health, role, and supporting personnel rather than combined into one passer-rating leaderboard.

For Jones, the first question is participation. If he plays, chart whether Indianapolis uses normal dropback, movement, and under-center actions or a constrained menu. Then chart operational details: getting the offense aligned, cadence, protection identification, time from snap to decision, pocket response, and willingness to finish throws when space compresses. A clean statistical line on screens and quick throws would not establish that every movement or downfield function has returned. Conversely, one pressured incompletion would not establish a rehabilitation failure.

For Richardson and Leonard, chart equivalent situations where possible. Separate first-read throws from full-field progression work; designed runs from scrambles; accurate decisions from fortunate outcomes; and pressure created by the quarterback from pressure allowed immediately by protection. A completion can still be a poor process if the throw is late and exposes a receiver. An incompletion can be good process if the quarterback recognizes a covered concept and safely discards the ball.

Stick's presence matters because four quarterbacks create allocation constraints. A low snap count may reflect limited possessions rather than an evaluation conclusion. Report who played with which line, receivers, backs, and opponent personnel. The most useful comparison is not raw yards but repeated execution of the same functional tasks: third down, backed-up offense, red zone, two-minute work, pressure answers, and ball security.

Useful quarterback fields include drop type, personnel grouping, pressure state, first-open-window decision, throw location, turnover-worthy action, scramble trigger, and down-and-distance result. Avoid pretending that a public chart can reproduce the coaches' grade; its purpose is consistency, not omniscience.

## Offensive line: combinations and communication

The open right-tackle problem makes line evaluation central, but “no sacks allowed” is insufficient. Sacks depend on quarterback timing, coverage, game situation, and whether pressure arrives from another player's assignment. A tackle can lose quickly on a completion; a quarterback can create a sack after sound initial protection.

Track five-man combinations and the quarterback behind them. For each pass rep, identify the protection only when evidence supports it, then record initial set, width and depth, first meaningful contact, whether the blocker preserves the pocket's shape, and whether help was built into the call. For runs, record alignment, first-step relationship, displacement or sealing, second-level timing, and whether penetration changes the back's track.

Communication failures deserve their own category. An untouched rusher may reflect a physical loss, a protection identification error, a late adjustment, or a deliberate unblocked defender in a read concept. The broadcast rarely resolves all four possibilities. Mark it “unresolved assignment” until end-zone film, replay, or credible explanation supports more.

Cross-training is roster evidence. A reserve taking snaps at guard and tackle, or center and guard, may be demonstrating contingency value even if he is not the best player at either single spot. The final comparison should combine execution quality with position coverage, because the game-day roster cannot dress a separate replacement for every offensive-line position.

## Skill positions: function over touches

Touches are opportunity, not a complete grade. The receiver and tight-end rooms must redistribute functions after Michael Pittman Jr.'s departure, while the running-back group needs complementary offensive and special-teams value behind Jonathan Taylor. Preseason can show which functions coaches are testing.

For receivers, chart alignment, release, route depth, separation at the decision point, contested-catch necessity, blocking assignment, motion, and special-teams use. A player with one catch may repeatedly clear space for another route or execute difficult run-blocking assignments. A player with six catches may have been targeted on low-difficulty concepts without demonstrating an outside or vertical role. Drops matter, but so do target quality and whether the receiver reached the expected landmark on time.

For tight ends, “starter” language is especially unhelpful because Tyler Warren, Mo Alie-Cox, Drew Ogletree, Will Mallory, Sean McKeon, and Carson Towt can be tested across attached, slot, backfield, motion, protection, and kicking-game jobs. Record personnel grouping and alignment before judging a depth order. A tight end who can survive several functions can change both the play menu and the number of specialists the offense must carry.

For backs, separate rushing output from the work that creates game-day trust: pass-protection identification, pickup technique, route timing, ball security, kickoff return, coverage, and the ability to execute from more than one backfield alignment. A 25-yard run through a clean lane is useful evidence of burst; it says little about blitz pickup. A reserve back's path to the roster may depend more on avoiding a free rusher and covering a kick than on leading the team in preseason carries.

## Defense: grade the chain, not only the finish

Lou Anarumo's defense and the turnover described in [[Indianapolis Colts defensive identity 2026]] make communication a preseason priority. The visible result often occurs at the end of a chain that begins with the front, travels through linebackers and safeties, and changes with motion or formation. The evaluation must preserve that chain.

For the defensive front, chart alignment, get-off, gap control, edge integrity, rush lane, counter use, and whether pressure is produced individually or by a stunt or simulated pressure. A defender can execute a successful rush by forcing the quarterback toward a teammate without receiving a sack. Against the run, a player can occupy a double-team and preserve a linebacker's path without making the tackle. Production totals should follow mechanism notes, not replace them.

At linebacker, record pre-snap communication when visible, run-fit entry, block response, coverage responsibility, pursuit angle, tackling finish, and special-teams phase. The post-Zaire Franklin room is not only choosing snap-takers; it is distributing calls and checks. A correct fit that spills the ball to help can be successful even when another defender makes the tackle. A tackle five yards downfield can conceal a late read.

In the defensive backfield, avoid cornerback box-score grading. Targets and completions must be paired with leverage, route, coverage family when reasonably identifiable, safety help, communication, and catch-point result. One-on-one losses are informative, but preseason also needs to reveal whether the secondary passes routes, handles bunches and motion, disguises without busting, and tackles in space. Slot, outside, safety, dime, and special-teams work are separate pieces of roster value.

Explosive plays require full-chain review. Ask whether the problem began with pressure containment, a fit, a coverage handoff, leverage, a missed tackle, or several failures. If the answer is unavailable, record the explosive and withhold the diagnosis.

## Special teams: roster evidence in four phases

The [[Indianapolis Colts special teams 2026]] evaluation should connect the Blake Grupe–Spencer Shrader kicking competition, the established Luke Rhodes–Rigoberto Sanchez snap-and-hold operation, return decisions, and the reserve-player contests that fill coverage units.

For placekicks, log distance, hash, environment, snap location, hold, operation quality, trajectory, and result. A make does not prove a clean operation; a miss does not always isolate the kicker. For punts, record field position, gross and net distance, hang and direction when available, return opportunity, coverage arrival, touchback, and pressure. For kick returns under the permanent dynamic-kickoff structure, compare the return with the rules-based alternative field position rather than celebrating raw return yards alone.

The bottom of the roster is shaped by four-phase work: kickoff, kickoff return, punt, and punt return. Chart who appears, in what role, and whether the player repeats sound alignment, release, block fit, lane discipline, ball security, and tackling. One splash tackle is weaker evidence than correct work across several phases. A receiver or defensive back who supplies a safe second punt catcher or a linebacker who covers multiple units may have functional value invisible in offensive or defensive totals.

Penalties belong to the responsible phase and mechanism. “Special-teams penalty” is too coarse. Identify whether the failure was alignment, an illegal block, holding, interference with the catch opportunity, or a late action, while acknowledging when the responsible assignment is unclear.

## A game-by-game workflow

Before each game, freeze the official roster and availability baseline. List the questions being tested without predicting answers: quarterback participation and order; offensive-line combinations; first-unit returners; defensive communication; and multi-phase reserve use. This avoids rewriting the purpose of the evaluation around whatever becomes dramatic on television.

During the game, log possessions and units rather than highlights. Note personnel, opponent level where identifiable, field situation, and the relevant mechanism. Preserve an “unknown” category. A chart that admits missing information is more reliable than a complete chart filled with guessed assignments.

Within 24 hours, separate three outputs:

- **Verified events:** snap counts, alignments, results, penalties, transactions, and explicit club statements.
- **Supported inferences:** repeated role allocation or execution patterns, with the evidence named.
- **Unresolved questions:** plays without sufficient assignment context, medical questions, and roster consequences not yet made official.

After the Atlanta joint-practice sequence, specifically test correction. Which problems repeated? Which appeared to change after coaching? Did a player execute the same responsibility in practice reports and the game? After Detroit, compare the final evidence with the whole month rather than allowing recency to dominate.

## What preseason can and cannot establish

Preseason can establish official participation, observable assignments, combinations, live execution, corrections, versatility, and repeated tendencies. It can strengthen an inference that a player is being prepared for a particular job. It can expose a mechanism that deserves more testing.

It cannot fully establish regular-season health, durability, opponent-adjusted quality, a complete scheme, or a permanent depth order. It cannot reveal every coach's assignment or private grade. It cannot make a small sample large by calling it “game action.” It cannot turn the initial 53 into a permanent roster, because waivers, claims, reserve designations, and practice-squad construction continue after the deadline.

The strongest possible preseason conclusion is therefore usually conditional: a player repeatedly executed a named job against a described level of competition and added a particular kind of roster utility. That claim is narrower than “he won camp,” but it is also more durable. Once Indianapolis makes official moves, the framework should record the outcome without retroactively treating every earlier rep as obvious evidence of it.
