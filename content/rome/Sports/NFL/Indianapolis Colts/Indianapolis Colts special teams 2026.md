## Direct summary

Indianapolis has unusual continuity in the operation beneath its placekicker. Long snapper Luke Rhodes and punter/holder Rigoberto Sanchez have worked together since 2017, and both appear on the club's July 2026 roster. Sanchez also remains the punter after a 2025 season in which he averaged 48.8 gross yards and 44.7 net yards on 44 punts, with 22 downed inside the 20 and no blocks. That is the most stable part of the unit.

The unresolved specialist question is kicker. Spencer Shrader made 13 of 14 field goals in 2025 before ACL and MCL injuries ended his season, while Blake Grupe made all 11 of his Colts field-goal attempts after arriving late in the year. Indianapolis re-signed Grupe in March 2026 while retaining Shrader. The club itself described the arrangement as a competition and reported that Shrader was expected to be fully healthy for 2026. Neither roster presence nor a perfect small sample makes the outcome predetermined.

Returns are more distributed. Anthony Gould led the 2025 Colts in both kickoff-return attempts and punt-return attempts; Ashton Dulin produced the team's best kickoff-return average among players with more than a handful of attempts; Josh Downs shared punt-return work; and injuries forced Coleman Owen and Ameer Abdullah into late-season roles. The current roster includes Gould, Dulin, Downs, and Owen, but that does not establish the eventual game-day assignment. Return security, blocking fit, offensive value, injury exposure, and coverage ability all affect the choice.

The correct 2026 test is therefore not “Who won a drill?” It is whether the entire unit repeatedly creates field position without avoidable volatility. For kicks, that means operation time, ball placement, distance, weather translation, and pressure performance. For punts, it means net outcome and opponent return suppression rather than gross average alone. For returns and coverage, it means decision quality, ball security, lane integrity, penalty avoidance, and whether the same player earns one of the scarce active-roster spots through another role.

## Why it matters

Special teams turn possession changes into hidden yards. A five-yard difference in average starting position does not look like a touchdown, but repeated across a game it changes which plays an offense can call, how often a drive reaches scoring range, and how aggressively a defense can play. A made field goal converts a stalled drive into points; a missed one combines lost points with field position. A well-covered punt can make an opponent traverse nearly the entire field. A return penalty can erase a good block and place an offense behind schedule before its first snap.

The unit also sits at the center of roster construction. Kicker, punter, and long snapper ordinarily occupy three dedicated spots. Most of the other ten players on a coverage or return unit must justify themselves on offense or defense as well. A reserve linebacker, safety, tight end, running back, or receiver who can cover kicks, block in space, and handle an emergency role may beat out a marginally better scrimmage player who cannot help on fourth down. That makes special teams one of the clearest places where the [[Indianapolis Colts 2026 roster map]] and the Sunday active list interact.

## The stable operation: Rhodes and Sanchez

Rhodes and Sanchez form the mechanical center of the field-goal and punt units. Rhodes won the full-time snapping job in 2017. Sanchez has been Indianapolis's punter since that season and is also the holder on placekicks. The sequence is interdependent: Rhodes must deliver a catchable ball with consistent location and rotation; Sanchez must catch, spot, orient, and stabilize it; the kicker must approach and strike on schedule. A result officially recorded as a kicker make or miss is therefore produced by a three-player operation plus protection.

Continuity reduces one class of uncertainty. Grupe told the club after re-signing that he had quickly found a rhythm with Rhodes and Sanchez. Shrader already worked with the same pair in 2025. That does not eliminate evaluation—snap location, hold quality, protection leakage, and timing still need to be charted—but it means the Colts are not simultaneously installing a new snapper or holder while choosing a kicker.

Sanchez's 2025 line gives a useful performance baseline: 44 punts, a 48.8-yard gross average, a 44.7-yard net average, 22 inside the 20, 19 returned for 120 yards, and none blocked. Gross average measures only punt distance. Net average incorporates return yardage and touchbacks, so it better approximates the field position actually preserved. Even net average is incomplete: field location constrains how far a punter can kick, a directional punt may trade distance for a smaller return lane, and hang time influences whether coverage can arrive. A 42-yard punt from midfield downed at the eight can be more valuable than a 55-yard punt from deep in one's territory that is returned 20 yards.

For 2026, Sanchez should be evaluated through distributions rather than a single average: punts from backed-up, open-field, and plus-territory situations; opponent return rate; touchbacks; punts inside the 10 and 20; directional accuracy; operation time; and failures such as blocks or shanks. **Inference:** because Rhodes and Sanchez are established and listed without a same-position challenger on the current roster, their jobs look substantially more secure than the kicker and return jobs. That is a roster inference, not an official depth-chart declaration.

## The kicking competition: Shrader and Grupe

Shrader's 2025 Colts results were strong before injury. The club's statistical table credits him with 13 makes in 14 attempts: perfect from 20–49 yards and 1-of-2 from 50–59. Indianapolis also noted that he earned AFC Special Teams Player of the Month for September. The injury is essential context, not a footnote: the Colts placed him on injured reserve after ACL and MCL injuries, and the club later reported an expectation that he would return fully healthy in 2026.

Grupe joined Indianapolis in Week 14 and made 11 of 11 field goals for the Colts, including 3-of-3 from 50–59 and 1-of-1 from 60-plus. The team re-signed him in March. Its own feature on the move explicitly presented the challenge as competing for the kicking role and explained that Grupe had initially been brought in for PATs and shorter field goals while another kicker handled longer attempts. The perfect field-goal line is real, but its sample is only 11 attempts and emerged within a role that changed over the closing weeks.

The fair comparison needs more than raw make percentage. Coaches can chart:

- **Operation consistency:** whether each kick gets away on time from a repeatable snap and hold.
- **Distance and placement:** not only maximum range, but the dispersion of misses and the height and trajectory needed to clear the line.
- **Extra points:** a separate, frequent scoring task that should not disappear inside field-goal summaries.
- **Kickoffs:** location, hang time, ability to execute called ball placement, and coverage consequences under the dynamic-kickoff structure.
- **Environment:** performance outside, in wind, on different surfaces, and after a poor rep. Lucas Oil Stadium conditions alone cannot answer road-game reliability.
- **Health and workload:** whether Shrader's mechanics and power hold under repeated work after knee rehabilitation.
- **Pressure evidence:** late-game and situational kicks, while recognizing that tiny “clutch” samples are noisy.

A camp chart saying one player went 8-for-8 and the other 7-for-8 is weak evidence without distances, hash marks, conditions, protection, and the quality of the miss. Preseason game attempts are more representative because the rush is live and game operations apply, but even three games rarely produce enough attempts across every distance band. Prior regular-season work deserves weight, yet it should be adjusted for recency, role, environment, and injury.

**Inference:** Shrader's pre-injury performance and Grupe's late-season perfection make this a credible competition rather than a ceremonial second leg. The decisive evidence may be operational and kickoff-related even if public practice totals look nearly identical. Indianapolis could also retain only one kicker on the initial 53 because every extra specialist displaces a coverage or scrimmage player; that is normal roster logic, not a reported decision.

These are official Colts totals, not an adjusted ranking. They have different sample sizes, blocking contexts, opponents, and return opportunities. Dulin's 32.2-yard average includes 16 returns and a long of 55; Gould's 25.9 includes 25 returns and a long of 39. Gould also fielded 13 punts for 10.1 yards per return, while Downs averaged 8.5 on eight returns. No Colts player scored a return touchdown in these listed samples.

Availability reshaped the late-season allocation. The Colts described Dulin as a crucial special-teams player when designating him to return to practice in December and reported that Owen and Abdullah had handled return work while Dulin and Gould were injured. That is important because return depth is not ornamental. A team needs a second safe catcher and a contingency plan for both kicks and punts; the primary returner may be injured, elevated into a larger offensive role, or deemed too valuable to expose on every return.

Punt return and kick return are not interchangeable jobs. A punt returner must track a descending ball while reading coverage, decide whether to return, fair-catch, or let it bounce, and protect possession in traffic. A kickoff returner operates under a different alignment, sees blocks develop in a compressed space, and must hit a crease with timing. Gould has recent evidence at both jobs. Downs has punt-handling experience but also a meaningful offensive role. Dulin has a longer record as a core special-teamer and produced explosive 2025 kickoff returns. Owen offers recent contingency experience. Their roster listing establishes candidacy, not order.

## How the dynamic kickoff changes evaluation

The NFL made its revised kickoff permanent in the 2025 rulebook. Ten kicking-team coverage players line up near the receiving team's setup zone, and most players cannot move until the ball lands or is touched. A kickoff that reaches the end zone on the fly for a touchback places the ball at the 35; other touchback circumstances can produce different spots under Rule 6. The league's structure is designed to generate more returns while reducing the long running starts associated with the old play.

For Indianapolis, this increases the value of coordinated, short-space football. Return blockers must identify leverage and sustain legal blocks without losing contact as the returner reaches the setup zone. The returner needs controlled acceleration: too much lateral movement can let compressed coverage close; a premature cut can abandon the designed crease. Coverage players need block avoidance, tackling balance, and lane discipline more than a straight 40-yard sprint from the old alignment.

Kickoff evaluation therefore should record expected field position, not just return average. A return to the 31 may be a poor decision when a touchback would have produced the 35. A return to the 32 from a ball that otherwise would have forced a worse spot may be positive. Analysts must tag where the kick landed, its flight and placement, the returner's decision, penalties, and the unblocked point of first contact. Without those variables, an average can blame the returner for a lost block or credit him for a rules-driven starting position.

On coverage, tackle counts alone are similarly incomplete. The first coverage player can force a returner sideways and enable a teammate's tackle without receiving the statistic. A gunner can draw two blockers. A player can stay in his lane and prevent a cutback that never occurs. Useful review asks whether the unit compressed space, maintained leverage, avoided illegal blocks and late hits, and finished tackles without allowing hidden yards after contact.

## Coverage and the bottom of the roster

Dulin is the clearest established bridge between the return, coverage, and offensive rosters. When the Colts re-signed him in 2025, the club reported that he had played at least half of its special-teams snaps in each of his five played seasons, excluding the 2023 season he missed with a torn ACL. That workload history is stronger evidence of a core role than one preseason tackle. It still does not guarantee 2026 health, form, or selection.

The rest of the coverage roster will emerge from linebackers, safeties, cornerbacks, tight ends, running backs, and receivers fighting for reserve roles. The mechanism matters: offensive and defensive coaches may rank two players closely, while special-teams coordinator Brian Mason can distinguish them through four-phase utility. “Four phase” commonly refers to kickoff, kickoff return, punt, and punt return. A reserve capable of playing on all four units gives the game-day roster resilience when an injury occurs elsewhere.

That creates evidence thresholds for August decisions. One open-field preseason tackle is noteworthy but insufficient. Repeated correct alignments, releases against jammers, block fits, finish angles, and penalty-free snaps across multiple games carry more weight. So do emergency competencies: a backup holder, a second punt catcher, or a player able to replace an injured front-line blocker. Practice reports can identify who is receiving opportunities, but only coaches' internal grading can reliably assign every player's responsibility on a play.

**Inference:** the Colts' final receiver, running back, linebacker, and defensive-back decisions will be partly special-teams decisions. Gould, Dulin, Downs, and Owen are visible return candidates, but coverage value from less obvious roster competitors may decide who is active even if that player never touches a return.

## A disciplined 2026 evidence ladder

The unit is unusually vulnerable to overreaction because each conspicuous event—a missed kick, muff, long return, or blocked punt—is memorable. A better evidence ladder is:

1. **Official status:** transactions, injury designations, the roster, and eventually the depth chart establish who is available, not who has secretly won.
2. **Role allocation:** repeated first-unit snaps, holding assignments, kickoff duties, and dual return work show what coaches are testing.
3. **Mechanism-level charting:** operation time, snap/hold location, hang time, placement, decision quality, lane integrity, ball security, and penalties explain outcomes.
4. **Representative game evidence:** preseason and regular-season reps against live rush and coverage test responsibilities practice cannot fully reproduce.
5. **Adequate samples:** results across distance bands, field locations, opponents, and weather deserve more weight than a single hot day.
6. **Roster interaction:** a returner or coverage player must be compared on total game-day value, not return average alone.

Claims should match the rung reached. “Grupe took the first kicks Tuesday” is an observation. “Grupe leads the competition” is an inference requiring repeated ordering and performance context. “Grupe won the job” requires an official move or an unambiguous regular-season assignment. The same discipline applies to a reported first-team returner.

## What would count as success

For the specialists, success is a clean snap-hold-kick operation, field-goal range that coaches can trust, and punts that minimize opponent starting position. For return units, it is secure possession and field position above the rules-based alternative, with explosives as upside rather than the only measure. For coverage, it is leverage, legal tackling, and low penalty cost. For roster construction, it is enough multi-phase depth that one injury does not force a starting offensive or defensive player into an unfamiliar emergency job.

The strongest current conclusion is narrow. Indianapolis has a veteran snapper-holder-punter foundation and multiple credible legs and returners around it. The competition is not resolved merely because the roster contains familiar names or because 2025 percentages look excellent. The 2026 unit will become trustworthy only when those names repeatedly execute the mechanisms that produce points and field position.
