## What this ledger does

This is an evidence ledger, not a medical forecast. It records what the Colts or NFL have actually published, when they published it, and what remains unknown at the ledger's **July 10, 2026 research cutoff**. The source time matters: a June rehabilitation update can be the newest available evidence without proving a player's condition on July 10. Likewise, appearing in the club's main roster group does not prove that a player is medically unrestricted, practicing fully, or certain to make the regular-season roster.

Three layers are kept separate:

1. **Official administrative state** is a transaction or list designation such as reserve/retired, physically unable to perform, non-football injury/illness, injured reserve, or exempt. It changes roster treatment, but the label alone should not be expanded into an undisclosed diagnosis.
2. **Club-described rehabilitation state** is a dated report about surgery, recovery, or practice work. It may be highly relevant, but it is not automatically a reserve-list transaction or a game-status designation.
3. **Game-week reporting state** consists of the league's practice-participation and game-status reports. No 2026 Colts game week has occurred, so this layer does not yet exist for the season.

That distinction prevents two common errors. First, “not on the injury report” cannot mean healthy when no current injury report exists. Second, a coach's expectation that a player will be ready for camp or Week 1 is a forecast, not confirmation of unrestricted participation on the later date.

## Rehabilitation watchlist before camp

### Daniel Jones — Achilles rehabilitation

The firm historical fact is that Jones tore his right Achilles against Jacksonville on December 7, 2025; Shane Steichen confirmed the season-ending tear the next day. The newest detailed club update reviewed here was published June 10, 2026. It said Jones had participated in seven-on-seven work during the final two weeks of the offseason program. Steichen said the expectation was for Jones to begin full-team portions when training camp opened, while also stressing that the Colts would continue to increase his workload deliberately.

Jones said in March that he expected to be 100 percent for Week 1 and anticipated practicing during camp. By June, the evidence had advanced from a stated goal to some on-field seven-on-seven participation. It had **not** advanced to a documented full 11-on-11 camp practice, because camp had not begun. The proper July 10 entry is therefore: rehabilitation in progress; limited offseason team-work evidence; club expectation of full-team camp work; no official camp list or Week 1 status yet.

This wording matters. “On track” describes progress against the club's plan at the source time. It does not prove post-rehabilitation performance, durability, or an exact workload. Jones's own Week 1 confidence is relevant first-person evidence of his target, not an independent medical clearance. The first decisive checkpoints will be whether Indianapolis assigns a camp list designation, whether he actually participates in full-team work, whether that work is consecutive or managed, and whether preseason or regular-season practice reports later disclose limitations.

### Alec Pierce — ankle procedure

The Colts reported on June 10 that Pierce had undergone a late-March ankle procedure after a post-season platelet-rich plasma injection did not resolve an issue that had affected him during 2025. Pierce did not participate in OTA or minicamp practices. Steichen said he expected Pierce back “at some point in training camp,” and the club article said the procedure could jeopardize his availability for the beginning of camp. General manager Chris Ballard had said in April that the Colts did not anticipate long-term implications.

The evidence supports a narrower state than either “out” or “ready”: post-procedure rehabilitation; absent from the offseason practices described by the club; return expected sometime during camp, with no exact date. Pierce explicitly declined to impose a precise return date, saying recovery would depend on how his body felt. That uncertainty should remain visible. An eventual camp absence would be consistent with the June report but would not, by itself, identify a new setback. Conversely, being present on the sideline or in meetings would not establish practice participation.

Pierce becomes a high-value ledger case at camp because the source already supplies a causal context for managed work. Track whether the club makes an administrative move, whether practice reporting describes individual, position, or team periods, and whether “returned” means partial work or unrestricted work. Do not convert a journalist's observation into a formal PUP/NFI status.

### Justin Walley — return from 2025 ACL tear

The Colts' June 1 feature said Walley tore his ACL during an August 2025 joint practice with Baltimore and framed his 2026 work as a return from that injury. The article provides relevant longitudinal context for the cornerback competition, but this ledger's research cutoff still lacks a camp-era administrative designation or a formal 2026 game-week report. Walley's correct state is therefore rehabilitation/return context documented by the club, with camp participation to be verified rather than presumed.

For future updates, the useful evidence is not merely whether Walley appears at practice. Record the level of work the club or direct observation can actually support, any rest or rehabilitation pattern across consecutive sessions, and any official list move. Avoid declaring that a prior ACL injury has “fully healed” based solely on participation; football readiness, medical healing, and performance recovery are related but different claims.

## Players who should not be silently upgraded or downgraded

Other Colts notes discuss availability risk, but this ledger does not promote every past injury into a current case. A historical injury can explain why a player deserves observation without establishing a July 2026 limitation. The same rule applies to vague spring absences: unless the club identifies a reportable condition, transaction, or rehabilitation context, absence should remain an observation with its date and setting.

Anthony Richardson Sr.'s roster and competitive situation should not be mislabeled as medical availability merely because earlier seasons included injuries. Jaylon Carlies's injury-interrupted 2025 is relevant background for the linebacker room, but the absence of a current official state prevents a present-tense designation here. The ledger should add either player only when new, dated evidence changes the 2026 availability picture.

This restraint is especially important before camp. Offseason practices are governed and described differently from regular-season game weeks, and club reporters may use phrases such as “worked on the side,” “did not participate,” or “was not seen.” Those observations can be recorded verbatim with a date. They should not be translated into diagnoses, expected missed games, or official practice-report categories that the club has not issued.

## The reporting calendar

The Colts announced that training camp begins Wednesday, July 29 at Grand Park, with 13 public practices scheduled through August 20. The first camp day is the next major administrative checkpoint: clubs commonly make list moves around reporting, and the roster page may gain categories that were absent in early July. The public schedule establishes when observation becomes possible; it does not guarantee that every player's exact medical status will be disclosed.

Once the regular season begins, the ledger should distinguish the **Practice Report** from the **Game Status Report**. The NFL's published Personnel (Injury) Report Policy describes practice participation as Did Not Participate, Limited Participation, or Full Participation. Full participation can still accompany a reportable injury; “full” describes workload, not the absence of an injury. The game-status categories in the policy are Out, Doubtful, and Questionable. A player can appear on practice reports during the week without receiving a game-status designation if the club is certain he will play.

The official policy page currently available through NFL Football Operations is the 2017 policy, so it is authoritative for the concepts it states but **not proof that every deadline or procedural detail is unchanged in 2026**. This ledger will use the current-season Colts reports themselves for weekly status and preserve their exact labels. It will not import the old “Probable” category from older policies or treat a historic PDF as a substitute for a 2026 rule publication.

Inactive status is another separate layer. A player can be inactive on game day for tactical or roster reasons without being injured, and an injured player on a reserve list may not appear in the ordinary weekly report in the same way as a player on the active roster. Future entries should therefore record the list, weekly practice status, game status, and game-day active/inactive outcome in separate fields.

## Update protocol

At every material checkpoint, use this order:

1. Check the Colts transaction log for a dated action.
2. Check the Colts roster page for current groupings and preserve any disagreement with the log.
3. Check the Colts injury-report page for a report bearing an explicit week and publication date.
4. Use Colts news, press conferences, or direct practice observation only to add dated context, not to overwrite an official designation.
5. State the research cutoff separately from the source's publication or effective date.

Each player entry should answer four questions: What did the official source say? When was that state effective or reported? What consequence follows without inference? What remains unknown? If a report changes during the week, retain the sequence rather than overwriting it—for example, limited Wednesday, full Thursday, no game designation Friday. A single final label discards the trajectory that often matters most.

Probability language does not belong in the ledger unless it comes from a transparent model with a defined reference class. “Expected back during camp” is a sourced club forecast. “Likely to play Week 1” is an analyst forecast and should live elsewhere unless its method is explicit. No current evidence supports assigning exact return probabilities to Jones, Pierce, or Walley.
