## What this map can and cannot establish

This is a transaction-and-membership map, not a projected 53-player roster or an unofficial depth chart. As of July 9, the Colts' roster page separates the club into an **Active** section and a **Reserve/Retired** section. In the offseason, however, the site's “Active” label should be read as the club's current main roster grouping, not as a promise that every listed player will be on the regular-season active roster. Indianapolis will reduce its expanded roster before the regular season, and the club has not yet supplied game-week availability reports for a season that has not begun.

The firmest facts are therefore narrow ones: a player appears in the main roster grouping; Reuben Lowery III appears separately on reserve/retired; or the transaction log records a signing, waiver, release, trade, designation, or reserve move on a particular date. Those facts do not by themselves establish first-team status, a final-roster place, a practice-squad place, or even a player's precise role within a flexible position group. Practice combinations after camp opens on July 29 will add evidence, but isolated repetitions will still be observations rather than a settled hierarchy.

That distinction matters because offseason rosters are designed to contain more possibilities than the team can retain. A useful roster map should preserve those possibilities without converting them into predictions.

Four quarterbacks give Indianapolis enough bodies for camp and create several distinct decisions: starter, game-day backup, developmental retention, and the number of quarterbacks ultimately carried. Those decisions should not be collapsed into one “competition.” Jones's re-signing and Stick's addition are verified roster-construction choices; neither tells us how coaches will distribute meaningful preseason or regular-season work. ### Running backs

Jonathan Taylor, DJ Giddens, Ulysses Bentley IV, Seth McGowan, Anderson Castle, and Lincoln Pare appear in the main roster grouping. Jordon Vaughn also appears there despite the May 18 release entry, so he remains a source discrepancy rather than a confidently counted participant.

This group illustrates the difference between a stable offensive center and an unsettled roster edge. Taylor's presence identifies an established returning runner, but membership alone says little about third-down, pass-protection, return, or special-teams assignments behind him. Giddens and Bentley bring prior NFL membership; McGowan, Castle, Pare, and the disputed Vaughn listing are rookies. The roster question is therefore not simply “who is RB2?” It includes which backs can cover complementary offensive and special-teams jobs, and whether those jobs justify carrying three or four backs on the eventual 53.

### Wide receivers

Alec Pierce, Josh Downs, Nick Westbrook-Ikhine, Ashton Dulin, Laquon Treadwell, Anthony Gould, Coleman Owen, Eli Pancol, Deion Burks, Sahmir Hagans, E.J. Horton, and Raylen Sharpe form the displayed receiver group.

The construction is notable for both continuity and turnover. Indianapolis re-signed Pierce on March 11 and Treadwell on March 13, signed Westbrook-Ikhine on March 18, and later drafted Burks. Michael Pittman Jr. is no longer part of the room after the March 13 trade to Pittsburgh, in which Indianapolis sent Pittman and a seventh-round selection for a sixth-round selection. That transaction changes the receiver room more materially than an offseason jersey-number announcement: it removes a veteran who had occupied a large role and creates work that must be redistributed.

The roster page cannot tell us whether that redistribution flows primarily to Pierce and Downs, to Westbrook-Ikhine, to heavier tight-end personnel, or to a younger receiver. Nor can a list settle the special-teams contest among reserve candidates. Camp should be used to track alignment, personnel grouping, route responsibility, blocking, and special-teams participation—not to label a winner after a single practice.

### Tight ends

Tyler Warren, Mo Alie-Cox, Drew Ogletree, Will Mallory, Sean McKeon, and Carson Towt are listed at tight end. Warren returns for his second season; Alie-Cox and Ogletree were re-signed in March; Towt was signed as an undrafted free agent after a college basketball career; Mallory and McKeon return within the broader group.

Six tight ends create room for role specialization. A nominal tight end can be asked to align attached to the formation, in the slot, in the backfield, or on special teams, and proficiency in one assignment does not imply proficiency in all of them. The eventual count will also interact with wide-receiver and offensive-line decisions: an offense that values multiple-tight-end personnel may allocate the final roster differently from one that treats the position primarily as a receiving rotation. The roster map should therefore preserve the six names while leaving usage to camp and preseason evidence.

## Defense

### Defensive front and edge

The listed interior defenders are DeForest Buckner, Grover Stewart, Adetomiwa Adebawore, Colby Wooden, Derrick Nnadi, Jerry Tillery, Tim Smith, and rookie Cameron Ball. The listed edge or defensive-end group is Laiatu Latu, Jaylahn Tuimoloau, Arden Key, Micheal Clemons, Durell Nchami, Caden Curry, George Gumbs Jr., and Mitchell Melton.

This is a deliberately broad front, with established interior anchors, veteran additions, returning young rushers, and rookies competing for narrower jobs. Wooden arrived in the March 11 trade that sent linebacker Zaire Franklin to Green Bay. Nnadi and Tillery were signed March 13 and March 17, respectively; Clemons and Key were signed March 12. The club then added Curry and Gumbs in the draft and Ball and Melton as undrafted free agents.

Those moves establish turnover, not a finished rotation. A defensive lineman's listed position does not specify early-down alignment, sub-package role, or whether he must contribute on special teams to dress on game day. The eventual front also has to be evaluated as a system: interior run defense, early-down edge setting, pass-rush combinations, and the ability to generate pressure without exposing the secondary are connected. Counting names is only the first step.

### Linebackers

CJ Allen, Austin Ajiake, Bryce Boettcher, Jaylon Carlies, Tahj Chambers, Akeem Davis-Gaither, Devin Veresuk, and West Weeks are listed at linebacker.

Franklin's trade and the departures recorded during free agency mean the returning hierarchy should not be assumed. Davis-Gaither was signed March 18, Allen was selected in the second round and signed May 7, Boettcher was drafted and signed June 2, and Chambers and Weeks joined as undrafted free agents. Carlies, Ajiake, and Veresuk supply returning organizational continuity at different experience levels.

Linebacker evaluation must connect defensive snaps to special teams. An expanded roster can carry several developmental linebackers through camp, but the regular-season math rewards players who cover kicks or punts while also providing defensive depth. Coverage-package usage is another separate question: a player who fits base defense may not take the same snaps in passing situations. Until camp and preseason show those assignments, the honest map is a membership list with a substantial amount of role uncertainty.

### Cornerbacks

Sauce Gardner, Charvarius Ward, Cam Taylor-Britt, Mekhi Blackmon, Jaylon Jones, Justin Walley, Cameron Mitchell, Rob Carter Jr., Johnathan Edwards, and Jai'Onte' McMillan form the displayed cornerback group.

The March signing of Taylor-Britt and June signing of McMillan added to a room already containing several outside-corner candidates and developmental defensive backs. The May 7 release of Kenny Moore II removed a long-standing Colts defensive back, making slot and package assignments especially important to observe rather than assume. “Cornerback” on the roster page does not distinguish outside, slot, matchup, dime, or core special-teams work. A player may cover more than one of those jobs, but that versatility has to be demonstrated in the Colts' current defense.

Because defensive-back practices generate highly visible one-on-one results, this room is particularly vulnerable to camp-winner narratives. One rep can reveal technique or a mistake; it cannot establish an NFL role. Better evidence will combine team periods, full preseason possessions, opponent and coverage context, tackling, communication, penalties, and special-teams use.

### Safeties

Cam Bynum, A.J. Haulcy, Austin Brown, Daniel Scott, Ben Nikkel, Jonathan Owens, Juanyeh Thomas, Trey Washington, and Hunter Wohler are listed in the main roster group. Lowery is separately listed on reserve/retired.

This is another remade group. Owens and Thomas were signed in March; Haulcy was drafted and signed May 7; Brown joined as an undrafted free agent. The club signed Nasir Adderley in April and then released him May 7, while the free-agency record identifies Nick Cross and Rodney Thomas II as departures. The resulting room contains experience but should not be converted into a two-name starting projection from transactions alone.

Safety responsibilities can include deep coverage, box fit, disguise, tight-end matching, pressure, and special teams. Lou Anarumo's defensive structure may ask different players to carry different combinations of those jobs. The evidence to collect is therefore alignment and responsibility over time, not merely who takes the first repetition of a public practice.

## What the offseason transactions reveal

The roster was not built by simply retaining the 2025 team and adding a draft class. Indianapolis used several different mechanisms:

- **Retention:** Jones, Pierce, Grupe, Alie-Cox, Ogletree, Treadwell, Tenuta, and Cameron Mitchell were among those re-signed.
- **Veteran acquisition:** Davis-Gaither, Westbrook-Ikhine, Taylor-Britt, Owens, Tillery, Thomas, Nnadi, Clemons, Key, and Stick arrived as free agents.
- **Trades:** Wooden arrived for Franklin; Pittman and a seventh-round pick went to Pittsburgh for a sixth-round pick.
- **Draft and undrafted intake:** eight draft choices and a large undrafted class entered the roster, with Farmer and Boettcher completing the draft-class signings June 2.
- **Administrative designations:** Matos was designated exempt/international, and Lowery was placed on reserve/retired.
- **Edge churn:** several reserve/future players were waived or released before camp, and the Vaughn transaction/display disagreement remains unresolved.

The important consequence is not a generic claim that competition increased. Turnover is concentrated in specific places: receiver after the Pittman trade, offensive tackle after Smith's departure, linebacker after the Franklin trade, safety after veteran departures, and the defensive front after multiple additions. Those are the units where the club must convert an offseason inventory into coherent assignments. A roster can be individually talented yet operationally unstable if communication, complementary skills, or injury cover do not fit together.

## Camp questions that can be answered with evidence

1. **Quarterback structure:** Who receives sustained work in which contexts, and how does the offense change with each quarterback? Rep order alone is insufficient.
2. **Right tackle and interior flexibility:** Which combinations persist across padded practices and preseason, and which reserves receive genuine cross-training?
3. **Receiver redistribution:** How are Pittman's former alignment and target responsibilities redistributed across receivers and tight ends?
4. **Linebacker packages:** Which players handle base, passing-down, communication, and special-teams jobs after the room's turnover?
5. **Secondary roles:** Who aligns outside, inside, deep, and in the box, and which players can change jobs without forcing a substitution?
6. **Defensive-line rotation:** Which interior and edge combinations appear by down, distance, and personnel rather than in an undifferentiated depth order?
7. **Roster mechanics:** Does the club add new reserve designations when camp reporting begins, and how is Matos treated under the international-player exemption?
8. **Source reconciliation:** Does the club remove Vaughn from the displayed roster or post a transaction restoring him?

## Why it matters

A precise preseason roster map is a guardrail against false certainty. It prevents released players, reserve players, and current members from being mixed in later analysis. More importantly, it makes the eventual decisions legible. When the Colts cut to 53, the meaningful question will not be whether a surprising player “won camp” in the abstract. It will be which offensive, defensive, and special-teams jobs the team chose to cover; which redundancies it kept; which developmental bets it accepted; and where it tolerated thin depth.

The map also preserves the difference between administrative status and football role. Transactions can prove that a player joined or left. The roster page can show the club's current grouping. Neither can prove the starting five, the nickel package, the third-down back, or a game-day uniform. Those conclusions require later evidence and should be dated when they arrive.
