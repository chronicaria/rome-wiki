Indianapolis has enough receiving talent to build a varied passing game in 2026, but Michael Pittman Jr.'s departure leaves several distinct jobs—not one generic target vacancy—to redistribute among Alec Pierce, Josh Downs, Tyler Warren, Nick Westbrook-Ikhine, and a crowded group of specialists.

## The structure, not the depth chart

As of July 10, the Colts have not played a 2026 training-camp practice, published a regular-season depth chart, or shown how their passing-game responsibilities will be divided. What can be established is the personnel inventory, the previous season's production, the offseason transactions, and several intentions stated by coaches. Those facts point to a receiving structure with three established centers: Pierce as an exceptional vertical producer, Downs as a separation-oriented receiver whose work has primarily come from the slot, and Warren as a formation-flexible tight end who immediately became a major target and personnel-grouping tool.

That is a promising starting point, not a complete answer. Indianapolis traded Pittman and a seventh-round pick to Pittsburgh for a sixth-round pick on March 13. Pittman had caught 80 passes for 784 yards and seven touchdowns in 2025, drawing 111 targets for the second consecutive season. His exit removes outside snaps, but it also removes intermediate and possession routes, blocking work, contested situations, red-zone opportunities, and the accumulated knowledge of a receiver who played six seasons in the system's weekly preparations.

No single current player must imitate all of that. A better construction would distribute functions according to demonstrated strengths while preventing the offense from becoming predictable. Pierce can threaten space that few receivers reach. Downs can stress leverage underneath and between defenders. Warren can change the apparent formation without forcing a substitution. Westbrook-Ikhine and Ashton Dulin can compete for outside and complementary work. The reserve receivers can earn narrower offensive or special-teams jobs. The important unanswered question is whether those parts can cover the whole passing-game menu when defenses deliberately take away the most obvious strength.

This article therefore is not a projected depth chart. It is a map of the jobs Indianapolis must assign and the evidence that should decide them.

## Three established pieces with different effects

### Alec Pierce: vertical gravity and difficult catches

Pierce's 2025 production was unusually concentrated in explosive gains. He caught 47 passes for 1,003 yards and six touchdowns, averaging 21.3 yards per reception. The Colts report that he led the NFL in yards per catch in both 2024 and 2025, becoming the first player since Flipper Anderson in 1989–90 to average at least 20 yards on 35 or more receptions in consecutive seasons. Those results establish far more than a fast workout: Pierce repeatedly converted NFL routes and throws into downfield production.

His value begins before the target. A receiver capable of winning deep can force a corner to protect vertical space and can affect safety depth or rotation. That can open intermediate windows, create room underneath, and punish a defense that crowds the run game. This is an analytical mechanism, not proof that every opponent will treat Pierce identically. Coverage structure, quarterback, protection, formation, and game situation determine whether the threat actually changes a specific snap.

Indianapolis re-signed Pierce in March, making his continued role an organizational choice rather than an assumption based on an expiring contract. Yet his immediate 2026 availability deserves a separate line in the analysis. Pierce disclosed in June that he underwent late-March ankle surgery after a post-season platelet-rich plasma injection did not solve an issue he played through in 2025. The club article said the procedure could jeopardize the beginning of training camp. That is not evidence that he will miss the regular season, nor should a self-reported recovery timeline be converted into an official designation. It does mean early camp absences or limitations should be read through the medical context rather than mistaken for a depth-chart demotion.

The next question for Pierce is not simply whether his target total rises after Pittman's 111 targets leave. More volume can reduce efficiency if it includes routes and situations poorly matched to a player's best skills. Indianapolis needs to preserve the vertical threat while establishing answers when opponents play with depth, disrupt releases, or force the quarterback to deliver quickly. Pierce's late-2025 tough catches suggest a broader capacity, but one season's aggregate does not identify which intermediate or red-zone responsibilities he should inherit. Route location, alignment, defensive leverage, and conversion by situation will be more informative than a raw target count.

### Josh Downs: more opportunity without erasing specialization

Downs caught 58 passes for 566 yards on 88 targets in 2025, down from 72 catches and 803 yards in 2024. Colts reporting connected part of that change to heavier tight-end personnel: Indianapolis played 383 snaps with at least two tight ends in 2025, compared with an average of 245 in 2023 and 2024. When a third receiver leaves the field for a second tight end, the slot receiver is often the affected player. That personnel math helps explain reduced opportunity; it does not independently grade Downs's play.

Head coach Shane Steichen said in March that the Colts would examine giving Downs more outside opportunities during OTAs and camp. That is useful evidence of an intended experiment, not a promise that Downs will become a full-time outside receiver. His listed 5-foot-9 frame does not prohibit outside work, and “slot” should not be treated as a lesser job. The relevant questions are whether he can release against different leverage, preserve timing from wider alignments, block adequately for the called concept, and still access the separation ability that made him valuable inside.

Moving Downs can create strategic benefits even if he remains primarily a slot player. Multiple alignments make it harder for a defense to assign one matchup or use a formation tendency as a shortcut. They also let Indianapolis keep Downs on the field when Warren and another tight end are used together. But versatility has a cost if it dilutes excellence. The offense should not pursue an abstract ideal of positionlessness at the expense of the routes and leverage reads Downs executes best.

The Pittman trade creates a plausible increase in Downs's targets, but a target forecast would be premature. The same offense plans to retain multiple-tight-end packages, Warren already commanded 76 receptions as a rookie, and Pierce is an established major-play option. Downs's 2026 growth is better measured by his share of high-value situations: third down, two-minute offense, pressure answers, red-zone spacing, and snaps in heavier groupings. If his total rises but he disappears when the quarterback needs a fast answer, the structural problem remains.

### Tyler Warren: the position label does not capture the job

Warren was not merely productive for a rookie tight end. In 2025 he started all 17 games, played 899 of the Colts' 1,075 offensive snaps, caught 76 passes for 817 yards and four touchdowns, and added a rushing touchdown. Indianapolis says his receiving yards and receptions were franchise rookie-tight-end records. He was later added to the Pro Bowl. Those are verified outcomes from a large first-year role.

His alignment record explains why Warren changes the entire pass-catcher structure. According to the club's rookie review, citing Pro Football Focus, he aligned in the backfield 64 times, as a receiver in the slot or outside for 384 snaps, and technically at tight end for 466 snaps, with three wildcat snaps as well. The categories show that “TE1” is too crude a description. Warren can allow Indianapolis to present a heavier personnel group and then spread the formation, or present a receiving surface and bring a physical blocker toward the formation without substituting.

That flexibility matters to both the pass and the run. If a defense answers two tight ends with a heavier body, Warren can be moved into space. If it stays light, the offense may have favorable bodies for Jonathan Taylor's run game. This is the logic of the personnel package, not a guarantee of an advantageous matchup or successful play. Warren must still release, separate, catch, block, and process assignments; the quarterback and line must make the structure functional.

His presence also complicates the phrase “replace Pittman's targets.” Warren already received a large share as a rookie and could improve in Year 2, but indiscriminately increasing his volume could remove the surprise and multiplicity that make him useful. Indianapolis should be evaluated on whether Warren creates answers across formations, not on whether he reaches a round statistical milestone. An 817-yard season that improves the run game, protection menu, and coverage identification can be more valuable than a larger receiving number produced in a narrower offense.

## The outside-receiver bridge

The least settled job is the complementary outside role after Pittman's trade. The official roster lists Westbrook-Ikhine, Dulin, Laquon Treadwell, Anthony Gould, Coleman Owen, Eli Pancol, Deion Burks, Sahmir Hagans, E.J. Horton, and Raylen Sharpe behind or alongside the established trio. Membership establishes competition, not hierarchy.

Westbrook-Ikhine supplies the clearest veteran résumé. Indianapolis signed him March 18 after five seasons in Tennessee and one in Miami. At signing, the club credited him with 137 catches for 1,862 yards and 19 touchdowns over 93 games, including 42 starts. His 2025 receiving line—11 catches for 89 yards in 15 games—warns against treating career totals as proof of a current high-volume role. Conversely, one low-volume season does not erase the route, blocking, and situational experience accumulated over six years. Camp should show whether the Colts view him as a frequent outside participant, a matchup and red-zone option, or a veteran floor for a broader competition.

Dulin has a different claim. The Colts' May discussion of the receiver opening identified him as a special-teams standout and valuable offensive reserve. That makes his roster value wider than target production. A fourth or fifth receiver often must cover kicks, block, and back up several alignments because game-day active spots are limited. If Dulin wins meaningful outside snaps, the evidence should be repeated first-team usage and passing-game responsibility rather than familiarity alone.

Treadwell and the developmental receivers represent further possibilities, but their presence should not be inflated into equal odds. An expanded offseason roster is supposed to contain more candidates than final jobs. Gould's speed and return background, Pancol's size, or another player's practice production may fit a narrow function; none of those observations by itself establishes a complete regular-season role. The most useful camp reporting will identify who plays in which alignment, against which unit, in what situation, and on which special-teams units.

## Deion Burks and the speed-overlap question

Burks, the No. 254 pick in the 2026 draft, gives the offense another verified speed trait. He ran a 4.30-second combine 40, fifth-fastest among receivers at the event. Across Purdue and Oklahoma he caught 151 passes for 1,669 yards and 14 touchdowns, including 57 for 620 yards and four scores in 2025. Those figures establish college production and rare straight-line testing at a minimal draft cost.

They do not establish that Burks can immediately reproduce Pierce's vertical effect. NFL speed must be accessed through releases, route pacing, tracking, timing with the quarterback, and catches through contact. Nor is duplicating Pierce necessarily the best route to a game-day place. Burks may be more valuable if he can threaten speed from a different alignment, handle manufactured touches, or contribute on returns and coverage units. His prior college return work suggests a possible evaluation path, not an awarded NFL job.

There is a broader roster question here. Two fast receivers do not automatically create complementary depth if both cover only the same route and neither supplies blocking or special-teams flexibility. The Colts should test whether Burks's speed expands the menu or merely repeats a trait already owned by a proven player. If it expands the menu, he can force defenses to honor width, motion, or multiple vertical releases. If it does not, he must be exceptionally good at the overlapping job to displace a more versatile reserve.

## The other tight ends are structural players too

Warren's receiving production should not make Mo Alie-Cox, Drew Ogletree, Will Mallory, Sean McKeon, and undrafted rookie Carson Towt invisible. Indianapolis re-signed Alie-Cox and Ogletree, and the club has already said multiple-tight-end usage is not going away. That retention is evidence that the offense values a multi-player room. It does not determine how many tight ends make the final roster or which player receives which snap.

The secondary tight ends affect the receivers because every personnel choice has an opportunity cost. When Warren and a second tight end play together, Indianapolis can remove a receiver, a fullback-type body, or another eligible player depending on the formation. The second tight end may be asked to block at the point, move across the formation, release late, protect, or play special teams. A player who receives few targets can therefore be essential to the pass structure if his blocking sells play action or keeps Warren available for a route.

Mallory offers a lighter listed profile than Alie-Cox and Ogletree; McKeon provides veteran competition; Towt brings a college-basketball-to-football conversion that is developmental by definition. Those descriptors are starting points, not role declarations. The correct evaluation is assignment-specific. Can a player hold up attached? Can he move without revealing the play? Does the quarterback trust his route depth? Can he cover kicks? The eventual tight-end count will reveal how strongly Indianapolis values heavy-personnel multiplicity relative to receiver and line depth.

## Five jobs the offense must cover

The pass-catcher room becomes clearer when organized by jobs rather than names.

1. **Vertical stress.** Pierce has demonstrated this at an elite efficiency level. The offense needs a contingency when he is limited and complementary routes that punish deep coverage.
2. **Quick separation and pressure answers.** Downs is the leading established candidate, while Warren's feel for space can contribute. The quarterback must still receive routes timed to the protection.
3. **Formation multiplicity.** Warren is the central piece, but outside/slot flexibility from Downs and the reserve receivers determines whether movement creates real uncertainty.
4. **Physical and contested situations.** Pittman's departure makes this especially visible on third down and in the red zone. Size alone does not award the job; route leverage, timing, hands, and quarterback trust must be demonstrated.
5. **Blocking and game-day labor.** Alie-Cox, Ogletree, Westbrook-Ikhine, Dulin, and other reserves can earn value here. Blocking must be evaluated within assignment, and special teams can decide which depth players are actually active.

Indianapolis does not need one player to lead all five categories. It needs combinations that do not announce the play. If the best blocker never runs a credible route, the defense can respond. If the quick separator is absent from heavy personnel, a defense gains a clue. If every explosive concept requires long protection, the unsettled offensive line and a quarterback returning from an Achilles injury become more exposed. Pass-catcher construction is therefore inseparable from [[Indianapolis Colts quarterback structure 2026|quarterback timing]] and [[Indianapolis Colts offensive line 2026|protection]].

## How to read camp without manufacturing certainty

Training camp begins July 29. Early observations can identify experiments, especially Downs's outside work, Pierce's participation level, the second tight end in different packages, and which reserve receivers join core special teams. But a single catch, drop, one-on-one win, or rep with the first unit cannot settle a role.

The stronger indicators are repeated patterns across padded practices and preseason games: personnel grouping, alignment, motion, down and distance, route type, defensive opponent, quarterback, protection, blocking assignment, and special-teams usage. Availability must be included. A receiver taking more repetitions while Pierce rehabilitates is receiving an opportunity, but the reason for the opportunity matters when projecting the regular season.

Target totals in preseason can also mislead because game plans are limited and teams may deliberately test a player. A more useful audit asks whether the offense preserves answers. Can it attack deep, intermediate, and short? Can it throw from two-tight-end personnel? Can it run when the defense stays light? Can it create a fast outlet against pressure? Can the reserve configuration perform those functions when one established player sits?

The 2026 pass-catcher room has a credible high-end shape: Pierce stretches, Downs separates, Warren bends personnel rules, and Taylor's rushing threat forces defenses to allocate bodies. Its risk is functional incompleteness after Pittman's departure. Westbrook-Ikhine, Dulin, the secondary tight ends, and the young receivers do not need to replace his name. They do need to make sure the offense retains outside stability, physical situational play, blocking, and enough redundancy to survive an absence. Camp can begin answering that question; only regular-season execution can settle it.
