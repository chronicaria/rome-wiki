Indianapolis used the 2026 draft to replenish the middle of its defense and add developmental speed, depth, and competition around a roster whose missing first-round pick had already been converted into cornerback Sauce Gardner.

## Direct summary

The Colts selected eight players: Georgia linebacker CJ Allen at No. 53, LSU safety A.J. Haulcy at No. 78, Kentucky guard Jalen Farmer at No. 113, Oregon linebacker Bryce Boettcher at No. 135, Florida edge George Gumbs Jr. at No. 156, Ohio State edge Caden Curry at No. 214, Kentucky running back Seth McGowan at No. 237, and Oklahoma receiver Deion Burks at No. 254. Five of the eight play defense. Allen and Haulcy are the clearest candidates for early defensive responsibility; the six Day 3 selections are better understood as a portfolio of specific traits and roster competitions than as presumed starters.

The class cannot be evaluated honestly without the asset the Colts bought before draft weekend. Indianapolis sent its 2026 and 2027 first-round picks plus receiver Adonai Mitchell to the New York Jets for Gardner in November 2025. That made Gardner, an established NFL corner, the functional premium acquisition attached to the 2026 draft cycle. General manager Chris Ballard then traded down from No. 47 to No. 53, received No. 135, and exchanged seventh-round positions with Pittsburgh. Indianapolis still obtained Allen and used the added fourth-rounder on Boettcher. The defensible early conclusion is therefore about process, not a grade: Indianapolis exchanged top-end draft optionality for Gardner, then used its remaining capital to create eight rookie bets concentrated at linebacker, safety, edge, and athletic depth.

All eight draft picks were under contract by June 2. None had played an NFL game as of this article's date. College production, testing, offseason practice, and coach comments can define the questions they enter camp with; they cannot establish that any rookie will translate, start, or provide surplus value.

## The asset map matters more than a letter grade

Indianapolis did not merely “lack” a first-round selection. It had deliberately spent that pick, along with a future first, in the Gardner trade. That distinction matters because a class-only judgment hides the largest allocation decision. Gardner should be judged through his actual Colts performance, availability, and the opportunity cost of the two first-rounders and Mitchell. The eight rookies should be judged against the picks Indianapolis actually exercised. Combining those two evaluations later will give a fairer account of the club's 2026 roster construction.

The trade with Pittsburgh shows a second, smaller allocation choice. The Colts began Day 2 at No. 47, moved six spots, and took Allen at No. 53. The official club account says Indianapolis obtained No. 135 and swapped seventh-round selections. Ballard said the club considered moving down again but stopped because it did not want to lose Allen. This was not costless cleverness: every trade down creates a chance that the target disappears. In this instance the Colts accepted six picks of risk, retained their target, and turned the added selection into Boettcher. The result can eventually be evaluated as an Allen-plus-Boettcher package, but it would be hindsight to call the move a win before either plays.

The class also reveals a clear distribution of bets. Indianapolis chose five defenders and three offensive players. Four of the first five selections were defenders, including two off-ball linebackers and a safety. Two more selections went to edge defenders with very different developmental histories. The offense received an experienced right guard, a powerfully built running back, and a receiver with verified straight-line speed. No quarterback, tackle, tight end, defensive tackle, or corner was selected. That does not prove the club regarded those positions as solved; draft boards, pick availability, and the prior Gardner trade also shape what is possible.

### CJ Allen: the intended center of the class

Allen is the selection carrying the largest immediate organizational expectation. Georgia started him as a freshman, and he finished three seasons with 205 tackles, 13.5 tackles for loss, 4.5 sacks, two forced fumbles, and 11 passes defended according to the club's selection announcement. In 2025 he led Georgia with 88 tackles, recorded eight tackles for loss and 3.5 sacks, and earned first-team AP All-America and first-team All-SEC recognition. Those facts establish extensive responsibility and production in a demanding college defense. They do not automatically establish NFL coverage range or block deconstruction.

Ballard explicitly described Allen as a MIKE linebacker and prospective green-dot communicator. The club also framed him as a replacement candidate for Zaire Franklin after Indianapolis traded Franklin to Green Bay in March. That is unusually clear role evidence for a rookie: the selection aligns a high pick, a vacated communication job, and a player who handled communication demands at Georgia. The inference is that Allen has the shortest path in the class to a large snap share. The unresolved test is whether he can make those calls while processing NFL motion, play-action, route distributions, and bigger blockers quickly enough to remain on the field in varied situations.

Allen should not be reduced to tackle totals. Off-ball linebacker evaluation is especially sensitive to assignment, defensive-line protection, pursuit leverage, and the difference between arriving at a play and actually improving its outcome. His early audit should track communication responsibility, missed tackles, coverage assignments, block avoidance, and third-down usage—not merely whether he leads the team in tackles.

### A.J. Haulcy: production with a versatility test

Haulcy arrived with the broadest statistical résumé in the class: 347 tackles, 10 interceptions, four forced fumbles, and 44 starts in 48 games across New Mexico, Houston, and LSU. In his only LSU season he produced 88 tackles, three interceptions, and first-team All-SEC recognition. The official selection article also reports zero penalties over 702 defensive snaps in 2025. His transfers mean the production came in multiple systems and levels of competition; that can indicate adaptability, but it is not proof that every college assignment maps cleanly to an NFL one.

Defensive coordinator Lou Anarumo's public comments place Haulcy in a safety room with veteran Camryn Bynum and emphasize the value of having multiple capable safeties. The useful inference is not that Haulcy has already won a starting job. It is that his experience and ball production give Anarumo another candidate for disguise and personnel flexibility. The Colts can test him deep, in split-safety structures, nearer the box, or in matchup work without requiring his job title to settle the defense's shape.

Haulcy's early evaluation should separate interceptions from repeatable coverage process. Turnovers fluctuate. Range, route recognition, leverage, tackling space, communication, and avoidance of explosive errors are more stable clues. If he earns work beside Bynum, the next question is which player handles which alignments, not which one receives a generic “free” or “strong” label.

### Jalen Farmer: an athletic interior-line investment

Farmer played sparingly in two seasons at Florida, transferred, then started 24 consecutive games at right guard for Kentucky in 2024 and 2025. The Colts listed him at 6-foot-5 and 312 pounds in the selection announcement and highlighted a 4.93-second 40-yard dash, the fourth-fastest combine time by a guard since at least 1999. That time is real evidence of unusual movement ability for his position. It is only a proxy for football movement: pass-set balance, hand placement, recovery, anchor, awareness of games, and angles in space matter more.

His path is shaped by the existing [[Indianapolis Colts offensive line 2026|offensive-line]] picture. Matt Goncalves started all 17 games at right guard in 2025, while Quenton Nelson anchors the other side. Farmer therefore enters with experience at a precise position but no entitlement to it. A sensible developmental objective is to become trustworthy interior depth while forcing a real competition through protection consistency and versatility. Whether he can play center or left guard in the NFL should remain a question until the team supplies practice or game evidence.

### Bryce Boettcher: a second linebacker bet with a different route

Boettcher's path was unusually indirect. He joined Oregon as a baseball player, walked on to football, continued as a two-sport athlete, and was selected by Houston in the 13th round of the 2024 MLB draft. He chose football and led Oregon in tackles in both 2024 and 2025. His four-season totals were 269 tackles, 16 tackles for loss, four sacks, four forced fumbles, and two interceptions; 136 tackles came in 15 games in 2025.

The pairing with Allen is important. Indianapolis did not use No. 135 merely to insure the MIKE selection. It added another productive linebacker whose clearest early route may include special teams and subpackage competition. Boettcher's baseball background and tackle volume make a compelling biography, not an NFL projection by themselves. The football questions are whether he can diagnose without false steps, take on NFL blocks, cover enough space, and translate his pursuit temperament to kicking downs. If he and Allen eventually play together, their complementary assignments will matter more than their shared position label.

### George Gumbs Jr.: the developmental conversion

Gumbs began at Northern Illinois as a receiver, moved to tight end, and changed to edge rusher in 2023. He transferred to Florida for his last two seasons and finished his college career with 98 tackles, 21 tackles for loss, 11 sacks, and four forced fumbles over 45 games. At Florida he recorded 7.5 sacks and 14.5 tackles for loss in 22 games. Those totals confirm that the conversion produced real college disruption; they also describe a player with far fewer years of defensive technique than a conventional prospect.

That makes Gumbs a classic upside selection. His 6-foot-4, 245-pound frame and offensive background may help with movement understanding, but neither guarantees an NFL pass-rush plan. His evaluation should focus on get-off, hand usage, counter development, run-edge integrity, and whether he can contribute on special teams while the defensive craft develops. The presence of Laiatu Latu and other established edge options can reduce the need to force immediate volume, although the roster itself—not draft status—will determine whether Gumbs earns that developmental runway.

### Caden Curry: production after patience

Curry spent his first three Ohio State seasons mostly in reserve work behind future NFL draft choices, then broke through in 2025. He started 14 games, recorded 66 tackles, and led Ohio State with 11 sacks and 16.5 tackles for loss. The club notes that he led the Buckeyes in both categories even though teammate Arvell Reese went fifth overall. Curry finished his four-year career with 14.5 sacks and 24 tackles for loss.

The sharp change in opportunity makes Curry one of the class's best calibration cases. One interpretation is that the final season exposed a player whose earlier depth-chart position concealed starter-level ability. Another is that one highly productive season overstates how consistently his tools will win against NFL tackles. Both remain possible. His Indiana roots—he attended Center Grove and won consecutive state titles—are locally meaningful but irrelevant to the performance forecast. The football value will come from whether his motor, leverage, versatility, and 2025 finishing production survive stronger and more technically refined opposition.

### Seth McGowan: a late power-back competition

McGowan's college path crossed Oklahoma, Texas College, Butler Community College, New Mexico State, and Kentucky. He returned to the FBS at New Mexico State in 2024 and ran 152 times for 823 yards. At Kentucky in 2025 he rushed for 725 yards and 12 touchdowns. Indianapolis listed him at 6 feet and 223 pounds, giving the class a back with a sturdier physical profile than its receiver selection.

Seventh-round running backs rarely receive roles on draft capital alone. McGowan must show what his production means snap by snap: vision and footwork behind NFL blocking, yards created after contact, ball security, receiving reliability, pass protection, and special-teams usefulness. Twelve touchdowns suggest finishing production but do not independently prove short-yardage skill; blocking, play design, and opportunity influence touchdown totals. His path is therefore a competition for a defined secondary job rather than evidence of an intended change atop the backfield.

### Deion Burks: a speed bet at the final pick

Burks closed the Colts' draft at No. 254, a compensatory selection. Across Purdue and Oklahoma he caught 151 passes for 1,669 yards and 14 touchdowns in 48 games, starting 31. In 2025 he produced 57 catches, 620 yards, and four touchdowns. His 4.30-second combine 40 was fifth-fastest among receivers at the event and supplies a clear reason for a late-round investment: Indianapolis can test a scarce athletic trait at a low draft cost.

Straight-line speed changes coverage only if a player can access it within an offense. Burks must release cleanly, maintain route timing, track and finish throws, work through contact, and show enough versatility to dress on game day. His earlier kickoff-return experience at Purdue provides a possible secondary path, but old college usage is not proof of an NFL return role. The right early question is whether his speed creates repeatable separation or special-teams value, not whether a 4.30 time makes him an automatic deep threat.

## What the class says about roster construction

The first theme is middle-of-defense renewal. Allen, Haulcy, and Boettcher add communication, tackling, and coverage candidates after roster changes at linebacker and safety. Because Anarumo uses disguise and varied personnel, mental processing may determine the class's early defensive value as much as raw athleticism. Allen and Haulcy could permit flexibility if they earn trust; if they do not, their positions can expose explosive plays quickly.

The second theme is multiple bites at pass-rush development. Gumbs offers conversion upside; Curry offers a late breakout with more conventional defensive experience. Taking both avoids making one developmental story carry the entire edge-depth bet. It also creates a clean comparison: which traits translate, which player can defend the run, and which can earn enough special-teams or rotational value to remain active while developing?

The third is athletic optionality on offense. Farmer's testing, Burks's speed, and McGowan's size each give coaches something identifiable to test. Yet the class did not spend premium capital on immediate offensive transformation. Its offensive upside depends on position coaching and narrowly won roles behind established players. This is why declaring the draft a success based on traits or perceived “value” would be premature.

Finally, the Gardner transaction concentrates risk outside the rookie group. A first-round pick is not just one prospect; it is a flexible asset that can be selected, traded, or used to move around the board. Indianapolis surrendered that flexibility in 2026 and 2027 for a known elite-career-profile corner entering a new team context. If Gardner supplies high-level, available play at a scarce position, the absence of a first-round rookie may be easier to justify. If not, later-round hits cannot erase the opportunity cost, though they can still improve the roster on their own terms.

## A fair evaluation timeline

Training camp can answer whether Allen receives the communication role, where Haulcy aligns, which rookies join core special-teams units, and whether Farmer takes meaningful interior-line reps. Preseason can add evidence about assignment execution and physical translation, but opponent quality and simplified game plans limit conclusions. The regular season will reveal who dresses, who earns defensive or offensive snaps, and whether early errors shrink with repetition.

A one-year review should emphasize roles earned, availability, and direction of development. It should not demand that Day 3 picks become starters. A three-year review can better test whether Allen and Haulcy became durable defensive pieces, whether at least some later picks produced useful snaps, and whether the trade-down generated value through the Allen-Boettcher pair. Any full-cycle assessment must also include Gardner and the forfeited 2027 first-rounder. That wider ledger prevents both easy optimism about eight new names and easy pessimism about the missing first-round selection.

## A scorecard that resists draft-grade hindsight

The cleanest way to revisit this class is to preserve the questions that existed before the results. Otherwise every outcome will acquire an apparently obvious explanation. A player who succeeds will be called an inevitable steal; a player who fails will be described as visibly flawed all along. Neither story captures the uncertainty Indianapolis accepted on draft weekend.

Four separate ledgers are more useful than one grade.

**Role conversion** asks whether the club turned college evidence into an NFL job. Allen's first benchmark is not a Pro Bowl; it is reliable communication and three-down usability. Haulcy's is trustworthy alignment and coverage work. Farmer's is dependable interior-line depth. The five later picks need a repeatable scrimmage or special-teams function before their traits have roster value.

**Availability and retention** ask whether those roles survive. Games active, practice continuity, snaps earned, and a place on the roster after Years 1 and 2 provide more information than preseason highlights. They must still be interpreted carefully: injury is not the same as poor play, and a crowded veteran depth chart is not the same as failed development.

**Resource efficiency** compares results with acquisition cost. Allen should face a higher expectation than Burks because No. 53 carried more opportunity cost than No. 254. The Allen-Boettcher pair should also be reviewed together because the trade down created the Boettcher selection. This does not mean adding their statistics. It means asking whether accepting the risk of moving from No. 47 produced two useful roles instead of one.

**The full transaction cycle** keeps Gardner separate from the rookies while still including him in the final judgment. Gardner's Colts performance can justify or weaken the decision to surrender premium picks, but it cannot retroactively change whether Allen read a route correctly or whether Curry developed an NFL rush plan. Conversely, a late-round hit does not refund the lost first-round optionality. The two decisions belong in the same roster-construction review but require different evidence.

This framework also protects against a common alternative explanation. If several rookies play early, that may reflect quality, but it may also reflect vacancies or injuries. If they play little, that may reflect weak performance, but it may also mean the veterans remained healthy and effective. Snap counts identify opportunity and trust; film, assignment evidence, and role stability are needed to explain why the counts changed.
