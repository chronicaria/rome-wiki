The Colts enter 2026 with a proven Jonathan Taylor-centered rushing engine, but its durability depends on developing a trustworthy second back, settling right tackle, and preserving quarterback-created conflict without assuming Daniel Jones's post-Achilles movement will immediately match 2025.

Up: [[Indianapolis Colts 2026]]

As of **July 10, 2026**, Indianapolis has much more than an aspiration to run the ball. The club's official 2025 statistics record 2,007 rushing yards, 27 rushing touchdowns, and 4.5 yards per carry. Jonathan Taylor supplied 1,585 yards and 18 of those touchdowns on 323 attempts. That production gives the Colts a real offensive foundation, not merely a philosophical preference.

It also exposes the central 2026 problem. Taylor accounted for about 79 percent of the team's rushing yards and 73 percent of its attempts, calculations from the official totals. The Colts can build around that concentration because Taylor is their best runner. They should not confuse dependence with depth. A complete 2026 rushing plan must preserve Taylor's ability to change games while identifying which other back can protect the quarterback, catch well enough to avoid becoming a tendency, and handle useful carries without forcing the offense into a different identity.

This page narrows the broader thesis in [[Indianapolis Colts offensive identity 2026]]. It does not predict a league ranking or prescribe an exact run-pass ratio. It asks a more useful question: **what has to work for Indianapolis's running game to remain structurally sound in 2026?**

## The 2025 baseline was productive and highly concentrated

The team-level numbers establish the floor of the analysis. Indianapolis ran 442 times for 2,007 yards, a 4.5-yard average, and 27 touchdowns. Opponents allowed by the Colts' defense ran 448 times for 1,732 yards and 16 touchdowns, but that comparison does not prove the offense was efficient in every situation. Team yards per carry can be affected by explosive gains, quarterback scrambles, kneel-downs, short-yardage attempts, game state, and opponent quality. It is best treated as an output measure rather than a full evaluation of blocking or play design.

Taylor's line is more revealing: 323 carries, 1,585 yards, 4.9 per attempt, an 83-yard long, and 18 rushing touchdowns. The Colts also credited him with 46 receptions for 378 yards and two scores. An official postseason account said he led the NFL in carries, rushing touchdowns, total touchdowns, and run snaps; finished third in rushing yards; and recorded 36 explosive runs. Those categories show both volume and big-play production. Taylor was not simply converting a stream of short opportunities near the goal line.

The distribution behind him was thin. Daniel Jones ranked second on the team with 164 rushing yards and five touchdowns on 45 attempts. Rookie DJ Giddens carried 26 times for 96 yards. Ameer Abdullah had 14 carries for 60 yards, Tyler Goodson nine for 24, Riley Leonard six for 27 and two touchdowns, and the remaining players contributed only small totals. Abdullah and Goodson are not on the club's current roster page. The present running-back group is Taylor, Giddens, Ulysses Bentley IV, rookie Seth McGowan, Anderson Castle, and Lincoln Pare; the official roster has also displayed a discrepancy involving Jordon Vaughn that [[Indianapolis Colts 2026 roster map]] keeps unresolved.

That turnover matters more than a generic label such as “RB2.” The 2025 offense received complementary rushing value from a quarterback and several backs, but very little of it came from a single reserve who now owns an established role. Giddens is the only returning back besides Taylor with more than one official 2025 carry. The Colts therefore have continuity at the most important spot and uncertainty everywhere behind it.

## Taylor changes the geometry of ordinary downs

Taylor's value begins before he touches the ball. A defense that expects him to receive an inside-zone handoff, press a gap run, or threaten the perimeter must commit players to fits and pursuit angles. That attention can create space behind linebackers, isolate defensive backs against receivers, or make a defender responsible for both a run and a pass option. [[Indianapolis Colts offensive identity 2026]] calls this a conflict offense because the run is not separate from the passing game; it helps define the defense's answer.

The runner makes that structure dangerous through patience and acceleration. A called lane is not always a fixed destination. Taylor can press the initial track long enough to keep linebackers committed, then cut when the blocking declares itself. If the first level is clean, his speed turns a modest schematic win into a long gain. The club's 2025 account of a victory at Tennessee offered a concrete example: Tyler Warren aligned at fullback on a long Taylor touchdown, and Shane Steichen accepted several short gains because the runner's second-level potential changed the value of continued attempts.

This does not mean every low-gain carry was secretly successful or that Indianapolis should run regardless of box count and score. It means evaluating the rushing attack requires more than its average. A two-yard gain can be a losing play; it can also help a coordinator learn how an opponent is fitting a formation, set up a constraint play, or keep a manageable down. Conversely, an 83-yard run can inflate an average while masking repeated penetration. The 2026 evidence should therefore include success by situation, negative-run frequency, explosive runs, short-yardage conversion, and the passing answers generated from the same looks.

Taylor's receiving work also prevents an easy defensive shortcut. Forty-six catches for 378 yards are meaningful participation, not proof that he is an elite route runner in every alignment. They do establish that a Taylor snap need not announce a handoff. Screens, checkdowns, swings, and releases can punish defenders who attack the run too aggressively. His value is greatest when the offense can leave him on the field without making the next action obvious.

## The second-back competition is an offensive-function test

DJ Giddens enters Year 2 with the clearest claim to the first opportunity behind Taylor, but not with a settled job. The Colts drafted him in the fifth round in 2025 after he produced 3,087 rushing yards and 679 receiving yards at Kansas State. His rookie NFL sample—26 carries for 96 yards and no receptions—is too small to establish his professional ceiling. It does show that Indianapolis has seen him in its system and trusted him for some regular-season rushing work.

The most important camp questions are broader than his yards per carry. Can Giddens identify pressure and execute protection assignments? Can he catch and finish underneath routes? Can he contribute on special teams when Taylor is active? Can the offense call its ordinary concepts when he replaces Taylor, or does the substitution narrow the menu? Public practices can offer clues, but protection recognition is difficult to judge without the called assignment, and preseason production against mixed personnel can mislead.

Seth McGowan adds a different competition point. Indianapolis selected the 6-foot, 223-pound Kentucky back at No. 237 in the seventh round of the 2026 draft. The official draft page credits him with 165 carries for 725 yards and 12 rushing touchdowns in 2025, plus 19 catches for 126 yards. His college record also includes 823 rushing yards and 377 receiving yards at New Mexico State in 2024. Those are facts about prior production, not evidence that he is already the Colts' short-yardage answer or preferred receiving back.

McGowan's path is likely functional. A late-round rookie can make the roster by becoming useful in several narrow jobs: coverage teams, return units, pass protection, four-minute offense, short yardage, or injury relief. His listed size may support a hypothesis that he can handle contact, but size alone does not prove vision, pad level, ball security, or NFL protection competence. The club's decision to draft him creates competition; it does not settle the outcome.

Ulysses Bentley IV, Anderson Castle, and Lincoln Pare complete the known summer group. Their presence keeps the competition open, especially because reserve backs often earn game-day status through special teams. The club should not be assumed to keep a fixed number before camp. A runner who looks better with the ball can still lose a roster place if another player covers kicks, protects more reliably, or supplies emergency flexibility. Conversely, one strong preseason rushing line should not be treated as proof that a complete third-down role has been won.

The best complementary outcome is not necessarily a stylistic opposite to Taylor. “Change of pace” is often used without specifying the problem being solved. Indianapolis needs someone who lets the offense survive Taylor's rest snaps and absences. If that player can also create a distinct advantage—more receiving range, short-yardage power, or special-teams value—that is useful. The essential standard is trustworthy function, not contrast for its own sake.

## Four returning linemen create a base; right tackle sets the uncertainty

The projected front returns Bernhard Raimann at left tackle, Quenton Nelson at left guard, Tanor Bortolini at center, and Matt Goncalves at right guard. The current official depth chart places Jalen Travis at right tackle, but July depth charts and roster displays should not be mistaken for a final Week 1 declaration. [[Indianapolis Colts offensive line 2026]] owns the detailed competition and explains why right-tackle evaluation must remain open.

For the running game, continuity matters because coordinated blocking is relational. Linemen must recognize fronts, pass movement between them, reach or displace defenders, and time climbs to the second level. A talented individual can still create a failed run by taking the wrong angle or losing a combination too early. Four returning projected starters give Indianapolis a shared vocabulary and experience base. They do not guarantee that every concept will work equally well.

The right side is the pressure point. Travis started the final four games of 2025, mostly at right tackle, after entering the season with little NFL experience at that position. That exposure is valuable but limited. Goncalves and Travis must establish whether they can sort stunts and changing fronts, create movement on downhill concepts, and secure the backside when Taylor cuts. If the pairing cannot hold up consistently, penetration can force Taylor to declare before the play develops, reducing the very patience and acceleration that make him dangerous.

Personnel beyond the five linemen also shapes the menu. Tyler Warren's ability to align as a conventional tight end, detached receiver, H-back, or backfield player can change the strength and presentation of a formation without a substitution. Mo Alie-Cox, Drew Ogletree, Will Mallory, and Sean McKeon offer different combinations of size, route value, and blocking experience. The important 2026 question is not which one receives the “blocking tight end” label. It is whether the group can execute enough distinct jobs that Indianapolis can run and pass credibly from the same personnel.

Receivers participate too. Crack blocks, stalk blocks, motion, and vertical routes affect how quickly secondary defenders enter the fit. Alec Pierce's verified downfield production can discourage a safety from collapsing recklessly. Josh Downs can stress underneath leverage. Michael Pittman Jr.'s departure removes an experienced receiver from the structure, and his blocking work cannot simply be assumed to transfer to a new player. The running-game replacement question therefore extends into [[Indianapolis Colts pass-catcher structure 2026]], not only the running-back room.

## Quarterback identity changes the extra-defender problem

Daniel Jones's 2025 rushing line—45 attempts, 164 yards, and five touchdowns—shows that he contributed materially around the goal line and in open space. It does not reveal how many attempts were designed runs, reads, scrambles, or kneel-downs. More importantly, it predates the torn Achilles that ended his season. As [[Indianapolis Colts quarterback structure 2026]] explains, Jones is the intended starter, but his repeated football movement is the gating variable.

If Jones can execute keepers, boots, and movement passes convincingly, the backside defender cannot automatically chase Taylor. Even a low-frequency quarterback threat has structural value if the defense must honor it. If rehabilitation limits that element early, Steichen may need to create conflict through motion, Warren's alignments, receiver screens, and play-action rather than exposing Jones to unnecessary contact. That would not erase the rushing attack, but it would alter how the offense keeps an extra defender from attacking Taylor.

Anthony Richardson Sr. represents the highest theoretical stress on a run defense. His size, speed, and arm can force a defender to account for Taylor, the quarterback keep, and space behind the fit. Riley Leonard also has functional movement and scored twice on six official rookie-season carries. Both are competing for the backup role, not sharing an announced 2026 package. It would be speculation to promise specialized quarterback-run series before the coaches show them.

The key distinction is between **capability** and **planned usage**. Indianapolis has quarterbacks who have produced on the ground. Only camp and games can show how much designed quarterback movement survives Jones's rehabilitation, whether a backup package exists, and how often run-pass options are truly called. Broadcast guesses about reads are not equivalent to the playbook.

## Workload management should preserve leverage, not chase symmetry

Taylor's 323 carries and league-leading 363 run snaps show a heavy 2025 burden. There is no public basis for declaring a correct 2026 carry target in July. Workload depends on game count, score, play volume, injury status, efficiency, and how much trust the complementary backs earn. A rigid preseason number would create false precision.

The useful principle is to spend Taylor's work where it creates the most leverage. High-value situations include short yardage, red-zone possessions, four-minute offense, and moments when his explosive capacity can punish a defense forced to defend the whole formation. That does not require removing him on ordinary downs; predictable “rest series” can make personnel a tell. It does require a second back capable of taking real snaps so that every absence is not an offensive concession.

The Colts also need to avoid interpreting durability only through games played. Repeated pass-protection collisions and routes add physical load that rushing attempts miss. Taylor's receiving value makes him useful on third down, yet using him for every protection and checkdown increases the concentration problem. A reserve who earns passing-down trust could reduce Taylor's total exposure without stripping the offense of credibility.

None of this argues for a committee backfield in the sense of equal touches. The evidence supports Taylor as the clear lead. The goal is asymmetric depth: Taylor remains the center, while another player becomes competent enough that the offense does not advertise weakness or overload its best runner.

## What would count as confirmation in 2026

The strongest evidence will arrive in stages.

**Training camp** can clarify who receives first-team reserve work, who participates in protection periods, and whether Jones performs sustained team movement. It cannot establish regular-season efficiency or durability.

**Preseason games** can show assignment execution, ball security, special-teams participation, and whether reserve backs stay on the field across run and pass situations. Opponent personnel and simplified plans limit the conclusions.

**Early regular-season games** can reveal the actual rotation, concept diversity, right-side cohesion, and quarterback constraints. One explosive outing still should not define the unit. Repeated success against different fronts is more persuasive.

For evaluation, watch five connected indicators:

1. **Negative runs:** frequent backfield contact would challenge the assumption that continuity is producing coordinated execution.
2. **Explosive runs:** Taylor's breakaway capacity is part of the engine, not a bonus detached from it.
3. **Situational conversion:** third-and-short, goal line, and four-minute possessions test displacement and decision-making.
4. **Reserve functional breadth:** protection, receiving, and special teams explain who can dress and remain unpredictable.
5. **Constraint effectiveness:** keepers, play-action, motion, and Warren's alignments should punish defenders who overplay Taylor.

The disciplined July conclusion is therefore conditional. **Indianapolis has a proven elite-volume lead runner and a coherent way to make the run shape the entire offense. It does not yet have a verified second back, a settled right tackle, or proof that the post-injury quarterback can preserve every 2025 constraint.** If those three questions resolve favorably, the run game can remain the Colts' most stable offensive advantage. If they do not, Taylor may still produce, but the system will be easier to load against and more vulnerable to one player's absence.
