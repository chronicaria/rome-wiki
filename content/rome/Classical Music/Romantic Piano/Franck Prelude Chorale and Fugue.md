Franck's *Prélude, Choral et Fugue* turns Bachian titles into a late-Romantic single-span drama: prelude, chorale, and fugue become stages in one cyclic argument about memory, harmony, texture, and release.

Up: [[Romantic piano repertoire]]

César Franck's *Prélude, Choral et Fugue*, FWV 21/CFF 24, was composed in 1884 for solo piano and dedicated to Marie Poitevin, who gave the premiere at the Société Nationale de Musique in Paris on January 25, 1885. The work is usually described as three sections without pause: *Prélude. Moderato*; *Choral. Poco più lento* in the orbit of C minor and related remote colors; and *Fugue. Tempo I*, beginning in B minor and ending in B major. That outline is accurate, but it is not enough. The title names old genres; the experience is a late-Romantic large form in which earlier material returns under new harmonic and textural pressure.

The inspected source chain for this note is primary-first but not complete. IMSLP identifies a public-domain Sibley scan of a Litolff/Enoch issue, listed as Braunschweig: Henry Litolff's Verlag, no. 1578, around 1885, reprinted by Enoch. Henle's Ernst-Günter Heinemann preface and comments identify two autograph fair copies: one in the Library of Congress Moldenhauer Collection and one in the Pierpont Morgan Library, the latter serving as engraver's copy for the 1885 first edition by Enoch Frères & Costallat with Litolff, plate E. F. & C. 1113. Henle's comments also distinguish later Litolff/Enoch issues and a later French re-engraving by Enoch & Cie. This matters because the piece's surface can look like a seamless spiritual utterance, but the documentary record is a set of concrete sources with variants.

The article below therefore separates four layers. **Score fact** names visible labels, section locations, and source-supported measure references. **Historical evidence** names publication, dedication, and source genealogy. **Analytical inference** explains how the work hears as cyclic form. **Performer choice** concerns voicing, tempo, pedal, and projection, none of which is settled by the title alone.

## Why this belongs here

This work is a major destination after [[The Romantic piano cycle]], [[Romantic phrase rhythm]], [[Pedaling evidence in Romantic scores]], and [[Urtext does not mean interpretation-free]]. Schumann and Hensel teach how Romantic piano music can make sequence and return meaningful. Brahms teaches how late-Romantic texture can turn lyric line into layered memory. Franck adds a different problem: how can venerable contrapuntal and sacred-adjacent rhetoric become a single modern piano span without becoming either an academic exercise or an organ transcription?

The answer is not "Franck wrote a prelude, then a chorale, then a fugue." The answer is that each title is transformed by cyclic hearing. The prelude is not merely introductory; it plants figuration, interruption, and harmonic restlessness that later need explanation. The chorale is not a simple hymn tune; it is a massive harmonic and registral event that changes the work's center of gravity. The fugue is not a detached proof of contrapuntal learning; it becomes the only texture capacious enough to reassemble what the prelude and chorale left unresolved.

That makes the work a late-Romantic large-form lesson. It asks the listener to hear through time. An event in the fugue may make the prelude sound more prophetic on a second hearing. The chorale's blocks may seem static at first, then become the missing weight that the fugue must carry. The final B major does not cancel the darkness of B minor; it reframes it after a long process of recall and combination.

## The old titles are not old functions

The title seems to promise Bach. Prelude and fugue point directly toward keyboard tradition; chorale points toward Lutheran and organ associations even though Franck was a Catholic organist in Paris. Treating the titles as references is useful. Treating them as literal genre compliance is misleading.

In a Baroque prelude-and-fugue pair, the prelude may establish key, texture, and affect before the fugue presents a subject and works it contrapuntally. Franck keeps the prestige of that model but alters its timing. He inserts a chorale between prelude and fugue, then makes the fugue answer the whole earlier span rather than simply succeeding the prelude. The result is closer to a triptych heard as one form than to three independent movements.

The prelude opens in B minor with unsettled figuration and a rhetorical profile that suggests improvisation more than exposition. The piano writing does not behave like light decorative passagework. It creates atmosphere, but it also creates a problem: motion without stable arrival. When stronger motivic interruptions appear, they sound like signs of something more definite trying to emerge.

The chorale changes the problem. It brings chordal, declamatory, and registrally weighty writing into the foreground. A listener may hear organ resonance in the spacing and rhetorical breadth, but that should remain an analytical analogy rather than a claim that the piano is pretending to be an organ at every moment. The more important point is functional: the chorale supplies vertical gravity. It says, in effect, that the work's lyric and figural anxiety must pass through something public, block-like, and harmonically saturated.

The fugue then transforms the prestige of counterpoint. In a narrow academic sense, a fugue can be judged by entries, episodes, invertible counterpoint, and stretto. Franck uses those resources, but the large-form point is not scholastic correctness. The fugue becomes a memory engine. Its subject and episodes allow previous ideas to return, collide, and acquire necessity. The title "Fugue" therefore names process as much as genre: the work must now bind earlier rhetoric into an audible structure.

## Score map

The following measure map uses the Henle comments and widely circulated section plan as orientation. Local measure numbering should be checked against Andrew's working edition, especially because source variants and later editions can affect accidentals, slurs, and performance markings.

**Prelude, measures 1-57.** The opening *Moderato* establishes B minor and a restless keyboard surface. The visible texture is figural, but the figuration is repeatedly interrupted by more declarative motivic material. The listener should not hear this as empty prelude haze. The figuration makes the work unstable; the interruptions make the instability memorable. Henle's comments already flag source differences early in the piece, including accidentals and dynamics around measures 5, 10, 11, 16, 26, 28, 52, 67, 74, 95, 96, 101, and 114, which is a reminder that "the text" has a source history even before interpretation begins.

**Chorale, measures 58-115.** The chorale begins after the prelude's unsettled span and introduces a different kind of time. Instead of continuous figural motion, the ear meets chordal proclamation, thicker sonority, and slower harmonic pressure. A recurring chorale idea appears in a remote harmonic world rather than as a plain diatonic hymn. The essential listening fact is not just that "a chorale theme appears," but that piano time changes: the hand now has to voice large vertical events as if they were both harmonic pillars and expressive speech.

**Transition, measures 116-156.** The *Poco allegro* area becomes the hinge into the fugue. Henle's comment on measure 157 is especially useful: A1 has *Tempo 1o Allegro*, while the other sources have only *Tempo 1o*, probably referring back to the *Poco allegro* at measure 116. That small source-critical point has musical consequences. It warns against imagining a completely fresh tempo world at the fugue. The fugue grows from the transitional acceleration and energy already in motion.

**Fugue, from measure 157.** The fugue begins in B minor and eventually ends in B major. Its subject gives shape to the accumulated pressure, but its large function is recapitulatory and transformational. Earlier materials return in the later fugue, especially in the broad cyclic recall near the final third of the work, where prelude-like material, chorale rhetoric, and fugal procedure become interdependent. The final pages are not merely a brilliant coda. They test whether the work's sacred, improvisatory, and contrapuntal rhetorics can sound like one memory.

## What to track on a first listen

For a first listen without the score, track three sounds.

First, track **motion versus declaration**. In the prelude, quick figuration creates continuity, but stronger ideas interrupt it. Ask whether those interruptions sound external, like events breaking into a texture, or internal, like ideas that the figuration has been trying to articulate.

Second, track **vertical weight**. When the chorale arrives, the work's physical scale changes. The piano's resonance matters: bass, middle-register filling, and upper-voice projection now create a sonority that feels architectural. This is where the piece can become muddy if heard only as "organ-like." The chorale must be voiced as harmony with direction, not as generalized grandeur.

Third, track **recognition in the fugue**. Do not reduce cyclic listening to a theme-spotting game. Recognition in Franck is useful only when it changes the present. When an earlier idea returns inside the fugue, ask: has the tempo-world changed? Is the material now chordal, linear, or contrapuntal? Does the harmony make it more stable or more remote? Is it being remembered, argued with, or fulfilled?

On a second listen with the score, mark the three section thresholds: the chorale entry around measure 58, the *Poco allegro* transition around measure 116, and the fugue at measure 157. Then follow how the later pages thicken. The aim is not to label every motive. The aim is to hear why the final major-mode release requires so much accumulated material.

## Cyclic harmony

Franck's cyclic technique is often described as thematic recurrence, and that is true as far as it goes. But in this piece the deeper cyclic force is harmonic. The work begins and ends around B, yet the path between B minor and B major is not a straightforward minor-to-major conversion. It wanders through chromatic regions, remote color, and enharmonic pressure. The chorale's harmonic world feels distant enough to interrupt the prelude, but related enough to be claimed by the fugue later.

This is why "cyclic harmony" is a better phrase than "same theme comes back." In a simple recall model, a theme returns and the listener recognizes it. In Franck's model, harmonic behavior also returns: chromatic side-slips, intensified dominant preparation, bass foundations that seem to turn the room under the melody, and cadential goals that arrive late. A recurring idea can therefore sound transformed even when its contour is recognizable, because the harmonic space around it has changed.

The final B major matters because it converts cyclic memory into tonal consequence. If the ending were only louder or more contrapuntally crowded, the work would close by accumulation. Instead, it closes by re-hearing the initial B-minor world from a changed standpoint. The major ending does not erase the prelude's anxiety or the chorale's struggle. It makes them retrospectively necessary.

This procedure links Franck to the wider late-Romantic problem of large form after Beethoven. The piece does not rely on sonata exposition-development-recapitulation. It relies on memory, transformation, harmonic return, and final reinterpretation. In that sense it belongs beside [[Liszt Sonata in B minor]], [[Brahms Handel Variations Op. 24]], and the cyclic thinking of [[Franck Violin Sonata in A major]], even though each solves large form differently.

## Keyboard texture

The work's keyboard writing is often called organ-influenced, and Henle's product page reasonably notes Franck's organ roots as an inspiration for his piano output. The danger is that "organ-like" can become a lazy description for anything thick or solemn. The piano writing is more specific.

The prelude uses figuration that depends on attack and decay. Unlike the organ, the piano cannot sustain a tone indefinitely. Franck therefore has to create continuity through movement, resonance, and registral connection. The figuration is not only decorative; it compensates for decay while also creating unease. A performer must decide how much pedal can bind the surface without dissolving the harmonic turns.

The chorale asks the opposite question. Here the sound can become too massive if every chord is treated as a block. The pianist has to voice top line, bass, and inner pressure so the chorale breathes. Chordal grandeur without phrase direction makes the section static; excessive lyrical shaping can weaken its monumental function. The best listening question is: which voice carries harmonic inevitability at this moment?

The fugue adds a third texture: layered linearity. Fugal writing on the piano is partly an illusion, because the two hands must distribute multiple voices across one percussive instrument. Franck's late-Romantic sonority thickens that challenge. A performer must make entries audible while still projecting a long crescendo of form. Too much clarity can make the fugue dry; too much resonance can make the counterpoint ceremonial rather than active.

This is why the piece is a useful partner to [[Pedaling evidence in Romantic scores]]. Pedal is not a wash added after the analysis. It is one of the ways the form becomes audible. In the prelude, pedal can connect figuration and harmonic atmosphere. In the chorale, it can sustain vertical resonance while risking blur. In the fugue, it must help line and architecture coexist. Each section asks for a different kind of resonance, and those differences are part of the form.

## Interpretive alternatives

**Spiritual monument or dramatic argument.** One performance may emphasize the work's solemnity: broad chorale, glowing resonance, and a final major apotheosis. Another may emphasize conflict: nervous prelude, strained chorale, and fugue as hard-won reconstruction. Both readings can fit the score. The test is whether the final B major sounds earned rather than pasted on.

**Organ sonority or piano rhetoric.** A pianist can lean toward organ associations through sustained bass, legato chordal projection, and long crescendos. Another can stress the piano's percussive attack, decay, and color changes. The first makes the sacred and architectural layer clear; the second prevents the work from becoming an imitation of another instrument.

**Fugue as discipline or fugue as release.** Some performances make the fugue a tightening of control after the prelude and chorale. Others make it the moment when the music finally gains enough motion to speak fully. The difference is audible in tempo relation, articulation of entries, and treatment of episodes. A controlled fugue can clarify structure; a more surging fugue can clarify necessity.

**Cyclic recall underlined or absorbed.** A performer may bring back earlier ideas with unmistakable voicing and timing, making the cyclic design easy to hear. Another may absorb the recalls into the fugue's momentum so recognition emerges gradually. The first helps orientation; the second may better preserve the sense that memory is part of process rather than a set of signposts.

## Why it matters

*Prélude, Choral et Fugue* matters because it shows how late-Romantic piano music could inherit old forms without restoring old listening habits. Franck does not simply revive Bachian genres. He turns them into a continuous psychological and harmonic span. The prelude is no longer only before the real work. The chorale is no longer a stable devotional center. The fugue is no longer only contrapuntal display. Each title becomes a phase in one act of cyclic recollection.

For Andrew's map, the work also clarifies a French late-Romantic path that is not reducible to salon miniature, Lisztian virtuosity, or German sonata inheritance. Franck was a formidable keyboard musician and organist, but this piece is not merely organ thinking exported to piano. Its pianism depends on the contradiction between aspiration to sustained, architectural resonance and the piano's actual mechanism of attack, decay, and color. That contradiction is one reason the piece feels large without using conventional sonata scaffolding.

It also helps make "cyclic form" less vague. In [[The Romantic piano cycle]], cyclic hearing can mean order across pieces, recurring frames, or return across a sequence. In Franck, cyclic form means a single span in which themes, harmonic routes, tempo-worlds, and textures remember one another. The final return is not a scrapbook of earlier motives. It is a reclassification of what the earlier material was for.

Finally, the work prepares comparisons outward. With [[Franck Violin Sonata in A major]], it shares the habit of making later music reinterpret earlier music. With [[Liszt Sonata in B minor]], it shares the problem of creating one large form from transformation rather than movement separation. With [[Brahms Intermezzo Op. 118 No. 2]], it shares the late-Romantic idea that return is never neutral. The Franck difference is the explicit transformation of historical rhetoric: prelude, chorale, and fugue become modern because they are made to remember.
