A piano score becomes usable evidence only after autograph, copy, proof, authorized print, later impression, critical edition, and performer additions are kept distinct.

Up: [[Romantic piano repertoire]]

## A source ladder

An autograph may precede revision; an authorized copy may transmit corrections; a proof may contain composer changes; parallel first editions may descend from different exemplars; a later impression may silently repair or introduce errors. A modern critical edition argues among these witnesses through an editorial policy and report. A performer edition adds fingering, pedaling, dynamics, phrasing, or redistribution that may document reception but is not automatically composer text.

The ladder is not a simple ranking. Authority depends on the question and the work's transmission. For pitch or rhythm, compare stemmata and corrections; for pedaling or fingering, ask whether a mark is autograph, publisher, editor, or performer; for listening history, a famous performer edition may be primary evidence of later practice even when textually secondary.

## Place in the map

Use this method before [[Reading Chopin early editions]] and work-specific edition laboratories. It prevents “Urtext” from meaning unmediated intention and gives every audible variant a traceable textual layer.
