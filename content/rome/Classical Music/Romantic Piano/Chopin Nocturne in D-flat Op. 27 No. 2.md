Chopin's D-flat Nocturne, Op. 27 No. 2, turns a bel canto surface into large form by making ornament, bass continuity, registral height, and delayed cadence answer one another across the whole piece.

Up: [[Romantic piano repertoire]]

## Score and edition identity

The work is the second of the two Nocturnes, Op. 27, for solo piano: No. 1 in C-sharp minor and No. 2 in D-flat major. IMSLP's work record gives the pair as Op. 27, IFC 67, with No. 2 marked *Lento sostenuto* in D-flat major, composed in 1836, first published in 1837, and dedicated to Countess d'Appony. Chopin Online's Annotated Catalogue of Chopin's First Editions lists the early publication network rather than a single transparent "first edition": a French first edition from Maurice Schlesinger, a German first edition from Breitkopf & Hartel, and an English first edition from Wessel, followed by later reprints and separate issues of No. 2. IMSLP's score listing gives the French first edition as Paris: Maurice Schlesinger, plate M.S. 1935, ca. 1836, and the German first edition as Leipzig: Breitkopf und Hartel, plate 5666, ca. 1836. Those catalogue differences are not a reason to invent a clean date; they are a warning that Chopin source history often lives in a network of national editions, proofs, reprints, and corrected copies.

The score map below follows the bar numbering in the National Edition of the Works of Fryderyk Chopin, Series A, Vol. 5, *Nocturnes*, edited by Jan Ekier and Pawel Kaminski, PWM 9235, available through IMSLP as a public-domain score excerpt. I inspected the PDF pages for Op. 27 No. 2 directly: the piece begins on the excerpt's page 54 with the marking *Lento sostenuto* and ends on page 59 at bar 77. The National Edition also signals places where source or performance commentary matters: alternate readings labelled *ossia*, footnotes pointing to source commentary or performance commentary, and a note at bar 60 that an accent in a pupil's copy refers to a specific pitch. This article does not reproduce that commentary and does not adjudicate every variant. It uses the visible score to establish the listening path and treats detailed variants as open questions.

A facsimile of the autograph Stichvorlage for Op. 27 No. 2 exists in the National Library in Warsaw and is sold by the National Fryderyk Chopin Institute with scholarly commentary by Mieczyslaw Tomaszewski. I did not inspect that facsimile here. Its existence matters because the safest article distinguishes four things: score fact in the inspected National Edition excerpt, first-edition metadata in Chopin Online and IMSLP, analytical inference from the musical process, and performer choice in realization.

## Why this belongs after Field

[[John Field and the nocturne before Chopin]] teaches the basic nocturne premise: a singing treble line over a resonant, repeating accompaniment field. Chopin inherits that premise but makes it more structurally demanding. In Field, a listener can often track melody, return, and ornament as a lyric design in compact form. In Op. 27 No. 2, the ornament is not just decoration added to a stable song. It becomes a way of delaying arrival, changing register, thickening texture, and making a final cadence feel earned.

The left hand is the first clue. The piece begins before the melody, with a continuous broken-chord figure in 6/8. That figure is not a neutral background. It creates a bar-by-bar current whose bass notes and inner tones let the right hand float without losing harmonic direction. If the listener hears only "rippling accompaniment," the piece can become beautiful but shapeless. If the listener hears bass support, inner harmonic color, and melody as different layers moving at different speeds, the form becomes much clearer.

The right hand is the second clue. Its opening melody enters *dolce*, with a long-breathed vocal shape. But almost immediately the melody is subject to pressure: small turns, chromatic inflections, accented notes, fioritura, and later high-register expansions. The surface can sound improvisatory; the score shows that the improvisatory impression is organized. Returns are not simple repeats. They ask whether the listener can remember the opening line while hearing it revoiced, heightened, and redirected.

## First-listen path

On a first hearing, do not try to follow every ornamental note. Use three passes.

First, follow the left hand. It begins alone and keeps moving through nearly the whole piece. Hear the regularity of the broken chords before attending to the melody. The useful question is not whether the accompaniment is "soft," but whether it gives the music a harmonic floor. When the bass darkens or the register changes, the vocal line above it changes meaning.

Second, follow the right-hand long notes, ignoring the fastest notes as much as possible. This exposes a melodic skeleton. In bars 1-10 the line seems spacious and songlike. Around bars 14-18 the line intensifies through repeated and decorated gestures. At bars 26-31, the opening world returns at a very soft dynamic, but it does not feel like the beginning again because the ear has already heard interruption and pressure.

Third, listen for the big registral events. The work's drama is not a sonata-like contrast of themes. It is the gradual conversion of cantabile into height, brilliance, and cadence. The long run at bars 52-53, the increasingly forceful writing through bars 54-60, the high answers around bars 62-69, and the final dissolving ascent in bars 75-76 all matter because they stretch the nocturne's song premise toward the edge of piano figuration.

## Score map

### Bars 1-9: the nocturne machine starts before the singer

The opening bar belongs to the left hand. It establishes D-flat major, 6/8, a soft dynamic, and the repeating arpeggiated field that will support the piece. The right hand enters in bar 2 with a long appoggiatura-like vocal gesture marked *dolce*. This order matters: the singer seems to emerge from resonance rather than command it from outside.

The first phrase is not harmonically inert. The left hand's broken figure repeats its physical pattern while the right hand leans on dissonance and release. At bars 4-6, the melody reaches and relaxes; at bars 7-9, ornament and a small registral lift make the phrase more active. The National Edition prints a footnote at the opening for another reading of a variant, and a second footnote around the early hairpin area notes a source of uncertain authenticity. For a first listener, those variants are less important than the principle they expose: even the apparently obvious opening belongs to a source history, not to an abstract "Chopin sound."

### Bars 10-18: lyricism becomes pressure

The *espressivo* marking at bar 10 coincides with a more insistent right-hand profile. The melody thickens into two-note sonorities and leans harder on accented tones. Bars 12-13 continue the process with increasingly animated decoration, and bars 14-17 bring the right hand into more florid turns over the same underlying 6/8 current. The left hand keeps the nocturne frame in place while the treble becomes less content to remain a plain song.

Bar 18 is a first local arrival: *con forza*, forte, and a more forceful vertical sonority. It should not be treated as the climax of the whole piece. It is a local stress that proves the opening lyric world can generate force without abandoning its accompaniment. If a performance begins too heavily, this bar has little structural work left to do. If the opening is too vaporous, bar 18 may sound like an arbitrary interruption. The score asks for continuity and contrast together.

### Bars 19-25: aftershock and withdrawal

The passage after bar 18 is a controlled aftershock. The music falls back to softer dynamics, but it does not simply restart. Bars 20-22 include an *ossia* indication and *sempre legatiss.* in the National Edition, then the music turns through chromatic coloring toward a quiet landing. Bars 23-25 narrow the activity and prepare the return.

This is a good place to separate score fact from analytical inference. Score fact: the left hand continues broken chords; the right hand carries short expressive figures; the notation includes soft dynamics, legato indications, and a *ritenuto* into bar 26. Analytical inference: the passage functions as a controlled release after the local force at bar 18, so that the return at bar 26 can sound like memory rather than repetition.

### Bars 26-37: return as ornamented memory

Bar 26 restores the opening material *a tempo* and *pp*. The return is recognizable by melody, accompaniment, and key, but Chopin immediately complicates it. Bars 29-31 use written-out ornamental alternatives and treble turns that make the melody feel more remembered than merely repeated. Bars 32-37 become increasingly decorative, with *leggieriss.* and *dolce* markings around the passagework.

This is where the piece teaches an important Chopin habit. Ornament can clarify form. A listener who hears fast notes only as surface prettiness misses their function: they mark return under changed conditions. The skeleton of the opening is still present, but it is now surrounded by more agile filigree. In vocal terms, the singer does not simply sing the stanza again; the line remembers itself through embellishment.

### Bars 38-48: the center grows from the same material

Bars 38-40 continue the ornamental expansion and move toward stronger chromatic pressure. The bass remains legible, but the harmonic field is less placid than at the opening. Bars 42-45 use repeated stresses, crescendo, and forceful accents; by bar 46 the music reaches *ff* and a broad return of the opening melodic contour at much greater intensity.

This span is not a contrasting "middle section" in the simple Field-nocturne sense. It is the opening idea under magnification. The same singing-over-arpeggiation premise now supports louder dynamics, sharper chromaticism, and broader register. The listener should ask: what has changed enough to make the familiar shape sound expansive rather than merely louder? The answer lies in accumulated ornament, harmonic pressure, and the fact that the right hand has already crossed into more brilliant pianistic territory.

### Bars 49-60: cadenza-like display becomes cadence preparation

Bar 49 begins another strong statement, and bars 50-53 include one of the work's most obvious cadenza-like gestures: a long, delicate, high run marked by the National Edition with performance-commentary cues. It is tempting to hear this as a free sparkle break. In the form, it is more precise. The run lifts the nocturne line out of ordinary song and suspends the listener before the next forceful passage.

Bars 54-60 answer with *con anima*, *con forza*, *appassionato*, crescendos, accents, and thicker figuration. The score's visible signs make the rhetorical escalation plain. Yet the accompaniment pattern still matters. Even in the more impassioned writing, the left-hand current prevents the music from becoming a disconnected fantasy. It remains a nocturne whose song has expanded into a more dramatic register.

### Bars 61-77: dissolution, cadence, and after-image

The last span gradually converts force into radiance and then into disappearance. Bars 62-64 are marked *dolciss.* and then diminish; bars 65-69 use high answers and octave indications over a continuing left-hand field; bars 70-73 bring *calando* and *smorzando*, allowing the music to cool without losing the D-flat frame.

Bars 74-77 provide the final cadence and after-image. The left hand keeps the broken-chord support almost to the end. The right hand's last decorative ascent at bars 75-76 is marked *dolcissimo* and *diminuendo*, not triumphant. The final bars therefore do not merely "end prettily." They solve the piece's central problem: how can an ornamented vocal line, after so much registral and dynamic expansion, return to stillness without sounding emptied out? Chopin's answer is to let figuration become vanishing motion.

## Interpretive alternatives

### Song-first versus architecture-first

A song-first reading emphasizes the right hand's bel canto line and treats the left hand as a resonant support. This can make the piece immediately intelligible and lyrical. Its risk is shapelessness if every phrase is equally beautiful. An architecture-first reading gives more attention to bass direction, repeated accompaniment, and large dynamic spans. Its risk is dryness if the melody becomes a structural diagram rather than a singing line. The score needs both: the melody must sing, and the accompaniment must explain why the song lasts seventy-seven bars.

### Ornament as shimmer versus ornament as syntax

One performer may make the fioritura sound like spontaneous vocal embellishment, integrated into a flexible line. Another may articulate the decorative groups so that their rhythmic placement and registral direction are more explicit. The first choice protects the nocturne illusion; the second clarifies how return and escalation are built. Neither is automatically correct. The listener can evaluate either by asking whether the melodic skeleton remains audible and whether the ornaments point toward arrivals rather than floating outside the form.

### Pedal as resonance versus pedal as transparency

The National Edition excerpt prints frequent pedal indications, but the sound cannot be inferred mechanically from the signs. A modern grand, a historical piano, a small room, and a resonant hall will all change how much overlap is possible before the bass loses clarity. One approach uses generous resonance to make the left-hand field envelop the melody. Another clears more often to protect chromatic turns and bass changes. The useful standard is not "more pedal" or "less pedal." It is whether the left hand remains harmonic support rather than fog, and whether the right hand can float without becoming detached from the bass.

### Tempo breadth versus phrase continuity

The marking *Lento sostenuto* invites breadth, but the 6/8 left-hand motion needs continuity. Too fast a tempo can make the ornament merely decorative and reduce the nocturne's suspended time. Too slow a tempo can turn the accompaniment into isolated harmonies and make the long form sag. Rubato can happen locally at cadential leaning points, high registral events, and phrase returns, but the listener should still feel the work's long current. This is a work-specific case for [[Rubato as several historical practices]]: nocturne flexibility is not the same problem as mazurka pulse or Brahmsian inner-voice timing.

## Place in the map

This note belongs after [[John Field and the nocturne before Chopin]], [[Schubert Impromptu in G-flat D 899 No. 3]], [[Romantic phrase rhythm]], [[Pedaling evidence in Romantic scores]], and [[Urtext does not mean interpretation-free]]. It pairs naturally with [[Chopin Prelude in E minor Op. 28 No. 4]] because both pieces make continuity into form, though by opposite means: the prelude uses repeated chords and descending inner pressure, while the nocturne uses flowing arpeggiation and ornamental return. It also points toward [[Chopin Ballade No. 1 Op. 23]], where Chopin's large-form rhetoric becomes more narrative and dramatic, and toward [[Liszt Consolation No. 3]], where another composer tests nocturne-like singing without becoming Chopin.

For Andrew's Romantic piano map, the reusable skill is hearing ornament as structure. In this nocturne, fast notes are not a decorative skin over a tune. They are part of how the work remembers, intensifies, delays, and releases its opening premise.
