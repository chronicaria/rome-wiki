John Field made the piano nocturne into a durable way of hearing melody, accompaniment, resonance, and suspended time; understanding that achievement requires treating his pieces as a varied Russian-era repertory, not merely as rough drafts for Chopin.

Up: [[Romantic piano repertoire]]

## The claim, stated carefully

John Field (1782–1837) did not invent night music, lyrical keyboard music, broken-chord accompaniment, or the word *nocturne*. Eighteenth-century *notturni* and related serenades already carried nocturnal associations, often in music for ensembles. Operatic and domestic repertories already paired a singing upper line with accompaniment. What Field did was more specific and historically decisive: during his long career in the Russian Empire, he established the *nocturne* as a title and a recognizable poetic premise for a solo-piano piece in which touch, register, ornament, harmony, and pedal-like resonance make an apparently simple melody inhabit its own acoustic space.

Even that formulation needs two cautions. First, Field's nocturnes do not all obey one recipe. Some are compact cantabile pieces; others approach romance, pastorale, rondo, or concerto slow movement. Second, their familiar numbering is not a stable authorial sequence. Cecil Hopkinson's bibliographical-thematic catalogue assigns the H numbers now used to identify Field's works, while nineteenth-century collected editions selected, reordered, renamed, and revised the corpus. “Nocturne No. 1” is convenient, but **H 24** is the firmer identifier for the E-flat piece.

This article therefore uses “before Chopin” chronologically, not hierarchically. Field's first published nocturnes preceded Chopin's mature nocturnes and helped make their genre intelligible. But Field's music also answered his own professional world: the pianos, salons, theatres, aristocratic estates, publishers, pupils, and listening cultures of St Petersburg and Moscow. Chopin is one branch of the nocturne's history, not the purpose toward which Field unknowingly worked.

## A Dublin and London pianist becomes a Russian musician

Field was born in Dublin on 26 July 1782 into a musical family and moved with his family to London in 1793. There he studied with Muzio Clementi, whose roles as pianist, composer, teacher, publisher, and piano manufacturer were intertwined. Field's apprenticeship was therefore not only compositional. It joined keyboard technique to the commercial and material networks through which pianos and printed music circulated.

In 1802 Field travelled with Clementi across Europe, reaching St Petersburg in 1803. He remained in the Russian Empire for most of his adult life, first chiefly in St Petersburg and later in Moscow, building a career as virtuoso, teacher, and composer. He made an extended western European tour from 1831, became seriously ill in Italy, and eventually returned to Moscow, where he died on 23 January 1837.

That geography matters to the nocturnes. W. Dean Sutcliffe argues that Field's genre should be understood as a Russia-based idiom and examined through the landscapes—actual, social, and subsequently imagined—of his career. “Irish composer” and “London-trained pianist” are true, but incomplete. Field's musical identity was also formed by decades of performing and teaching in Russian cities and country residences. Later reception sometimes turned Russia into picturesque atmosphere around the nocturnes; responsible history instead asks what Field's location enabled: patrons, instruments, performance occasions, circulation routes, and a distinctive public identity as “John Field of St Petersburg” or Moscow.

Field's concertos were central to his public career, and the nocturne should not be isolated from them. A concerto slow movement can suspend virtuoso display and foreground a cantabile line, while its surrounding movements establish the pianist's brilliance. The self-contained nocturne transfers that concentrated lyric situation into a smaller frame. Conversely, some pieces later counted among the nocturnes derive from, resemble, or circulate alongside other genres. The boundary was permeable, not a taxonomic wall.

## What Field's texture actually does

The textbook description—right-hand melody over left-hand arpeggios—is accurate but insufficient. Many pieces have that disposition; relatively few create Field's particular equilibrium. Four interacting mechanisms are more revealing.

**The melody behaves like a performed voice.** It often enters plainly, with room around its first notes, then acquires turns, appoggiaturas, runs, or filigree on repetition. Decoration is not merely applied sparkle. It changes how a stable melodic identity inhabits time: a return may be recognized beneath a newly flexible surface, as if a singer remembers and intensifies a line.

**The accompaniment converts harmony into motion.** Broken chords distribute chord tones across register rather than striking them as a block. This keeps rhythmic activity continuous without forcing the bass into a competing theme. Wide spans can separate bass foundation from inner filling, leaving an acoustic corridor for the melody. The hand's recurring pattern also creates expectation; a change of bass, spacing, or harmonic rhythm becomes audible even when the surface remains gentle.

**Sustain is composed through notation and touch.** A piano tone decays after attack. A legato melody must therefore be made to seem continuous through finger connection, overlapping resonance, balance, and, where appropriate, pedal. Field's texture turns that limitation into poetry: melody notes emerge as distinct attacks yet are perceived as one line above a softly renewing harmonic field. On an early nineteenth-century piano, quicker decay and differentiated registers could make the layers unusually clear. On a modern concert grand, generous sustain can enrich the sonority but also obscure bass changes and melodic articulation. See [[The nineteenth-century piano as a changing instrument]].

**Form proceeds by return, intensification, and release.** The pieces are not formless moods. A melody is stated, diverted or darkened, decorated, brought back, and closed. The scale may be small, but the listener must retain the opening well enough to hear what a later return preserves and what it alters. The nocturne's suspended time is thus made, not merely slow: regular accompaniment and lyrical recurrence allow local harmonic events, delayed resolutions, and ornamental expansions to feel disproportionately significant.

These mechanisms also explain why Field's writing can be difficult without looking spectacular. The pianist must keep the accompaniment alive but subordinate, voice repeated or embellished melody notes, time dissonances without distorting the span, and clear harmony with judicious resonance. A mechanically even left hand and an indiscriminately loud right hand destroy the premise. So does treating every ornament as an interruption. The ornament must sound generated by the line.

## Score laboratory I: E-flat major, H 24

The commonly numbered Nocturne No. 1 in E-flat major, H 24, is the best first laboratory, provided the edition is named. The score consulted here is the public-domain 1859 Schuberth edition prepared by Franz Liszt, available through IMSLP. It is not an autograph or first edition and therefore cannot silently stand for Field's earliest text. It is useful as a documented nineteenth-century reception witness.

Its opening prints *Molto moderato* in 12/8. After an upbeat, the melody enters on E-flat and immediately extends through long note values while the bass staff articulates flowing groups of six eighth notes. In the first full measure, the accompaniment repeatedly outlines E-flat harmony across a wide span: low bass, inner chord tones, and returning figures create motion beneath a melody that initially seems almost stationary. The next melodic gesture introduces a small grace note and descending notes. Before looking for “night,” notice the physical design: sustained treble values against many softer bass attacks.

A useful score-reading exercise is to mark only bass changes for the opening phrase. Then mark the melody's non-chord tones and resolutions. The accompaniment's continuity can make harmony feel static, but the bass map reveals direction; the melody map reveals where expressive pressure is concentrated. Finally, observe where the upper line becomes more active. The piece's rhetoric depends on the contrast between an initially spacious song and increasingly mobile decoration, not on an unchanging wash of prettiness.

For listening, use landmarks independent of recording timings:

1. Hear the upbeat and first long E-flat as an entrance, not as the first item in an accompaniment pattern.
2. Follow the lowest note of each broken-chord group. If that bass line disappears, the harmony will seem vague.
3. At the first decorated return, ask whether the original melodic skeleton remains audible.
4. In the closing span, listen for the point at which motion ceases to promise a new departure and begins to confirm arrival.

The goal is not to demand one tempo or pedaling scheme. It is to test whether a realization preserves three layers: bass direction, inner harmonic motion, and vocal projection. Pedal can help bind those layers, but a pedal change that lags behind a bass change may turn harmonic resonance into blur. A period instrument may invite different durations and overlaps than a modern grand; neither result should be treated as neutral.

## Score laboratory II: C minor, H 25, and the problem of versions

H 25 exposes why a single “complete nocturnes” volume can mislead. IMSLP's work record reports that the piece appeared in 1814 in two versions: as the second of *Three Nocturnes* and as the third of *Three Romances*. In the Romance version, bars 40–48 contain decorative passagework; in the Nocturne version, the corresponding place restates the theme more simply. That is not a trivial engraving variant. It changes the balance between recollection and display.

The opening marking, *Moderato e molto espressivo*, already resists the notion that a nocturne must be uniformly dreamy. C minor gives the lyrical premise a different weight from H 24's E-flat ease. When approaching bars 40–48, compare both textual possibilities before deciding what the piece “is.” A simple thematic return can make memory and formal closure central. The decorative alternative makes transformation and pianistic presence more conspicuous. Neither should be called Field's unique final intention merely because it appears in a convenient later edition.

This case supplies a reusable method:

- identify the piece by H number and key, not number alone;
- record the publisher, editor, date, plate, and scan used;
- distinguish an early title or version from a later collected-edition label;
- compare the exact passage, not just title pages;
- describe the audible consequence before arguing which witness has priority.

The method connects Field directly to [[Piano source layers from autograph to performer edition]]. It also protects performance history from false cleanliness. A later editor's conflated or revised text may have shaped generations of hearing even when it is not the best evidence for an earliest state.

## The corpus is plural

H 24 and H 25 should not become a new stereotype. The A-flat H 26, A-major H 36, B-flat H 37, F-major H 40, C-major H 45, and E-minor H 46 expand the field of comparison. Later works and pieces absorbed into nocturne collections broaden it further. H 59 in D minor, first published in Paris in 1834 with the subtitle *Lied ohne Worte* (“song without words”), makes the song analogy explicit. The score record also notes that this first edition appeared with a solo-piano version of the second movement of Field's Seventh Piano Concerto. Here genre history is visible on the publication surface: nocturne, song without words, and concerto-derived lyric movement meet rather than occupy sealed boxes.

Other labels complicate the corpus: *romance*, *rêverie*, *pastorale*, “cradle song,” and characteristic titles. H 30, for example, began as a Romance in E-flat and was reportedly labelled a nocturne by publishers only from the 1830s. Such cases show that “the Field nocturne” was partly constructed by republication. A collected edition offers repertory, but it also offers an editor's map of repertory.

For a first survey, listen or read in contrasting pairs rather than straight numerical order: major against minor; early compact piece against later expansive one; plain return against decorated return; work originally called nocturne against one later recruited under the title. Ask the same questions each time:

- Does melody arrive immediately, or is it prepared?
- Is accompaniment a stable pattern, a changing figuration, or an intermittent support?
- Where does register separate the layers?
- Does ornament intensify a return, connect phrases, or create a quasi-improvised episode?
- Is the close a simple cadence, a recollection, or a display of passagework?
- Which of those observations belongs to Field's recoverable text, and which to the inspected edition?

Answers will show a family resemblance rather than a fixed form.

## Liszt's Field and the danger of transparent editions

Franz Liszt's 1859 Schuberth collection of eight nocturnes is historically valuable because it belongs to the genre's nineteenth-century reception and because Liszt wrote appreciatively about Field. It is also later than Field and editorially active. A Liszt edition cannot be treated as a transparent photograph of an early Russian print. Fingerings, articulation, dynamics, titles, order, and even musical substance must be checked against earlier witnesses.

The issue is not that later editions are “bad.” They answer different questions. An early authorized print may help reconstruct publication history. Liszt's edition helps reconstruct how a major later pianist-composer presented Field to another public. A modern performing edition may solve page turns or offer practical fingering. A critical edition may collate sources and explain its choices. No one source is best for every question.

Field's fluid corpus makes this distinction especially consequential. If an edition silently mixes versions, the listener may attribute later editorial drama to Field's original conception. If a historian dismisses all later changes, however, they lose evidence of how the nocturne was transmitted and heard. The right response is layered attribution.

## Field and Chopin without a ladder of progress

Chopin knew a musical world in which Field's nocturne was already a legible genre. The shared resources are easy to hear and see: cantabile melody, broken accompaniment, ornamental renewal, expressive dissonance, flexible phrase, and a compact single-movement frame. Chopin's nocturnes greatly expanded the genre's harmonic, contrapuntal, formal, and dramatic range. Yet “expanded” should not become “completed.”

The ladder model—Field invents a primitive shell, Chopin perfects it—fails in three ways. It makes value depend on later complexity; it erases the Russian professional setting in which Field's idiom functioned; and it teaches listeners to search Field only for embryonic Chopin. Field's relative restraint can instead be heard as a positive allocation of attention. When accompaniment remains regular, a single altered harmony or delayed melodic note can carry unusual force. When the opening tune is plain, ornamented recall can register as memory rather than virtuoso conquest. The narrower frame is not necessarily an inability to imagine a broader one.

A better comparison asks what each piece requires its listener to retain. Compare Field H 24 with a Chopin nocturne only after mapping each separately: bass path, melodic skeleton, dissonances, returns, climactic register, coda. Then compare functions, not isolated similarities. Does ornament conceal or clarify return? Does the middle create genuine opposition or deepen one affect? Does the bass repeat, sequence, or become contrapuntal? This method allows influence without reducing either composer to a bundle of fingerprints.

It also places Field within a larger pre- and early-Romantic ecology. The sung style, operatic ornament, domestic piano market, improvised prelude, romance, concerto slow movement, and changing piano all contributed to the nocturne's possibility. Historical originality is often a persuasive recombination that becomes nameable and repeatable. Field's achievement was to make this texture-function-title complex available as a genre other musicians and publishers could recognize.

## A practical first study session

Begin with the Liszt-edited scan of H 24 because its source identity is explicit and its opening texture is legible. Do not begin by playing. On paper, circle melody attacks through the first phrase, underline bass notes, and bracket each complete accompaniment unit. Play the melody alone, then the bass alone, then the accompaniment without melody. Only then combine them. The aim is to discover that “left-hand arpeggios” contain a bass line plus inner harmonic renewal.

Next, play the opening on the shallowest workable pedal. Add resonance only where it preserves bass change. Record no claims about “Field's pedal” unless the consulted source prints pedal indications and their provenance has been checked. Instead describe the experiment: on this instrument and in this room, a particular pedal depth or change point preserved or obscured the layers.

Then compare H 25 around bars 40–48 in the Nocturne and Romance versions. If both scans are unavailable, do not substitute a verbal report for comparison; mark the task as pending. Once both are present, reduce each passage to melodic skeleton and bass. The exercise reveals how a textual variant changes form, not merely difficulty.

Finally, sample H 59 to hear or read how the later corpus intersects with “song without words.” Resist a single evolutionary sequence. The point is to leave with three distinct Field solutions and an awareness of the editions mediating them.

## What can and cannot be verified here

The biographical frame and Russia-centered interpretation are supported by modern scholarship and institutional work records. The concrete opening observations for H 24 derive from direct inspection of the named Liszt score. The H 25 bars 40–48 version claim and H 59 publication data are taken from IMSLP work records, which identify editions and scans but are collaboratively maintained catalogue pages rather than critical reports. They are strong leads and are stated with attribution, not elevated into a complete stemma.

This article does **not** claim to have established Field's autograph text, a definitive count of nocturnes, or the authority of every familiar number. Hopkinson's catalogue remains the basic bibliographical reference, while Robin Langley's modern edition and critical work should be consulted for a source-by-source performing text. Nor does this article infer performance practice from an unheard recording. Its listening landmarks are score-based prompts that can be applied to any identified performance.
