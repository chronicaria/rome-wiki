Liszt's third Consolation shows that Romantic virtuosity can mean sustained singing control: the difficulty is not speed, but keeping melody, inner resonance, and harmonic delay alive inside apparent simplicity.

Up: [[Romantic piano repertoire]]

## Identity and the text used here

The familiar third Consolation is the *Lento placido* in D-flat major from the second version of Franz Liszt's *Consolations*, S. 172, published by Breitkopf & Härtel in 1850. Henle's edition history is useful because it warns against treating "the Consolations" as a single stable object from the start. Liszt began work on a cycle in the 1840s, had an earlier version engraved but not printed, and then replaced and simplified material for the better-known 1850 version. The third piece in S. 172 is therefore not merely one item in a sentimental album; it is part of Liszt's revision of a cycle toward a more concentrated, approachable, songful pianism.

The access score for this guide is the public-domain S. 172 material available through IMSLP, checked against the basic version history reported by Henle. This note does not make critical-edition claims about variants. It treats the piece as a listening and score-reading entry into Liszt: D-flat major, *Lento placido*, a singing upper line, a rocking or arpeggiated support, and a long demand for balance. When a modern edition differs in fingering, pedal, redistribution, or expressive nuance, those are editorial and performance questions rather than facts established here.

The third Consolation is often compared with Chopin's nocturnes, especially because of its singing melody over flowing accompaniment and its date close to Chopin's death. That comparison can help the ear, but it can also flatten Liszt. This is not Chopin with a different signature. Liszt's problem is how to make a broad, almost devotional song unfold through pianistic resonance while avoiding both theatrical excess and decorative prettiness. The page looks far less spectacular than a Hungarian Rhapsody or Transcendental Etude, but the control of sound is still a virtuoso demand.

## Why this is a Liszt gateway

Liszt's public image can make the wrong first question seem natural: where is the difficulty? In the third Consolation the difficulty is hidden in slowness. The player must sustain a melody that cannot be hammered, keep accompaniment motion alive without turning it into foreground figuration, shape harmonic arrivals that are delayed rather than explosive, and use pedal as a color instrument rather than as a blur. These are all central Romantic piano skills.

That makes the piece a companion to [[Mendelssohn Songs Without Words as a genre]] and [[Chopin Prelude in E minor Op. 28 No. 4]]. Like Mendelssohn, it asks how the piano can sing without text. Like the Chopin prelude, it asks how slow harmonic movement can become form. Unlike both, it belongs to Liszt's special world of sonority: a melody seems suspended above a resonant field, and the listener's attention is drawn to how sound continues after attack.

It also prepares a more mature understanding of [[Virtuosity as musical rhetoric]]. Virtuosity is not only fast octaves, leaps, or public brilliance. It can be the capacity to make one melody note carry weight without breaking the phrase, to voice the top line over moving harmony, or to let a dissonance glow long enough for its resolution to matter. The third Consolation is a study in restrained virtuosity.

## First-listen path

On a first listen, follow the melody and ignore the accompanimental detail at first. Notice how much of the piece depends on a long singing line that must cross bar lines without losing its calm. The melody does not feel conversational in short fragments; it feels like a sustained utterance. The tempo marking *Lento placido* helps define the problem. Slow does not mean inert, and placid does not mean blank. The line needs continuity.

On a second listen, move attention to the left hand and inner figuration. The support is not an accompaniment machine. It supplies pulse, harmonic color, and resonance. If the lower texture is too loud, the melody loses its floating quality. If it is too soft or too pedaled, the harmony becomes a pleasant mist and the phrase loses direction. The listener should hear the lower layer as moving water under a sung line, not as separate counter-melody and not as anonymous filler.

On a third listen, mark the moments when the harmony seems to withhold arrival. D-flat major is the home color, but the piece's expressive force comes from how it delays, shades, and re-approaches that home. A cadence that arrives too plainly can sound salon-like. A cadence that is prepared through breath, harmonic tension, and register can sound consoling in a more serious sense: not cheerful, but steadied.

## The opening texture

The opening teaches the whole piece. A slow melody enters over a pattern that creates resonance before it creates drama. The danger is to treat the accompaniment as a harp-like wash. Liszt's texture is more disciplined than that. The supporting motion sets up a continuous field in which the melody can appear suspended, but the harmonic rhythm still matters. Each change below the melody alters the emotional temperature of the line.

The melody's first phrases should be heard as vocal, but piano-vocal. A singer must breathe; a pianist must make breath through timing, touch, and decay. Too much rubato at every arrival breaks the line into sighs. Too little flexibility turns it into a slow exercise. The useful interpretive question is: where does the phrase need time because the harmony has changed, and where does it need motion because consolation depends on continuing?

This is where [[Romantic phrase rhythm]] becomes practical. The phrase may appear regular, but the listener should ask where the true weight falls. A melodic high point, a chromatic inflection, or a harmonic arrival can pull against the notated bar. The performance should make that hierarchy audible without making the meter irrelevant.

The opening also teaches voicing by subtraction. Nothing in the texture is thick enough to hide a heavy thumb, an over-bright accompaniment note, or a pedal that remains after the harmony has changed. In a brilliant Liszt work, technical strain may be visible as leaps, octaves, or figuration. Here it appears as the need to keep the upper voice present without hardening it. The accompaniment has to sound continuous while each harmony remains clean enough to register. That is a different kind of virtuoso exposure.

For a score-assisted listen, separate three layers: the soprano melody, the moving support pattern, and the bass roots or bass implications that stabilize D-flat major. The hands may not divide those layers neatly. A single hand may carry melody and accompaniment at once, so the player must create instrumental "orchestration" inside one span of keys. The listener can hear this by asking whether the melody still feels sung when the accompaniment changes register or density. If it does, the performance has preserved a line across changing pianistic surfaces.

## Middle intensification

The central span increases intensity without abandoning the piece's basic premise. Liszt does not suddenly reveal a hidden etude. Instead, he asks the same singing texture to carry more pressure. The register may widen, the accompaniment may feel more active, and harmonic color may deepen, but the music remains governed by the need to sustain a continuous line.

For listening, the key is proportion. A pianist can make the middle too dramatic, as if a public Lisztian climax must be imported because the composer is Liszt. That exaggerates the wrong identity. Another pianist can underplay the middle so much that the return has no reason to feel changed. The better question is how the increased pressure alters the meaning of the opening material. Does the melody seem more exposed after the intensification? Does D-flat major feel more earned? Does the accompaniment sound like it has learned to carry more weight?

The middle also tests voicing. Inner notes can become expressive without becoming a second soloist. A single inner motion may explain why the melody feels pulled forward. If every inner note is brought out equally, the texture becomes busy. If none is heard, the harmony becomes static. Liszt's apparent simplicity depends on selective disclosure.

## Return and disappearance

When the opening world returns, the performance should not sound as if nothing has happened. The same basic texture now carries memory. The listener has heard the melody intensify and the harmony travel; the return can therefore be quieter without becoming smaller. In a strong reading, the final span feels less like a repeat than like a re-inhabiting of the opening calm after disturbance.

Pedal is central, but it is not a fixed recipe. Later in Liszt's life, he showed interest in the sostenuto pedal and reportedly discussed adapting passages of this Consolation for Steinway's tone-sustaining pedal. That historical fact should not be retrofitted into every modern performance as a command. It does, however, remind us that Liszt cared about sustained resonance as a compositional problem. On a modern grand, the pianist can preserve bass or inner tones longer than on many earlier instruments, but every extra resonance has a cost. It can support the melody, or it can make harmonic changes vague.

The ending should feel like sound has been purified rather than merely diminished. The melody does not need theatrical farewell. Its task is to let the central pressure settle back into a quieter version of the initial sonority. If the listener can still sense the phrase breathing while the texture thins, the performer has solved the main problem.

This ending is a useful antidote to the idea that Liszt always argues by accumulation. In the third Consolation, the final persuasion comes from restraint. The sound world does not need a grand apotheosis because the piece has never promised one. Its large gesture is the restoration of calm with more memory inside it. That makes it an ideal route into later Liszt as well: the spectacular works and the austere late works share a concern with transformation, but transformation can happen through color, spacing, and resonance instead of through obvious thematic metamorphosis.

## Interpretive alternatives

### Nocturne-like reading

A nocturne-like reading emphasizes the singing upper line and treats the accompaniment as a flowing support. This can be persuasive because the piece genuinely shares a cantabile surface with Chopin's nocturne world. The risk is that Liszt's harmonic pacing and sonority become generic. A good nocturne-like performance must still make the accompaniment's harmonic changes audible and must avoid turning every phrase ending into the same sentimental relaxation.

### Devotional reading

A devotional reading stresses stillness, purity of attack, and long resonance. It fits the title's atmosphere and the broad D-flat calm. The risk is sanctifying the piece into immobility. The music still needs motion. Consolation is not achieved by stopping time; it is achieved by letting time move without panic.

### Bel canto reading

A bel canto reading makes the melody the center of everything: long line, shaped high notes, careful breath, and flexible arrival. The risk is excessive vocal imitation. A piano note decays immediately after attack, so a pianist must create the illusion of sustained tone through connection and voicing. Over-stretching the line can expose the illusion rather than strengthen it. The best bel canto reading uses the piano's decay as part of the phrase.

## Place in the map

This note belongs after [[Mendelssohn Songs Without Words as a genre]], [[Romantic phrase rhythm]], and [[Pedaling evidence in Romantic scores]]. It prepares [[Liszt transcription as analysis]], [[Liszt Sonata in B minor]], and [[Thalberg's three-hand texture]] by showing a non-spectacular Liszt texture first. It also points sideways to [[Chopin Nocturne in D-flat Op. 27 No. 2]] and [[Brahms Intermezzo Op. 118 No. 2]], where the problem of singing line over moving inner life returns in different idioms.

The reusable skill is hearing virtuosity as control of resonance. If the piece sounds easy, listen harder to the spacing between melody, accompaniment, harmony, and pedal. That spacing is where Liszt's craft lives.

It also helps keep the Romantic piano map honest about difficulty. A page can be physically approachable and musically exacting. The third Consolation asks for no circus of keyboard conquest, but it exposes whether the player can listen after every attack. That makes it a better Liszt gateway than many more dazzling works for a listener building analytical habits: the piece slows the ear down until color, voicing, and harmonic delay become the main events.
