Piano rolls and shellac discs both preserve early piano performance evidence, but rolls encode selected actions for mechanical reproduction while shellac records an acoustic event through a fragile sound chain.

Up: [[Romantic piano repertoire]]

## The comparison problem

For [[Romantic piano repertoire]], early media are tempting because they seem to bring nineteenth-century pianism within earshot. Pianists born in the 1830s, 1840s, and 1850s could survive into the age of piano rolls and discs. Later pianists who studied with nineteenth-century teachers recorded Chopin, Schumann, Liszt, Brahms, Grieg, Debussy, Rachmaninoff, and other repertoire on commercial media. These sources can challenge modern habits: hands may not align exactly, phrase rhythm may flex more locally than a modern conservatory norm expects, cadences may be spread, basses may be delayed or anticipated, and written tempo can become a living hierarchy rather than a metronomic grid.

The danger is that "recording" sounds like one category. A shellac disc and a reproducing piano roll are not two imperfect versions of the same evidence. A disc preserves a trace of sound pressure and playback reconstructs that trace through a groove. A roll preserves perforations and control information that cause a player mechanism to make a piano sound again. The disc is mediated acoustic evidence; the roll is mediated action evidence. The difference matters before any stylistic conclusion can be drawn.

This note is a practical bridge between [[Shellac recording and the compression of Romantic performance]], [[How to compare piano recordings at one passage]], and future listening laboratories. It does not report direct inspection of any specific roll, disc, transfer, or playback. It compares the source types from accessible archival and preservation descriptions, then states what each can and cannot support.

The core rule is simple: ask what the artifact actually carries. A piano roll may be strong evidence for note order, punched durations, encoded tempo movement, and some system-specific dynamic or pedal information. It is weaker evidence for the exact sound of the historical piano, room, touch color, resonance, and live acoustic balance. A shellac disc may be strong evidence for an acoustic result at one session and take, especially attack timing and audible synchronization. It is weaker evidence for original tone, exact dynamics, pedal depth, room sound, and unrecorded alternatives. Neither medium proves "how Romantic pianists played" by itself. Each can support smaller claims when its mediation is named.

## What a piano roll preserves

A piano roll is a strip of paper whose perforations direct a player piano. Basic rolls encode notes through holes that pass over a tracker bar. When the mechanism reads the holes, it activates the corresponding notes on a piano. The University of Illinois player-piano guide separates arranged, hand-played, and reproducing rolls. That distinction is not a collector's nicety; it controls the evidence claim.

An arranged or metronomic roll may be closer to a machine-readable arrangement than to a captured performance. The note sequence can be prepared by an arranger, punched by hand or machine, and then shaped by a human operator at the player piano through tempo and dynamic controls. Such a roll may tell us much about commercial arrangement, domestic use, repertory circulation, and the kinds of music imagined for self-playing instruments. It is weak evidence for a named pianist's timing unless a reliable recording process and performer identity are established.

A hand-played roll has a stronger performance claim. It may derive from a pianist playing on a recording piano connected to a perforating process, with roll editors or producers later marking or coding tempo and dynamic instructions. That means the roll may preserve performance-derived timing or at least a performance-derived skeleton. It also means editing, correction, scaling, and production decisions may sit between the pianist and the sold copy. If the article question is "Did this source preserve an exact unedited human event?", a hand-played roll requires caution. If the question is "What kind of tempo flexibility did this commercial source attribute to this performer or style?", the roll may be valuable.

A reproducing roll is the strongest roll category for performance-practice work because it can include extra information for dynamics, articulation, and sometimes pedal or expression effects, depending on the system. Duo-Art, Ampico, Welte-Mignon, and other systems were built around the promise that a famous performer could be mechanically reproduced. Stanford's account of the Denis Condon Collection frames reproducing pianos and rolls as major early twentieth-century evidence for performance history, while also emphasizing preservation, restoration, cataloging, and the need for functioning instruments. The medium is not merely paper; the evidence includes the roll, the reproducing system, the condition and regulation of the player piano, and any modern scanning or digital realization.

This gives rolls one distinctive advantage over discs: they can separate some performance actions from historical sound capture. A roll can cause a modern or restored piano to play note events with encoded timing and expression data. Surface noise, horn response, shellac wear, and groove equalization do not obscure the notes in the same way they obscure a disc. For studying attack order, broad tempo shaping, hand displacement, or repeated rhythmic freedoms, a well-identified roll may reveal patterns that an acoustic disc blurs.

But the same advantage is also the main limitation. The roll does not preserve the original sound wave. It does not preserve the historical instrument's tone as heard in the room. It does not preserve the tactile cause of the resulting sound. It asks a later mechanism to perform encoded instructions. If the player piano is poorly regulated, if the pneumatic system leaks, if the roll has shrunk or torn, if the scan is misaligned, or if a digital realization uses an arbitrary piano model, the result may sound precise while silently importing new distortions.

## What shellac preserves

A shellac disc is a grooved carrier. The Library of Congress National Jukebox describes acoustic recording as a pre-microphone process in which sound and speech were funneled through horns to a diaphragm and stylus that etched sound waves onto a rotating wax disc. The University of Illinois preservation guide describes phonograph records as grooved discs whose modulated information is played back by a stylus, and it identifies shellac-type discs as coarse-groove carriers made from shellac resin plus variable fillers and additives.

The disc's great advantage is that it preserves an acoustic event. However mediated, a performance produced air pressure that moved a recording mechanism. When the source is identified securely, a disc can document one performer, one work or excerpt, one take or issued side, one capture process, and one historical production context. It can show how a pianist actually sounded to the recording apparatus, not how a later mechanism reconstructed instructions.

That is especially useful for ensemble relations, audible articulation, chord spreading, melody-bass asynchrony, attack timing, and the continuous character of a side-length take. Even a restricted acoustic record may show whether a pianist tends to place the melody after the bass in a passage, whether repeated chords are vertical or rolled, whether a return is faster than an opening statement in the same transfer, or whether a cadence receives a local expansion. Electrical shellac recordings after the commercial adoption of microphone-based recording can carry a broader range of piano tone and balance, though still through a disc and transfer chain.

The disc's limitation is that the sound chain is long. The room, piano, recording method, engineer, wax master, metal parts, pressing, shellac compound, wear history, stylus, playback speed, equalization, restoration, and digital format all shape the file a modern listener hears. The preservation literature is clear that shellac discs vary materially and that playback requires appropriate speed, stylus, and equipment. Early discs were not always cut at exactly the later nominal 78 rpm. Groove size varies. Surface contamination, wear, cracks, and breakage can damage both the carrier and the inference.

For Romantic piano, the most fragile shellac claims concern tone and pedaling. An acoustic disc may underrepresent bass, resonance, and soft attacks. A worn transfer may make a pianist seem dry, hard, or unclear. Noise reduction may sharpen intelligibility while shaving off attack or decay. A shellac source may still suggest broad pedaling habits or resonance choices, but it rarely yields exact pedal depth or release timing. "The transfer presents blurred bass resonance" is a safer claim than "the pianist held the pedal through this harmony" unless the pattern is repeatedly audible and supported by other evidence.

## Evidence comparison

The strongest difference is between encoded action and captured sound. A roll tells a machine what to do; a disc stores the result of sound having acted on a recording mechanism. This means a roll can be closer to a performance map while a disc is closer to an acoustic witness.

For **note sequence**, rolls are often strong evidence because note perforations can be inspected visually or digitally. But they may include editorial correction, arrangement, or simplification. Discs are also evidence for note sequence when pitches are audible, but surface noise, speed, register loss, or overloaded textures can obscure details. For checking a textual variant, neither medium outranks a score source unless the question is what this performer or product presented.

For **timing**, both can be powerful. Rolls may preserve punched onset spacing with unusual clarity, especially when scanned. Discs preserve timing as heard in the captured acoustic event. A roll's timing can be distorted by roll speed, editing, tracker response, and reproducing mechanism. A disc's timing can be distorted by transfer speed, wow, side constraints, and restoration. Local relationships within one source are usually safer than absolute metronome claims across sources.

For **dynamics**, reproducing rolls may carry explicit expression coding, but system design and calibration matter. The same perforation language does not automatically mean the same sound on every instrument or modern realization. Shellac preserves dynamic result only through the recording chain. It can show broad contrasts if they survive the medium, but it cannot reliably reveal original decibel range or key velocity. A roll may be better for encoded dynamic intention; a disc may be better for the acoustic result presented by a specific take.

For **tone color**, shellac has the advantage of being a sound record, but that advantage is compromised by capture and transfer. Rolls are weaker evidence for the original tone because the reproduced tone belongs to the playback piano and mechanism. A roll can help compare timing without shellac's surface noise, but it should not be used to claim that a pianist had a certain live sonority unless the playback context is historically controlled and the claim is modest. A disc can support claims about the recorded tone of a transfer; it needs corroboration before becoming a claim about touch.

For **pedaling**, neither is transparent. Some reproducing roll systems encode pedal or sustaining effects, but the resulting resonance depends on the reproducing piano and its regulation. Shellac may make pedal effects audible as harmonic overlap, bass persistence, or release blur, but the acoustic or electrical chain can suppress or exaggerate those effects. Pedaling claims should be phrased as possibilities or source-specific observations unless they are supported by roll coding, audible repetition, score context, and independent documentation.

For **continuity**, shellac sides before tape editing usually preserve a continuous side-length take, though a released record may be one selected take among several. Rolls can be edited more deeply. Stanford's discussion notes that rolls were an early recording medium to allow editing, which is exactly why they are powerful and risky. A corrected roll may preserve an idealized performance product rather than a single run-through. This does not make it worthless; it changes the question from "what happened in one sitting?" to "what performance did this roll system publish?"

For **metadata**, shellac has mature discographic tools such as the Discography of American Historical Recordings, which documents matrixes, companies, dates, issues, and related data for the 78-rpm era. Roll metadata can be more scattered: system, roll number, performer attribution, original recording date, issue date, edited or recut status, and compatible instrument may all need separate confirmation. In both media, a commercial label or online upload title is not enough.

## Media limitations

The first limitation is **category confusion**. "Piano roll" can mean arranged roll, hand-played roll, reproducing roll, recut roll, scan, MIDI conversion, or modern digital rendering. These are different witnesses. "Shellac" can mean acoustic disc, electrical disc, test pressing, commercial pressing, later pressing, worn copy, archival transfer, or processed streaming file. These too are different witnesses.

The second limitation is **selection bias**. The surviving media do not represent all pianists, all repertoire, or all practices. Roll catalogues and disc catalogues were commercial and institutional products. They favored performers, works, durations, systems, markets, and rights situations that companies could sell or preserve. Absence from rolls or discs does not prove absence from concert life. Presence on a roll or disc does not prove centrality to teaching practice.

The third limitation is **mechanical adaptation**. Pianists may have adapted to recording systems. A performer facing an acoustic horn might play with a different balance or choose a work that records well. A roll session might invite the pianist or editor to create an effective reproducing product rather than a documentary field recording. Studio constraints and commercial expectations are part of the evidence, not contamination that can simply be subtracted.

The fourth limitation is **condition and transfer**. Shellac can break, shed, wear, and accumulate noise. Paper rolls can deteriorate, shrink, tear, become brittle, or require conservation before scanning or playback. Stanford's account stresses roll preservation and restoration because a roll plus a dysfunctional player piano does not yield reliable sound. The same is true for discs: a disc plus an unsuitable stylus or undocumented equalization does not yield neutral evidence.

The fifth limitation is **rights and access**. Many early recordings, scans, and transfers are searchable but not downloadable or reusable. Public streaming access does not mean a source is legally or methodologically free for reproduction. Rome notes should cite stable source pages and describe evidence in prose; they should not reproduce copyrighted transfers or extended modern editorial material.

## Romantic-piano implications

Piano rolls are especially valuable for testing claims about Romantic timing. They can preserve local flexibility at a resolution that invites measurement: phrase expansion, contraction, asynchrony, rolled chords, and tempo relations across repeated passages. For repertoire where modern performance has become metrically standardized, roll evidence can remind the listener that earlier pianists may have treated written rhythm as a hierarchy of gesture, harmony, and phrase function. That is relevant to [[Romantic phrase rhythm]] and [[Rubato as several historical practices]].

Shellac discs are especially valuable for testing how timing, articulation, and sound interacted in a recorded acoustic product. A pianist's delay between bass and melody is not just a number; it produces a heard relationship. A cadence is not just longer or shorter; it has attack, decay, resonance, and weight as captured. Shellac therefore keeps analysis anchored in audible consequence, even when its tone evidence is compromised.

The two media become most useful when they check one another. If a pianist's roll and disc of similar repertoire show comparable local asynchronies, the practice becomes harder to dismiss as a medium artifact. If they disagree, the disagreement may teach more than agreement: perhaps the disc session constrained the pianist, the roll was edited, the works invite different treatment, or the transfer/playback chain altered what survives. The task is not to choose one medium as authentic. The task is to identify which question each can answer.

For composer-performer evidence, the stakes are high. A Rachmaninoff roll or disc, for example, would not simply reveal "the composer's intention" in a permanent legal sense. It would document a historically situated performance product by the composer-pianist under specific technological and commercial conditions. That is powerful evidence, but it is still one source layer beside score, editions, letters, teaching reports, reviews, and other performances.

For listening pedagogy, the comparison suggests a rule for future Rome articles: never write "early recordings show" when the evidence is actually one roll type, one disc transfer, or one undocumented upload. Write the medium. Write the object. Write the inspected source. Then write the claim at the scale the source can bear.

## A practical source checklist

For a piano roll, record: roll type; maker and system; roll number; performer attribution; work title and arrangement; recording or issue date if known; whether it is arranged, hand-played, reproducing, recut, scan, MIDI, or modern realization; compatible instrument; playback or scan method; restoration status; source archive; and exact URL or shelfmark. Mark unknown fields instead of guessing.

For a shellac disc, record: performer; work and arrangement; label; catalogue number; matrix and take when available; recording date and place; acoustic or electrical process; disc size and speed information; source copy or archive; transfer engineer or institution; speed, stylus, equalization, and restoration if disclosed; and exact URL or shelfmark. Again, unknown is better than invented.

For both, separate four statements in notes: **artifact fact**, **audible or encoded observation**, **analytical inference**, and **historical hypothesis**. "This is a Duo-Art reproducing roll" is an artifact fact. "The encoded/playback performance places melody attacks after bass attacks in this passage" is an observation, if actually inspected. "That separation projects the melody as singing over harmonic support" is an analytical inference. "This reflects a nineteenth-century Chopin lineage" is a historical hypothesis and needs much more evidence.
