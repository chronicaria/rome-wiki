Chopin's parallel publication history makes disagreement among early editions evidence to classify, not noise to erase.

## Why one “first edition” may not settle the text

Chopin's works could reach French, German, and English publishers through different combinations of autograph, copyist manuscript, proof, and printed exemplar; the exact chain has to be established work by work rather than assumed for the whole corpus. Autograph status, copyist intervention, proof correction, and publication sequence therefore matter. A printed difference may reflect authorial revision, house practice, an engraver's error, or a later editor; visual age alone cannot decide among them.

The practical reading order is: identify the exact impression and plate number; compare autograph and authorized copies where they survive; separate pitch and rhythm from articulation, pedaling, fingering, and layout; then consult a critical report before calling a variant intentional. Performer editions belong in reception history unless their added layer is explicitly attributed.

## Place in the map

This note precedes work guides and comparative listening. It connects source criticism to audible questions without pretending that an early print automatically recovers a single final intention.
