Beethoven's Op. 109 turns a compact sonata into a lesson in Romantic listening: interruption, compression, variation, and return become more important than outward size.

Up: [[Romantic piano repertoire]]

## Opening summary

Ludwig van Beethoven's Piano Sonata in E major, Op. 109, is useful here not because it is simply "Romantic" by date or mood, but because it teaches several habits that nineteenth-century piano music will keep developing: small forms that carry large consequences, a singing line that resists square phrase closure, sudden breaks between public action and private reflection, and a finale in which variation becomes spiritual and structural memory at once. It was composed in 1820 and published by Schlesinger in Berlin in 1821. Beethoven-Haus places it with the last three sonatas, Opp. 109, 110, and 111, which Beethoven planned for Schlesinger; the same work record names the three movement headings and gives Maximiliane Euphrosine Kunigunde Brentano as dedicatee.

The piece is a threshold work for [[Romantic piano repertoire]] because it changes what a sonata can ask from a listener. The first movement is short, lyrical, and repeatedly interrupted by an Adagio fantasy voice. The second movement answers with a compressed E minor Prestissimo that behaves like a scherzo and a dramatic sonata movement at once. The third movement then refuses a conventional finale: it offers a songlike theme and six variations, ending not with triumphal display but with the theme's return after it has been dissolved into trills, registral space, and motion.

This guide is score-based. I inspected public source records and accessible score witnesses, but I did not directly inspect audio recordings for this run. The interpretive alternatives below are therefore framed as choices the score invites, not as claims about named performances.

## Score and edition identity

The work identity is: Ludwig van Beethoven, Piano Sonata No. 30 in E major, Op. 109, for solo piano, three movements. IMSLP's source index lists the movements as "Vivace ma non troppo, sempre legato - Adagio espressivo" in E major, "Prestissimo" in E minor, and "Gesangvoll, mit innigster Empfindung. Andante molto cantabile ed espressivo" in E major. The same index gives the composition year as 1820, first publication as Berlin: Schlesinger, 1821, plate 1088, and dedication as Maximiliane Brentano.

The primary manuscript witness is the holograph score in the Library of Congress Gertrude Clarke Whittall Foundation Collection, catalogued under the title *Sonate fur das Hammerklavier*, dated 1820, with the call number ML30.8b .B4 op. 109 1820. The Library of Congress record describes it as a holograph in ink with pencil notations and gives the physical extent as 20 leaves, or 40 pages, of manuscript music. The autograph is crucial for source identity, but it is not the easiest practical score for a first listening map.

For the working score map, use a clean public-domain printed score while remembering that editions are not neutral. IMSLP lists several witnesses, including the Schlesinger first edition, an 1862 Breitkopf & Hartel complete-works score, Heinrich Schenker's Universal Edition score, and later performer-edited editions by von Bulow, Lamond, Casella, Schnabel, and others. For listening rather than textual criticism, a clean Schenker or Breitkopf-derived public-domain score is usually usable, but interpretive markings in later performer editions must not be treated as Beethoven's text without attribution. The safest habit is to cite the score witness used and to avoid silently mixing bar numbers from different editions.

There is a live source issue behind this caution. Beethoven-Haus lists first-edition copies and related letters, and its work page says the publication of Op. 109 involved a long proofreading period. The work record also notes that the later Op. 111 edition contained many errors, enough that Beethoven wanted a corrected Viennese edition. That warning belongs in any late-Beethoven score habit: the printed page is evidence, not an automatic final answer.

## Movement-by-movement listening path

### I. Vivace ma non troppo - Adagio espressivo

The first movement begins as if it might be a delicate, legato miniature. Its E major opening is marked *Vivace, ma non troppo* and *sempre legato*, and the surface immediately matters: broken, lightly connected figures make melody and accompaniment feel interwoven rather than separated into song-plus-accompaniment. A first listener should notice how little rhetorical announcement Beethoven gives. There is no grand sonata portal; the music has already begun moving before the ear can decide whether it is hearing a theme, figuration, or both.

The decisive event is the interruption. After the opening idea, Beethoven changes character to *Adagio espressivo*. The meter, texture, dynamic rhetoric, and sense of time all widen. This is not just a second theme in the ordinary sense. It feels like another mode of thought inserted into the movement: arpeggiated, recitative-like, and exploratory. The listening question is simple: does the Adagio sound like a memory, an objection, an inward commentary, or a door into another room?

When the Vivace music returns, it does not erase the interruption. The movement's form depends on the listener remembering that two incompatible time-worlds have been placed side by side. This is one reason Op. 109 belongs in a Romantic piano path. Later nineteenth-century piano music often makes form out of such shifts: lyric motion interrupted by recitative, dance interrupted by dream, song interrupted by harmonic suspension. Beethoven's first movement compresses that habit into a small frame.

For a second listening with score, track three things. First, notice how the opening legato figuration keeps the melody from sounding squarely periodic. Second, mark every Adagio intervention and ask what register, texture, and harmony do to the sense of pulse. Third, listen for how the end does not feel like a full public closure; it prepares the possibility that the next movement must supply the violence withheld here.

### II. Prestissimo

The second movement starts in E minor and changes the whole physical temperature of the sonata. It is marked *Prestissimo*, with forceful unisons and a compressed, driven profile. In a conventional three-movement sonata, a middle movement might offer repose. Here the middle movement is the shock. It turns the tonic world from E major into E minor and makes speed, accent, and tight motivic pressure answer the first movement's fragile alternation.

The useful first-listen path is to hear the movement as a concentrated storm rather than as a decorative scherzo. The opening is blunt and rhythmically exacting. Then the material is pushed through sequences, contrasts, and returns that make the movement feel larger than its duration. It is short, but not slight. Beethoven's compression is the point: every return of the opening profile carries the memory of velocity and pressure.

With score, follow the contrast between vertical attack and linear continuation. The music often sounds as if it is built from hard-edged gestures, but those gestures are bound into longer spans by sequence and harmonic direction. The listener should also notice the social difference between the first two movements. The first movement asks the pianist to move between intimate legato and expressive recitative; the second asks for something close to controlled ferocity. A performance that makes only a pretty first movement and a loud second movement misses the deeper relation: the Prestissimo is the first movement's suppressed instability made public.

The ending matters because it does not solve the sonata by force. It clears space for the finale's different answer. After velocity and minor-mode pressure, Beethoven does not write a bigger fast finale. He turns instead to a theme that asks to be sung.

### III. Gesangvoll, mit innigster Empfindung

The finale's German heading, commonly rendered as "songfully, with the most inward feeling," is paired with the Italian *Andante molto cantabile ed espressivo*. Both point to the same problem: the theme must sing without becoming sentimental in a generic way. It is in E major and unfolds as a poised, chorale-like melody with repeats. It sounds simple, but its simplicity is constructed. Phrase, harmony, and register leave room for later transformation.

For a first listen, do not try to count every variation immediately. Learn the theme's contour and breathing. Hear its opening gesture, its cadential settling, and the sense that the melody is both complete and waiting to be reinterpreted. Then listen for what each variation preserves. Sometimes the theme survives as melody. Sometimes it survives as harmonic outline, phrase length, bass support, register, or a memory of cadence. The finale is not a chain of decorative costumes. It is a test of how much can change while identity remains audible.

This makes Op. 109 a bridge between Classical variation technique and Romantic memory. A Classical theme-and-variations movement may demonstrate invention over a stable plan. Beethoven does that, but he also lets the variations become a narrative of inward transformation: lyric ornament, kinetic animation, brilliance, contrapuntal density, trilled dissolution, and return. By the time the theme comes back, it no longer means what it meant at the beginning.

## Variation-finale score map

Because bar numbering and repeat counting can differ between editions, this map identifies each section by score label, meter, character, and listening function rather than pretending that every edition has the same measure references.

**Theme: Gesangvoll, mit innigster Empfindung. Andante molto cantabile ed espressivo.** The theme is the identity source. It is songlike but not operatic, inward but not static. Listen for a melody supported by a calm harmonic frame, with repeat structure that lets the ear learn the phrase before Beethoven begins altering it. The main task is balance: the melody must be voiced enough to sing, while the harmony must remain clear enough to make later variations intelligible.

**Variation 1.** This variation keeps the theme close but makes the line more ornamented and mobile. It is the first lesson in transformation by surface: the theme has not vanished, but it now breathes through a more active figuration. Listen for whether the pianist treats this as decoration or as a warmer restatement of the theme's singing impulse. The danger is prettiness without direction.

**Variation 2. Leggiermente.** The texture becomes lighter and more animated. The theme is now heard through broken motion and quickened surface energy. A useful listening question is whether the pulse feels stable beneath the lightness. Too much heaviness turns *leggiermente* into mechanical figuration; too much looseness makes the variation float away from the theme's structure.

**Variation 3. Allegro vivace.** This is the most obviously brilliant early transformation. It changes the finale's scale of activity and pushes the theme into a quick, virtuosic profile. The listener should not hear it as an unrelated showpiece. The harmonic path and phrase shape still come from the theme. Its function is to test whether song can become fleet motion while remaining legible.

**Variation 4. Etwas langsamer als das Thema. Un poco meno andante cioe un poco piu adagio come il tema.** The marking asks for a slightly slower, more inward pace. The texture becomes more sustained and layered, with a more contemplative profile after the brilliance of Variation 3. This is the finale's deepening point before the contrapuntal intensification. Listen for spacing and voicing: the variation can sound like a private chorale if inner voices are allowed to speak.

**Variation 5. Allegro ma non troppo.** This variation brings contrapuntal energy to the foreground. It is often described as fugato-like, and the important listening point is not the label but the way imitative motion changes the theme's identity. Song becomes argument. Voices enter, answer, overlap, and press forward. The pianist's problem is to make the counterpoint audible without making it pedantic. If the variation becomes merely fast and busy, its structural role is lost.

**Variation 6. Tempo primo del tema.** Beethoven returns to the theme's tempo world, but the return is not simple. The variation begins with a calmer recollection and then intensifies through repeated notes, trills, registral expansion, and increasingly active figuration. This is where the finale seems to dissolve the theme into vibration. The famous long trills are not ornamental frosting; they are a way of suspending harmonic and melodic identity until the theme can reappear as memory.

**Return of the theme.** After the trilled intensification, the original theme returns. This is the movement's central interpretive fact. The return can sound like consolation, exhaustion, blessing, or release, but those are listener interpretations rather than score facts. What the score does show is that Beethoven brings back the theme after subjecting it to ornament, motion, contrapuntal pressure, and sonic dissolution. The return is simple only because the listener has changed.

## Interpretive alternatives

**How continuous should the first two movements feel?** Some performers connect the first movement's end to the Prestissimo with very little psychological distance, making the second movement sound like an eruption from inside the first. Others give the first movement more independent closure, so that the Prestissimo becomes a new scene. The score supports both as long as the relation between fragile E major and forceful E minor remains audible.

**Is the Adagio in the first movement a second theme or a recitative interruption?** It can be shaped as a formal contrast within sonata logic, or as a freer fantasy voice that disrupts sonata logic. The first approach clarifies architecture. The second emphasizes late Beethoven's tendency to make form from discontinuity. A strong reading usually needs some of both: the Adagio must belong to the movement, but it should still feel like a change in kind.

**How songlike is the finale theme?** The title asks for song, but piano song is not literal vocal imitation. A heavily sustained, over-pedaled reading may make the theme beautiful while blurring the harmonic skeleton needed for the variations. A dry reading may clarify structure while missing the inward character. The practical question is not "sentimental or unsentimental," but how much resonance lets the melody sing without hiding cadence, bass, and inner motion.

**How fast are the animated variations?** Variations 2, 3, and 5 need distinct energies. If they are all treated as generic quickness, the finale loses its sequence of transformations. Variation 2 needs lightness, Variation 3 needs brilliance, and Variation 5 needs contrapuntal propulsion. Their tempos should make those functions audible, not merely display speed.

**What are the trills in Variation 6?** They can be heard as technical display, as suspended resonance, as an intensification of the theme's repeated-note life, or as a passage where the piano approaches pure vibration. Score fact: Beethoven writes an intensifying texture that leads back to the theme. Analytical inference: the trills make return possible by stretching identity almost to the breaking point. Performer choice: whether they sound luminous, tense, fragile, or ecstatic depends on voicing, tempo, pedal, and dynamic control.

**How should the final theme return be voiced?** One option is to make the return plain and almost objective, trusting the prior variations to supply the emotion. Another is to let the return glow with accumulated memory. The first approach emphasizes structure; the second emphasizes transformation. The risk in the first is emotional blankness. The risk in the second is imposing an ending affect that the score does not explicitly name.

## Place in the map

Op. 109 belongs near the beginning of this Romantic piano path because it changes the listener before the later repertoire arrives. It prepares [[Romantic phrase rhythm]] by showing how a singing phrase can avoid square predictability. It prepares [[The Romantic piano cycle]] by making return and memory more important than simple sequence. It also prepares later variation works, from Beethoven's own *Diabelli Variations* to Brahms's [[Brahms Handel Variations Op. 24]], because it treats variation as a way of thinking rather than a decorative genre.

It also clarifies why "late Beethoven" cannot be reduced to monumentality. After the enormous Op. 106 "Hammerklavier" Sonata, Op. 109 is compact. Yet its compactness is radical: the first movement compresses fantasy and sonata behavior, the second condenses dramatic force, and the finale lets a small theme carry the work's deepest argument. Later Romantic composers will often learn from this disproportion between outward scale and inward consequence.

For Andrew's map, this page should sit between foundational listening skills and central nineteenth-century repertoire. It is a good predecessor to [[Schubert Impromptu in G-flat D 899 No. 3]] on songlike piano time, to [[Schumann Kinderszenen Op. 15]] on concentrated character and memory, and to [[Clara Schumann Variations on a Theme by Robert Schumann Op. 20]] on variation as relationship rather than display. It also points forward to [[Urtext does not mean interpretation-free]], because the piece is surrounded by autograph, first-edition, later edition, and performance-practice questions that cannot be solved by saying "just follow the score."
