The Tourte-model bow consolidated material features that widened sustained and spring articulation possibilities, but it did not mechanically create one uniform Romantic style.

Up: [[Romantic violin repertoire]]

## Material affordance, not technological destiny

François-Xavier Tourte's late-eighteenth- and early-nineteenth-century work is associated with a concave camber, a higher head, a screw-adjusted frog, and a ferrule that spreads hair into a ribbon. Pernambuco's strength and elasticity supported a long, tapered stick with a balance suited to both sustained contact and articulated rebound. These features became an influential model rather than an instantaneous universal standard: makers, players, instruments, and regional practices continued to vary.

The bow changes what can be negotiated—attack, release, bow distribution, dynamic span, sustained line, and off-string response. It does not tell us how Beethoven, Paganini, or a later player actually phrased. That requires notation, treatises, marked parts, and work-specific evidence. Surviving “Tourte model” trade bows also show how the design became a reproducible nineteenth-century category beyond Tourte's own workshop.
