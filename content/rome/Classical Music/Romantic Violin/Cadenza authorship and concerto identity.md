A cadenza is not decorative surplus: its author, date, source, and treatment of the concerto’s materials determine whose argument the listener hears at the movement’s most exposed threshold.

Up: [[Romantic violin repertoire]]

## The unstable ownership of a cadenza

Near the end of a concerto movement, the orchestra often halts on a cadential six-four or another charged suspension and leaves the soloist alone. The resulting cadenza occupies an ambiguous legal and musical territory. It belongs to the concerto’s formal design, yet its notes may come from the soloist rather than the named composer. It can sound spontaneous while being meticulously written, published, edited, memorized, and inherited. It can become so conventional that audiences hear it as part of the work even though another author composed it decades later.

That ambiguity makes authorship musically audible. A cadenza decides which themes deserve recall, how closely they should be transformed, how far harmony may wander, what kind of virtuosity represents the soloist, and how suspense returns to the orchestral coda. Choosing Joachim rather than Kreisler for Beethoven’s Violin Concerto is therefore not merely choosing a longer or shorter display. It selects a historical reading of Beethoven, a model of violinistic eloquence, and a relationship between performer freedom and work identity.

The first rule is bibliographic: never call a cadenza “the cadenza” until its author and source have been identified. A part may bind one cadenza into the concerto without making it authorial. A performer may adapt a published cadenza without announcing every change. A recording catalogue may name the concerto composer while omitting the cadenza composer. The missing credit hides a collaboration across time.

## From improvised act to transmitted composition

The eighteenth-century cadenza was associated with the performer’s invention at a designated cadence. Convention constrained that invention: the soloist was expected to remain answerable to the movement, prepare the trill and return, and avoid turning a formal suspension into an unrelated fantasy. Yet the notes did not always circulate as fixed text. Written cadenzas could function as models, safeguards, teaching material, or substitutes for performers who did not improvise.

During the nineteenth century, composition and improvisation did not simply exchange places overnight. Notated cadenzas proliferated while the cultural image of spontaneous creation persisted. Dana Gooley calls this an “improvisation imaginary”: a prepared text could still signify immediacy. Christina M. De Simone’s study of Joseph Joachim emphasizes that his cadenzas stand at precisely this intersection of compositional craft and performative virtuosity. Multiple notated versions of Joachim’s Beethoven cadenza also warn against assuming that writing instantly froze the act.

The shift matters because a cadenza increasingly became reproducible repertoire. Publication placed one violinist’s solution on thousands of stands. Conservatories transmitted it. Recordings associated it with famous interpreters. What began as an open place in a composer’s design could acquire a customary occupant. That occupant might then be mistaken for the design itself.

The history is not a story of decline from free improvisation into obedience. Written cadenzas can carry sophisticated analysis, and modern improvisation can be historically uninformed or formally weak. The useful distinction is between degrees of fixation and kinds of authorship: improvised in performance, composed by the soloist, supplied by the concerto composer, written by a collaborator, adapted from another version, or assembled by a later editor.

## Four dimensions of concerto identity

### Formal function

A cadenza must negotiate where it stands. In a first-movement sonata-concerto design, it normally interrupts the final cadence before the coda. The orchestra’s arrival has already promised closure; the soloist delays it. A convincing cadenza can compress the movement’s argument, reopen its conflicts, or challenge the stability the orchestra was about to claim. Its closing trill is not simply a technical convention but a handoff back to collective time.

Some later concertos internalize or relocate this function. Mendelssohn’s Violin Concerto places a written cadenza before the recapitulation, making it part of the movement’s composed developmental machinery rather than an optional insertion near the end. Other composers write cadenza-like passages into the continuous score. Still others omit the expected opportunity. “Does this concerto have a cadenza?” therefore requires a formal answer as well as a search for an unaccompanied flourish.

### Thematic identity

A cadenza can quote a theme, fragment it, combine motives, or introduce largely new figuration. The choice announces a theory of the concerto. Emphasizing a recurrent rhythmic cell suggests that the movement’s identity lies in motivic integration. Dwelling on the lyrical second group makes songfulness the soloist’s culminating claim. Introducing brilliant generic passagework can assert the performer’s independent persona against the score.

De Simone notes that Joachim did not merely quote themes in his cadenzas but developed them. Her account of the Beethoven cadenza highlights the repeated-note motive that marks the concerto’s orchestral opening. Bringing that motive into the soloist’s unaccompanied space does more than prove thematic memory: it transfers an orchestral signature to the violin and makes the cadenza answer the work’s beginning.

### Virtuosic identity

Technique in a cadenza is characterization. Double stops can imply polyphonic command; arpeggiated spans can enlarge the violin into a harmonic instrument; high-register lyricism can suspend weight; rapid passagework can portray danger, ease, wit, or transcendence. Different cadenza writers choose different historical vocabularies of difficulty.

The question is never only “how hard?” A cadenza may be technically demanding yet deliberately subordinate display to thematic development. Another may foreground the violinist as virtuoso-author. The same concerto can consequently project symphonic integration in one cadenza and theatrical individuality in another. These are not neutral vehicles carrying the same content.

### Historical identity

Every cadenza also dates the concerto in performance. A cadenza written in the 1850s for a work of 1806 carries mid-century assumptions about Beethoven, violin technique, and formal seriousness. A twentieth-century cadenza may respond to recorded virtuosity, changing instruments, or a new ideal of textual fidelity. Its anachronism is not necessarily a fault; it is a historical relationship that should be named.

This makes a cadenza valuable evidence for reception. How did Joachim want Beethoven to sound? What kind of Brahms did Kreisler or Reger imagine? Which motives did a later violinist believe an audience needed to recognize? A cadenza is criticism written in playable notes.

## Beethoven Op. 61: a work without an authorial violin cadenza

Beethoven’s Violin Concerto is the central case because authorial evidence and performing custom do not align neatly. The Beethoven-Haus dates the concerto’s composition to late 1806, its premiere by Franz Clement to 23 December, and revisions to 1807. The surviving work materials do not give modern violinists a standard Beethoven-authored violin cadenza.

Beethoven did, however, write cadenzas for the piano-and-orchestra version prepared after Muzio Clementi encouraged the arrangement. The autograph first-movement cadenza held by the Beethoven-Haus extends across twelve manuscript pages and includes a separate timpani part. The timpani recalls the four quiet drum strokes that open the concerto, making the cadenza an extraordinary interpretation of the work’s motivic identity. But its solo writing is pianistic. To use it in the violin concerto requires transcription or recomposition, and that mediating author must be credited.

This evidence supports two statements that are often blurred. Beethoven supplied an authorial cadenza related to Op. 61; he did not thereby leave a ready-made authorial violin cadenza. An arrangement of the piano cadenza may bring Beethoven’s thematic thinking closer to the violin performance, but it remains an arrangement with decisions about what one violin can preserve, redistribute, or omit.

The gap invited later violinists to define the concerto. Joachim’s cadenzas became especially important. Beethoven-Haus’s exhibition history describes nineteenth-century interpreters—including Joachim—as composers and publishers of cadenzas for Beethoven’s concertos. Recent scholarship has complicated the transmission further: De Simone studies multiple versions, and an *Early Music* report identifies Joachim’s earliest Beethoven cadenza in an autograph album connected to Julius Rietz. “Joachim cadenza” can therefore name a family and history, not one timeless artifact.

Fritz Kreisler supplied another enormously influential solution. The Beethoven-Haus catalogue records the published Kreisler cadenzas for Op. 61 and identifies their 1928 copyright. Their continued binding with editions and use in performance demonstrates how a later performer-composer can become embedded in the public identity of an earlier work. A listener who recognizes the concerto partly through Kreisler’s gestures is hearing reception history inside the form.

The responsible comparison does not crown a universally correct choice. It asks which text, version, and edition is being used; which concerto motives are selected; how the cadenza handles the opening timpani rhythm and other thematic materials; where it moves harmonically; how it prepares the trill and coda; and what model of violin virtuosity it projects. Measure-level claims require inspected scores of the exact cadenzas, not recollection from performances.

## Brahms Op. 77: collaboration rather than vacancy

Brahms’s Violin Concerto presents a different authorship structure. It was written for and in consultation with Joseph Joachim, who also supplied the first-movement cadenza most closely associated with the work. Here the cadenza is not a later response by a violinist who never knew the composer. It belongs to a documented composer-performer collaboration.

That collaboration still does not merge the two authors. Brahms wrote the concerto; Joachim advised on violin writing and wrote his cadenza. The distinction matters because it lets us hear the work’s social production without reducing Joachim to a technician or assigning his every choice to Brahms. Correspondence and manuscript layers can show consultation, but approval of a collaborator’s cadenza is not identical to authorial composition.

Joachim’s position gives the cadenza unusual historical authority: dedicatee, premiere soloist, adviser, and cadenza author converge. Yet later violinists still wrote alternatives. Those alternatives do not necessarily reject Brahms; they reinterpret the balance between symphonic argument and solo agency. The very stability of Joachim’s customary solution can make another cadenza newly audible as criticism.

Audience behavior supplies an illuminating clue. A contemporary report quoted in Cedille’s documented account of the concerto’s genesis says that the cadenza at an early performance drew applause into the coda. Whatever modern concert etiquette suggests, the cadenza could register as an event with its own public force. Formal integration and virtuoso recognition were not mutually exclusive.

## Cadenza authorship as a metadata problem

A reproducible cadenza study needs more fields than composer and concerto title. Record:

- concerto composer, title, key, opus or catalogue number, movement, and cadenza location;
- cadenza author and, if different, arranger or transcriber;
- date of composition, earliest documented performance, and publication date, each kept separate;
- manuscript shelfmark or publication details, including editor, publisher, plate number, and edition;
- whether the text is an autograph, copy, first print, later edition, transcription, recording-only version, or reconstruction;
- version relationships and cuts;
- performer, date, venue, conductor and ensemble for a studied performance;
- whether the performer follows, adapts, combines, or improvises the identified text.

These fields prevent common category errors. A manuscript dated 1891 does not prove the cadenza was first conceived then. A publication of 1905 may preserve a version performed decades earlier. A recording may contain revisions absent from the player’s published edition. An autograph proves who wrote that document, but not necessarily the first moment of the music’s invention.

Catalogues themselves can expose hidden authorship. The Morgan Library describes Joachim’s 1891 autograph cadenza for Viotti’s E-major concerto as an independent manuscript object, with creator, associated composer, date, inscription, and accession number. That record models the correct relationship: Joachim is the cadenza’s creator; Viotti is the composer whose concerto it inhabits.

## A listening and score-study protocol

First establish the concerto text before comparing cadenzas. Locate the formal arrival, the orchestral six-four or other preparatory harmony, the point where the soloist enters, the closing trill or gesture, and the orchestral coda. Identify themes and motives from the movement that a cadenza writer could plausibly revisit.

Second, inspect the exact cadenza edition. Mark quotation, fragmentation, sequence, recombination, new material, harmonic departure, registral climax, technical texture, and return preparation. Separate explicit findings on the page from interpretive descriptions. “The opening rhythm is quoted” is textual; “the soloist appropriates orchestral authority” is an interpretation built from that fact.

Third, compare two cadenzas with the same questions rather than a leaderboard. Which begins by remembering the concerto, and which begins by announcing the violinist? Which motive organizes the whole rather than appearing briefly? Does the harmony prolong the concerto’s dominant field or create a remote excursion? Is virtuosity continuous, episodic, contrapuntal, lyrical, or theatrical? How is the return to the orchestra made inevitable—or deliberately abrupt?

Fourth, listen to identified performances. Do not assume the recording follows the printed text merely because the cadenza bears the same name. Note cuts, recomposed transitions, altered bowings, tempo flexibility, and the timing of the final trill. A recording is sonic primary evidence for that performance, while the score is textual evidence for the transmitted cadenza. Agreement has to be demonstrated.

Finally ask what changes in the concerto’s identity. Does the cadenza make an orchestral motive central? Does it reinterpret a lyrical concerto as virtuoso theatre? Does it smooth a rupture or intensify it? Does an authorial or collaborator cadenza carry special documentary weight, and if so, what freedoms remain? The answer should remain specific to the work and witnesses.

## Authorship labels that preserve complexity

Useful labels include “composer-authored,” “dedicatee-authored,” “premiere-soloist-authored,” “later performer-authored,” “transcribed from the composer’s alternate version,” “adapted from,” and “improvised in this performance.” These can overlap. Joachim’s Brahms cadenza is both dedicatee- and premiere-soloist-authored within a collaboration. A violin version of Beethoven’s timpani cadenza is composer-derived but arranger-mediated.

Avoid “authentic” as an all-purpose verdict. It may mean written by the concerto composer, historically connected to the premiere, compatible with period convention, or simply customary. Those are different claims. “Traditional” also needs a date and community: a cadenza can be traditional in a twentieth-century conservatory lineage without belonging to the concerto’s premiere tradition.

Likewise, a new cadenza is not automatically self-indulgent, and an old one is not automatically stylistically correct. Evaluate formal hearing, source honesty, historical positioning, and musical consequence. A performer who composes a cadenza participates in a long concerto practice; a performer who selects Joachim or Kreisler also makes an interpretive choice, even when convention makes it appear neutral.

## The cadenza as distributed authorship

The Romantic violin concerto is often marketed through a single possessive name—Beethoven’s, Brahms’s, Tchaikovsky’s. Cadenzas reveal a more distributed object. Composer, dedicatee, editor, arranger, teacher, performer, publisher, and recording artist can all shape the version audiences know. Recognizing those contributions does not dissolve the work. It makes the boundaries of the work historically intelligible.

This perspective also corrects the idea that textual fidelity eliminates performance authorship. Selecting a cadenza, choosing among its versions, deciding whether to cut or adapt it, and shaping its temporal relation to the orchestra are acts of interpretation. Even the decision to play the customary cadenza reproduces a reception history.

The cadenza is thus a compact laboratory for [[Violin source layers from autograph to performer edition]]. It brings the source ladder onto the concert stage at the moment when one person seems most free. The paradox is productive: the soloist’s apparent freedom is carried by manuscripts, editions, institutions, and inherited expectations, while the fixed concerto remains open enough to absorb another author’s voice.
