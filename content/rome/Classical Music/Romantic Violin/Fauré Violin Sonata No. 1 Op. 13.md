Fauré's A-major sonata turns lyrical abundance into four-movement momentum by making violin and piano continually complete, redirect, and energize one another.

Up: [[Romantic violin repertoire]]

Gabriel Fauré composed most of his Sonata for Piano and Violin No. 1 in A major, Op. 13, in 1875, completed it in 1876, and fixed its published form while correcting proofs in January 1877. It is scored for violin and piano and dedicated to Paul Viardot. The four movements are *Allegro molto*, *Andante*, *Scherzo: Allegro vivo*, and *Finale: Allegro quasi presto*. Marie Tayau and Fauré gave the public premiere at a Société nationale de musique concert on 27 January 1877; Paul Viardot and Fauré had played it privately the previous evening at the home of Camille Clerc. Breitkopf & Härtel issued the first edition in Leipzig in February 1877, plate 14541.

Those facts locate a work whose historical importance can otherwise become a slogan. Op. 13 did not single-handedly invent French chamber music, but it did give the post-1870 institutional revival an early, durable success. The Société nationale, founded in 1871, supplied a forum for new French instrumental music; Fauré had become its secretary in 1874. Saint-Saëns greeted the sonata as evidence that a French instrumental repertoire could contend in a field long associated with German models. The claim becomes musically useful when heard as a combination rather than a patriotic label: sonata-scale thematic development and a strongly active piano coexist with rhythmic suppleness, rapid harmonic recoloring, and a preference for continuity over monumental rhetoric.

The best route into the piece is therefore not to ask whether it sounds "French" or "German." Ask how it moves. The opening movement grows from restless keyboard motion and long-breathed violin ideas; the slow movement converts dialogue into grave, rocking song; the scherzo makes exchange itself the joke; and the finale gathers lyrical breadth and rhythmic drive into a sustained joint argument. Across all four, the violin's ability to sing is obvious. Less obvious, and more structurally decisive, is how often the piano initiates motion, alters the harmonic meaning of a sustained note, or supplies the pulse by which the violin's freedom remains intelligible.

## Identity, genesis, and first reception

The surviving documents record composition as a collaborative and revisional process rather than solitary inspiration transferred cleanly to print. According to Fabian Kolb's Henle preface and critical commentary, Fauré conceived nearly the whole sonata during summer 1875 at Sainte-Adresse, near Le Havre, while staying with Camille Clerc's family. After a pause, he completed it in late summer and autumn 1876. Early sketches preserve much of the first movement's exposition, the scherzo opening, and measures 61–76 of the Andante. The larger autograph began relatively cleanly but accumulated corrections; its finale remains an incomplete, heavily revised draft.

Violinist Hubert Léonard, a Belgian-born pupil of Habeneck and Mendelssohn, played newly composed passages and advised Fauré on practical details. Traces in the autograph indicate that it functioned as a playing copy in that private work. This does not authorize attributing every playable detail to Léonard. It establishes something narrower and more valuable: the violin writing was tested in dialogue with a professional player before publication.

Clerc sent a now-lost fair copy to Breitkopf & Härtel on 13 October 1876. The publisher accepted the sonata only if Fauré waived a fee. Proofs traveled from Leipzig to Paris early in January 1877 and returned on 15 January. The public premiere consequently preceded the finished print by several weeks. Fauré's contemporary letter to Marie Clerc called Tayau's interpretation perfect and described a success beyond his expectations; the scherzo was repeated. This is firm evidence of a warm first reception, not proof that every later audience must find the work effortless or charming.

The dedication to Paul Viardot belongs to the source itself: it appears on the autograph and first edition. Viardot was Pauline Viardot-Garcia's son, a violinist and Léonard pupil, and Fauré's friend. The dedication should not be inflated into a claim that Viardot composed or authorized the entire violin text. The more defensible history distinguishes dedicatee, adviser, private performer, public-premiere violinist, composer-pianist, and publisher. Several people helped bring the sonata into sounding and printed form, but their roles were not interchangeable.

## First-listen landmarks

On a first hearing, retain five events rather than trying to catalogue every theme.

- **The opening engine:** the piano begins alone with coursing figuration. When the violin enters, hear its broad idea against motion already in progress. The violin adds line; it does not create the temporal world by itself.
- **The first movement's pressure and release:** notice how lyrical spans repeatedly gather intensity, reach large joint statements, and fall back into motion. The argument is less theme-versus-theme than song continually subjected to harmonic and rhythmic pressure.
- **The Andante's rocking tread:** a darker, more measured pulse replaces the opening movement's sweep. Listen for the difference between the piano supporting a violin line and the piano changing the line's direction through bass and harmony.
- **The scherzo's chase:** short, light gestures pass so quickly between the instruments that foreground and accompaniment become unstable. The central contrast briefly broadens the scale before the quick exchange returns.
- **The finale's renewed launch:** the violin states an urgent idea over another active keyboard field. Through recalls, transitions, and accumulating climaxes, hear the two instruments move from alternation toward shared propulsion.

These landmarks prepare a second hearing focused on function. At every entry, ask who supplies attack, who carries continuation, who changes harmony, and who closes the phrase. That is the method of [[How to hear violin and piano as partners]], and Op. 13 rewards it immediately.

## A score-based form map

The map below follows the authorized published design and uses measure references from the Henle commentary where they are especially useful. It describes audible functions rather than pretending that one analytical label settles every boundary.

### I. Allegro molto

The piano's opening figuration establishes velocity and harmonic expectancy before the violin's principal line arrives. The broad theme and its active field belong together: separating "melody" from "accompaniment" conceals how the piano's changing bass makes the violin's sustained notes lean forward or settle. A contrasting lyrical area begins around measure 23; the autograph's changing expression marks there—from an earlier *dolce e espressivo* toward *con anima*—show that Fauré reconsidered its character. What sounds like repose still carries animation.

The exposition proceeds through further exchanges and a closing field, then the development begins around the first ending/continuation after measure 103. Fragments circulate, keyboard motion thickens, and apparent arrivals become departures. The recapitulation returns near measure 286, with the principal functions recognizable but enlarged by what the duo has traversed. Parallel passages around measures 23/289 and 70/340 are especially revealing in the editorial record: slurs, dynamic hairpins, and verbal directions are not entirely uniform. The movement ends not with private lyricism but with a forceful joint closing span.

For listening, the essential formal distinction is between **presentation** and **pressure**. In presentation, one player can appear to own a theme. Under pressure, ownership dissolves: the piano's sequence, bass, or rhythmic continuity drives while the violin sustains, intensifies, or transfers a fragment back. The large sonata form is made audible through these changing jobs.

### II. Andante

The Andante shifts to D minor and a rocking compound-meter world. The opening is restrained rather than static. Piano pulse and bass establish a grave field; the violin's line enters as speech over that motion. Repeated phrases are colored by changing register, harmony, and division of material, so the movement should not be reduced to a single uninterrupted lament.

A more agitated middle span grows from the same partnership rather than abandoning it. Around measures 61–76—a passage already present in early sketches—motion and harmonic tension sharpen. The instruments increasingly share the burden of forward motion. The opening character later returns, but return after agitation is memory, not reset. Near the end, both players must make diminuendo and temporal release sound jointly forecast; otherwise the piano appears to follow an expressive solo after the fact.

The movement's most productive question is how accompaniment changes meaning. A repeating piano pattern can be heard as cradle, tread, unease, or continuity depending on voicing, bass direction, and the violin's placement. The score does not assign those nouns. It supplies relations from which listeners and performers construct character.

### III. Scherzo: Allegro vivo

The scherzo's quick duple motion makes instrumental exchange immediately audible. Light repeated notes, clipped articulations, off-beat displacements, and rapid handoffs turn coordination into form. Rather than a soloist flashing over a neutral keyboard texture, the movement behaves like a chase in which either player can provoke the next gesture. Its brevity and surface brilliance explain why an audience could demand its repetition at the premiere, but virtuosity here is relational: wit depends on the exact timing of response.

A broader central episode provides contrast without becoming a separate slow movement. Line lengthens, sonority fills out, and the listener briefly hears a more sustained kind of partnership. The return of the quick material then feels sharper because the duo has changed scale. In the closing bars, speed should not obscure grammar: accents and releases tell us which gesture answers which.

The Henle commentary documents numerous small differences among autograph, printed score, and separate part in this movement. They concern slurs, articulation, dynamics, and placement rather than a wholly different composition. Yet those details govern whether a figure sounds connected across the bar, lightly repeated, or tossed to the other instrument. "Scherzo character" is built from textual particulars.

### IV. Finale: Allegro quasi presto

The finale reestablishes urgent forward motion. An active piano texture and an expansive violin idea again resist the solo/accompaniment model: keyboard rhythm is the condition of the violin's breadth. Subsequent material creates contrast through lyric expansion and harmonic redirection, but momentum is rarely abandoned for long. Themes return under increasing pressure until the closing pages bring both players into a brilliant shared trajectory.

The finale matters architecturally because it transforms traits heard separately earlier. The first movement's surge, the Andante's long line, and the scherzo's quick exchange are not literally quoted into one cyclic theme; rather, their modes of motion become compatible. Fauré achieves four-movement coherence through recurring relational habits—piano launch, violin extension, harmonic redirection, traded fragments, joint climax—as much as through tune recognition.

That distinction protects analysis from a common overstatement. Op. 13 is unified, but unity need not mean a hidden motto mechanically controlling every movement. The listener can hear a consistent musical intelligence in how phrases grow and how instrumental roles change, even when exact thematic derivation is debatable.

## Violin and piano as consequential partners

The violin's central expressive resource is sustained continuity. It can carry a line across changes of piano attack, connect registers, and make an arrival feel vocal. But its long notes are harmonically incomplete by themselves. A bass change beneath one pitch can turn expectancy into suspension or release. The pianist therefore shapes violin expression even while the violin remains the audible foreground.

The piano's writing is often dense and energetic, but "orchestral" should not mean uniformly loud. Its roles include motor, harmonic field, bass narrator, thematic proposer, countervoice, and climactic mass. The opening movement and finale particularly depend on clarity within continuous figuration. If that figuration becomes generalized wash, the violin seems to float over accompaniment and the formal direction weakens. If every keyboard event asserts equal weight, lyric line loses scale. Voicing is structural because it tells the ear which strand currently carries consequence.

Two ensemble decision points recur. First is **handoff without a seam**. When a fragment passes between instruments, the second player must inherit direction rather than merely enter on time. Differences in attack can clarify the exchange, but the phrase must retain one horizon. Second is **freedom against an active pulse**. The violin may broaden a line while the piano supplies smaller values; flexibility works only if both know whether the motion stretches, continues underneath, or resumes after a cue. These are not rules about one correct tempo. They are questions the score forces each duo to answer.

The scherzo offers the reverse problem. Here, exactness can become lifeless if every response is metrically correct but rhetorically neutral. A chase needs provocation, reply, interruption, and recovery. The Andante, by contrast, asks how two unlike sound decays can share a cadence: the violin can sustain after the piano chord fades, while the pianist can prepare the harmony that gives that sustain meaning. Partnership changes with texture; equality never means identical behavior.

## Edition and performance questions

The principal source is not simply "the autograph." The Bibliothèque nationale de France holds the working manuscript as Ms. 20298 and sketches as Ms. 20293. The autograph is detailed in the first three movements but increasingly revisional; its finale is only a rough, incomplete draft. A lost October 1876 fair copy served as engraver's model. For Henle's Urtext, the February 1877 first-edition score is therefore the principal source for the piano, and its separate violin part the principal source for the violin. Both can claim composer-revised, authorized status.

Crucially, the score and separate part do not always agree. Kolb infers that Fauré probably corrected the full-score proofs alone, while Léonard, Paul Viardot, or Marie Tayau may have advised changes to the separate violin part. Those authorized changes were not transferred back into the score. This is a carefully framed inference, not identification of a particular hand. It makes score-part comparison essential: a violinist reading only the violin staff in the full score can encounter different slurs, dynamics, or articulations from a colleague using the separate part.

The history also complicates tempo. The first print supplies metronome marks, including an almost certainly erroneous half note = 192 in the separate part for Movement I where the score gives 152. Later reported annotations associated with Fauré propose substantially different numbers; markings in a family copy are probably by his son Philippe, not automatically by the composer. No single later penciled number should silently override the authorized print. A responsible edition explains provenance, and a performance tests whether a tempo preserves the marked character, articulation, and large-scale continuity.

Henle prints both marked and unmarked violin parts. Igor Ozim's fingerings and bowings in the marked part are modern performance guidance, visibly separated from the Urtext layer. This is good editorial hygiene and a reminder to consult [[Reading editorial bowings and fingerings]] and [[Violin source layers from autograph to performer edition]]. A persuasive bowing may solve a phrase on a particular instrument without becoming Fauré's intention.

No recordings were auditioned for this article, so it makes no claims about particular players' portamento, vibrato, tempo, balance, or cuts. A future comparison should identify performers, date, venue or studio where known, edition evidence where visible, and transfer provenance for historical discs. It should then restrict itself to narrow windows: the first violin entry, the Andante's return, the scherzo handoffs, and one late finale climax. Without that audit, named performance claims would be decoration rather than evidence.

## Why it matters

Op. 13 anchors the French violin-and-piano tradition before [[Franck Violin Sonata in A major]]. Franck's later sonata often receives the simpler historical headline, but Fauré's work already demonstrates that French chamber music after 1870 could sustain a large sonata design, demand a genuinely consequential keyboard part, and turn harmonic mobility into dramatic continuity. Its success also links composition to institution: the Société nationale created a place where the new work could be tested before a public, while a German publisher enabled wider circulation.

The sonata is equally valuable as a source-studies case. Its working autograph, lost fair copy, corrected proofs, authorized but divergent score and part, and later annotated copies prevent a simplistic opposition between "composer text" and "editorial corruption." Performance entered the document before publication. The task is not to purge every practical intervention, but to identify layers and avoid assigning them false authorship.

Finally, the work teaches a reusable way of listening. Lyricism is not the opposite of form. In Fauré, line becomes formal because the piano changes its harmonic stakes, because fragments migrate, and because each return carries accumulated momentum. Hear the sonata after [[How to hear violin and piano as partners]], then compare its continuously energized dialogue with Franck's overt cyclic recalls and canon, and with [[Brahms Violin Sonata No. 1 Op. 78]], where rhythmic memory binds a more inward three-movement arc.
