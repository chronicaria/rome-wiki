Respighi's 1917 Sonata in B minor, P 110, makes violin and piano carry a late-Romantic dramatic argument whose destination is a passacaglia: old variation procedure becomes the means of achieving modern continuity, weight, and renewal.

## Identity and source boundary

Ottorino Respighi completed the sonata in 1917 for violin and piano. It is generally identified as P 110 in the catalogue associated with Potito Pedarra. The Fondazione Giorgio Cini's digital description of materials in the Ottorino Respighi collection identifies the work, scoring, 1917 date, Ricordi publication, a printed score with separate violin part, and Ernesto Consolo as dedicatee. A University of Roma Tre library record likewise describes the 1919 Milan Ricordi edition, its three movements, and the P 110 designation. These institutional records provide a firmer identity base than recycled concert-note chronology.

The printed text belongs to 1919, two years after composition. That distinction matters: “1917” identifies the work's completion, whereas “1919” identifies the Ricordi publication represented in accessible catalogues. The Cini record also lists later print states and notes handwritten annotations in the preserved copy. Such annotations are evidence about that object's use, not automatically Respighi's revisions. Without a provenance study, they must not be promoted into composer text.

The three printed movement headings are:

1. *Moderato*
2. *Andante espressivo*
3. *Passacaglia: Allegro moderato ma energico*

The sonata is therefore not a Baroque suite masquerading as Romantic chamber music. Two expansive, rhetorically flexible movements precede a finale that explicitly names a historical form. The question is not whether Respighi “copies the Baroque,” but how the finale's disciplined recurrence answers pressures accumulated by the first two movements.

Accounts of the earliest performances differ in secondary circulation. A Bologna performance on 3 March 1918, with violinist Federico Sarti and Respighi at the piano, is widely documented in recent label scholarship; some sources also identify an earlier Naples performance by Arrigo Serato and Alessandro Longo. Because the present research did not inspect a surviving program or contemporary review for the Naples event, this guide does not declare a single uncontested world-premiere claim. The chronology should remain a research question rather than a falsely precise fact.

## Where the sonata sits

The sonata stands at a useful historical crossing. Its broad climaxes, chromatic intensification, long-breathed violin writing, and saturated piano textures inherit nineteenth-century expressive habits. Yet its finale turns toward an older recurring-bass principle, while the whole work belongs to an Italian instrumental revival often associated with Respighi and other composers born around the 1880s. “Late Romantic” describes much of the rhetoric; it does not exhaust the work's historical identity.

Respighi was already deeply engaged with historical repertories and with orchestral color. Those interests meet here without requiring an orchestra. The piano often behaves as a field of registers and masses rather than as a neutral harmonic support. The violin can sing above that field, cut through it, double it, or surrender the foreground to it. The result makes the page a particularly good sequel to [[How to hear violin and piano as partners]]: equality does not mean that both instruments are equally prominent in every bar. It means that the argument cannot be understood by following only one of them.

The piece also offers a different model of continuity from the cyclic recall associated with [[Franck Violin Sonata in A major]] or the transformed song-rhythm of [[Brahms Violin Sonata No. 1 Op. 78]]. Respighi's most conspicuous unifying decision is architectural: the first two movements repeatedly test how lyrical material can survive acceleration and thickening, and the finale subjects continuity itself to variation over a returning foundation.

## First listening path

On a first hearing, follow changes of agency rather than attempting to name every theme.

In the first movement, hear the opening as an invitation into motion, not as a fixed monument. The violin's lyrical profile and the piano's active underlay are intertwined from the beginning. Listen for moments when forward pressure rises—through denser figuration, stronger accent, a wider register, or harmonic urgency—and then for Respighi's ways of restoring space. The important event is often a redistribution of energy rather than the arrival of an entirely new tune.

In the slow movement, begin with the piano. Its opening establishes a poised expressive space into which the violin enters. Ask whether the violin is revealing what the piano already implied, intensifying it, or changing its emotional temperature. When the music becomes more impassioned and mobile, the return of the opening character should not sound like a reset. It carries the memory of the disturbance.

In the finale, identify the grave, deliberate foundation before tracking surface display. The first statement gives a reference against which later changes can be felt. Each new span alters some combination of register, texture, pace, accent, harmony, and division of labor. Do not reduce the movement to “the same bass repeated.” Its drama comes from how clearly recurrence can remain perceptible while its expressive meaning changes.

Across all three movements, notice the balance between sweep and articulation. Respighi's textures invite a listener to experience large waves, but the score's changes of tempo, character, and agency prevent a single undifferentiated surge. A convincing map should preserve both scales.

## Movement I: lyric motion under pressure

The *Moderato* opens in a compound, gently rocking rhythmic world that can suggest a siciliano without becoming a stylized museum object. The quality is important because it gives the movement a pliant baseline. Even before overt agitation, the music is already moving; lyricism is carried by pulse rather than laid over stasis.

The violin's role is not simply “main melody.” Its line teaches the ear how to connect across the piano's more articulated motion. When the piano thickens, the violin may preserve directional continuity by sustaining or spanning longer gestures. At other moments the piano seizes rhetorical initiative through registral expansion or accumulated figuration. This exchange is the form's audible engine.

The movement's more agitated regions should be heard as transformations of its opening disposition. Rhythmic pressure becomes more explicit, phrase endings feel less settled, and both instruments occupy a larger sonic space. The emotional contrast is real, but the movement does not become a sequence of unrelated tableaux. Its recurring characters—supple lyricism, urgency, relaxation, renewed assertion—remain mutually intelligible.

For score study, mark three kinds of transition: where tempo language changes, where the violin and piano exchange foreground, and where accompaniment pattern changes before thematic identity becomes obvious. These layers rarely turn at exactly the same instant. That nonalignment is one reason the music feels continuously developing. The listener may sense that a new phase has begun before being able to point to a cadential boundary.

The practical ensemble question is consequently one of hierarchy. In a dense passage, which line supplies direction? In a lyrical return, is the piano merely quieter, or does an inner voice prepare the next harmonic move? The score rewards attention to these roles without requiring a universal prescription about balance or technique.

## Movement II: expression as altered return

The *Andante espressivo* offers repose, but its repose is active. The piano's opening is not empty staging for a soloist; it establishes the harmonic and rhetorical conditions of the violin's entrance. This makes the movement a compact laboratory in partnership. Follow the piano line through the violin melody and the violin line through the piano's changes, rather than hearing tune plus background.

Respighi builds intensity by changing several parameters together. The music grows more passionate, motion increases, and register and texture widen. Yet the central contrast does not abolish the opening world. When quieter material returns, it has acquired distance: what initially sounded poised may now sound recovered, remembered, or temporarily sheltered.

One can hear the movement as a large inhalation and exhalation, but that metaphor should not blur its internal joints. The score's character and tempo language indicates degrees of animation; the performers must negotiate when a new impulse belongs to one player and when it is shared. The listener can test this by asking who first destabilizes the calm. Sometimes harmony in the piano changes the horizon before the violin's surface rhetoric catches up.

The violin's upper register contributes radiance and vulnerability, but it should not be mistaken for the whole expressive story. A high point becomes meaningful because the piano has built a route to it and because both instruments must find a route away. The movement's craft lies as much in release as in climax.

## Movement III: passacaglia as destination

The finale's title announces its governing idea. A passacaglia traditionally constructs continuity through recurrence, commonly around a bass or harmonic pattern. Respighi uses that inheritance as a dramatic resource rather than an antiquarian rulebook. The opening presents the foundation with weight and definition; subsequent variations make that ground support contrasting textures and affects.

The most useful listening question is not “Can I count every variation?” but “What remains recognizable, and by what means?” At some points the bass profile may be prominent. Elsewhere harmony, phrase length, cadence, or the expected point of return carries identity while the surface becomes crowded. The ground is thus both an object and a memory. Once learned, it can govern even when it is not the loudest layer.

Variation changes the partnership repeatedly. The piano can state or imply the foundation while the violin elaborates; the violin can contribute structural accents rather than uninterrupted song; the two can lock into a shared rhythmic profile; or density can turn them into a composite sonority. These arrangements matter more than a simplistic opposition between accompaniment and solo.

The movement's marking, *Allegro moderato ma energico*, guards against two mishearings. “Moderato” resists a breathless race that would erase the ground's weight, while “energico” resists treating historical form as solemn stasis. The music needs forward force and structural legibility at once. That double demand is the finale's expressive thesis.

As the variations accumulate, the old form permits modern volatility. Meter, texture, and mood can change sharply because recurrence supplies coherence beneath them. The finale therefore resolves a problem posed throughout the sonata: how can large Romantic surges remain intelligible when local character changes quickly? Its answer is not a return to simplicity. It is to make constraint generate abundance.

The ending's force derives from accumulated recognition. What sounds affirmative is not merely louder or faster than the opening; it gathers the history of the ground's transformations. The listener hears the foundation as something capable of bearing lyricism, conflict, compression, and collective drive.

## Violin writing as expressive function

The work's violin writing ranges from long cantabile spans to energetic articulation, registral ascent, chordal emphasis, and rapid figuration. These are not detachable hurdles. Each mode answers a formal need. Sustained line can bind a harmonically active field; repeated or articulated figures can intensify pulse; chordal writing can add structural mass; high register can expose or crown a phrase.

That distinction protects the guide from prescriptive technique. The score may demand formidable control, but the historically and analytically useful question is what the writing communicates in context. A fingering or bowing in a later performer edition may solve a local problem, yet it is not automatically evidence of Respighi's intention. Readers should compare the 1919 score and separate part, identify editorial additions explicitly, and consult any modern critical apparatus available to them.

The passacaglia especially discourages a technique-list reading. A surface device acquires meaning from its position over the returning structure. Similar figuration can sound expectant early, combative during intensification, or conclusive near the end. Context changes character.

## Two ensemble decision points

The first decision point is the opening movement's transition from supple lyric motion into agitation. If one listens only to the violin, the change may seem to begin with an overtly intensified gesture. The piano, however, can prepare it through harmonic pressure and texture. An ensemble account must decide where the transition becomes inevitable, not merely where it becomes loud.

The second is the passacaglia's growing density. The ground must remain cognitively available even when it is not foregrounded. This does not require pounding every bass note. It requires agreement about phrase length, structural arrival, and which voice carries identity at each stage. The balance problem is therefore informational: the listener needs enough evidence to remember the foundation while hearing genuine transformation.

## Edition and recording boundaries

The accessible historical reference is the 1919 Ricordi print described by Cini and the Roma Tre library. It includes score and separate violin part. Those two artifacts should be compared, because bowings, fingerings, slurs, dynamics, and even corrections may differ in provenance. The Cini object's handwritten annotations are not a license to generalize; their author and date must be established first.

Modern recordings can demonstrate different solutions to continuity, pacing, balance, and the finale's weight, but this guide did not audition audio and therefore makes no claims about particular performances. The Naxos and Hyperion booklets cited below are used for documented identity, movement, publisher, and reception context, not as substitutes for listening evidence. A future comparison should identify recording date, edition, and any cuts before attributing interpretive differences.

## Why it matters

Respighi's sonata widens the Romantic-violin map in three directions. It places Italian instrumental culture beyond the familiar virtuoso showpiece; it makes the piano an equal bearer of orchestral weight and formal direction; and it shows historical form operating as a future-facing compositional resource. The passacaglia does not cancel Romantic expression. It gives expression a durable frame in which change can be heard as transformation rather than succession.

For a listener coming from Franck or Brahms, the work offers a productive comparison. All three sonatas seek large continuity, but Respighi places unusual weight on an explicitly recurring finale structure. For a listener moving toward interwar neoclassicism and modernism, it demonstrates that engagement with older music did not require emotional austerity. Historical consciousness and saturated rhetoric could coexist.
