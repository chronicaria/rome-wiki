Joachim’s and Kreisler’s cadenzas do not merely offer different quantities of virtuosity: Joachim turns Beethoven’s orchestral argument into a violin-borne thematic synthesis, while Kreisler’s compact, densely marked 1928 text makes a later virtuoso’s rhetorical timing and instrumental fluency unusually explicit.

## The comparison requires named texts

Beethoven left a visible opening in the first movement of his [[Beethoven Violin Concerto Op. 61]], but he did not leave a standard cadenza for solo violin. He did write cadenzas for the concerto’s later piano-and-orchestra version, Op. 61a, including the famous first-movement cadenza with timpani. Those are Beethoven compositions for piano, not missing violin pages. Every violin cadenza therefore adds another authorial and instrumental layer, even when it quotes Beethoven closely.

“The Joachim cadenza” is an especially unstable label. Joachim wrote and revised several first-movement cadenzas over decades. The identifiable public witness used here is *Cadenzen zu Beethovens Violin-Concert für die Violine allein componirt von Joseph Joachim*, published in Berlin by Schlesinger in 1894, plate S. 8599. Its eleven printed pages contain cadenzas for all three movements and two alternatives for the first. The first, longer first-movement text occupies four pages; the print then labels another “Kadenz zum ersten Satz (kürzere Bearbeitung).” The set also contains a second-movement cadenza and two finale *Eingänge*, or lead-ins.

That 1894 print must not be confused with the version in volume 3 of Joseph Joachim and Andreas Moser’s *Violinschule* (Simrock, 1905). Christina M. De Simone identifies the pedagogical version as a revision of a cadenza composed in 1852. Recent work has made the family still larger. Steven Zohn reports two complete autograph witnesses of the 64-bar cadenza associated with Joachim’s youthful Beethoven performances: an album leaf for Julius Rietz and a version in the Willem Willeke collection. Zohn distinguishes this 1844 family from a 73-bar 1852 cadenza published by Carl Haslinger in 1853, an unpublished related fragment, the abbreviated 43-bar version later printed in the *Violinschule*, and a substantially new 68-bar cadenza published by Rózsavölgyi in 1885. Any claim about “Joachim’s opening” or “Joachim’s length” is meaningless unless the witness is named.

The Kreisler object is more compact bibliographically. *Three Cadenzas for the L. van Beethoven Violin Concerto Op. 61*, by Fritz Kreisler, was published in New York by Charles Foley in 1928, plate 24647. The title page dedicates it “To my dear wife.” The print contains one cadenza for each movement. Beethoven-Haus records the continuing Schott issue as ED 1446, with 1928 copyright, renewal in 1956, plate 53056, and ISMN 979-0-001-03320-6. Thus the familiar modern Schott item transmits Kreisler’s twentieth-century performer-composer text; its binding beside Beethoven does not make its dynamics, fingerings, character words, or notes Beethoven’s.

This study compares the 1894 Schlesinger Joachim print with the 1928 Foley Kreisler print. It uses De Simone and Zohn to situate Joachim’s variants. It does **not** claim that either print records every alteration its author made in performance, or that a recording using the author’s name necessarily follows the print note for note. That separation follows [[Violin source layers from autograph to performer edition]].

## The fixed dramatic problem

The first movement’s cadenza arrives late, after Beethoven has spent an immense span distributing agency among orchestra, soloist, and individual winds. The quiet repeated-note timpani idea heard at the opening has migrated through the movement. The solo violin has entered not as an immediate conqueror but as a new high-register viewpoint on an already established orchestral world. At the cadential six-four and fermata, the orchestra stops and the soloist inherits the problem of making a return to the closing tutti persuasive.

A cadenza can respond in several ways. It can treat the pause as an exhibition external to the movement; summarize themes already associated with the soloist; appropriate material previously owned by the orchestra; revisit surprising tonal regions; or use idiomatic display to intensify the dominant and final trill. Joachim and Kreisler both bind the interpolation to Beethoven’s movement by recalling its materials. The contrast lies less between “thematic” and “virtuosic” than in how thematic memory becomes violin rhetoric.

The repeated-note idea is a decisive test. Quoting it does more than certify knowledge of the concerto. Because the motive begins with timpani and circulates orchestrally, giving it to one unaccompanied violin changes its agent and color. The cadenza can make the soloist remember the orchestra, impersonate it, or temporarily absorb it. That is why the opening motive’s treatment has formal meaning beyond clever reference.

## Joachim: a soloist carrying the ritornello

The long first-movement version in the 1894 Schlesinger print begins with double-stopped statements of the repeated-note figure. The violin sounds more than one pitch at once, so the orchestral signal enters as a harmonized object rather than a bare tap. The page then develops a dialogue among emphatic chords, triplet motion, arpeggiation, and thematic recollection. The shorter alternative is not simply the same page with measures deleted: it presents a separately shaped solution, which is precisely why “long” and “short” should accompany any edition credit.

De Simone’s analysis of the related *Violinschule* first-movement version explains the larger compositional principle. Joachim uses material from the opening orchestral ritornello that the soloist does not play in Beethoven’s movement. In particular, he draws on the disruptive B-flat-major outburst from Beethoven’s first tutti. In the parent movement this passage interrupts a lyrical environment and belongs to the orchestra; in the cadenza it becomes initiating solo material. The move gives the violin access to the concerto’s public, forceful side rather than restricting it to recollections of its own earlier melodies.

Joachim also does more than quote. De Simone traces the repeated-note motive at the cadenza’s opening, a move from B major toward B-flat major, a later transformation of the violin’s initial entrance in an unstable diminished-seventh setting, and subsequent presentation of that entrance material in the flat submediant. The flat submediant therefore becomes a secondary tonal focus linking orchestral and solo-associated materials. Her conclusion is careful and useful: the cadenza develops Beethoven’s cells in a quasi-developmental way, yet can also behave recapitulatively by recovering material and relationships that the movement has left behind.

The result deserves the term **symphonic**, provided it is defined rather than used as praise. Joachim asks one violin to evoke multiple agents through double stops, registral contrast, chordal writing, and the transfer of ritornello material into the solo domain. “Symphonic” here does not mean that a violin literally reproduces Beethoven’s orchestration. It means that the cadenza remembers the orchestra–soloist dialectic and compresses several of its roles into a single line and multi-stop texture.

The newly recovered youthful cadenza shows that thematic synthesis was not merely a late pedagogical ideal. Zohn’s table of the 64-bar text identifies quotations and combinations from the soloist’s initial entrance, the second theme, the timpani motive, accompanimental figures, and the first ritornello. Particularly striking are double-stopped triplets that combine the second theme, timpani motive, and solo accompaniment, followed later by the timpani motive under trills. This is not the same text as the 1894 Schlesinger long version, but it documents a durable Joachim question: how can one violin make several layers of Beethoven’s movement present at once?

Joachim’s historical position amplifies the question without converting his text into Beethoven’s. His 1844 London performance under Mendelssohn was central to the concerto’s nineteenth-century acceptance. Zohn shows that Joachim brought his own cadenzas to that event, and contemporary reception praised their invention and thematic coherence. His authority is therefore reception-historical: he helped establish what the work could mean and how it could survive in concert. He was not Beethoven’s collaborator, dedicatee, or premiere soloist, and his later printed revisions remain Joachim compositions.

## Kreisler: notated virtuoso rhetoric as a complete set

Kreisler’s 1928 print immediately declares a different kind of authorial presence. The first-movement cadenza begins *Nobilmente* and forte. It launches upward through widely spaced chords and rapid passagework before settling into sequences and reiterated figures. The character word is not Beethoven’s; it is Kreisler’s instruction for how this inserted voice should enter Beethoven’s formal pause. “Nobly” frames brilliance as elevated public speech, not casual improvisatory play.

Across the three-page first-movement cadenza, the Foley print makes instrumental execution unusually visible. Fingerings, open-string indications, string numerals, accents, slurs, expressive words, dynamic changes, and tempo modifications fill the page. These details are evidence for Kreisler’s composed performing text, not a transcription of Beethoven’s wishes or a universal recipe for Kreisler’s actual sound. They coordinate rapid triplet patterns, double stops, chords, arpeggiated spans, register changes, and lyrical relief into an executable argument.

The architecture depends on contrast. Broad chordal calls and fast scalar or arpeggiated lines establish range; a *dolce e espress.* passage softens the rhetoric and gives melodic writing space within the surrounding motion. Later, repeated high figures are placed against accented lower notes, followed by chordal and bariolage-like textures in which a sustained or recurrent pitch can be heard through rapid alternation. Near the close, Kreisler marks *molto espress.* and then *poco a poco rit.* as the texture narrows toward trills. These observations come from the identified 1928 print. They do not establish a fixed duration or prove how Kreisler or any later violinist timed them.

Kreisler’s first-movement text recalls Beethoven while foregrounding the means by which a twentieth-century virtuoso shapes recall. Its chordal density and rapid figuration can make the solitary violin sound self-sufficient, but lyrical islands keep display from becoming one undifferentiated acceleration. The cadenza’s route to the final trill is strongly curated on the page: expressive labels and practical markings script changes of demeanor as well as notes.

The set’s other two pieces clarify that Kreisler conceived cadenza authorship across the whole concerto. The second-movement page begins *Andante*, moves into a notated *più vivo* section, and eventually marks *poco a poco rit.* before its closing gesture. It is a bridge at a fermata, not a replacement for the Larghetto. Its changes of speed and register turn the transition toward the finale into a miniature rhetorical arc.

The third-movement cadenza begins with accented low-register gestures that grow into double stops and denser figuration. The page belongs to the rondo’s world of return and kinetic exchange rather than importing the first movement’s exact rhetoric. Calling all three items “the Kreisler cadenza” hides this functional differentiation. A useful performance credit names the movement or says “Kreisler cadenzas” for the complete set.

## What changes when the texts are exchanged

Joachim’s characteristic question is **who owns the concerto’s material?** By transferring ritornello material to the soloist and placing themes in new tonal relationships, he lets the violin temporarily carry an orchestral memory. The cadenza becomes an analytical resumé: not a neutral synopsis, but a recomposition that makes the concerto’s opposition of tutti and solo newly audible.

Kreisler’s characteristic question is **how can a composed cadenza control virtuoso address?** The 1928 page specifies bearing, fingering, string use, dynamic profile, expressive contrast, and changes of pace. The violin’s instrumental identity is not concealed in the name of abstract fidelity. It is the medium through which Beethoven’s formal gap acquires a twentieth-century public voice.

Neither description supports a hierarchy of seriousness. Joachim’s multiple stops and developmental transformations are virtuoso acts. Kreisler’s dense idiom also serves memory, contrast, and return. Nor should one assume that Joachim is “historical” while Kreisler is “modern”: Joachim’s 1894 publication is already a late witness to decades of revision, and Kreisler’s 1928 set participates in an inherited nineteenth-century cadenza culture. The difference is one of documented solutions, not a contest between fidelity and ego.

For listening, hold four questions constant. First, how soon does each cadenza establish a recognizable Beethoven motive? Second, does thematic material sound quoted, fragmented, combined, or transformed? Third, how does the violin alternate among single line, chords, double stops, and rapid figuration? Fourth, what makes the final trill and orchestral return feel prepared? Those questions permit comparison without pretending that the score alone fixes pacing, tone, or spontaneity.

For source study, record at minimum: Joachim long or short 1894 version, *Violinschule* version, another dated Joachim witness, or Kreisler 1928/Schott transmission; the edition and plate number; movement; any performer cuts or recomposition; and whether the evidence comes from score or recording. A printed cadenza is textual evidence. A documented performance is sonic evidence. The two should be compared, never silently merged.

## Why it matters

The Joachim–Kreisler comparison turns an apparently optional flourish into a lesson about how repertory is made. Beethoven composed the surrounding movement and left no standard solo-violin cadenza. Joachim, Kreisler, publishers, teachers, and performers filled that space with texts that became recognizable parts of the work’s public identity. The concerto therefore remains stable enough to be Beethoven’s and open enough to carry audible histories of later violin playing.

It also corrects a misleading use of “authentic.” Joachim has exceptional authority in the concerto’s nineteenth-century revival, but not Beethovenian authorship. Kreisler’s cadenza can be customary without becoming original text. Beethoven’s Op. 61a cadenza is authorial but pianistic, so a violin realization remains arranger-mediated. These are different kinds of proximity, and [[Cadenza authorship and concerto identity]] supplies the broader vocabulary for keeping them apart.

Most importantly, each cadenza changes how the orchestra is remembered just before it returns. Joachim lets the violin absorb and recombine the concerto’s divided agents. Kreisler writes a highly specified virtuoso monologue whose changes of bearing, texture, and pace point back toward ensemble time. Choosing between them is therefore not choosing decoration. It is choosing which reception history will complete Beethoven’s first-movement arc tonight.
