An early disc documents one identified performance under recording constraints; it does not by itself define an era or national school.

Up: [[Romantic violin repertoire]]

## The evidence packet

Before interpreting phrasing, identify performer, accompanist, date, place, label, catalogue and matrix numbers, take, side length, transfer, and speed or pitch uncertainty. The Library of Congress record for Maud Powell's 1911 “Le cygne,” for example, distinguishes catalogue 64265, matrix B-11147, take 2, a ten-inch side, and the recording date. Those fields make comparison reproducible.

Acoustic recording also shaped the event. Balance was organized around a horn, duration was bounded by a side, and an error could require a complete retake. Surviving sound can support passage-specific observations about timing, articulation, portamento, or vibrato, but frequency response and transfer choices narrow what can be claimed about tone color and dynamics.

## Responsible inference

Treat one take as evidence of what this performer did here. A claim about personal habit needs repeated examples; a lineage claim also needs documentary connections; a national-school claim requires multiple performers and institutions plus counterexamples. Never infer an inaudible distinction or generalize from a noisy transfer.
