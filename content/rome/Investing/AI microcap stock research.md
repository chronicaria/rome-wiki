AI microcaps should be researched as public-market venture bets where disclosure quality, dilution, liquidity, and promotion risk matter as much as the AI thesis.


## Define the universe

Separate three buckets:

- Small/mid-cap AI-adjacent companies: real businesses, still volatile, usually not "penny stocks."
- Exchange-listed microcaps: more disclosure and listing standards, but often fragile.
- OTC microcaps/nanocaps: highest information risk, liquidity risk, and promotion risk.

The SEC describes microcaps as companies often under roughly $250-300 million in market capitalization, and nanocaps as often under $50 million. Penny stocks are usually low-priced shares, often under $5, but "penny stock" and "microcap" are not identical.

## Source stack

Use screeners only to discover names. The actual research should come from primary documents:

- SEC EDGAR: 10-K, 10-Q, 8-K, S-1/S-3, DEF 14A, Form 4, 13D/G.
- OTC Markets page: market tier, disclosure status, warnings, quote/liquidity context.
- Company materials: investor deck, website, product docs, customer case studies, pricing, demos.
- Independent checks: customer references, patents, GitHub activity if relevant, job postings, litigation search, management history.
- Promotion checks: paid newsletters, social media spikes, press-release mills, unexplained volume.

## AI reality test

For every company claiming "AI," answer:

- What is the actual product?
- Is the model core to the business, or a marketing adjective?
- What proprietary data, distribution, workflow integration, regulation, or switching cost could create a moat?
- Are there named customers, recurring revenue, deferred revenue, retention data, or paid pilots?
- What are the inference/training costs, and do gross margins survive real usage?
- Why would this company beat hyperscalers, incumbents, open-source models, or better-funded startups?

If the answer is mostly press-release language, assume the market is selling theme exposure rather than business quality.

## Financial survival test

The core question is not "could this 10x?" It is "can this company survive long enough without destroying shareholders?"

Check:

- Cash runway: cash divided by recent operating cash burn.
- Gross margin and whether AI usage makes it better or worse.
- Share count history over several years.
- Warrants, convertibles, toxic financing, ATM programs, equity lines, and preferred shares.
- Auditor language: going-concern warnings, internal-control weaknesses, auditor changes.
- Related-party transactions and executive compensation.
- Reverse splits, name changes, and repeated pivots into hot themes.

Fully diluted market cap matters more than share price. A stock at $0.50 can be expensive if the capital structure is a mess.

## Market microstructure test

For a speculative position, the ability to exit matters.

- Use average daily dollar volume, not just share volume.
- Check bid-ask spread as a percentage of price.
- Use limit orders.
- Avoid positions that would take multiple normal trading days to exit without becoming the market.
- Treat sudden volume on no fundamental news as a warning until proven otherwise.

## Hard-no filters

Immediate rejection is reasonable if any of these show up:

- No current, credible public financials.
- The AI claim cannot be tied to a product, revenue line, or technical asset.
- Paid promotion is louder than business execution.
- Insider or promoter selling is the main visible activity.
- Liquidity is too thin for the intended position size.
- Dilution is structurally built into the financing.
- Management has a pattern of failed reverse mergers, name changes, sanctions, or promotional pivots.

## Before-buy memo

Every buy should fit on one page:

- Ticker, market cap, fully diluted market cap, cash, debt, burn, runway.
- What the company actually sells.
- Why the market might be mispricing it.
- The next two catalysts.
- What would falsify the thesis.
- Maximum dollar loss.
- Exit rule for success and failure.
- Date for review.

This turns the speculation into a small research lab, which fits the [[Quant research]] path better than impulse trading does.
