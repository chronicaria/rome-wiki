Election results pass through distinct evidentiary and legal stages: media calls interpret incomplete returns, canvass and certification make the administrative result official, recounts re-tabulate under state law, contests adjudicate legal claims, and each house of Congress retains constitutional authority over its own membership.

## Why it matters

On election night, several sentences may all be true at once: one candidate leads the reported count; a news organization has called the race; many ballots remain unprocessed; no official has certified a winner; and the trailing candidate retains legal options. Collapsing those sentences into “the result” produces either false certainty or false doubt.

The stages serve different purposes. Unofficial returns give the public timely information. A decision desk asks whether the trailing candidate still has a realistic mathematical path. The canvass reconciles the election records. Certification formally attests the result under state law. A recount checks tabulation. A contest asks a tribunal to resolve alleged legal defects. For a congressional seat, Article I ultimately makes the House or Senate the judge of its members' elections, returns, and qualifications.

These distinctions matter to chamber-control reporting. A projected winner can make control apparent without making it certified. A narrow uncertified race can leave the arithmetic unresolved without implying misconduct. A recount can be legally required without making the first count suspect. And filing a contest establishes that a claim exists—not that the claim is meritorious or that the certified result has been displaced.

## The status ladder

A reliable race ledger should use explicit statuses rather than one “winner” field:

| Status | What it establishes | What it does not establish |
|---|---|---|
| Reported unofficial returns | Votes currently tabulated and reported by election offices or a data collector | Complete count or legal finality |
| Media race call | An organization's analytical conclusion that no opponent can overtake the leader | Government certification |
| Local canvass complete | Reconciliation and official aggregation at the local level under governing procedures | Necessarily the final statewide certificate |
| Certified result | The designated official or board has attested the official result | Immunity from recount, contest, or congressional review |
| Recount complete | Ballots were re-tabulated under the applicable recount law | Resolution of every claim about eligibility or election conduct |
| Contest resolved | The authorized court, tribunal, or legislative chamber disposed of pleaded claims | A general audit of every aspect of the election |
| Member seated | The chamber accepted the credentials and administered the oath, subject to its rules and constitutional authority | That no later internal proceeding is possible |

Not every state uses identical labels or orders. A recount may occur before certification, after certification, or both in different forms. A court order can alter a canvass. A certificate can be amended. The ledger must follow the statute and official process for the state and office rather than assume a universal national sequence.

## Unofficial returns are real data, not final data

Election-night totals generally originate with local election officials. They can change as jurisdictions add mail ballots, provisional ballots, cured ballots, late-arriving ballots permitted by law, and corrected uploads. A precinct-reporting percentage is not necessarily a ballot-completion percentage: precincts differ in size, and some ballot categories are not naturally represented as election-day precincts.

“Outstanding vote” is itself an estimate until officials know how many submitted ballots satisfy legal requirements. A mail ballot may await signature review; a provisional ballot may depend on eligibility research; a voter may have a statutory cure period. These ballots are not surprise votes merely because their adjudication occurs after election night. They are part of the ordinary process defined before the election.

Updates can move in a partisan direction because ballot methods and geography are not random. If one county reports election-day voting before mail voting, its early margin may not resemble its final margin. A raw lead should therefore be paired with what remains: jurisdiction, ballot type, approximate quantity, applicable receipt or cure deadlines, and how much of that vote the trailing candidate would need.

The phrase **unofficial result** describes legal status, not data quality. Election officials often publish careful, accurate preliminary totals. The label signals that reconciliation, canvass, and certification are unfinished and that lawful additions or corrections remain possible.

## A media call is an analytical judgment

The United States has no single national election authority that announces winners in every congressional race on election night. News organizations collect official local returns and operate decision desks. The Associated Press says it declares a winner when its decision team concludes that trailing candidates no longer have a path to victory; it considers reported votes, remaining ballot types and locations, historical patterns, and other election data.

Organizations differ in terminology. Some say “call,” “project,” or “declare.” AP explicitly avoids the word “projection” for its own calls, but all such decisions remain nongovernmental analytical judgments. A call can be highly reliable and socially consequential without being a certificate. A candidate's victory declaration or concession is also political speech, not the legal act that completes the canvass.

The decision problem is asymmetric. Calling too early risks naming the wrong winner; waiting until certification would withhold a conclusion long after the remaining vote could no longer alter the outcome. A sound call asks whether any plausible composition of outstanding eligible ballots could erase the margin. It does not require every vote to be counted. A landslide can be called with a small reported fraction; a near tie can remain uncalled after almost all ballots are tabulated.

Race-call disagreement is possible because organizations use different data feeds, turnout estimates, thresholds, and review rules. Report which organization made the call and when. Do not turn “AP called” into “officials certified,” or “not yet called” into “the count is tied.”

AP also distinguishes **too early to call** from **too close to call**. It calls a race too early while regular ballot tabulation remains active, even if counting will take days. It reserves too close for a tabulation that has reached what AP calls its primary conclusion—apart from categories such as provisional and late-arriving absentee ballots—without a clear winner. These are AP editorial classifications, not state-law statuses. Quoting the precise term prevents an ordinary unfinished count from being misreported as a near tie.

## Canvass reconciles the election record

The canvass is the administrative process that turns precinct and ballot records into the jurisdiction's official vote statement. The Election Assistance Commission describes canvass as the stage in which officials compile and review returns, confirm that required materials are present, reconcile quantities, investigate discrepancies, and aggregate results according to law.

Exact tasks vary, but a canvass may compare ballots cast with voter-credit or check-in records, review precinct tapes and memory devices, incorporate accepted provisional and cured ballots, correct transcription errors, and obtain explanations for discrepancies. This is not simply waiting for a computer to finish counting. It is a controlled reconciliation across records produced by decentralized election administration.

Local canvassing bodies often certify local returns and transmit them to a state canvassing authority. Statewide and multi-county contests are then aggregated at the state level. The National Association of Secretaries of State's state-law survey shows substantial variation in canvassing deadlines, responsible officials, automatic-recount thresholds, candidate-request rules, and payment provisions.

Normal canvass changes should be described precisely. “County added 2,400 valid mail ballots” is more informative than “vote total mysteriously changed.” Conversely, a reconciliation discrepancy deserves documentation rather than reflexive reassurance. The proper questions are what record differed, which rule governed the correction, who authorized it, and whether the updated official report explains the change.

## Certification creates the official administrative result

Certification is the formal act by which the legally designated official or board attests the results. It ordinarily follows completion of the required canvass. The certificate supplies governments and legislative chambers with the official return and credentials on which they initially act.

Certification is not a prediction and is not performed by a news organization. Nor is it a declaration that no error is imaginable. It means the responsible authority completed the duties state law assigns at that stage. Whether the certifying official has discretion, and what happens if an official refuses, are questions of the particular statute and any controlling court order—not a generic power to revisit the electorate's choice.

Deadlines differ across states and offices. House races contained within one state follow that state's canvass and certification calendar; Senate races are statewide. A national chamber table that labels every projected winner “certified” on one date will be wrong. Store the certificate date and source for every contest.

Certification can coexist with later proceedings. Some recounts occur after it. A court can order corrections or a new certificate where authorized. Congress can examine credentials and contests. Thus **certified** is a strong official status, but not the same proposition as **incapable of legal challenge**.

## Audits and recounts answer different questions

A post-election audit evaluates some aspect of election equipment, records, or tabulation under a prescribed procedure, often by manually examining a sample of voter-verifiable records. A risk-limiting audit is designed to provide statistical evidence that the reported outcome is correct, escalating toward a fuller hand count when the evidence is insufficient. Audit design and legal consequence vary by state.

A recount re-tabulates ballots in a specified contest. It may be automatic when the margin falls within a statutory threshold, requested by a candidate or voter, or ordered by a court. State law controls timing, eligible requestors, deposits or costs, scope, counting method, standards for ballot review, observers, and whether the recount changes the certified return.

Neither procedure inherently alleges fraud. An automatic recount is triggered by arithmetic. A candidate-requested recount may be a statutory right exercised because the margin is small. Most recounts make small changes because the same ballots are being counted under established standards; “small” cannot be assumed in advance, and the legally completed recount controls where the law says it does.

Recount coverage must name the trigger and denominator. “Within 0.5 percent” can mean a percentage of votes cast for certain candidates, total contest votes, or another statutory measure. The same numerical margin may qualify in one state and not another. Report who requested it, who pays initially, which ballots are reviewed, and whether certification is stayed or amended.

A recount ordinarily answers “What is the correct tabulation of the ballots within scope?” It does not necessarily answer whether a candidate was eligible, whether unlawful conduct invalidated votes, or whether an election should be annulled. Those are potential contest questions.

## An election contest is an adversarial legal claim

An election contest invokes a statutory or constitutional procedure to challenge the right to office or the validity of a result. Grounds, deadlines, pleading requirements, evidence, burden of proof, remedies, and tribunal all depend on the office and governing law. Possible allegations include candidate ineligibility, erroneous inclusion or exclusion of ballots, misconduct, or irregularities alleged to have affected the result.

A contest is not a synonym for recount. It may use recount evidence, seek inspection of ballots, or request correction, exclusion, inclusion, or a new election. A court does not normally treat every administrative imperfection as outcome-changing. The claimant must meet the relevant legal standard and connect proved facts to an authorized remedy.

Reporting should separate three levels:

1. **Allegation:** what the pleading asserts.
2. **Evidence and ruling:** what admissible evidence the tribunal credited and what legal conclusions it reached.
3. **Remedy and status:** whether totals, certification, seating, or the election itself changed, and whether review remains.

“Candidate files challenge” establishes only level one. Repeating allegations in declarative prose launders a party's claim into a fact. Conversely, dismissing a case merely because it is partisan ignores that election-contest systems exist to resolve genuine disputes. The pleading, docket, orders, and final judgment are the primary sources.

## House contests have a federal statutory channel

Article I, Section 5 says each house is the judge of the elections, returns, and qualifications of its own members. For House elections, the Federal Contested Elections Act supplies the principal modern procedure for a candidate to bring a contest to the House. The statute, codified at 2 U.S.C. §§ 381–396, defines contestant and contestee, sets notice and answer procedures, permits depositions and evidence gathering, and leads to a record for House consideration.

The Act is not a federal court trial and does not transfer the House's final constitutional judgment to an agency. It applies to official general or special elections for Representative, Delegate, or Resident Commissioner—not party primaries. CRS explains that a House contest can also arise through other parliamentary routes, but the Act is the principal candidate-initiated channel in modern practice.

State recounts and House authority can coexist. Constitution Annotated's discussion of *Roudebush v. Hartke* explains that a state recount does not prevent the Senate from independently judging an election because the recount does not bind the chamber. The same structural point matters for Congress generally: state processes develop the official return and evidentiary record; Article I preserves the relevant house's final judgment over membership.

## Senate contests follow Senate constitutional practice

The Federal Contested Elections Act is a House statute. The Senate instead relies on Article I, its rules, committees, precedents, and resolutions. The Senate's official history says it has developed its own procedures for settling contested elections and guards the power to judge qualifications and returns.

A Senate dispute may be referred to the Committee on Rules and Administration or handled under a resolution tailored to the case. The Senate can examine ballots and evidence, authorize an investigation or recount, decide whom to seat, or determine that no candidate is entitled to the seat. Historical procedures are instructive, but they do not create one automatic modern timetable equivalent to the House Act.

This difference must appear in reporting. “Congressional contest” is not one uniform federal lawsuit. A House candidate invoking 2 U.S.C. chapter 12 and a Senate claimant petitioning the Senate operate through different channels, even though both ultimately implicate Article I.

## Seating, exclusion, and qualifications

Credentials based on state certification ordinarily support initial seating, but the chamber's constitutional judgment remains. Article I identifies age, citizenship, and inhabitancy qualifications for representatives and senators, while Section 5 assigns judging authority to each house. Congress cannot casually invent additional constitutional qualifications; it can judge whether the fixed qualifications and election requirements are met.

The distinction between excluding a member-elect who lacks qualifications and expelling a seated member matters. Expulsion requires a two-thirds vote under Article I, Section 5. A contested-election resolution concerns entitlement to the seat, not punishment by expulsion. These doctrines can become legally complex, so a live case should cite the chamber's resolution, committee report, and controlling judicial precedent rather than rely on slogans.

Administration of the oath is an important status marker, but a chamber may preserve a contest after seating a member. Seating can allow representation while an investigation continues; it need not predetermine the final merits. The exact resolution and reservations control.

## How to report chamber control

Chamber-control claims inherit the status of their decisive seats. A useful ledger reports counts in parallel:

- **certified or otherwise legally final winners**;
- **projected winners not yet certified**;
- **leaders in uncalled races**;
- **races in recount or contest**, with certification status;
- **seated members** once the new Congress convenes.

If one party has enough called winners to cross the control threshold, it is reasonable to report that a named decision desk projects control. It is inaccurate to say all those wins are certified. If the threshold depends on an uncalled race, say control remains uncalled even if one party leads. If a recount is pending in a race whose outcome cannot change which bloc crosses the threshold, explain that the proceeding matters to the seat but not the present control projection.

AP's own balance-of-power rule is seat based: it says a party controls the House only after AP has declared 218 winners for that party, and controls the Senate after 51 winners—or 50 when the vice president is of that party. AP says it does not use a national statistical model to project eventual chamber control. Other decision desks may use different terminology or thresholds, so attribute the control call as carefully as the underlying race calls.

Do not subtract every contested seat from a certified count as if filing automatically vacated the certificate. Do not add every concession as if it were certification. And do not call control “official” without defining whether that means certified state returns, no outcome-determinative litigation, or chamber organization. Those are different milestones.

## A verification workflow

1. Record the state election authority, local sources, and governing calendar before voting.
2. Preserve unofficial totals with timestamps rather than silently overwriting them.
3. Track outstanding ballot categories, deadlines, and estimated quantities.
4. Attribute every race call to the organization and time that issued it.
5. Obtain canvass reports and the actual certification, not a campaign statement.
6. Read the recount statute before describing a trigger, threshold, scope, or payer.
7. Treat a contest filing as an allegation packet; follow the docket and orders.
8. For House contests, distinguish state litigation from the Federal Contested Elections Act record.
9. For Senate contests, follow Senate committee and floor action rather than importing House procedure.
10. State chamber control at the weakest status of the decisive seat and update the label when that status changes.

This produces a record that can survive both speed and scrutiny. It gives timely projections their proper value, official administration its proper authority, and legal challenges their proper burden without confusing any one stage for all the others.
