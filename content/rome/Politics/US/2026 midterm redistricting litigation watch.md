The redistricting watch should classify the legally operative House map first, then separately flag litigation or legislative paths that could still change election administration before November.

## Summary

As of **July 10, 2026**, redistricting has already changed a large part of the 2026 House playing field, but not every lawsuit or special-session story should move the control map. The working rule is: **use the map that currently controls filing, primaries, and election administration unless an official order, statute, referendum certification, or election authority changes that status.** A case can be important without making a different map operative.

The cases and processes most capable of still affecting 2026 administration are:

- **Missouri:** 2025 map in effect while referendum signatures are reviewed; Aug. 4 is both the signature-review deadline identified by NCSL and the primary date. This is the clearest near-term procedural watch.
- **Florida:** May 2026 map is in effect for 2026, with state constitutional litigation ongoing and an August primary. Treat as operative-but-challenged, not settled on the merits.
- **Alabama:** the Supreme Court stayed a lower-court order on June 2, allowing Alabama's 2023 legislature-drawn map to be used while merits proceedings continue. Treat the 2023 map as operative under stay, not as a final merits victory.
- **Texas:** the Supreme Court stayed the injunction against Texas's 2025 map on Dec. 4, 2025; the map is operative for 2026 while appeal posture continues. Treat as operative under stay.
- **Louisiana:** the Supreme Court struck down the prior map in *Louisiana v. Callais*, and the state enacted a new congressional map on May 29, 2026 with postponed elections. Treat the new map as operative, while watching implementation dates and any remedial follow-on litigation.
- **Tennessee:** the May 2026 map is in effect and legal challenges have faced dismissal, but AP still reported pending challenges in its national synthesis. Treat as operative unless an appellate order revives a live map-changing path.

Several highly visible states should be classified as **not changing the 2026 map** absent extraordinary new action: **Virginia** remains on the 2021 special-master map after the state supreme court invalidated the 2026 amendment process; **Maryland** and **New York** are discussing constitutional amendments that, on current reporting and calendars, are about future cycles rather than 2026; **Georgia, Indiana, South Carolina, and Washington** did not enact usable new maps for 2026.

## Status taxonomy

The redistricting desk needs narrower labels than "pending" or "blocked." A House race note should inherit one of these statuses:

| Status | Meaning | Forecast discipline |
|---|---|---|
| Operative by enacted map | Legislature, commission, voters, or court adopted a map that election officials are using now. | Use the new lines for district baselines, candidate geography, and ratings. |
| Operative by stay | A challenged map is being used because a higher court stayed an injunction or remedial order. | Use the stayed map, but describe the stay rather than calling the merits resolved. |
| Operative by court remedy | A court-ordered or court-selected map controls election administration. | Use the court map and track appeal posture only if a stay or reversal could arrive in time. |
| Operative old map; future-cycle process | A state is debating or preparing a later redistricting path, but the 2026 calendar is not changing. | Do not change 2026 House baselines. Add a future-cycle note if needed. |
| Referendum or ballot process can still affect map | A valid referendum, signature dispute, or ballot measure could change whether enacted lines remain operative. | Track the exact certification deadline and whether the remedy would affect 2026 voting or only later cycles. |
| Litigation pending, no operative change | A complaint, appeal, or analysis exists, but no order has changed the map used by election officials. | Mention only in caveats; do not move a race rating from litigation alone. |
| Too late for 2026 absent extraordinary order | Legal or political interest remains, but filing, primary, or ballot deadlines make a 2026 change implausible. | Exclude from the 2026 control ledger unless an official emergency order changes the calendar. |

This taxonomy prevents two common errors. First, it avoids treating a complaint as a map. Second, it avoids treating a stay as a final merits decision. A stayed map can be the correct map for the election while the legal theory remains unresolved.

## Why the 2026 map is unusually fluid

The normal congressional-redistricting rhythm is once per decade after the census. The 2026 cycle is different because mid-decade partisan redistricting, Voting Rights Act litigation, state constitutional litigation, and remedial orders converged close to the midterm calendar.

The Supreme Court's April 29 decision in *Louisiana v. Callais* is the central federal-law event. The Court held that Louisiana's then-current congressional map was an unconstitutional racial gerrymander and updated how Section 2 Voting Rights Act claims interact with race-conscious districting. The practical consequence is not simply "Voting Rights Act weakened" or "racial-gerrymandering claims won." For election tracking, the consequence is that states and litigants are now testing which uses of race, partisan goals, and incumbent-protection goals survive under the post-*Callais* framework.

AP's June national synthesis captured the electoral stakes: Republicans believed new maps in Texas, Missouri, North Carolina, Ohio, Florida, Tennessee, Louisiana, and Alabama could produce a large House-seat gain if they performed as intended, while Democrats pointed to California and Utah as offsetting map gains. AP also noted that nearly 145 million people lived in states with new congressional districts for this election. That is a playing-field fact, not a result. A designed partisan advantage can fail because of turnout, candidate quality, local backlash, national environment, or overextension.

Brennan Center's litigation tracker is useful background because it shows how broad the post-2020 litigation wave has been: by its Dec. 17, 2025 update, maps in 30 states had been challenged, and congressional and/or legislative maps had been redrawn under court order in 13 states. But the tracker is not itself an operative-map source for a 2026 district note. The operative source remains a court order, enacted map, state election authority, or official ballot-calendar action.

## State-by-state watch list

### Alabama: operative by Supreme Court stay

**Current classification:** operative by stay; high electoral relevance; merits not final.

Alabama is a live example of why "operative" and "lawful on the merits" must be separate columns. After the earlier *Allen v. Milligan* litigation, Alabama used a court-ordered map that produced two Democratic-held seats with substantial Black electorates. In 2026, Alabama sought to return to a 2023 legislature-drawn map after *Callais*. A federal court blocked that move, but on **June 2, 2026** the Supreme Court stayed the lower-court order.

The stay order means Alabama's 2023 map is the map to use for 2026 administration unless another official order intervenes. The dissent emphasized that the switch could require rapid reassignment of voters and candidates, which underscores the administrative stakes, but the controlling point for the desk is narrower: the Supreme Court stay makes the 2023 map operative while review continues.

**House-watch implication:** rebuild Alabama baselines on the 2023 map, especially the Democratic-held seat affected by the prior court remedy. Do not describe the stay as a final holding that the map satisfies the Voting Rights Act or the Constitution.

### Florida: operative enacted map with state litigation ongoing

**Current classification:** operative enacted map; litigation pending, no current injunction.

NCSL records Florida's congressional map as enacted on **May 4, 2026**, with candidate filing deadlines moved to **June 12, 2026** and litigation ongoing. It states that the new maps will be in effect for the 2026 cycle and that the challenge argues the maps violate the Florida Constitution. State Court Report likewise covered a Florida judge's refusal to temporarily block the new congressional map.

That posture is control-relevant because AP reported that Florida's revised House districts improve Republican chances in several seats. But the legal status should remain restrained: the map is being used; the state constitutional case is not over; there is no current injunction changing the operative lines.

**House-watch implication:** use the May 2026 map for race baselines. Flag the pending Fair Districts-style state constitutional challenge in affected race notes, especially any seats whose partisan baseline changed sharply. A future order could matter because Florida's primary is Aug. 18, but litigation alone should not be counted as a likely map change.

### Louisiana: new post-*Callais* map and delayed election calendar

**Current classification:** operative enacted map after Supreme Court merits decision; watch implementation and any follow-on challenges.

*Louisiana v. Callais* struck down Louisiana's prior map as an unconstitutional racial gerrymander. NCSL reports that Louisiana enacted a new congressional map on **May 29, 2026**, with congressional primaries postponed from May 16 to **Nov. 3** and general elections set for **Dec. 12**. That calendar makes Louisiana unusual: its House contests may be resolved later than most other states, and the map change is tied directly to a Supreme Court merits decision rather than ordinary legislation alone.

The map status should not be described as merely "pending litigation." The prior map was invalidated, and a new map has been enacted for 2026. What remains important is implementation: candidate filing, primary mechanics, any remedial challenge, and whether the delayed calendar affects control arithmetic after Election Day.

**House-watch implication:** use the May 2026 map and separately track Louisiana's late primary/general calendar. Do not compare old LA-06 and new LA-06 as if the constituency is continuous.

### Missouri: enacted map plus referendum and signature-review uncertainty

**Current classification:** operative enacted map with live referendum/signature process that can still affect 2026 status.

Missouri is the cleanest procedural watch because the question is not only a merits lawsuit. NCSL reports that Missouri enacted congressional maps on **Sept. 28, 2025**, but opponents submitted signatures for a referendum seeking to repeal the maps. It also reports that the Missouri Supreme Court ruled on **May 12, 2026** that the 2025 maps would remain in effect while signatures are verified; the review process must be complete by **Aug. 4**, the same date as Missouri's congressional primaries. State Court Report's case tracker separately notes that the Missouri Supreme Court rejected a challenge arguing the state constitution prohibited mid-decade redistricting.

This creates two distinct Missouri entries. First, the state constitutional anti-mid-decade claim has been rejected. Second, the referendum/signature path remains a timing and ballot-status watch. Those should not be merged. The first reduces one litigation risk; the second can still create administrative uncertainty if the referendum qualifies and state law treats the map differently.

**House-watch implication:** use the 2025 map today, but review Missouri again on Aug. 4. The affected Kansas City-area district should carry a legal-status footnote until signature review and referendum certification are resolved.

### Tennessee: enacted map; litigation noise should be checked against orders

**Current classification:** operative enacted map; challenge status needs order-level verification before being treated as live.

NCSL reports Tennessee adopted new congressional maps on **May 7, 2026** and that the maps will be in effect for the 2026 cycle. State Court Report's May update said a three-judge panel dismissed the Tennessee state court challenge, finding the legislature had authority to engage in mid-decade redistricting. AP's June national synthesis still described legal challenges as pending.

The safest classification is not "settled" and not "likely to change." It is: operative map, with legal-challenge claims requiring docket-level verification before a race note treats the map as vulnerable. The affected electoral question is straightforward: AP reported the new Tennessee map improves Republican chances by carving up the lone Democratic-held, majority-Black Memphis-based district.

**House-watch implication:** use the May 2026 map. Add a caveat only if an appeal, stay request, or new order is identified from official state-court records.

### Texas: operative by Supreme Court stay

**Current classification:** operative by stay; merits not final.

Texas adopted a 2025 congressional map after a mid-decade push for partisan advantage. A federal district court enjoined the map, finding racial-gerrymandering defects, but the Supreme Court stayed that order on **Dec. 4, 2025** in *Abbott v. League of United Latin American Citizens*. The order allowed the Texas map to govern while appellate proceedings continue and emphasized election timing and Purcell-style concerns. NCSL consequently lists the 2025 Texas map as in effect for the 2026 cycle.

This is one of the most important examples of "operative by stay." It would be wrong to ignore the challenged map because the merits litigation continues. It would also be wrong to say the Supreme Court finally upheld the map. The stay controls election administration unless the Court or another competent court changes that posture.

**House-watch implication:** use the 2025 Texas map for all 2026 baselines and candidate-field work. Keep a merits caveat on districts materially redrawn around Latino and Democratic electorates, including seats AP identified as targeted by the redraw.

### Utah: operative court-remedial map

**Current classification:** operative by court remedy; appeal posture mostly de-risked for 2026.

Utah is not a pending emergency in the same way as Missouri or Florida, but it matters because it is one of the Democratic-offset maps. State Court Report summarizes the Utah litigation as a successful state constitutional challenge to the legislature's repeal and replacement of voter-approved redistricting reforms. The trial court struck the congressional map, the Utah Supreme Court denied emergency relief, and in November 2025 the court adopted an alternative map proposed by plaintiffs. NCSL reports that Utah's court-ordered map is in effect for 2026 and that the Utah Supreme Court ruled against lawmakers in February 2026.

**House-watch implication:** use the court-ordered map. Treat Utah as an already-realized map change, not as a pending litigation swing unless new official orders appear.

### Virginia: old map operative after failed amendment process

**Current classification:** operative old map; failed 2026 change.

Virginia is a caution against overstating proposed maps. State Court Report explains that the Virginia Supreme Court invalidated the process used to amend the state constitution to allow mid-decade congressional redistricting. The ruling left the 2021 special-master map operative for the 2026 midterms. NCSL likewise reports that the 2026 congressional maps were overturned by the state supreme court on **May 8, 2026** and that the 2021 maps are currently in effect.

**House-watch implication:** do not use the proposed Democratic-favorable map in the 2026 ledger. Use the 2021 special-master map unless a new, official, timely order changes the status.

### Maryland, New York, Georgia, Indiana, South Carolina, Washington: mostly future-cycle or failed 2026 efforts

These states should be watched for future-cycle consequences, not treated as current 2026 map movers.

Maryland is the most tempting to overstate because AP reported on July 8 that lawmakers will meet beginning **Aug. 3** to consider a constitutional amendment dealing with future congressional redistricting after an earlier 2026 map effort failed in the Senate. AP's reporting says any amendment would go to voters in November and could clear the way for a future map. NCSL's current tracker says new Maryland maps will not be in effect for 2026.

New York passed first-stage constitutional-amendment legislation on June 17, 2026, but NCSL says new maps will not be in effect for the 2026 midterms. A separate New York congressional redistricting order affecting NY-11 was stayed by the U.S. Supreme Court and voluntarily dismissed in March.

Georgia's legislature declined to take up redistricting in the June 2026 special session; Indiana's Senate voted down new maps in December 2025; South Carolina's new-map legislation died in the Senate; Washington's proposed constitutional amendment died at adjournment. Those facts can shape 2028 watch work, but they should not move 2026 district baselines.

## How to update House race notes

For every affected district, record four facts before making an electoral claim:

1. **Map source:** enacted statute, commission map, court order, voter-approved measure, or old map.
2. **Operative reason:** final enactment, stay, court remedy, no timely new action, or referendum status.
3. **Calendar consequence:** filing deadline, primary date, runoff/general date, voter-reassignment deadline, or unknown.
4. **Electoral discontinuity:** whether the district number represents a materially new constituency.

Then separate legal status from electoral effect. "Republicans designed this map to gain a seat" is not the same as "Republicans will gain a seat." "A state court challenge is pending" is not the same as "the map will change." "The Supreme Court stayed an injunction" is not the same as "the Supreme Court approved the map on the merits."

The best House-control output is therefore a versioned map ledger. A row should say, for example: "TX-34, 2025 Texas map, operative by Supreme Court stay, merits caveat, primary already held," or "MO-05, 2025 Missouri map, operative pending Aug. 4 signature-review deadline." That gives the forecast enough information to update quickly without smuggling legal speculation into race ratings.
