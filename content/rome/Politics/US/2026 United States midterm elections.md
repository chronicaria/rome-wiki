The 2026 congressional contest is entering a more measurable phase: authorized House and Senate candidate committees file quarterly reports by July 15, while a Supreme Court ruling has removed federal limits on coordinated party expenditures.

## Current state

The FEC's April 9 statistical summary covered the first twelve months of the cycle: congressional candidates reported about $1.5 billion in receipts and $851.9 million in disbursements; party committees reported $834.1 million in receipts and $664 million in disbursements; and PACs reported $4.6 billion in receipts and $3.4 billion in disbursements. These are aggregate flows through December 31, 2025, not current balances or proof that any particular seat moved.

The July 15 quarterly deadline covers activity through June 30, beginning April 1 or the day after the previous report closed. Race dossiers should preserve reporting periods and distinguish candidate cash from party and outside spending.

On June 30, the Supreme Court held in *NRSC v. FEC* that the Federal Election Campaign Act's coordinated-party-expenditure limits violate the First Amendment. This changes the legal channel through which national and state parties can support federal candidates, but it does not by itself show where parties will spend or which races will move. See [[NRSC v. FEC and the 2026 midterms]].

## Why it matters

Control analysis should combine official candidate and finance records with district fundamentals, transparent ratings, original polls, and election law. Market prices and ratings are estimates, not outcomes.
