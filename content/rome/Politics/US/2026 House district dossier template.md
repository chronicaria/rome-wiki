The House district dossier is a publication gate: create or revise a district note only when the minimum official identity, map, candidate, finance, voting, and uncertainty fields can be filled with dated evidence.

## Why this template exists

The desk's long-term goal is a canonical note for every 2026 House contest, but the right unit is not 435 empty cloned pages. A district dossier should answer a live analytical question: what is this district, what legal lines is it using, who can appear on the ballot, what prior evidence describes the electorate, what campaign evidence has actually arrived, and how could the district matter to House control? Until those fields have enough evidence, the district belongs in [[2026 House battleground map]], a candidate-field tracker, a filing checkpoint, or an Agent-only queue rather than in a public race page.

The core danger is false precision. A page with a district number, an incumbent name, a stale race rating, and a fundraising total looks complete even when it has not established whether the district is open, whether the map changed, whether the named candidate is ballot-qualified, whether the FEC committee is the right committee, whether the finance period is comparable, or whether a rating is an official fact. This template is designed to prevent that failure. It makes the first facts boring and authoritative before permitting interpretation.

Use this article as the minimum-fields standard for district notes titled `2026 <state abbreviation>-<district number> House election`, for example `2026 AZ-01 House election`. A district note may be shorter or longer than a standard explanatory article when it is a living dossier, but it must not be thin. If the page cannot explain its identity, evidence, and uncertainty in useful prose, do not publish it merely because the race exists.

## Publication rule

Create a public district dossier only when at least one of these conditions is true:

- the district is in the core, defense, or expansion ring of [[2026 House battleground map]];
- an official map, filing, ballot, vacancy, primary, recount, certification, or court event changes the district's control relevance;
- finance, polling, candidate, or local evidence is strong enough to alter the district's monitoring status;
- the district illustrates a reusable mechanism such as redistricting, coalition change, incumbency without normal incumbency, candidate quality, rural depopulation, suburban realignment, or Hispanic-vote change;
- the note is needed as a verified anchor for another substantial article.

Do not create a page solely because the district is one of the 435 House seats. The desk can maintain complete coverage through compact ledgers until a district has enough evidence to deserve its own explanatory center. That rule is especially important before the July 15 FEC quarterly deadline and before all state ballot lists and post-primary candidate fields are settled.

## Minimum dossier fields

### 1. District identity and current representation

Every dossier begins by identifying the district as a legal and electoral object, not a campaign label. The opening paragraph should state the state, district number, 2026 election year, current incumbent or vacancy, current incumbent party, and whether the 2026 contest is open. Use the House Clerk's member directory and official list of members for current House representation and vacancies. The Clerk is authoritative for current House membership, office status, and vacancies; it is not a state ballot authority for the November election.

Required fields:

| Field | Rule |
| --- | --- |
| canonical title | `2026 <state>-<district> House election` |
| district key | state abbreviation, district number, election year, and map vintage |
| current representative | name, party, Clerk URL, and access date, or vacancy status |
| open-seat status | retiring, running for another office, deceased, resigned, redistricted out, or not open; cite the official or high-quality source |
| incumbent party for control math | current holder and whether the seat is a defense or pickup in the scenario being discussed |
| related map node | link to [[2026 House battleground map]] if the race is in a monitoring ring |

For vacancies, write the vacancy plainly and separate it from the November race. A vacant 119th Congress seat does not automatically mean the 2026 general election has no incumbent-party baseline; a special election, redistricting, or nomination process may change the live and November facts in different ways.

### 2. Operative map and legal status

A House district number is not enough. The dossier must say which congressional map is operative for the 2026 election, whether the state used mid-decade lines, whether litigation remains live, and what source would trigger a rewrite. Use state election authorities and court records first. The Census Bureau's congressional-district products and `My Congressional District` tool can describe ACS profiles for congressional districts, but they do not decide ballot lines or resolve litigation.

Required fields:

| Field | Rule |
| --- | --- |
| map vintage | 119th Congress lines, court-ordered lines, legislature-enacted 2026 lines, commission map, or other specific authority |
| legal status | operative, pending appeal, stayed, remedial process pending, or unclear |
| source of boundaries | state map/election authority, court order, or official redistricting page |
| baseline warning | note when old election results must be re-tabulated on new lines |
| next map checkpoint | filing deadline, court hearing, stay deadline, ballot-certification date, or none known |

If the map is uncertain, the district note should not pretend to have a stable partisan baseline. Say what is known, what is pending, and what would change the page. For map-changed states, do not carry prior presidential or House results under the same district number without confirming they were calculated on the operative lines.

### 3. Ballot and candidate field

The FEC is necessary but not sufficient. A federal candidate can file with the FEC without appearing on a state ballot, and a state can certify a ballot status before the FEC finance picture is complete. State election authorities control ballot qualification, candidate lists, primary results, write-in rules, and election calendars. The FEC supplies federal candidate IDs, committees, statements of candidacy, reports, receipts, disbursements, debt, independent expenditures, and reporting deadlines.

Required fields:

| Field | Rule |
| --- | --- |
| election dates | primary, runoff if applicable, general election, and certification or canvass checkpoint when available |
| ballot-qualified candidates | source from the state authority; distinguish filed, certified, nominated, withdrawn, and write-in |
| FEC candidate IDs | include only after matching name, office, state, district, party, and cycle |
| principal committees | candidate committee name, FEC committee ID, cycle, and report status |
| primary status | unresolved, nominees selected, runoff pending, uncontested, or special circumstance |
| candidate-quality notes | incumbency, previous office, prior runs, scandals, endorsements, or local office only when sourced and electorally relevant |

The minimum field is not "candidate name." It is candidate status. A district note should say whether a candidate is an FEC filer, a state-certified ballot candidate, a party nominee, a withdrawn candidate, or a reported but unverified entrant. Those categories often diverge early in the cycle.

### 4. Electoral history and fundamentals

The fundamentals section builds a prior without turning it into destiny. It should record recent presidential and House performance on the operative lines, incumbency, district demographics, economic or geographic features that plausibly affect voting, and relevant turnout history. Use official election returns when possible, re-tabulated district results when map changes require them, and Census/ACS data for resident characteristics. Use EAC and state election-administration data for voting method, registration, turnout, ballot processing, and jurisdictional context when those facts explain vote order or turnout uncertainty.

Required fields:

| Field | Rule |
| --- | --- |
| recent presidential baseline | year, party margin, source, and whether re-tabulated on current lines |
| recent House baseline | last two general elections if comparable; flag uncontested or redistricted races |
| district profile | ACS year, table/tool, and a small set of relevant resident facts, not a demographic dump |
| voting administration | mail/early/Election Day mix, registration context, or canvass rules only where relevant |
| local geography | counties, media markets, urban/suburban/rural composition, or industry anchors when they explain politics |

Do not overfit demographics. ACS estimates describe residents with sampling limits, not the eventual electorate. If a dossier says a district has a large Hispanic, college-educated, military, retiree, union, agricultural, or immigrant population, it must also explain why that feature matters in the 2026 context and what evidence would confirm electoral relevance.

### 5. Finance and spending

Finance belongs in the dossier only with reporting-period discipline. Record receipts, disbursements, cash on hand, debt, transfers, coordinated party support, independent expenditures, and outside spending as separate channels. Candidate cash is not party spending; outside spending is not candidate cash; a legal change in coordinated spending does not prove money has arrived in the district.

Required fields:

| Field | Rule |
| --- | --- |
| report period | exact coverage dates and filing deadline |
| candidate committee totals | receipts, disbursements, cash on hand, debts/loans, and amendments if material |
| committee identity | FEC committee ID and candidate ID; note if multiple committees or old-cycle committees exist |
| outside spending | independent-expenditure source, date, spender, support/oppose target, and whether spending is district-specific |
| interpretation | capacity, prioritization, burn rate, or donor signal; never dollars-to-votes conversion |

The July 15 quarterly reports are a common checkpoint because they cover activity through June 30 for many committees. Before that data is complete and reconciled, avoid declaring a race financially transformed unless a separate official filing or independent expenditure record justifies it.

### 6. Polling and ratings

Polls and ratings are evidence, not outcomes. A district poll should not enter the dossier unless the page records sponsor, pollster, field dates, mode, sample size, population, weighting, question wording, undecided handling, and release source. A race rating should include outlet, category, date, and a short description of what the rating means. Cook Political Report and Inside Elections are useful because they publish public House rating pages and, in Inside Elections' case, baseline-methodology explanations. They still remain analyst judgments.

Required fields:

| Field | Rule |
| --- | --- |
| rating ledger | Cook, Inside Elections, Sabato, or other transparent source; category and as-of date |
| rating movement | date, prior category, new category, and cited reason when available |
| poll metadata | sponsor, pollster, field dates, mode, sample, population, wording, undecideds |
| evidence independence | whether a poll, rating move, finance surge, and market move come from one original signal or multiple signals |
| uncertainty | what would move the race inward or outward in the monitoring rings |

Do not average rating categories mechanically. A Toss Up from one outlet and Tilt Republican from another is a disagreement to explain, not a fraction to compute. Ask whether they differ because of map assumptions, candidate assessment, national environment, local reporting, or timing.

### 7. Control-path role

The dossier's control-path section explains how the district matters to 218. It should identify whether the seat is a Democratic defense, Republican defense, pickup target, offset risk, or correlated expansion seat. It should say which scenario makes the district pivotal and what other seats move with it. Link back to [[2026 House battleground map]] rather than recreating the whole battlefield.

Required fields:

| Field | Rule |
| --- | --- |
| monitoring ring | core, defense/offset, correlated expansion, watch-only, or outside current battleground |
| scenario role | marginal seat, required defense, optional expansion, regional cluster, map-driven seat, or candidate-specific exception |
| correlation notes | national environment, state cluster, shared media market, map strategy, demographic coalition, or common polling error |
| falsifiers | evidence that would move the district out of the role |

This section should prevent one of the common House mistakes: counting only pickup opportunities while ignoring defensive losses. A Democratic-held Toss Up and a Republican-held Toss Up do opposite things in net-gain arithmetic.

### 8. Update ledger and review discipline

Every living dossier needs an update ledger. The reader should be able to tell when the district was last checked, what changed, what did not change, and which source controls the next revision.

Required fields:

| Field | Rule |
| --- | --- |
| `as_of` | frontmatter date for the latest complete evidence check |
| `review_after` | next meaningful checkpoint, not an arbitrary reminder |
| update notes | dated bullets only for material changes |
| verification notes | what was checked independently and what remains single-source |
| open questions | concrete missing evidence, not generic "more data" |

For small updates, revise the existing section instead of adding a headline stack. Add an `Update -- YYYY-MM-DD` section only when the underlying district thesis changes.

## Why it matters

House control is built from district facts, but district facts are easy to misuse. A national environment can move many seats together; a court order can change which voters belong to a district; a state filing list can contradict campaign chatter; a finance report can reflect perceived competitiveness rather than create it; and a race rating can summarize unpublished reporting without being an official measure.

This template makes district notes comparable without making them mechanical. Each dossier should preserve the same minimum fields, but the prose should explain what is distinctive about the seat. AZ-01 as an open-seat primary, FL-25 on new lines, PA-08 after a rating move, TX-34 as a Democratic defense, and WA-03 as a crossover-incumbency test should not read like interchangeable pages. The common structure exists so differences become visible.

## Place in the map

The template sits between [[2026 House battleground map]] and future race-specific dossiers. The battleground map remains the compact control ledger; individual dossiers should carry deeper evidence for districts that are already pivotal or analytically revealing. When a dossier changes the control picture, update the battleground map through the committee receipt rather than editing shared state outside the assigned path.

In practical terms, the district dossier should answer four questions before a reader trusts it:

1. Is this the right district on the right legal lines?
2. Are the candidates described by their actual ballot and FEC status?
3. Are fundamentals, finance, polls, and ratings dated and separated?
4. Does the page explain how the seat affects the path to 218 without pretending to forecast a result?

If the answer to any of those is no, the page is not ready for public district-level publication.
