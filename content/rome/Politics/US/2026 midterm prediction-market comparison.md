## The right comparison

As of July 10, 2026, midterm prediction markets are no longer a fringe sidebar. Kalshi lists CFTC-regulated event contracts on party control, Polymarket displays a large midterms board with chamber-control and race-level markets, and election-watch pages increasingly embed or quote the same prices. That makes market prices tempting for [[2026 Senate control path]] and [[2026 House battleground map]]: a single number looks cleaner than a paragraph about ratings, candidate quality, district fundamentals, and polling uncertainty.

The clean number is exactly the danger. A market price is not a certified forecast, a poll, a model, or an official result. It is a tradable claim under a particular rule set. The question is not "what do markets say?" The useful question is: what claim is being traded, how much real liquidity supports the displayed price, what information could move it, and does that information independently appear in the evidence stack?

The comparison should start with contract identity. A contract about which party controls the House is not the same as a contract about who wins the national House popular vote, the number of seats a party holds, the identity of the first Speaker, or a bundled "balance of power" scenario. A Senate-control contract can depend on the vice presidency, caucus decisions by independents, runoff timing, vacancies, or market-specific resolution sources. A race-winner contract can depend on ballot label, candidate replacement, court-ordered election procedures, recounts, or when a platform decides the outcome is conclusive. Before any price enters a Rome article, the article should preserve the market title, platform, as-of time, displayed bid/ask or chance, volume, and resolution rule.

That discipline matters because market prices mix several signals at once. They may incorporate public polls, private polling leaks, rating changes, fundraising, candidate announcements, legal rulings, national macro conditions, and trader beliefs about turnout. They also incorporate non-electoral features: fees, trading limits, user geography, platform trust, rules disputes, position limits, thin order books, social-media herding, and the value of publicity to traders. A price can be informative and still be a noisy or biased measurement of the election.

## Current live examples

The public pages visible on July 10, 2026 give useful examples of what to record, not permanent facts to carry forward without rechecking.

Kalshi's House-control page showed the Democratic Party at 82 percent and the Republican Party at 18 percent, with Democratic yes contracts displayed around 83 cents, Republican yes around 18 cents, and roughly $21.0 million in volume. Kalshi's page also carried a risk warning and stated that Kalshi is subject to CFTC oversight. That makes the entry useful for a dated snapshot, but it does not answer how much depth sits at the displayed price, whether the midpoint changed after a poll or after market-maker activity, or how the contract handles edge cases beyond the visible summary.

Polymarket's midterms page showed a similar House-control tilt, with Democrats at 84 percent and Republicans at 16 percent on the House market, and showed the Senate-control market with Republicans at 53 percent and Democrats at 46 percent. The same page showed a "Balance of Power" market with scenarios such as a Democratic sweep at 45 percent and Republican Senate/Democratic House at 41 percent. The balance-of-power contract page records more of the resolution architecture: control of the House means a majority of voting seats; control of the Senate means more than half of voting Senate members, or half plus the vice president; ambiguous House control falls back to the first Speaker's party; ambiguous Senate control falls back to the first Majority Leader's party; and the market's named resolution sources are AP, Fox News, and NBC, with official certification as a fallback if those sources do not converge.

Those details change how the price should be read. The House price can be compared with district-level ratings and generic-ballot models. The Senate price must be compared with the 34-31 off-ballot base, the 35 seats up in 2026, plausible loss-offset routes, caucus/control rules, and state-level candidate fields. The balance-of-power price is a correlation claim: it is not merely "Democrats favored in the House" plus "Republicans favored in the Senate." It embeds beliefs about whether national conditions move both chambers together and whether the Senate map can resist a Democratic House environment.

The price examples also show why Rome should not write "markets predict Democrats will win the House" as an article thesis. The proper thesis is narrower: as of the checked time, two visible market pages priced Democratic House control as more likely than Republican House control, while Senate control remained closer and platform-specific. That signal should be placed beside ratings, polls, fundraising, district fundamentals, and legal-status evidence. If the market disagrees sharply with those sources, the disagreement is the story; if it agrees, the market is corroborating sentiment, not a separate proof.

## A five-part ingest rule

Every midterm market citation should pass five checks before it updates a race or control note.

First, preserve the exact claim. "Party control" can mean control after all races are called, control at swearing-in, first Speaker, first Majority Leader, certified seat counts, or a named news-organization call. The contract rule, not the article headline, defines the evidence. If the rule is unreadable, inaccessible, or ambiguous, the market can be mentioned only as a lead for further checking.

Second, preserve the market mechanics. A displayed "chance" may be an implied probability, last trade, midquote, platform-calculated probability, or a transformed price. A yes contract at 83 cents is close to 83 percent only before fees, spreads, depth, and settlement risks. For practical election writing, the note should store the displayed price and the bid/ask if available rather than pretending to have a calibrated probability.

Third, preserve liquidity and freshness. A high-volume chamber-control market can still have shallow current depth; a low-volume district market can move because one trader crossed a spread. Volume is cumulative unless the page says otherwise. A price copied at 04:00 on July 10 may be stale by lunch if a rating changes, a candidate withdraws, or a poll releases. Every volatile price paragraph therefore needs an explicit as-of timestamp and should be revised or removed once it is no longer current.

Fourth, compare against independent evidence. If a market says a candidate is favored, the desk should ask whether official ballot status, fundraising reports, nonpartisan ratings, district partisanship, and polls agree. If the evidence is only the price, the public note should not claim the race moved. If the evidence explains the price, the note should cite the underlying evidence and use the market only to show how traders digested it. If the price diverges from the underlying evidence, record possible reasons: liquidity, rule wording, correlated national-risk beliefs, trader demographics, unavailable private information, or simple overreaction.

Fifth, keep incentives and regulation visible. The CFTC's February 2026 enforcement advisory described misuse of nonpublic information and fraud concerns in event-contract trading and included examples involving Kalshi contracts. Kalshi's public page states that trading involves risk and that Kalshi is subject to CFTC oversight. Polymarket's public materials emphasize real-time odds and trading volume, while AP reporting in July 2026 described the platform's attempt to rebuild a regulated U.S. presence after earlier legal problems. These facts do not disqualify the prices, but they are reminders that the market is an institution with rules, incentives, legal risk, compliance choices, and user-selection effects.

## How to compare markets with polls

Polls and markets measure different objects. A poll asks a sampled population a question under a survey design. Its main uncertainties are sampling, weighting, population definition, turnout modeling, wording, mode, nonresponse, and field timing. A market price is a tradeable settlement claim among participants willing and able to trade. Its uncertainties are information quality, liquidity, contract design, market access, fees, manipulation, correlated trader beliefs, and resolution risk.

The two can be compared only after translating both to the same election question. A generic ballot poll that shows Democrats leading among registered voters is not directly comparable to a House-control contract. The generic ballot must move through district distribution, incumbency, candidate quality, maps, turnout, and correlated error before it implies a seat count. A market price may already price those transformations, but it does not reveal which transformation traders used. If the market and the generic ballot disagree, the desk should not assume the market is smarter; it should identify which intermediate step creates the disagreement.

The same rule applies to state races. A Senate race poll with named candidates, field dates, mode, sample size, and likely-voter screen can update one contest. A Senate-control market prices a chamber outcome created by many contests at once. If a poll moves Maine, Georgia, Texas, or Alaska, the control market may move only if traders think that state is pivotal under enough scenarios. A race market can move without the control market moving if the seat is already priced as low-leverage, offset elsewhere, or correlated with the same national factors already priced into chamber control.

Markets can help identify where poll readers are double-counting. If several district polls, a generic-ballot shift, and a House-control price all move after the same national approval story, those are not three independent data streams. They may be three reflections of one underlying event. The desk should avoid writing "polls, ratings, and markets all independently moved" unless the evidence actually has separate origins.

Markets can also help reveal hidden assumptions. A model may show Democrats favored for the House but Republicans favored for the Senate. A balance-of-power market that prices a Democratic sweep close to the split-control scenario is effectively making a statement about correlation between chambers. That does not make the market right, but it poses a useful question for [[2026 Senate control path]]: what Senate states would have to break with a Democratic House environment, and what evidence supports that separation?

## How to compare markets with ratings

Ratings are expert classifications of race competitiveness. They usually combine district or state fundamentals, candidate quality, fundraising, polling, national environment, local reporting, and professional judgment. A rating such as Toss-up, Lean, Likely, or Safe is not a probability unless the rating organization publishes a probability scale. A prediction-market price is numeric, but it may be less transparent than a rating because the trader mix and order book are hidden.

The best comparison is categorical. If a district is rated Safe Republican but a liquid market prices Democrats near even, Rome should not average the two. It should ask whether the contract is about the same district, whether boundaries changed, whether a candidate withdrew, whether the rating is stale, whether the market is thin, whether the page is misread, and whether a court or primary event changed the situation. If a Senate race is rated Lean Republican and a market prices Republicans around 57 percent, the two may be broadly consistent; the article should still cite the rating for its reasoning and the market for sentiment or risk appetite.

Market prices are most useful when they expose disagreement among evidence layers. A stable rating with a sharp market move can flag a fresh event that the desk needs to verify. A rating move with no market reaction can mean traders already priced the event, the market is illiquid, or the rating had little marginal information. A market that persistently prices an outlier scenario may be reflecting a contract rule that differs from the political shorthand. That is especially plausible in control contracts where independents, vacancies, runoff timing, or leadership-selection rules can matter.

For public articles, the phrasing should be modest. "Kalshi and Polymarket prices were consistent with a Democratic House advantage as of July 10" is safer and more informative than "markets forecast a Democratic House." "The Senate market was closer and depended on control-rule assumptions" is better than "markets say Republicans keep the Senate." Rome's job is to preserve the reasoning chain, not to turn a market board into a scoreboard.

## Race-level use

Race-level markets should be treated as leads unless they are liquid, well-specified, and independently corroborated. The Polymarket midterms page on July 10 displayed race-level examples including Texas Senate, Maine Senate, Alaska Senate, and individual House districts. Those cards can help the desk see where trader attention is concentrated. They cannot replace official candidacy checks, ballot-access deadlines, FEC filings, nonpartisan ratings, or full poll releases.

The first race-level question is whether the candidates and parties are legally and politically current. A market may list a candidate before the primary is over, after a filing deadline but before ballot certification, or while a challenge is pending. [[2026 midterm candidate filing checkpoint]] exists to stop that problem. Any race market should be matched to official ballot and FEC evidence before a public race dossier cites it.

The second question is whether the market's settlement object matches the election article's object. "Texas Senate Election Winner" is simpler than "next majority leader," but it can still depend on runoff rules, recounts, candidate replacement, party labels, and platform resolution sources. A district market can be especially fragile if redistricting litigation changes the district, if the platform title lags the legal map, or if a special election and general election use similar names.

The third question is whether a price change has an identifiable cause. A race market that moves after a reputable poll, court decision, filing deadline, or rating change can be cited alongside that event. A race market that moves without visible cause should be recorded as a monitoring note, not promoted into the public thesis. The desk should resist the urge to explain every tick. Thin markets often invite post-hoc stories.

## What this changes in Rome

The prediction-market lane should be a comparison layer, not a forecast engine. [[2026 House battleground map]] should cite market prices only when they help explain the public control narrative, reveal disagreement with district fundamentals, or motivate a verification task. [[2026 Senate control path]] should treat Senate-control prices as an aggregate sentiment signal and race markets as leads that require state-level evidence. [[2026 midterm polling error watch]] should use market moves to ask whether traders are overreacting to poll design, not to validate a poll's result.

The minimum table for any future market snapshot is:

| field | why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| platform and URL | separates Kalshi, Polymarket, PredictIt-style, and embedded third-party displays |
| market title | preserves the exact traded claim |
| as-of time and timezone | prevents stale prices from becoming evergreen facts |
| displayed price or probability | records the visible signal without overcalibration |
| bid/ask and depth, if available | separates a real market from a last-trade headline |
| cumulative and recent volume | distinguishes attention from current liquidity |
| resolution source and edge cases | identifies what actually settles the contract |
| independent evidence checked | shows whether the market is corroborated or merely interesting |
| action taken | update article, monitor only, reject, or carry forward |

This structure lets Rome use markets without being used by them. A market price can be a useful compressed summary of public and private expectations. It can also be a shallow number created by fees, access limits, attention, or a rule quirk. The committee should preserve both possibilities until evidence resolves them.

## Why it matters

The 2026 midterms will produce many numbers before Election Day: poll margins, model probabilities, rating labels, fundraising totals, ad-reservation totals, search interest, voter-registration changes, and prediction-market prices. The numbers are not interchangeable. Treating them as interchangeable makes the graph look quantitative while stripping away the mechanism that gives each number meaning.

For Andrew, the useful habit is to ask what work each number is doing. Polls sample voters or possible voters. Ratings classify races. FEC filings describe money flows through legal channels. Court records define the map and rules. Prediction markets price a contract among traders. Chamber control emerges only after those layers interact across districts and states. A good midterm note should show the interaction rather than crown one layer as the master signal.
