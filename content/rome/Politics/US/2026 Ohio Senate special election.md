The public thesis is narrow. Ohio is a live Senate-control race because it is one of the 22 Republican-held contests in the 2026 Senate ledger. But it is not one of the 20 regular Republican-held Class II seats. The winner is not being elected to the normal 2027-2033 Class II term. The winner is filling the remainder of the six-year term Vance won in 2022, with the official Ohio ballot label ending the term on January 3, 2029. That makes this page a special-election vacancy dossier, not a clone of a regular Class II state file.

## Vacancy chain and legal identity

The starting fact is the vacancy, not the campaign. Ohio Secretary of State Directive 2025-54 states that JD Vance announced his resignation from the U.S. Senate effective January 10, 2025. It then states that, under Ohio law, the vacancy must be filled by a special election at the next regular state election and that partisan candidates must be nominated in a special Senate primary. Governor Mike DeWine issued the writ of election on October 29, 2025.

That directive fixes the 2026 calendar: the special primary for U.S. Senator was conducted during the May 5, 2026 primary election, and the special election to fill the unexpired term ending January 3, 2029 will be held during the regular state election on November 3, 2026. The same directive tells prospective party candidates to use Form 2-C, independent candidates to use Form 3-D, and write-in candidates to use Form 13-C. Those form references matter because they show that Ohio is administering a vacancy election with its own ballot-access track, not merely appending a generic Senate label to the regular midterm calendar.

The appointment is the second fact. Governor DeWine's office announced on January 17, 2025 that he had appointed Lieutenant Governor Jon Husted to serve as Ohio's next U.S. senator. Husted's official Senate site says he began serving Ohioans in the U.S. Senate in January 2025. For this dossier, that establishes Husted as the appointed Republican incumbent. It does not establish his November ballot status by itself. The November ballot status must come from Ohio election materials, not from the existence of a Senate office or a campaign.

The term end is the third fact. Directive 2026-18, the official ballot directive for the May 5 primary/special election, places "U.S. Senator (Unexpired term ending 1-3-2029)" in the office order for Democratic, Republican, and Libertarian ballots. The Democratic sample ballot lists Sherrod Brown and Ron Kincaid under that office. The Republican sample ballot lists Jon Husted. The Libertarian sample ballot lists Jeffrey M. Kanter and William B. Redpath. These sample ballots are not a final November candidate list, but they are official Ohio evidence for the primary ballot and the office label.

## Candidate field

The major-party field is sufficiently verified for a public dossier. Husted appeared on the Republican primary sample ballot as the only listed Republican candidate for U.S. Senator, unexpired term ending January 3, 2029. Brown and Kincaid appeared on the Democratic sample ballot. The general-election pairing should still be checked against official Ohio results and the final general-election candidate list, but the source record supports treating Husted and Brown as the principal major-party candidates in the control discussion. News summaries and polling pages describe the same matchup, but the dossier should rely on Ohio and FEC evidence first.

Brown's identity is unusual because he is not merely a challenger in a vacuum. He held Ohio's other Senate seat from 2007 to 2025, lost the 2024 Senate election, and is now running for the unexpired Vance seat. That biography explains why the race attracts national attention and money, but it should not become a forecast. A former statewide winner and former incumbent can improve Democratic recruitment quality while still facing a state that has moved right at the federal level. The official candidate fact is narrower: Brown was on Ohio's Democratic primary sample ballot and appears in FEC records as a 2026 Ohio Senate Democratic challenger.

Husted's identity is also more specific than "incumbent." He is an appointee to a vacancy seat, not the person who won the term in 2022. That distinction matters for both campaign analysis and control arithmetic. Husted gets the advantages of incumbency in officeholding, media, constituent services, and party unification. He does not get the direct electoral history of having won this Senate seat statewide in 2022; Vance did. The November race therefore tests whether an appointed statewide Republican can retain the seat against the Democrat Ohio voters removed from the Senate in 2024.

The Libertarian and independent lanes are not ready for a final general-election table. Ohio's Libertarian sample ballot lists Kanter and Redpath. Ohio Directive 2026-24 says independent candidate Greg Levy filed petitions for the November 3 general election and instructs county boards to verify signatures, with certification forms due to the secretary of state's office by noon on July 15, 2026. Because this note is as of July 10, Levy should be described as an independent petitioner under verification, not as a qualified November candidate. The same caution applies to any claim that Redpath is the final Libertarian nominee or that Kanter is absent from the general-election path. A later revision should use the official November ballot or candidate certification rather than a news summary.

## FEC evidence and its limits

The FEC layer supports campaign-finance identity. It does not decide Ohio ballot access. Husted's FEC candidate page identifies `HUSTED, JON`, candidate ID `S6OH00304`, as a Republican candidate for Senate in Ohio, with type of candidacy listed as incumbent. It lists `HUSTED FOR SENATE` as the principal campaign committee and `HUSTED FOR OH SENATE REPUBLICAN NOMINEE FUND 2026` as another included committee. The page reports coverage dates from January 1, 2025 to April 15, 2026 and shows total receipts of about $10.5 million and total disbursements of about $2.36 million in that window.

Brown's FEC candidate page identifies `BROWN, SHERROD`, candidate ID `S6OH00163`, as a Democratic Senate candidate in Ohio, with type of candidacy listed as challenger. It lists `FRIENDS OF SHERROD BROWN` as the principal campaign committee and notes that 2025-2026 financial data now appears under the committee name `DIGNITY OF WORK PAC`, because the principal campaign committee was converted to a PAC and previously used the Friends of Sherrod Brown name. The page reports coverage dates from August 11, 2025 to April 15, 2026 and shows total receipts of about $26.0 million.

Those numbers are useful, but they should not become the current money section. First, FEC pages warn that newly filed summary data may not appear immediately. Second, the committee's own filing checkpoint says July 15, 2026 is the next major quarterly deadline for House and Senate authorized committees, with monthly party and many outside-actor updates after that. Third, Ohio's race may include candidate committees, joint fundraising, party coordinated spending after [[NRSC v. FEC and the 2026 midterms]], independent expenditures, and petitioning or ballot-access costs. A matched post-July-15 table should separate receipts, disbursements, cash on hand, debt, transfers, party spending, and outside spending. Before then, the safe finding is that Brown's FEC-visible fundraising through April 15 substantially exceeded Husted's candidate-page total, while Husted had an appointed incumbent position and Republican-control incentive behind him.

The FEC API was not usable during this run because the public `DEMO_KEY` was rate-limited. That is not a factual problem for the article because the public FEC candidate pages were available through search snippets and the FEC election portal was available, but it is a verification limit. The next run should pull a fresh candidate search, committee list, filings table, and independent-expenditure table directly from FEC data once the API is available or a non-rate-limited key is used.

## Control role

Ohio is one of the cleanest examples of why [[2026 Senate control path]] separates legal universe from modeled battlefield. In the legal universe, Ohio counts exactly like any other Senate result once the winner is seated: the winner will caucus with one side or another and affect the 100-seat chamber. In the election-type universe, Ohio is different because it fills an unexpired term. In the campaign universe, Ohio is different again because it pairs an appointed Republican incumbent with a former Democratic senator who just lost a separate Ohio Senate race.

For Democrats, Ohio can be one of the four Republican-held wins required if every Democratic-held seat is defended. If Democrats lose a Democratic-held state such as Georgia or Michigan, Ohio alone does not solve the problem; it offsets one defensive loss only if Democrats add enough other Republican-held wins. That is the right way to describe Brown's strategic value. He may make Ohio one of the more plausible Democratic pickup opportunities, but the race is not mathematically indispensable unless a forecast defines the rest of the map so narrowly that Ohio becomes one of the few available routes.

For Republicans, Ohio is part of the defensive firewall. Republicans can lose a net three Senate seats and still organize a 50-seat chamber under the current vice-presidential tie-break assumption. A loss in Ohio would consume one of those allowed losses. A hold would force Democrats to find pickups elsewhere. That makes Husted's campaign a control-defense campaign even if Ohio's recent federal baseline leans Republican.

The special-election label also affects post-election timing. Ohio's directive says the special election fills the unexpired term, but this dossier has not yet verified the exact seating sequence after the November general election: certification, credentials, possible recounts, Senate acceptance, and whether the winner can alter the late-119th-Congress count before January 3, 2027. That should stay as an open question until the desk checks Ohio vacancy law, certification law, and Senate practice. The race matters for the 120th Congress either way; the unresolved piece is the timing of any pre-January seating effect.

## Polling and ratings

Ratings and polls agree on one thing: Ohio is being treated as a competitive Republican-held race. They do not agree, by themselves, on a forecast. Cook Political Report's public Ohio Senate special page calls the race an incumbent-running contest and lists the filing deadline as February 4 and primary as May 5. Its public text highlights Brown's first-quarter fundraising and two March polls that pointed in different directions: an OnMessage survey with Brown ahead by two points and a Quantus Insights poll with Husted ahead by one. Search results and campaign reactions also indicate Cook moved Ohio to Toss Up in spring 2026, but the exact public rating category should be rechecked from Cook's live table or an accessible dated snapshot before this dossier freezes a ratings ledger.

Sabato's Crystal Ball has a public 2026 Senate page updated June 11, 2026. The search-accessible summary identifies the map as 35 seats including special elections in Florida and Ohio. That is useful for the map count but not enough, from the accessible text alone, to quote an Ohio-specific rating in this article. Inside Elections has a Senate ratings page, but the search pass did not return a clean Ohio-specific public row. For now, the dossier should say that ratings place Ohio in the competitive set only when a dated source row is available, and otherwise leave the exact rating table for a later revision.

Polling is more concrete but still incomplete. A Bowling Green State University/YouGov April 7-14, 2026 survey of 1,000 registered voters, according to search-accessible toplines, showed Husted at 50 percent and Brown at 47 percent. Earlier Emerson and BGSU polls, and several partisan or sponsor-linked polls, have tested Husted against Brown with small leads in both directions. That pattern supports a "competitive but not settled" reading, not a polling average. The dossier should not average registered-voter and likely-voter samples, pre-primary and post-primary environments, partisan memos, and independent university surveys into a single false precision number.

The useful polling conclusion is therefore conservative: public polling before July 10 shows the Husted-Brown matchup within a range where normal polling error, turnout composition, third-party inclusion, and national environment can change the apparent leader. Polls are evidence that Ohio belongs on the Senate-control dashboard. They are not evidence that Brown has solved Ohio's Republican lean or that Husted's appointed-incumbent status is sufficient to hold the seat.

## Why this is not a regular Class II dossier

A regular Class II dossier begins with the Senate class list and a term ending January 3, 2027. Ohio does not have a regular Class II Senate contest in 2026. The state appears on the Senate-control map because Vance's 2022 term was interrupted by his resignation after election as vice president. Ohio's election materials repeatedly label the office as an unexpired term ending January 3, 2029. That date is the core identity fact.

This difference changes the candidate block. A regular incumbent either seeks another full term, retires, loses a primary, or leaves an open seat. Here, Husted is an appointee defending a seat he did not originally win. Brown is a former senator seeking to return through a different seat from the one he lost in 2024. Any note that calls the race simply "Brown versus Husted for a six-year term" would be wrong.

It also changes the calendar block. The May 5 primary was a special Senate primary embedded in the regular primary/special election. The November 3 election is the special election during the regular state election. Independent candidate petition verification has its own June directive and July 15 county-certification deadline. These are not merely generic midterm dates; they are vacancy-election mechanics.

Finally, it changes control interpretation after Election Day. The winner's effect on the 120th Congress is straightforward if seated by organization. The winner's effect on the remainder of the 119th Congress depends on certification and Senate seating timing. A regular Class II winner normally waits for the new term. A special-election winner may have a different path. The dossier should preserve that question rather than hiding it behind the common phrase "Senate race."

## What would change the dossier

The first required update is official general-election ballot status. The Ohio secretary of state should publish or identify the final November candidate list after primary certification, independent petition verification, substitution windows, and write-in deadlines. That list should replace the current primary-ballot and petition-status table for Redpath, Levy, and any write-in or minor-party candidate. It should also confirm Brown and Husted as the general-election nominees through Ohio's own election record.

The second required update is finance. July 15 candidate reports should update Husted, Brown, and any qualified minor-party or independent candidates through June 30. July 20 reports and later independent-expenditure filings should show whether party and outside spending are changing the effective money balance. Brown's early FEC-visible receipts are a capacity signal, but a serious race table needs cash on hand, debt, burn rate, transfers, coordinated party support, and outside spending.

The third required update is the ratings ledger. Cook, Inside Elections, Sabato, and any other transparent rater should be entered with exact category, date, prior category, and link. If sources disagree, the article should explain whether the difference comes from candidate assumptions, polling, fundraising, Ohio baseline, or national environment. Do not collapse ratings into probability unless the source publishes a probability model.

The fourth required update is polling disclosure. Add only polls with field dates, sponsor, pollster, mode, population, sample size, weighting, question wording, candidate list, undecided treatment, and original toplines or crosstabs. A partisan memo can be logged as campaign behavior, but it should not move the assessment by itself.
