Senate race dossiers should rank state ballot authority, Senate roster facts, FEC records, disclosed polls, ratings, and reporting by what each source can actually prove.

Up: [[2026 Senate control path]] · Related: [[2026 Senate race dossier template]] · [[2026 Senate race prioritization]] · [[2026 midterm candidate filing checkpoint]] · [[Campaign cash as an electoral signal]]

## Why a hierarchy is necessary

A 2026 Senate race page can go wrong even when every source it cites is real. The most common error is not fabrication; it is source overreach. A candidate website proves what a campaign says. An FEC Form 2 proves federal campaign-finance candidacy activity. A state candidate list proves ballot-process status at that state authority's as-of moment. A poll estimates a defined population under a defined method. A race rating is an analyst judgment. A news story can verify a campaign event, quote, endorsement, withdrawal, or allegation, but it does not become the legal record of ballot access unless the state authority says so.

The 2026 Senate map makes these distinctions more important than usual. It has 33 regular Class II elections plus special elections in Florida and Ohio. It has open Democratic defenses in states such as Michigan, Minnesota, Illinois, and New Hampshire, Republican-held targets in states such as North Carolina and Maine, and special-election mechanics that can be miscounted if all Senate races are flattened into one category. The committee also has a near-term finance cliff: July 15 quarterly filings for House and Senate authorized committees and July 20 monthly-filer reports. A dossier written before those reports can describe the legal field and last available finance, but it should not claim a current cash hierarchy unless the current records exist.

The hierarchy below is a rule for what each source is allowed to establish. It is not a rule that lower-tier evidence is useless. Campaign reporting, analyst ratings, and high-quality journalism are essential. They become unsafe when they are promoted to prove facts that only official sources can prove, or when they are allowed to erase uncertainty that remains unresolved.

## Tier 1: official election authority

For candidate qualification, ballot access, withdrawals, disqualifications, substitution, recounts, canvass, certification, and state election calendar, the responsible state election authority is the controlling public source. In a Senate dossier, that usually means a secretary of state, state elections division, state board of elections, or equivalent office. The page may be a candidate listing, official ballot, election calendar, sample ballot, directive, canvass manual, recount procedure, or certified result.

This tier answers narrow questions:

- Is the race on the state ballot calendar?
- Which office label and term does the state use?
- Which candidates are listed, qualified, disqualified, withdrawn, substituted, or write-in eligible?
- What are the primary, runoff, independent, minor-party, and general-election deadlines?
- Who canvasses and certifies results?
- What recount or contest procedures apply?

It does not answer whether a listed candidate is active, viable, well-funded, electable, or likely to win. A candidate may appear on a state primary listing after suspending campaign activity. A candidate may have campaign activity before ballot qualification. A disqualified candidate may remain visible in an official table because the table preserves status history. The dossier should preserve that status rather than compress it into "running" or "not running."

Michigan illustrates the point. The Michigan Bureau of Elections' official 2026 August Primary Candidate Listing shows Abdul El-Sayed, Mallory McMorrow, Haley Stevens, and Mike Rogers in the U.S. Senate row, and marks Republican Bernadette Smith as disqualified. AP reporting says McMorrow suspended her campaign after ballots had already gone out. The correct source hierarchy records both facts: state-listed on the primary ballot, campaign-suspended in public reporting. The news event does not silently rewrite the state table.

## Tier 2: official Senate and constitutional facts

The U.S. Senate's official roster and class pages establish the chamber-side identity of regular seats. The Class II page says Class II terms run from January 3, 2021, to January 3, 2027, and lists the senators whose terms expire at the end of the 119th Congress. For regular 2026 races, that fixes why the seat is up and which term is expiring.

This tier answers:

- Is the regular seat Class II?
- Who currently holds the seat?
- What party is listed for the incumbent?
- What term is expiring?
- Is the dossier dealing with a regular term or a vacancy/special-election complication?

It does not prove the state ballot, nominee, campaign activity, or rating. A Senate roster is not a candidate filing list. A senator can retire, lose a primary, be appointed, or leave office, but the race's class identity remains a separate legal fact. A dossier should state "Class II term ending January 3, 2027" before discussing whether the incumbent is seeking another term or whether an open-seat field has formed.

Special elections need additional official state and Senate evidence because they may fill unexpired terms outside the Class II cycle. Ohio and Florida should not be treated as ordinary Class II races merely because they appear on the 2026 control map. A special-election dossier should identify the vacancy chain, temporary appointment, term end, state election authority, certification timetable, and seating implication before discussing ratings or polling.

The constitutional layer matters for control arithmetic. Article I, the Seventeenth Amendment, Senate classes, and the vice president's tie-breaking role define how seats and vacancies become organizational control. They do not forecast who wins any state. That distinction is why [[2026 Senate control path]] separates mathematical routes from modeled routes.

## Tier 3: FEC records and finance law

FEC records are authoritative for federal campaign-finance activity. They are not authoritative for state ballot qualification. A candidate's FEC record can show a Statement of Candidacy, office sought, state, party, candidate ID, principal campaign committee, authorized committees, reports filed, receipts, disbursements, cash on hand, debts, loans, independent expenditures, and reporting periods. Those are valuable facts. They should be joined by IDs, not by loose name matching.

The FEC layer answers:

- Has a person filed as a federal candidate for this office and cycle?
- What candidate ID and committee IDs are associated with the campaign?
- Which reports exist, when were they filed, and what period do they cover?
- What receipts, disbursements, cash on hand, debts, loans, transfers, and refunds are reported?
- What independent expenditures, communication costs, or electioneering communications are reported?
- Which deadlines govern the next update?

It does not answer whether the candidate is qualified for the state ballot. It also does not by itself establish electoral strength. A high receipt total may signal donor enthusiasm, national attention, self-funding, or a real organizational edge; cash on hand, burn rate, debt, and outside spending have to be separated. A candidate can raise money and still fail to qualify for a ballot. A candidate can qualify and still have weak federal finance activity.

For July 2026, finance timing is especially important. The FEC July reporting reminder sets July 15 as the quarterly deadline for House and Senate authorized committees covering activity through June 30, and July 20 for monthly filers. A race dossier accessed July 10 should not pretend to know the post-June-30 cash picture. It can say the next report is imminent and mark finance as provisional.

The FEC's 2026 primary-date PDF is useful but should be classified carefully. It is an official federal compilation sourced from state offices, statutes, and state parties. It lists primary dates, runoffs, filing deadlines, independent deadlines, and the federal general-election date. But the PDF itself warns that dates are subject to change and that candidates should consult states for other deadlines and requirements. Use it for national comparison and as a checkpoint against state pages, not as the final word on state qualification.

## Tier 4: original polls and methodological disclosure

Polling can be powerful evidence only when its target and method are visible. A Senate dossier should not cite a topline without sponsor, pollster, field dates, sample size, target population, mode, geography, weighting, question wording, candidate list, undecided handling, and access to the original release or crosstabs when possible. The standard from [[Reading congressional polls without overreacting]] is that polls estimate a defined population; they do not become a direct measurement of an eventual electorate unless the design supports that inference.

This tier answers:

- Which population was sampled: adults, registered voters, likely voters, primary voters, or another defined group?
- Which candidates were tested and in what order?
- Was the poll conducted before or after a major candidate event?
- Is the sponsor partisan, campaign-connected, independent, or undisclosed?
- What is the uncertainty, including sampling and design uncertainty?
- Are crosstabs or questionnaires available?

It does not answer ballot qualification, actual turnout, or certification. A Democratic primary poll in Michigan before Mallory McMorrow suspended her campaign does not estimate the same choice as a post-suspension poll. A general-election poll testing Haley Stevens versus Mike Rogers does not answer Abdul El-Sayed versus Rogers. A registered-voter poll in June should not be mixed with an August likely-voter poll without labeling the population change.

Internal polls and memos require special handling. They can reveal campaign behavior and strategic claims, but without full methods they should be quarantined as campaign evidence. A partisan sponsor does not automatically invalidate a poll; hidden methods and selective release are the problem. The dossier can include a campaign memo in a "claims and leads" paragraph while refusing to treat it as the main polling evidence.

## Tier 5: race ratings and models

Race ratings from Cook Political Report, Inside Elections, Sabato's Crystal Ball, and similar analysts are disciplined synthesis. They are valuable because they compress state fundamentals, candidate quality, national environment, polls, money, and reporting into a category. But the category is an analyst judgment, not an official status, poll result, or legal fact.

This tier answers:

- How does a named analyst classify the race as of a date?
- Did the source move the race since a prior rating?
- Does the source explain whether the movement reflects candidate field, polling, finance, national environment, or another factor?
- Do multiple raters converge or diverge?

It does not answer whether a candidate is on the ballot, how much cash a campaign has, or what a poll's topline says. It also should not be converted into a probability unless the source publishes a model probability and method. "Toss Up" means the rater's category, not 50.0%.

Rating pages can also have date ambiguity. A live table may display a category while the page header carries an older publication date. A third-party ratings aggregator may preserve a dated representation of a rating, but it is not the primary source. The safe dossier practice is to cite the original rating page when accessible, preserve the access date, and describe any ambiguity. If a rating source is inaccessible or unclear, write "not cleanly verified" rather than fabricating a category.

Ratings are most useful after official and FEC facts are pinned. They can tell the reader whether the outside analytical community sees Michigan as a defensive risk, North Carolina as a pickup opportunity, or Texas as an expansion race. They should not decide whether those races exist or who is actually qualified to run.

## Tier 6: high-quality reporting

AP, Reuters, major local outlets, specialist campaign reporters, and transparent beat reporting are necessary for campaign events that official pages may not explain promptly. Reporting can verify suspensions, endorsements, debate moments, strategic spending, party pressure, replacement processes, candidate statements, and litigation developments. It can also supply context about why an official event matters.

This tier answers:

- Did a candidate suspend a campaign, concede, endorse, or change strategy?
- Are parties planning a replacement process or convention?
- What do named sources and public records say about outside spending or strategic concern?
- How are candidates framing the race?
- Which official source should be checked next?

It does not replace state ballot authority, FEC records, court dockets, or official certification. Reporting about an allegation should be handled with particular care: it can establish that an allegation was made and that the campaign or party responded, not necessarily that the underlying allegation is true. Reporting based on anonymous sources should be labeled and corroborated before it changes a durable dossier claim.

In a fast-moving Senate race, reporting often leads the committee to a better official source. If AP says a candidate suspended, check whether the state listing changed. If a local outlet reports a court order, find the docket or order. If a campaign story cites an ad buy, search FEC independent-expenditure data and ad-library records where available. Reporting is an evidence path, not a stopping point.

## Tier 7: campaign, party, and market claims

Campaign websites, press releases, emails, social posts, party memos, endorsements, and prediction-market prices are low in the proof hierarchy but still useful. They show what actors are saying and doing. They can identify candidate positions, endorsements, fundraising claims, strategic emphasis, and expectations. They become dangerous when used as independent proof of opponent behavior, ballot status, vote totals, fraud, or control probabilities.

This tier answers:

- What does a candidate claim to support?
- Which endorsements or campaign messages are being promoted?
- How are parties trying to frame the race?
- What are traders pricing under a specific contract's wording, liquidity, and rules?

It does not answer whether the claim is true, whether the candidate is qualified, or whether the price is an objective probability. A campaign's issue page is authoritative for that campaign's stated position. It is not authoritative for claims about an opponent. A market price is evidence about market behavior under a contract. It is not an election result, a poll, or a rating.

Use this tier as a lead generator. If a campaign says it filed signatures, find the state filing list. If a party says a race is moving, find ratings, polls, finance, or field evidence. If a prediction market moves, check whether there was a source event, liquidity change, or contract ambiguity. If no higher-tier evidence appears, keep the claim out of the public race assessment or mark it explicitly as unverified actor behavior.

## A practical dossier build order

Every Senate race note should be built in this order:

1. Establish race identity with the Senate class page or special-election authority.
2. Establish state ballot and calendar status from the responsible state authority.
3. Add FEC candidate, committee, filing, and reporting-period data without treating it as ballot status.
4. Add ratings as dated analyst judgments.
5. Add polls only when disclosure is sufficient.
6. Add reporting to explain campaign events and unresolved changes.
7. Add campaign, party, and market claims only as actor behavior or leads.
8. Write the control-role section last, after evidence type is separated.

This order prevents the most expensive mistakes. It keeps a suspended campaign from being erased from a state ballot prematurely. It keeps an FEC candidate ID from becoming ballot qualification. It keeps an analyst rating from becoming an official fact. It keeps an internal poll from becoming a forecast. It keeps market price from becoming a result.

## Why it matters

Senate control is a small-number problem. A single race can change whether Democrats need four Republican-held pickups, five, or more. A single special-election misclassification can distort the ledger. A single premature finance claim can make a campaign look stronger or weaker than the official reporting period supports.

The hierarchy gives each source a job and a limit. State authorities govern ballot status. The Senate roster governs class identity. FEC records govern federal campaign finance. Polls estimate defined electorates. Ratings synthesize. Reporting contextualizes. Campaigns advocate. Markets price contracts. A dossier that respects those limits will be easier to update after July 15 filings, August primaries, November returns, canvasses, certifications, recounts, and seating.
