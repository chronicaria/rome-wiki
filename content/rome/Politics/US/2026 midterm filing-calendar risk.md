The 2026 filing calendar creates analytical risk because ballot access, primaries, independent petitions, FEC reports, withdrawals, and substitutions close on different clocks.

Up: [[2026 United States midterm elections]] · Related: [[2026 midterm candidate filing checkpoint]] · [[2026 midterm election-law docket]] · [[2026 House district dossier template]] · [[2026 Senate race dossier template]]

## Scope

This note is not a duplicate of [[2026 midterm candidate filing checkpoint]]. The checkpoint tells the desk how to ingest the July candidate and finance evidence without confusing ballot records, FEC records, and interpretation. This note is narrower and more durable: it explains the filing-calendar risks that can make an early 2026 House or Senate dossier look settled when the legal candidate universe is still moving.

The core problem is that "filing" is not one event. A major-party candidate may file for a primary. An independent candidate may file petitions for the general election. A new party may face a different recognition deadline. A candidate may win a primary but later withdraw. A party may be allowed, or not allowed, to replace a nominee. A court may change a map or reopen a deadline. A special election may have its own compressed calendar. A federal committee may file with the FEC before or after any of those state-law events. Those clocks overlap, but they do not collapse into a national "field is set" date.

As of July 10, 2026, the FEC's congressional primary-date and candidate-filing sheet is the best federal route map for the full congressional calendar. It is explicit about its limits: dates are subject to change; the sources are state election offices, statutes, and state parties; the general election date is November 3, 2026; and independent-petition dates may omit earlier declarations, petition-checking deadlines, or new-party requirements. That caveat is not boilerplate. It is the reason a race dossier should cite state authorities before treating a candidate list as final.

## The risk surface

Filing-calendar risk appears when the desk writes a race note before all relevant calendar gates have closed or before official sources have reconciled them. The risk is not simply that an article might be outdated later. All election coverage ages. The more serious risk is category error: calling a person a nominee who is only a federal filer, calling a primary field settled while an independent path remains open, or calling a candidate gone from the race while the finance record still matters.

The FEC sheet shows the spread. Delaware's 2026 primary is September 15, with a July 14 primary filing deadline and a September 1 independent filing deadline. Indiana's primary was May 5, but the FEC sheet lists a July 15 noon independent deadline. Michigan has an August 4 primary and an independent deadline listed as July 16. Rhode Island has a September 9 primary, with a July 10 independent deadline in the FEC table. New Hampshire's Senate primary is September 8 and independent deadline September 2. Texas had a March 3 primary, a May 26 runoff, and a June 25 independent deadline. Louisiana is structurally unusual: the FEC sheet lists a May 16 Senate election and a November 3 House election, with different runoff dates, after the House election timing changed following *Louisiana v. Callais*.

Those examples show why a single July dossier rule will fail. A Texas Senate general-election field can be much more mature than a New Hampshire Senate field on the same day. A Michigan Senate note in early July may know the primary date but not the final independent universe. A Delaware House or Senate-related note may still be months away from a primary. A Louisiana House note needs election-structure text before ordinary primary-to-general language.

## Five clocks to keep separate

The first clock is the primary filing clock. This decides who can appear on a major-party primary ballot, subject to challenges, withdrawals, and state procedures. It is usually the clock that campaigns and reporters mean when they say "the filing deadline." But it is only one gate.

The second clock is the primary and runoff clock. A primary date can settle a nominee, but only if the state uses a conventional primary, the race is uncontested or clearly resolved, and no runoff or contest remains. The FEC pre-election reporting page also uses primary and runoff dates to generate campaign-finance reporting obligations, so the election date can affect both ballot interpretation and financial disclosure timing.

The third clock is the independent, minor-party, or third-party clock. The FEC sheet warns that its independent filing column is a general reference and that candidates should check states for other requirements. A declaration may precede a petition filing. Petitions may need official checking. New parties may face separate deadlines. A race dossier that says "the field is set" after the major-party filing deadline but before the independent deadline should say what kind of field is set.

The fourth clock is the withdrawal, substitution, and disqualification clock. State law can allow or forbid replacement candidates depending on timing, reason, party status, and office. A withdrawal can remove a candidate from the ballot but leave FEC reports, debt, refunds, and spending history behind. A disqualification can alter the electoral field without making prior campaign activity irrelevant. This clock is especially important in high-attention Senate races because a candidate crisis can change the control path only if the legal replacement route is real.

The fifth clock is the federal finance clock. The July 15 quarterly report measures candidate-committee activity through June 30. Monthly party committees report June activity by July 20. Pre-election reports, post-election reports, 48-hour notices, independent-expenditure reports, and special-election notices can add more dates. Finance dates can sharpen evidence, but they cannot decide ballot access.

## Calendar risk by article type

Race dossiers have the highest exposure. A dossier uses a named race and often a candidate table. If the table mixes federal filers, ballot-qualified candidates, declared candidates, primary candidates, and independent petitioners without labels, it will mislead. The safe solution is a status field. A person can be "FEC filer," "primary-ballot candidate," "primary nominee," "independent petition pending," "general-election candidate," "withdrawn," or "disqualified." Those labels can coexist with a finance row.

Control-path notes have a different exposure. They can overstate how fixed a chamber route is. A Senate route built from ratings before late primaries and independent deadlines should be labeled as a modeled route, not a settled candidate universe. A House battleground map can say a district is structurally competitive before the final field is known, but it should not infer nominee quality, cash comparison, or ballot strategy until the local calendar supports it.

Finance notes face a timing mismatch. A candidate may have a strong June 30 cash position and then lose a primary, withdraw, or miss ballot access. That does not make the report useless. It means the report belongs to the campaign-history and spending-capacity record, not necessarily the November choice set. Conversely, a late independent candidate may have ballot relevance before a meaningful FEC report appears.

Polling notes face candidate-definition risk. A poll fielded before the final ballot is known may test a hypothetical matchup. That can be analytically useful, but the note should label it as such. A likely-voter model for a primary is not a general-election estimate. A poll that includes a candidate who later fails ballot access becomes historical evidence about a possible field, not current evidence about the certified field.

Election-law notes face remedy timing risk. A court can alter a map, move a filing deadline, change a ballot-access rule, or block a candidate. But not every legal filing changes the election calendar. [[2026 midterm election-law docket]] should track whether a case has an order, stay, deadline, or remedy that reaches the 2026 ballot. Filing-calendar risk begins when a legal possibility is written as an operative election fact.

## Official-source hierarchy

The FEC calendar is a starting point, not the final authority for state ballot status. It is valuable because it compiles congressional primary dates, runoff dates, primary filing deadlines, and independent filing deadlines across states. It is also valuable because it flags special sequencing and caveats. But the sheet itself points back to state election offices, statutes, and state parties.

For a race dossier, the preferred hierarchy is:

1. State election office candidate list, filing list, ballot certification, or election calendar for ballot access.
2. Court order or official administrative notice if litigation changes the calendar or candidate status.
3. FEC candidate and committee records for federal finance identity.
4. FEC reporting-date pages for federal reporting obligations and close-of-books dates.
5. NCSL or another nonpartisan state-law summary for cross-state context.
6. Campaign statements and press reports as leads, clearly labeled, until official status is available.

The hierarchy prevents two common publication errors. It stops an FEC committee from becoming a ballot-qualified candidate by implication. It also stops an outdated state candidate list from erasing a campaign-finance obligation. The two records answer different questions.

## A filing-risk field for every dossier

Every early race dossier should include a compact filing-risk field until the general-election field is certified or otherwise officially settled.

| Field | Required answer |
| --- | --- |
| Primary filing deadline | Date, source, and whether challenges remain. |
| Primary date and runoff date | Date, source, and whether the nominee is official, unofficial, or pending. |
| Independent/minor-party deadline | Date and caveat about petitions, declarations, or new-party rules. |
| Official candidate source | URL and timestamp of state list or calendar. |
| FEC identity status | Candidate ID, committee ID, report period, and whether it has been matched to ballot status. |
| Withdrawal/substitution exposure | Known rule, unresolved question, or not checked. |
| Next calendar gate | The next date that can change the field or reporting obligations. |

This field should stay visible even when the race is not highly competitive. Safe seats can still produce errors if an article treats a primary winner as certified too early or omits an independent petition route. Competitive seats need the field because candidate identity can change ratings, finance interpretation, polling matchups, and control math.

## Examples of risk patterns

Late primary risk appears in states such as Delaware and New Hampshire. The national desk may want to compare them with earlier-primary states because all are on the same November ballot. But a September primary means a July article is often describing a pre-nomination field. Ratings and finance can still be useful, but the dossier must say whether it is analyzing the likely general-election matchup or the official primary field.

Independent-window risk appears when the major-party picture looks settled but an independent or minor-party deadline remains open. Indiana's July 15 noon independent deadline and Michigan's July 16 independent deadline in the FEC table illustrate the problem. A race note written July 10 can be current and still not final. The sentence should be "major-party primary status is X; independent filing deadline is Y," not "the field is settled."

Structural-calendar risk appears in Louisiana. The FEC sheet notes that Louisiana House elections were postponed to November 3, 2026 following *Louisiana v. Callais*, while Senate and House entries have different listed sequences. A Louisiana House dossier should not use a generic primary-runoff template without checking the state's current election calendar and any operative court order.

Special-election risk appears on the FEC dates-and-deadlines page. Special elections have their own notices and reporting dates, and the page warns that election dates are subject to change. A regular-cycle House or Senate table should not silently absorb a special election without preserving its election date, reporting obligations, and term or vacancy context.

Withdrawal and replacement risk appears whenever a candidate crisis happens after a primary or after a filing deadline. The analytical question is not only whether the candidate will leave. It is whether state law and party rules allow replacement, by what date, and how ballot printing or certification affects the result. Until that is verified, the desk should avoid writing a new nominee into the control path.

## Publication rules

Do not call a field "settled" unless the note says which field: primary ballot, major-party nominees, independent/minor-party access, general-election candidate list, or certified ballot. "Settled" without a modifier is too vague for 2026.

Do not compare candidates' cash as general-election capacity unless both candidates are in comparable electoral positions. A nominee with June 30 cash and a primary contender with June 30 cash are not the same object. A candidate who loses a primary can still affect spending history, but not the November field.

Do not move a race rating explanation into a dossier without checking whether the rating assumes a candidate matchup that is still provisional. Ratings may be forward-looking. Rome's note should tell Andrew when that is happening.

Do not delete or ignore a withdrawn candidate's FEC record. The committee may still file reports, carry debt, issue refunds, or disclose spending that explains the race's history. The ballot record and finance record should diverge visibly rather than being forced together.

Do not use one state's calendar pattern as a national rule. A March primary, a September primary, a top-two system, a runoff state, a party-convention state, and a special election can all appear in the same congressional cycle.

## Why it matters

Filing-calendar discipline is not paperwork pedantry. It protects the main interpretive claims in the 2026 midterms set. Candidate quality cannot be evaluated cleanly until the candidate role is known. Finance cannot be interpreted cleanly until the report period and ballot status are matched. Polls cannot be compared cleanly until the tested field is identified. Ratings cannot be explained cleanly until the analyst's assumed field is visible. Control paths cannot be trusted if a race is counted as settled before the legal field is actually settled.

The practical consequence is simple: every early race note should carry its next calendar gate. If the next gate is a primary, the note is pre-nomination. If the next gate is an independent deadline, the major-party field may be settled but the general-election field is not. If the next gate is a finance report, the ballot field may be known while campaign capacity is still stale. If the next gate is a court hearing, the map or ballot rule may be provisional. Naming the gate is how Rome prevents stale confidence.
