The first full 2026 Senate dossiers should go to races that can change the control path and clarify uncertain evidence, not simply to races that attract the most noise.

## The prioritization rule

The committee should build the first full Senate race dossiers in this order: North Carolina, Maine, Georgia, Michigan, Ohio, Iowa, Texas, Alaska, New Hampshire, Minnesota, Florida, Montana, then the rest of the Class II and special-election map as official candidate and finance evidence matures. This is a research queue, not a prediction. It says where a full dossier will produce the most control-relevant understanding first.

The queue starts from [[2026 Senate control path]]. The official Senate Class II roster gives 33 regular seats whose terms expire January 3, 2027. The 2026 ballot also includes special elections in Florida and Ohio. That makes 35 Senate contests. The continuing seats create a 34-seat Democratic-caucus and 31-seat Republican off-ballot base in the current two-bloc simplification. Democrats need 17 of the 35 winners to reach 51 seats; Republicans need 19 winners to reach 50 seats, which is enough to organize the Senate while the current Republican vice president holds the tie-breaking vote.

That arithmetic is the first filter. A race deserves early dossier work when it changes one of four things:

1. It is a plausible Democratic pickup in a map where Democrats need at least four Republican-held seats.
2. It is a plausible Democratic defense whose loss forces Democrats to find a fifth pickup.
3. It is a special election, vacancy contest, open seat, or calendar outlier whose official status could be miscounted.
4. It has enough conflicting evidence that a dossier would prevent the committee from confusing attention with leverage.

The last point is the reason this note exists. Attention is visibility: media volume, market volume, national fundraising, candidate celebrity, scandal, ideological stakes, or activist interest. Leverage is marginal control value: how much resolving the race changes the set of feasible Senate majorities. Attention can reveal leverage, but it can also distract from it. A safe-state open seat can dominate headlines without being the next decisive control unit. A quieter defensive seat can matter more if losing it lengthens every route to a majority.

The ordering is deliberately asymmetrical. Democratic pickups and Democratic defenses both appear near the top because control is net arithmetic. If Democrats hold all 13 Democratic-held contests, they still need four Republican-held seats. If they lose Georgia or Michigan, they need five. That makes a Democratic-held defense as control-relevant as a Republican-held target when it is close enough to threaten the base.

North Carolina belongs first because it sits at the intersection of all the relevant filters: Republican-held, regular Class II, plausible pickup path, and a state where national and state-level signals can plausibly diverge. It is also the sort of race where a later dossier would have to reconstruct too much: official candidate status, primary history, state filing calendar, finance through the July 15 reporting window, public polling, and analyst ratings. Building it first gives the committee a reusable model for a high-leverage Republican defense.

Maine belongs second for the opposite reason. It is not a generic battleground state. Its incumbent, candidate field, small electorate, and independent-minded voting history make it easy to overfit anecdotes. That is exactly why it needs a dossier early. If the race remains one of the shortest Democratic pickup routes, it has obvious leverage. If candidate or ballot volatility damages the Democratic path, the dossier should show that mechanism explicitly rather than laundering it into a vague "Maine chaos" narrative.

Georgia and Michigan are the first defensive dossiers because they protect the denominator of the Democratic route. Georgia is an incumbent Democratic defense in a state that remains central to national coalition analysis. Michigan is an open Democratic seat. The right question is not whether either race gets the same amount of attention as Texas; it is whether a loss would force Democrats to find an additional Republican-held pickup. The answer is yes. That makes both races early dossier material even if a ratings table at some moment treats them differently.

Ohio ranks above some regular Class II races because it is a special election. The official FEC primary-date compilation and state election materials treat Ohio as a Senate election on the 2026 calendar, but the race is filling an unexpired term rather than the regular Class II term. A full dossier should preserve the vacancy chain, term end, candidate status, certification, and seating implications. It is not enough to write "Ohio Senate" and move on.

Iowa, Texas, Alaska, New Hampshire, Minnesota, Florida, and Montana form the next ring. Each can matter, but for different reasons. Iowa and Texas can expand the Democratic pickup set; Alaska can behave differently from a simple red-state model; New Hampshire and Minnesota are defenses whose open-seat status must be tested; Florida is a special election that must not be lost in a regular-seat table; Montana may become real if candidate, polling, or money evidence improves. The dossier queue should not flatten those reasons into one "competitive" label.

## Why attention is not leverage

The cleanest mistake is to sort races by how often they are discussed. That feels empirical because it counts something visible. But visibility is not the same as control value.

A race can receive attention because a candidate is famous, because a primary is ugly, because donors like a symbolic fight, because a market contract is liquid, because national reporters are nearby, because a scandal is legible, or because a party wants the other side to spend defensively. None of those facts by itself tells the committee how the race changes the 35-seat Senate equation.

Control leverage has a narrower definition. A race has high leverage when changing its expected winner materially changes the feasible majority paths. In 2026, a Republican-held race becomes high leverage when it is part of a plausible Democratic set of four pickups or when it can offset a Democratic loss. A Democratic-held race becomes high leverage when losing it pushes Democrats from four required pickups to five, six, or more. A special election has extra leverage when misclassifying it could distort the control ledger, seating timeline, or term being filled.

This distinction also protects against fundraising overinterpretation. July 15 reports will be useful because they put House and Senate authorized committees on a matched reporting deadline for activity through June 30. But money can be cause, effect, signal, or decoy. A fundraising surge in Texas may mean a real race is developing; it may also mean national donors are emotionally invested in a high-profile contest. A smaller finance change in North Carolina or Maine may have more chamber-control value if the race is already in the decisive set.

Ratings should be handled the same way. Cook, Inside Elections, and Sabato's Crystal Ball are useful because they publish nonpartisan or explicitly analytical race ratings, dates, and categories. They are not official records. A rating tells the committee how an outside analyst synthesizes fundamentals, candidates, and environment at a point in time. It does not prove ballot status, nominee identity, cash on hand, polling quality, or a chamber-control path. The dossier should record ratings as one evidence layer, then explain what would make the race more or less decisive.

Prediction markets and campaign spending are even more dangerous if they are treated as leverage. A liquid contract can measure trader expectations under the contract's wording; it can also reflect fees, eligibility, hedging, attention, or thin information. A party expenditure can reveal strategy; it can also try to shape strategy. Both belong in a dossier when documented, but neither replaces official status, rating context, original polls, and the control ledger.

## What each early dossier must prove

Each first-wave dossier should start with the [[2026 Senate race dossier template]] and then answer the race-specific leverage question.

For North Carolina, Maine, Iowa, Texas, Alaska, Florida, Ohio, and Montana, the key question is pickup value: does the race belong in the core Democratic pickup set, an offset layer, or only an expansion layer? The answer should cite official race status, state calendar, FEC candidate and committee records, ratings, polling if any, and finance evidence by reporting period. It should not cite national attention as a substitute for those inputs.

For Georgia, Michigan, New Hampshire, and Minnesota, the key question is defensive fragility: how much evidence shows that a Democratic-held seat could fall, and what would that do to the net-four route? These dossiers need the same official and finance blocks as Republican-held races, but their control section should be written from the loss-offset table. "Democrats must hold it" is too blunt. The correct sentence is: "If Democrats lose this seat, the required Republican-held pickup count increases by one."

For Ohio and Florida, the key question is legal identity before competitiveness. A special-election dossier should state the original vacancy, appointee, term being filled, state authority, primary and general calendar, certification process, and seating implication. Only after that should it analyze polling, ratings, money, and candidate quality. A special election can be decisive even if it is not a regular Class II seat; it can also be over-discussed because the word "special" makes it sound exceptional. The dossier's job is to separate those effects.

For Texas, Alaska, and Montana, the committee should apply an attention discount. That does not mean ignoring them. It means asking what independent evidence would move them ahead of a core race. Examples: multiple transparent ratings moving the race into a more competitive category; public general-election polling with usable disclosure; July 15 finance evidence showing not merely enthusiasm but capacity against a defined opponent; a confirmed candidate or ballot event; or a state-specific issue that plausibly changes turnout or persuasion. Without that evidence, these races remain important monitoring targets but should not consume the first dossier slots.

## The practical build sequence

The first pass should create complete but not bloated race files. A good order is:

1. North Carolina and Maine as Republican-held core pickup tests.
2. Georgia and Michigan as Democratic defensive tests.
3. Ohio as the special-election template.
4. Iowa and Texas as the first expansion-versus-attention comparison.
5. Alaska, New Hampshire, Minnesota, Florida, and Montana as second-wave dossiers after official candidate and finance evidence is easier to compare.

The July 15 FEC reporting deadline is the next major checkpoint. The committee should not freeze a finance hierarchy before those filings are processed. It can still build official-status and calendar blocks now, but candidate cash, party support, independent expenditures, debt, burn rate, and post-*NRSC v. FEC* party behavior need reporting-period discipline. The July 20 monthly-filer deadline matters for national party committees and other monthly filers, so a race dossier completed before both windows should carry a visible review-after date.

Late-primary states require special caution. The FEC calendar lists different primary, runoff, independent, and minor-party filing dates by state, with dates subject to change. New Hampshire's September primary and Delaware's September primary are not on the same evidence timeline as states with March, May, or June primaries. A dossier should state whether it is describing a settled general-election field, a primary field, or a provisional candidate universe.

The committee should also resist building all 35 dossiers at identical depth immediately. Every race eventually needs a canonical page, but the first full dossiers should be the pages that reduce the most analytical uncertainty. Safe-state pages can begin as official-status records. Competitive and confusing races need full explanatory treatment first.

## Why it matters

A Senate control model can be mathematically correct and still narratively wrong. It can know that Democrats need 17 winners from the 35 contests while still spending too much time on races that do not change the feasible route. It can know that Republicans can retain control with 19 wins while underweighting the defensive seats that make the Democratic route longer.

The remedy is a dossier queue that is explicit about its theory of value. First build the races that answer: what are the four most plausible Democratic pickups, which Democratic defenses could force a fifth, which special elections can be miscounted, and which high-attention races need evidence discipline? Then update the order whenever official status, transparent ratings, disclosed polls, finance reports, or legal events change the answer.

That keeps Rome's 2026 Senate coverage useful. It treats attention as a lead, ratings as judgments, money as capacity evidence, polls as estimates, and official sources as the legal skeleton. Chamber control depends on how those layers combine, not on which race is loudest on a given day.
