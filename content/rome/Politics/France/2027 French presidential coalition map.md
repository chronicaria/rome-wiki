France's 2027 presidential field is organized by competing coalition projects, but a presidential coalition is a sequence of coordination problems—not a fixed transfer of party votes to one candidate.

Up: [[2027 French presidential election]] · Related: [[How the French presidential election works]] · [[From the Élysée to a National Assembly majority]] · [[2027 French presidential candidate field]]

## How to read this map

The useful unit in a French presidential pre-campaign is neither the individual name alone nor a permanent party “bloc.” It is a **coalition project**: an attempt to combine a candidate, participating organizations, elected officials, activists, money, access to the 500 presentations, and voters who may behave differently in the two rounds. The projects below describe the field as of 9 July 2026. They are analytical families, not a prediction that each will produce exactly one ballot line.

Three distinctions prevent false certainty.

First, a party alliance in the [[National Assembly of France|National Assembly]] is not automatically a presidential alliance. Legislative coalitions divide 577 constituency nominations and can tolerate several national personalities. A presidential election selects one person on one national ballot. The choice of candidate therefore concentrates disputes about leadership, program, and strategy that a parliamentary arrangement can postpone.

Second, a declared candidacy is not a qualified candidacy. Marine Le Pen, Gabriel Attal, Édouard Philippe, Bruno Retailleau, Jean-Luc Mélenchon, and other figures may announce that they intend to run, but only the Conseil constitutionnel's later list establishes who has satisfied the legal gateway described in [[The 500 presentations in French presidential elections]]. This note maps political coordination; it does not confer official status.

Third, first-round coordination and second-round transfer are different mechanisms. A party can withdraw its own contender and still fail to deliver all of its electorate to the endorsed person. Conversely, voters can converge on a candidate through a *vote utile*—a strategic “useful vote”—without a formal party agreement. The runoff then creates a new binary choice in which abstention, blank voting, and rejection of the opposing finalist matter alongside endorsement.

## Rassemblement National and the nationalist right

The **Rassemblement National** (RN) begins with the most centralized large coalition project. Its core combines the party organization, a disciplined national brand, a substantial parliamentary presence, Marine Le Pen's long presidential record, and Jordan Bardella's leadership profile. The central coordination problem is consequently less about holding an open primary than about the allocation of roles at the top.

That allocation changed materially on 7 July 2026. The Paris Court of Appeal upheld Le Pen's conviction in the European Parliament assistants case but reduced the unsuspended portion of the ineligibility sentence to a period Reuters reported had already been served; her further appeal suspends the appellate ruling while cassation is pending. Le Pen then said she would run, pursue that appeal, and campaign with Bardella as her intended prime minister. The legally careful description is therefore: Le Pen is **declared**, the appellate sentence presently leaves a path to the election, and further litigation creates residual uncertainty. It would be inaccurate either to describe her as already qualified or to write that all legal uncertainty has disappeared.

This proposed Le Pen–Bardella division also reveals why a presidential coalition cannot be evaluated only through first-round polls. A national candidate needs an account of governing personnel and a path through the Assembly. Naming Bardella for Matignon can signal continuity and mobilize his supporters, but the president cannot award a durable legislative majority by announcement. [[From the Élysée to a National Assembly majority]] explains the separate parliamentary test.

The RN's outer boundary remains a political question. Allies to its right or drawn from the traditional right may support the same presidential contender, compete independently, or bargain over later legislative nominations. Éric Zemmour's Reconquête and smaller sovereignist candidacies can appeal to partially overlapping voters without belonging to the RN organization. A poll that places them on separate first-round lines does not measure the share of a single transferable nationalist bloc; a poll that omits them tests a different choice architecture.

## The post-Macron center and center-right

The space built around Emmanuel Macron faces a succession problem because Article 6 of the Constitution bars a third consecutive presidential term. The organizations associated with the outgoing majority—notably Renaissance, Horizons, MoDem, and smaller allied formations—retain elected officials and governing experience, but they do not inherit a constitutionally designated nominee.

Gabriel Attal and Édouard Philippe illustrate two rival methods of consolidation. Attal, the Renaissance leader and former prime minister, declared his candidacy in May 2026. Philippe, former prime minister and leader of Horizons, had declared earlier. Public Sénat reported in June that a liaison committee acknowledged disagreement between them over a possible primary: Attal defended a selection mechanism while Philippe rejected that route. That is not a minor procedural quarrel. A primary can create a visible winner and common campaign, but it also defines who may vote, which parties accept the result, and whether candidates from the traditional right participate. Refusing it preserves a candidate's independent mandate while risking several adjacent first-round lines.

The phrase “central bloc” can also conceal a programmatic tension. Some potential partners define the project as continuity with the reformist, pro-European Macron coalition. Others want a broader right-and-center agreement that places immigration, security, public spending, or institutional authority nearer the center of gravity. Still others fear that a pre-election bargain with Les Républicains would erase the identity that separated Macronism from the traditional right. Coalition breadth can therefore reduce fragmentation while increasing internal vetoes.

There is no reliable arithmetic in which the prior votes of Renaissance, Horizons, MoDem, or a center-right candidate simply add together. Their electorates overlap but are not identical. Candidate image, incumbent-government responsibility, and the tested field affect whether voters remain, defect right, move toward a social-democratic option, or abstain. The live question is whether this family can create a focal candidacy before strategic voting creates one from below.

## Les Républicains and the conventional right

**Les Républicains** (LR) occupies a difficult position between organizational autonomy and electoral overlap with both the post-Macron center and the RN. Bruno Retailleau declared his candidacy in February 2026. Other figures on the conventional right have also been discussed or have developed their own political vehicles. The durable coalition question is whether LR seeks its own presidential line, participates in a wider right-and-center selection, or fragments across candidates who offer different relationships to Macronism and the RN.

The party has resources that raw national polling can understate: local elected officials, territorial organization, parliamentary experience, and potential presentation networks. But those assets do not themselves solve the first-round coordination problem. A candidate may possess credible ballot access yet struggle to become the strategic choice of right-leaning voters. Conversely, a centrist contender might attract some LR voters without securing party leaders, activists, or legislative cooperation.

Calling the entire space “the right” therefore collapses three choices. There is a choice of candidate; a choice about whether the governing break is primarily with Macronism or with the RN; and a choice about post-presidential legislative cooperation. These choices can point in different directions. A presidential endorsement may be easier than a common program, while an agreement over program may still founder on leadership.

## The fragmented left and rival routes to unity

The left contains the clearest example of a legislative coalition failing to settle presidential leadership. The Nouveau Front populaire created rapid constituency coordination for the 2024 legislative election among La France insoumise (LFI), the Parti socialiste (PS), Les Écologistes, and the Parti communiste français (PCF). That agreement demonstrated the ability to avoid some first-round legislative competition. It did not create a permanent organization or an uncontested method for choosing one presidential candidate.

At least three presidential strategies now overlap.

One is the **LFI route**, centered on Jean-Luc Mélenchon and the movement's own program, activist structure, and electoral base. LFI has resisted a selection process in which other organizations could determine its nominee or dilute its program. Its leverage rests partly on Mélenchon's prior first-round strength and the argument that convergence should occur behind the best-established left candidacy. Its constraint is that voters and organizations elsewhere on the left may reject both the candidate and the terms of that convergence.

A second is the **unitary-primary route** associated with Les Écologistes and other participating organizations. An official Ecologists statement on 9 June 2026 reaffirmed support for a primary of the left and ecologists and put the proposal to members, while preserving Marine Tondelier's candidacy if the process failed. Earlier Front Populaire 2027 material described a planned 11 October 2026 vote. The key analytical questions are participation, eligibility, voter registration, programmatic commitments, and whether absent organizations promise to respect the result. A primary can validly select a candidate among its participants without becoming a primary of the entire left.

A third is an **independent social-democratic or party route**, potentially including figures associated with the PS or Place publique and others who may see a larger electorate between LFI and the center. Its argument is that left unity under an unacceptable platform can repel more voters than it combines. Its risk is duplication: several candidates can appeal to similar voters and each fall below a runoff threshold that a single candidacy might reach.

The PCF and smaller far-left formations add further choices. Some value an autonomous first-round expression of program and organization; others may bargain for unity. The left's coalition map is thus not a clean binary between “unity” and “division.” It is a dispute over which voters define the coalition, which program binds it, how the candidate is selected, and whether legislative reciprocity follows.

## Ecologists, social democrats, and the center-left hinge

Ecologists and social democrats deserve separate attention even when they participate in broader left processes because they can function as a hinge. Their voters may prioritize defeating the RN, maintaining a distinct ecological or pro-European program, avoiding LFI, or preventing capture by the outgoing center. Those motivations do not always nominate the same person.

For Les Écologistes, the primary proposal creates an explicit contingency: attempt common selection, while retaining an autonomous candidate if agreement fails. That makes the participation rules and deadlines politically consequential, not administrative detail. For the PS, internal disagreements over government censure, relations with LFI, and the presidential route can affect both whether the party joins a primary and whether all of its currents accept the outcome. Place publique's relationship to a unitary process similarly matters because a candidate with a pro-European center-left profile may draw support from voters who would not back an LFI-led offer.

This hinge also affects the post-Macron center. A center-left contender can draw disaffected governing-coalition voters without joining the outgoing majority. A centrist contender can seek social-democratic voters through a republican-front appeal against the RN or LFI. Such appeals are conditional and candidate-specific; they are not proof that a permanent “anti-extremist bloc” exists.

## Smaller candidacies are part of the mechanism

Communist, far-left, sovereignist, regionalist, rural-interest, and single-issue candidacies should not be treated only as noise around four large families. A candidacy can influence the agenda, reveal a durable constituency, qualify for the higher campaign-reimbursement ceiling by crossing the statutory vote threshold, or alter which large candidate appears viable. Reimbursement remains conditional on compliant accounts; it is not an automatic grant for appearing on the ballot. Yet discussion is not ballot access. Smaller organizations face the same presentation, finance, and account requirements as larger ones, often with less margin for invalid forms or fundraising problems.

The presence of a smaller candidate also changes poll interpretation. When an institute removes a name, supplies a different center candidate, or tests Le Pen rather than Bardella, it reallocates attention and choice rather than observing the same electorate under identical conditions. [[How to read French presidential polls]] should therefore be used before converting hypothetical slates into a coalition ranking.

## Five tests for coalition strength

The apparent size of a coalition should be checked against five distinct capacities.

1. **Selection:** Is there an accepted procedure or only competing declarations? A primary, congress, party endorsement, negotiated withdrawal, and strategic-voter convergence are different mechanisms.
2. **Qualification:** Does the prospective candidate have a credible path to 500 valid, geographically distributed presentations and the required declarations? Party popularity is not enough.
3. **First-round mobilization:** Can the coalition turn sympathy into valid votes under the tested slate, including turnout and competition from adjacent candidates?
4. **Runoff expansion:** Can the finalist attract voters who did not support the coalition in round one without demobilizing the core? Endorsements are evidence of elite choice, not guaranteed transfer rates.
5. **Governing extension:** Can the presidential project support a government in the Assembly? A broad negative coalition against an opponent may win the Élysée while remaining too thin for 289 deputies.

These capacities can move independently. A candidate can poll well but struggle with elected-official presentations. A disciplined party can qualify easily but fail to expand in the runoff. A broad second-round winner can enter office without a parliamentary majority. “Coalition strength” is therefore a vector, not one percentage.

## What would change the map

The map should be revised after a binding selection result, verified withdrawal, party endorsement with clear scope, consequential court decision, new parliamentary alliance, or official qualification evidence. It should not be rewritten for every anonymous report of negotiations.

Near-term resolution points include the rules and participation of the proposed left primary; the center and center-right decision over a common selection method; any formal LR nomination procedure; the legal timetable of Le Pen's further appeal; and evidence that presidential coordination includes—or explicitly excludes—legislative reciprocity. Each event can narrow the field without guaranteeing that voters transfer as party leaders intend.

## Why it matters

France's two-round presidency rewards coordination but does not make coalitions mechanical. Mapping only candidates turns institutions into personality drama. Mapping only parties ignores leadership and strategic voting. Mapping only polls treats hypothetical menus as durable blocs. A coalition map instead asks who can select, qualify, mobilize, expand, and govern. Those five tests explain why a crowded field can consolidate suddenly—and why apparent unity can fracture between the presidential first round, runoff, and Assembly.

## Open questions

- Which organizations will formally participate in the proposed 11 October left primary, and what electorate and result-binding rules will apply?
- Will the post-Macron center adopt a binding selection method or allow strategic voting to choose among several declared candidates?
- How will the Cour de cassation timetable and any resulting judgment affect the precise legal description of Marine Le Pen's eligibility?
- Which presidential agreements will include reciprocal legislative nominations or an explicit governing coalition?
- Which original polling series can test coalition breadth while holding the candidate slate and turnout assumptions sufficiently stable?
