French presidential polls are conditional measurements of opinion under a specified candidate list, electorate model, questionnaire, fieldwork window, and adjustment procedure—not forecasts or vote counts.

Up: [[2027 French presidential election]]

The most useful question to ask of a French presidential poll is not “who is winning?” but “what exactly was measured, among whom, under which electoral scenario, and how much of the published result depends on modelling choices?” A headline percentage is the end of a chain: recruitment, screening, candidate prompt, turnout filter, weighting, vote-recall adjustment, and rounding. Reading the chain makes polls informative without granting them a precision they do not possess.

## What an intention of vote measures

An intention-of-vote question records a respondent's answer at the time of interview to a hypothetical ballot presented by the pollster. It is not a ballot, a probability that a candidate will win, or a prediction of the final result. Before the official candidate list is settled, it is also a test of a pollster-defined scenario. The named candidates, their order or rotation, and the availability of “blank,” “null,” “abstain,” or “undecided” responses can affect what respondents do.

The denominator matters. A report may begin with all adults, then retain registered voters, then apply an expressed likelihood-to-vote threshold, and finally calculate candidate shares only among respondents who express a choice. A candidate shown at 20% of *expressed voting intentions* has not necessarily been chosen by 20% of the original sample. The notice and questionnaire should reveal the sequence. If they do not, the topline cannot be interpreted confidently.

First- and second-round questions answer different counterfactuals. A first-round measure distributes preferences over a broad candidate set. A second-round measure forces a choice within a named pairing, often with abstention or blank/null options. Second-round estimates published before the first round are especially conditional: the pairing itself may never occur, and some voters have not yet formed a transferable preference. Never treat a hypothetical runoff as if the first round had already selected it.

Finally, timing is part of the measurement. Fieldwork conducted over two or three days is a snapshot of answers collected during that interval. An event on the last evening may reach only part of the sample. Publication date and fieldwork date are not interchangeable. Compare the dates of interview, not merely the dates of the headlines.

## The French legal transparency layer

France provides an unusually useful audit trail. The governing framework is Law no. 77-808 of 19 July 1977, substantially updated in 2016. It covers polls that bear directly or indirectly on the electoral debate. The law requires specified information to accompany first publication and requires the polling organisation to deposit a technical notice with the independent [[Commission des sondages]] before publication or diffusion.

Under article 3, the notice must include the poll's object; the method used to choose respondents and compose the sample; interviewing conditions; non-response proportions; any respondent incentive; and, when applicable, the criteria used to adjust raw results. The Commission makes these notices publicly available. Its role is regulatory oversight: it can examine the material behind a poll and publish a corrective statement. A notice does not certify that a number is “true,” and the Commission does not choose the politically correct result. The notice instead makes essential parts of the production process inspectable.

The law also restricts publication, diffusion, and commentary on electoral polls from the day before a round of voting until the closing of the last polling station. The exact application should be checked against the current consolidated law and the Commission's election-specific instructions rather than inferred from an old campaign calendar.

For a reader, the practical rule is simple: find the corresponding notice in the Commission's database. Match the institute, sponsor, fieldwork dates, topic, and sample. A press story that links only to its own chart has omitted the best diagnostic document. Absence of a readily found notice warrants caution and further checking; it is not, by itself, proof of misconduct because search labels, filing dates, and the legal scope of a survey can complicate discovery.

## How the number is made

### Recruitment and quotas

French political polls commonly use quota samples rather than simple random samples. Institutes recruit respondents—often from online access panels—and seek a sample whose composition matches selected population characteristics such as sex, age, occupation or socio-professional category, region, and size of urban area. Quotas improve balance on known variables. They do not make every eligible voter equally likely to be selected, and they cannot guarantee balance on unmeasured traits such as political attention, trust, or willingness to join panels.

The relevant sample is often smaller than the prominent total. A survey might interview a representative sample of adults but calculate voting intentions only among registered voters who pass a turnout-likelihood rule and express a candidate preference. Precision should be assessed using the base for the reported estimate, not the largest number on the first page.

Online interviewing is not inherently disqualifying. It changes the coverage and recruitment problem. The reader should look for the panel source, eligibility checks, quality controls, interview mode, and whether respondents received points or another incentive. Mode can shape disclosure and attention: an online questionnaire avoids an interviewer's presence but depends on screen design, respondent engagement, and panel quality.

### Weighting and redressement

Institutes weight responses to bring the sample closer to population benchmarks. They may also use recalled votes from previous elections to adjust political composition. In French practice this is called *redressement*. The published estimate therefore is not normally a raw count divided by the sample size.

Adjustment can correct visible imbalances and recurring under-reporting. It also introduces judgement. Vote recall is imperfect: people forget, reinterpret, or decline to report past behaviour, and the electorate changes between elections. Institutes may use different variables, targets, caps, and models. Those details can cause two competent pollsters with similar raw interviews to publish different estimates.

Do not respond by rejecting all adjusted numbers. Raw data are not automatically neutral: recruitment and response already select a non-random group. Instead, inspect the notice for adjustment criteria, distinguish demographic weighting from political vote-recall adjustment, and compare like with like. A stable series from one institute often says more about movement than isolated numbers from several institutes because the recurring “house” methodology is held more nearly constant.

### Turnout modelling

Presidential voting intentions depend heavily on who is classified as likely to vote. Pollsters may ask respondents to rate their certainty of voting on a numeric scale and include only those above a threshold, sometimes alongside registration and prior participation indicators. The notice or questionnaire should show the question and filter.

This is consequential because turnout is not known in advance and is not evenly distributed. A small change in the likely-voter rule can change both the eligible base and candidate shares. Months before voting, a declaration of certainty is better understood as present engagement than as a binding commitment. When comparing polls, check whether one reports all registered voters and another only high-propensity voters.

### Candidate lists and questionnaires

Before candidacies and sponsorships are final, pollsters construct ballot scenarios. One poll may include a potential candidate, another a rival from the same camp, and a third both. Those are different experiments. The percentages cannot be joined into a continuous trend without accounting for the changed choice set.

Read the full questionnaire when available. Check the exact wording, whether candidate order was rotated, which questions preceded the ballot, and whether the survey tested several scenarios on the same respondents. Earlier questions can prime issues or personalities. Repeated hypothetical ballots can encourage respondents to manufacture a preference. These effects need not invalidate a study, but they limit naive comparisons.

## Uncertainty is larger than one margin

Polling uncertainty has at least four components:

1. **Sampling variation.** Even a well-designed sample would vary if repeated. Institute notices often publish indicative margin-of-error tables. For a random sample of 1,000, a share near 50% has a conventional 95% margin of roughly three percentage points; smaller shares have narrower textbook intervals.
2. **Design and coverage error.** Quota and panel samples are not simple random samples. The Commission and institute materials caution that conventional random-sample margins are approximations when applied to quota designs, not exact guarantees.
3. **Measurement error.** Question wording, candidate list, order, mode, timing, and respondent uncertainty affect answers.
4. **Model error.** Weighting, political adjustment, turnout filtering, and treatment of non-response translate interviews into the published estimate.

The familiar “±3 points” shorthand addresses only the first component under simplifying assumptions. It does not include future campaign events, candidate withdrawals, turnout shocks, or errors in the adjustment model. Nor does overlap between two candidates' separate intervals directly give the uncertainty of their gap: the covariance and common design matter.

Rounding creates another false precision. Reported whole percentages can conceal movements of less than one point or make a tiny change appear larger. A one-point shift between two waves is usually weak evidence by itself. More persuasive evidence is a repeated movement over multiple comparable waves, ideally seen across independent institutes using unchanged scenarios and field procedures.

Sample size also has diminishing returns: textbook sampling error falls approximately with the inverse square root of the sample size. Doubling a sample does not halve the error. And a huge sample does not repair biased coverage, weak likely-voter modelling, or a changing candidate list.

## A comparability checklist

Before calling two results a “trend,” compare these fields:

- **Fieldwork window:** Were interviews conducted at comparable moments, and did a major event fall inside one window?
- **Institute and method:** Same pollster, panel, mode, quota variables, and weighting approach?
- **Sponsor and series:** Is this the same recurring barometer or a separate commissioned study?
- **Population:** Adults, registered voters, likely voters, or expressed voters?
- **Voting-intention base:** What was the actual denominator after filters and non-response?
- **Candidate scenario:** Exactly the same names, qualifications, and first- or second-round pairing?
- **Question wording and order:** Same prompt, response options, rotation, and preceding questions?
- **Turnout filter:** Same likelihood scale and threshold?
- **Fieldwork size:** Same relevant base, not merely the same total sample?
- **Redressement:** Same demographic and vote-recall variables, so far as disclosed?
- **Display:** Raw or adjusted figures, rounded or decimal, percentage of all respondents or expressed choices?
- **Uncertainty:** Is the claimed change larger than routine sampling and modelling noise, and does it persist?

If several answers differ, describe the polls as distinct scenarios rather than a time series. Poll averages can reduce random noise and institute-specific effects, but only if their compiler handles changed scenarios, fieldwork dates, repeated samples, and house effects transparently. An average cannot turn fundamentally incomparable questions into a single electorate-wide fact.

## A disciplined reading routine

Start with the headline, then move immediately to the notice. Record the institute, sponsor, interview dates, mode, total sample, voting-intention base, candidate list, and adjustment criteria. Open the questionnaire or full report and identify the turnout question and denominator. Only then inspect the toplines.

Translate the finding into a full conditional sentence: “Among this pollster's filtered and adjusted respondents, interviewed on these dates and shown this candidate configuration, X% of expressed choices went to candidate A.” This wording is cumbersome, which is precisely why it disciplines thought. It prevents the estimate from silently becoming “X% of France.”

Next, compare the result with the previous wave of the *same* series. Check that the scenario and filters stayed constant. Then look across other institutes for convergence in direction, not for an identical point estimate. If a movement exists only in one wave or depends on one candidate configuration, label it tentative.

Separate three outputs:

- **Measurement:** what respondents said under the study's conditions.
- **Inference:** what that pattern may suggest about the electorate now.
- **Forecast:** what might happen on election day.

A poll directly supports the first, supports the second with qualifications, and supports the third only through an explicit forecasting model that adds assumptions beyond the poll.

## Common interpretation failures

“Candidate A leads by two points” can be arithmetically true yet analytically uninformative when ordinary uncertainty is larger than the gap. Call it a nominal lead, not a secure rank. “Candidate A gained two points” is weak if the prior wave used a different list or likely-voter base. “The poll was wrong” may confuse a snapshot weeks earlier with a forecast, although a final-wave miss still merits examination of design and modelling.

Another error is treating undecidedness as absent because a chart reports only expressed choices. The excluded or non-expressing share may be politically central. Likewise, second-round “vote transfers” are usually stated intentions in a hypothetical pairing, not literal tracking of individual first-round voters unless the study design supports that inference.

Do not rank pollsters from one election result alone. Electoral polling errors are correlated: institutes confront the same late events, turnout uncertainty, and social environment. Evaluation should consider repeated performance, transparency, methodological change, and whether the final comparison uses the correct fieldwork cutoff.

## Why it matters for 2027

The [[2027 French presidential election]] is a two-round contest in which small apparent differences near qualification thresholds can dominate coverage, donor expectations, endorsements, and strategic-voting narratives. That makes methodological literacy substantively important. A provisional candidate list can create an apparent hierarchy before voters know who will qualify; a hypothetical runoff can be mistaken for destiny; and a noisy change can feed a self-reinforcing story about “momentum.”

Careful reading does not mean ignoring polls. Polls are among the best systematic evidence about stated preferences available between elections. Their value increases when readers use the legal notice system, compare stable series, look for replication, and describe uncertainty honestly. The goal is neither credulity nor cynicism, but calibrated evidence: take patterns seriously in proportion to their design, comparability, and persistence.
