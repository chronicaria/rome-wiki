Cohabitation is the Fifth Republic's constitutional stress test: when the president and a durable National Assembly majority belong to opposing camps, the government answers to that majority and controls most policy, while the president retains the powers the Constitution gives personally to the head of state.

## What cohabitation is—and is not

France does not elect a single executive ticket. Voters elect the president directly, but the government must remain compatible with the separately elected National Assembly. **Cohabitation** names the situation in which those two electoral mandates point in opposite partisan directions: the sitting president faces an Assembly majority able to sustain a prime minister and government from the opposing camp.

The term describes a political configuration, not a special constitutional regime. No article of the 1958 Constitution declares that cohabitation has begun, reallocates a list of ministries, or suspends presidential powers. The same text applies before and after the legislative election. What changes is the political condition under which its powers operate. A president supported by the Assembly majority can usually secure compliance through partisan leadership. A president opposed by a coherent Assembly majority cannot make that majority's government politically dependent on the Élysée.

This is why cohabitation must be distinguished from two nearby situations.

First, an unpopular president governing with a friendly parliamentary majority is not cohabitation. Political weakness does not itself reverse the partisan alignment. Second, a **hung or fragmented Assembly** is not automatically cohabitation. Classic cohabitation presupposes an alternative majority capable of supporting a government. If no bloc can assemble or tolerate 289 deputies, the central problem is not a disciplined opposition government confronting the president but the absence of any stable governing coalition. France after the 2024 dissolution illustrated that different problem: divided government without the clear majority structure that existed in 1986, 1993, and 1997.

## The constitutional mechanism

The mechanism begins with four parts of the constitutional text that are sometimes obscured during periods of unified partisan control.

### The government, not the president, “determines and conducts” policy

Article 20 says that the government “determines and conducts the policy of the Nation” and is responsible to Parliament under Articles 49 and 50. Article 21 says that the prime minister directs the action of the government, is responsible for national defence, ensures execution of statutes, and exercises regulatory power subject to the president's specified powers. These clauses give the parliamentary majority a governing instrument of its own.

The government can be removed by the National Assembly. Under Article 49(2), a motion of no confidence must meet procedural requirements and win an absolute majority of the Assembly's members; Article 50 then requires the prime minister to tender the government's resignation. The president, by contrast, is not politically responsible to the Assembly. Cohabitation therefore does not make the president a parliamentary opposition leader inside government. It creates a divided executive in which the cabinet's survival turns on Parliament rather than presidential preference.

### Appointment is constrained by parliamentary survival

Article 8 gives the president the formal power to appoint the prime minister. It does not say that the Assembly elects the premier, and it does not expressly command the president to choose the leader of the largest group. Yet the appointment cannot be understood apart from Articles 20, 49, and 50. A president who appointed someone unacceptable to a cohesive opposition majority would invite the government's defeat or make governing impossible. In all three completed cohabitations, the president appointed a prime minister drawn from the victorious opposing majority: Jacques Chirac in 1986, Édouard Balladur in 1993, and Lionel Jospin in 1997.

Article 8 also matters in the other direction. The president ends a prime minister's functions only when the prime minister presents the government's resignation. During ordinary aligned government, presidents have often been able politically to induce that resignation. Under cohabitation, the president cannot simply dismiss a prime minister who retains Assembly support. The words of the article become a real constraint once presidential party discipline disappears.

### Countersignature separates personal from shared powers

Article 19 supplies a practical map. Most presidential acts require the countersignature of the prime minister and, where appropriate, responsible ministers. A limited set does not: the first paragraph of Article 8, the referendum power in Article 11, dissolution under Article 12, exceptional powers under Article 16, messages to Parliament under Article 18, and specified referrals or appointments involving the Constitutional Council under Articles 54, 56, and 61.

This distinction prevents either simple slogan—“the president still rules” or “the president becomes ceremonial”—from being accurate. The president retains meaningful autonomous levers, including appointment of the prime minister, dissolution subject to Article 12's conditions, and Constitutional Council referrals. But broad administrative and legislative action normally moves through a government that controls ministries, prepares most bills, executes statutes, and holds regulatory authority. Where countersignature is required, the president cannot treat a hostile government as a staff subordinate.

The 1986 dispute over ordinances made the point concrete. The Chirac government sought authority under Article 38 to legislate by ordinance on privatisation and other reforms. President François Mitterrand declined to sign several ordinances, forcing the government to pursue ordinary legislation. That episode showed both presidential resistance and its limits: refusal could alter the route and timing, but it did not give the president a general veto over a parliamentary majority's programme.

### Foreign affairs and defence are shared, not a textual presidential reservation

Commentary often speaks of a presidential *domaine réservé* in foreign affairs and defence. The phrase describes political practice, not a constitutional article assigning those fields exclusively to the president. Article 5 makes the president guarantor of national independence, territorial integrity, treaties, and regular institutional functioning. Article 15 makes the president commander-in-chief and chair of higher defence councils. Article 52 says the president negotiates and ratifies treaties.

But Articles 20 and 21 still apply: the government determines national policy, and the prime minister is responsible for national defence. Ministers and the administrative apparatus answer through the government. Cohabitation thus produces negotiation and coordination between two constitutionally armed poles. Presidents maintained a prominent international and defence role in all three cases, but the prime minister did not lose constitutional competence. Where the two agreed, France could present continuity; where they differed, institutional practice required compromise, careful representation, or visible duality.

## The three historical cohabitations

### 1986–1988: the precedent is made

The first cohabitation followed the March 1986 legislative election. François Mitterrand, a Socialist elected president in 1981 for a seven-year term, remained at the Élysée while the right won the Assembly election. He appointed Jacques Chirac as prime minister. The novelty mattered: before this result, constitutional argument had not been converted into a full operating precedent.

The arrangement quickly established enduring rules. Chirac's government pursued a right-wing domestic programme, including privatisation and changes in economic and security policy. Mitterrand used the presidency's textual resources, public speech, and refusal to sign some ordinances to resist parts of that programme. Yet he did not claim a power to remove Chirac while the government possessed parliamentary support. In foreign affairs, both executives remained active, demonstrating that cohabitation would mean shared state representation rather than the president's disappearance.

The arrangement ended through elections, not institutional breakdown. Mitterrand defeated Chirac in the 1988 presidential election. He then dissolved the Assembly under Article 12, and the subsequent legislative election produced a relative left majority. The first case therefore proved that opposing executives could complete the interval until the scheduled presidential contest and submit their rivalry back to voters.

### 1993–1995: a broad opposition majority and a weakened presidency

The second cohabitation followed the right's overwhelming victory in the March 1993 legislative election. Mitterrand was again president, nearing the end of his second term, and appointed Édouard Balladur as prime minister. The configuration was less openly combative than the first. Balladur had an exceptionally large parliamentary base, while Mitterrand's political authority was diminished by his camp's electoral defeat and his declining health.

The constitutional allocation, however, was not a new model. The government directed domestic policy and possessed the legislative majority; the president retained his office, personal powers, and international role. The difference in atmosphere shows that cohabitation is not a fixed level of conflict. Its operation depends on electoral arithmetic, personalities, the president's remaining time and legitimacy, and whether the prime minister is already competing for the next presidency.

This cohabitation ended when Jacques Chirac won the 1995 presidential election. Because the elected president and the existing Assembly majority were then aligned on the right, no immediate legislative election was needed to restore congruence.

### 1997–2002: dissolution creates the longest case

The third cohabitation began differently. President Jacques Chirac already had a supportive right-wing Assembly majority, but in April 1997 he dissolved the Assembly early. The left won the resulting election, and Chirac appointed Socialist leader Lionel Jospin as prime minister. The president's own use of Article 12 had produced the adverse majority.

Unlike the two Mitterrand cases, which occupied the final two years of seven-year presidential terms, this cohabitation lasted a full five-year legislature. The Jospin government enacted a substantial domestic agenda, including the statutory 35-hour workweek, universal health coverage, the civil solidarity pact, and further decentralising or social measures. The duration and policy output refute the idea that cohabitation necessarily means paralysis. It can instead generate a clear domestic governing centre—provided the Assembly majority is cohesive—while forcing coordination at the top of the state.

The long experience also strengthened the political case for reducing the presidential term from seven years to five. Voters approved the *quinquennat* in the 2000 referendum. An organic law then placed the 2002 legislative election after the presidential election. Chirac was reelected and his supporters won the Assembly contest. Since then, the paired calendar has tended to align the two majorities, although it does not legally guarantee alignment.

## What the historical cases establish

Three cases are enough to establish constitutional possibility, but not a mechanical script for every future divided Assembly.

They establish, first, that the Fifth Republic can operate without presidential–parliamentary partisan unity. The institutions did not collapse in 1986, 1993, or 1997. Governments legislated, budgets passed, and France continued to act internationally. Cohabitation is therefore neither an unconstitutional anomaly nor necessarily a caretaker interlude.

Second, they reveal that presidential primacy is partly political rather than purely textual. In aligned periods, the president usually chooses the prime minister politically, sets broad direction, and can expect government compliance. Under cohabitation, Articles 20 and 21 become much more visible. The shift is sometimes called a “parliamentary reading” of the Constitution, but the president does not cease to be directly elected or surrender personal constitutional powers.

Third, the cases show that institutions channel conflict without eliminating it. Disputes can arise over senior appointments, ordinance signatures, diplomatic representation, defence choices, European negotiations, and credit for policy. The Constitution contains both separate competences and interlocking procedures. Outcomes therefore depend on law, convention, electoral legitimacy, and bargaining.

Finally, cohabitation changes political accountability. The prime minister has the administrative machinery and Assembly programme but may be blamed for day-to-day outcomes. The president can criticise domestic policy while remaining head of state, yet still shares responsibility in fields where presidential action is visible. Voters must judge two competing executives, each able to claim a national mandate of a different date.

## Implications for 2027

The 2027 presidential election cannot by itself decide whether France will be governed through presidential primacy, cohabitation, minority government, or coalition bargaining. The National Assembly elected after the 2024 dissolution normally remains in office beyond the presidential election; absent another dissolution or legal change, no legislative election follows automatically in spring 2027. The decisive question is therefore the majority the new president confronts—either in that existing Assembly or in a later Assembly if Article 12 is used. A new president could win the Élysée but fail to secure an aligned Assembly. Conversely, a president whose camp holds only a plurality may face fragmentation rather than classic cohabitation.

Three distinctions should discipline analysis of 2027.

**A presidential win is not a legislative mandate.** The two-round presidential contest rewards a person who can assemble a runoff majority, sometimes through defensive voting against the opponent. Legislative elections operate constituency by constituency and can produce a differently composed Assembly. Calling them merely the presidential election's “third round” captures campaign sequencing but understates their independent constitutional function.

**The largest bloc is not necessarily a cohabitation majority.** Article 8 does not mechanically assign the premiership to the head of the largest group. The relevant question is whether an appointee can lead a government that the Assembly will tolerate and that can pass budgets and legislation. A plurality opposed by several mutually incompatible blocs may lack that capacity. A cross-party agreement or narrower cabinet with negotiated support would not reproduce the disciplined left–right cohabitations of 1986, 1993, and 1997.

**The usual calendar reduces risk but does not abolish it—and the 2024 dissolution disrupted that sequence.** Since 2002, legislative elections held soon after the presidency have usually created momentum for the new president. They cannot compel voters to confirm the presidential result, prevent mid-term dissolution, stop coalition fracture, or guarantee a majority. Because the 2024 dissolution started a new Assembly term, a president elected in 2027 would need to use Article 12 to seek an immediate electoral reset rather than receiving one automatically. Article 12 requires consultations and bars another dissolution within the year following an election caused by dissolution. The failed 1997 dissolution and the fragmented result after the 2024 dissolution are reminders that the president chooses whether to return to voters but not what voters decide.

For forecasting, the most useful questions are institutional rather than theatrical: Which candidate's coalition can nominate viable legislative candidates nationwide? Can it reach an absolute majority, or only a plurality? Is there a coherent opposing majority capable of sustaining one prime minister? Which parties would support, join, or merely refrain from censuring a government? Without answers to those questions, declaring “cohabitation” after a presidential poll or even after a first legislative round is premature.

## Why it matters

Cohabitation explains why the 2027 race is not a contest for unlimited control of the French state. The president's office carries exceptional visibility, direct electoral legitimacy, autonomous constitutional tools, and central roles in defence and diplomacy. Yet the government possesses the ordinary machinery of national policy and must survive in the Assembly. The actual balance depends on the relationship between two electoral mandates.

That relationship also changes how campaign promises should be read. A presidential candidate can credibly describe how they would use personal powers, nominate a prime minister, or seek a legislative majority. They cannot guarantee enactment of a domestic programme without parliamentary support. If an opposing majority emerges, its government—not the defeated presidential platform—will usually control taxation, spending, social legislation, and most regulatory policy, while the president retains the capacity to shape, delay, refer, represent, and sometimes force a new electoral confrontation within constitutional limits.

The deeper lesson is that cohabitation is not a strange import from another presidential system. It grows from the Fifth Republic's own mixed architecture: a directly elected president alongside a government responsible to Parliament. Understanding it on French terms prevents two opposite errors—treating the president as all-powerful, or treating cohabitation as if it instantly converted the presidency into a ceremonial office.

## Open questions

- If 2027 produces no absolute majority, what observable threshold would justify describing the arrangement as cohabitation rather than minority or coalition government?
- How would a future cohabitation divide representation in European Council meetings when president and prime minister disagree on a live policy question?
- Which senior civil and military appointments would become the most consequential sites of countersignature bargaining?
- Would a president elected in 2027 accept an opposition prime minister immediately after a clear legislative defeat, or test whether a narrower government could survive censure?
- How much has post-2002 presidentialisation changed political expectations even though the underlying Articles 8, 20, 21, and 49 remain in force?
