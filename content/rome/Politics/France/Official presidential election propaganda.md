France's *propagande électorale officielle* is a state-supervised delivery system for a presidential candidate's standard statement, posters and ballot—not a state endorsement of the candidate's claims.

## A term of administration, not an accusation

In ordinary English, “propaganda” often means manipulative or deceptive communication. French election law uses *propagande électorale* more neutrally. It covers campaign material used to persuade voters: posters, candidate statements, leaflets and, in the broader administrative vocabulary, ballot papers. The adjective *officielle* identifies the tightly regulated channel in which public authorities reserve equal facilities, check formal compliance, organize nationwide distribution, and assume specified costs. It does not mean that the state wrote, fact-checked or approved the candidate's platform.

That distinction matters for reading the [[2027 French presidential election]]. The packet delivered to a voter is neither an impartial voter guide nor a collection of government summaries. Each candidate authors a political argument. The public apparatus standardizes opportunity and circulation: one national statement per candidate and round, prescribed documents, equal poster space, deadlines, accessible electronic versions, and a chain of national and local control. The architecture aims at equality between candidates and reliable access for electors while leaving political responsibility for the message with the candidate.

The governing presidential rules are principally in the decree of 8 March 2001 implementing the 1962 law on direct presidential election, especially articles 13 and 16–23. General provisions of the electoral code also apply. The exact operational calendar, tariffs, quantities and technical instructions for 2027 will depend on later decrees, orders and instructions. This note therefore explains the durable machinery as in force on 10 July 2026 rather than predicting unissued 2027 details.

## The three physical objects

### The declaration or *profession de foi*

The familiar *profession de foi* is called the candidate's “text of declarations” in the presidential decree. Before each round, a candidate may have only one such text sent through the official channel to electors. It is printed as a double sheet and must comply with the applicable paper and format rules. Most importantly, the text must be uniform throughout the Republic. A presidential candidate cannot use the state mailing to send one platform in metropolitan departments and a politically tailored version elsewhere.

The candidate or candidate's representatives arrange printing. The state does not compose the document. The candidate deposits the printed text with the national control commission by the same deadline that applies to the principal declaration poster: no later than 8 p.m. on the third Friday before the first round, and the second Thursday before the second round. Local delivery has its own practical cutoff. A local commission need not mail material handed in after the date fixed for that round by the representative of the state. Formal entitlement is therefore inseparable from production logistics: a candidate who misses a delivery deadline cannot demand that the administration overcome the resulting impossibility.

The official mailing makes this statement unusually powerful. It reaches electors without requiring them to encounter a rally, television appearance or social-media account. Yet receipt should not be confused with certification. The document remains advocacy. The commission's scrutiny concerns compliance with law and regulation, national uniformity, and protection of the sincerity of the vote—not whether a tax proposal is economically sound or a historical assertion is persuasive.

### Posters

Municipalities reserve special poster sites under the electoral code, and each candidate receives an allotted panel in the order of the official candidate list established by the Constitutional Council. The equal surface is a visible expression of the principle that presidential candidates receive the same facilities from the state.

For each round, a candidate may place on the reserved site one poster setting out declarations and another announcing campaign meetings. The principal declaration poster may be no larger than 841 by 594 millimetres. The meeting poster is 297 by 420 millimetres and has a restricted informational function: it may identify the meeting's date and place, speakers, the candidate, a campaign website, social-media identifiers and, if desired, the time of the candidate's official broadcasts. The rules prevent the nominal “meeting notice” from becoming an unlimited second platform poster.

The declaration poster must use one text throughout the Republic. Its text and an electronic version go to the national commission by the statutory deadline. The commission sends the approved reference onward to state representatives. Unlike presidential ballot papers, however, posters are printed and physically posted by the candidate or the candidate's representatives. Article 20 nevertheless places the regulated paper, printing and posting costs of the article 17 posters among the expenses directly assumed by the state, subject to the applicable tariffs and justification.

General poster rules still matter. The electoral code regulates matters such as dimensions and color use and reserves designated spaces. Official panels are therefore not a free-form canvas detached from ordinary election law. A candidate's ability to campaign through other lawful channels also does not convert every privately circulated image or leaflet into official propaganda eligible for the same administrative handling or payment.

### Ballot papers

The presidential ballot is the clearest limit on candidate control. Article 23 of the 2001 decree says that ballots follow a uniform model for all candidates, contain only the candidate's surname and given name, and are printed and supplied to local control commissions by the administration. This differs from elections in which lists or candidates print and lodge their own ballots.

The local commission sends ballots onward, including to municipalities. At the polling station, ballots received from the local control commission are made available under the responsibility of the presiding officer. The administration's role protects uniformity and reduces the chance that design, paper or extra slogans create an advantage or confusion.

It also makes ballot authenticity legally consequential. A ballot different from those supplied by the administration is not counted, nor is one naming a person outside the official list established by the Constitutional Council for that round. The online or mailed *profession de foi* is not a ballot and cannot be used as one. The distinction among advocacy statement, display poster and voting instrument is thus substantive, not merely graphic.

## National control, local execution

Article 13 establishes the Commission nationale de contrôle de la campagne électorale, commonly described as the CNCCEP. It safeguards the equal campaign facilities guaranteed to presidential candidates and may intervene with competent authorities to secure equality and observance of the campaign rules. It is installed the day after publication of the decree summoning electors.

Its institutional design signals legal rather than partisan supervision. The five-member commission includes, as members by right, the vice-president of the Conseil d'État as chair, the first president of the Cour de cassation and the first president of the Cour des comptes, plus two current or honorary members drawn from those three senior jurisdictions. It may use rapporteurs and is assisted by officials representing the ministries responsible for the interior, overseas territories and foreign affairs. Its decisions may be challenged before the Conseil d'État. If it learns of irregularities that may affect campaign accounts, it transmits them to the campaign-finance commission, the CNCCFP.

The national commission receives the reference texts for declaration posters and statements. When a submitted document violates applicable law or regulation, or could impair the sincerity of the vote, article 18-1 requires an adversarial sequence. The commission states its reasons and asks the candidate to make specified corrections within a deadline. A candidate who disagrees may submit observations. If the commission still considers the response inadequate, it may issue a reasoned refusal to transmit the material into the official distribution chain.

This is more than a printing check, but less than comprehensive truth arbitration. A risk to the sincerity of the vote can justify intervention. Ordinary disagreement over a candidate's policy analysis does not turn the commission into a ministry of truth. The reasoned process and possibility of judicial review restrain both candidates and administrator.

Local control commissions translate the national framework into distribution. One is established in every metropolitan and overseas department, every overseas collectivity and New Caledonia, under the authority of the national commission. They are created by prefectural order and installed no later than the fourth Friday before the election, subject to the constitutional emergency rule referenced in the decree. Their organization incorporates the electoral code rules for propaganda commissions; they may add rapporteurs drawn from judges or serving or honorary state officials.

After the national commission transmits the candidate's declaration, the representative of the state checks that the printed run conforms to the approved reference. The local commissions then arrange mailing to electors. They also handle the ballot-distribution channel. This layered sequence—candidate authorship and printing, national legal control, territorial conformity check, local dispatch—explains why public delivery does not imply public authorship.

## Accessibility is part of the official channel

The paper statement now has mandatory digital companions. A presidential candidate must deposit an audio recording and an electronic text readable by screen-reading software, together with the printed declaration. After the national commission checks that the recording conforms to the printed text, each candidate's declarations are placed online in text and audio form when the official campaign opens.

The candidate must also provide a version written for people with disabilities or comprehension difficulties. The decree describes a language using common words, short sentences and pictograms; the Interior Ministry explains this as *Facile à lire et à comprendre* (FALC). These electronic texts are published for everyone. The obligation applies to presidential elections, and the ministry's practical accessibility guidance distinguishes the presidential site managed through the CNCCEP process from its general candidate-program portal for other national elections.

Accessibility should therefore not be treated as a voluntary afterthought to the paper mailing. At least four representations have legal or operational significance: the printed national declaration, machine-readable electronic text, a conforming audio recording, and the simplified accessible version. They serve different needs. A scanned image of a leaflet may reproduce appearance while remaining unusable by a screen reader; an audio file may make the words available while not helping every person with comprehension difficulties; FALC addresses intelligibility rather than merely file access.

There is also a limit worth keeping clear. The commission verifies the audio against the printed declaration, but a FALC version necessarily involves simplification and presentation choices. Voters and researchers should compare versions when precision matters. Accessibility expands the audience for campaign communication; it does not change its status as candidate advocacy.

## Distribution and public payment

The official system divides operational responsibility more finely than the phrase “state-funded propaganda” suggests. Candidates create their messages and, for declarations and posters, commission production. The administration prints presidential ballots. National and local commissions verify reference materials and organize official circulation. Municipal authorities reserve poster sites and polling stations receive standardized ballots.

Article 20 identifies the costs directly borne by the state: the official audiovisual campaign; paper and printing for ballots and article 18 declarations; paper, printing and posting for article 17 posters; and the operations and functioning of the national and local commissions. Joint orders by the interior and finance ministers set printing and posting tariffs under the electoral code framework. Direct coverage is bounded by the regulated operation and tariff; it is not a blank cheque for all creative, digital, staffing, transport or surplus-printing choices made by a campaign.

For printed declarations, reimbursement also has an environmental-paper condition. On supporting documents, the state assumes paper and printing costs only for statements made with ecologically qualifying paper meeting at least one of the criteria in article R. 39 of the electoral code. The national commission can waive that requirement when a candidate was unable to obtain qualifying paper, but a refusal to grant the exception must be reasoned.

This direct-cost channel must be distinguished from the later public reimbursement attached to the candidate's campaign account. The CNCCFP reviews election receipts and expenses and determines the account consequences under separate rules. Official-propaganda costs governed by articles 20 and 21 occupy their own legal channel. Costs outside the reimbursable quantities, formats or tariffs, and related campaign expenses that are not part of the official operation, may be treated differently in campaign accounting. For 2027, candidate teams will need the election-specific CNCCEP and CNCCFP guidance rather than assuming that every invoice mentioning a poster or statement is automatically payable.

## What the machinery guarantees—and what it does not

The system provides five important guarantees. First, every officially admitted candidate can use the same category of facilities. Second, national uniformity prevents territorial micro-targeting inside the official mailing and poster declaration. Third, formal control reduces unlawful or misleadingly irregular materials entering the state distribution chain. Fourth, local commissions provide a nationwide logistical network. Fifth, electronic, audio and simplified versions make the official statement accessible beyond the printed sheet.

None of these guarantees equal substantive endorsement. The commission does not certify that every promise is feasible, every statistic correctly framed or every opponent fairly characterized. Nor does delivery prove that every elector actually received the packet; postal production and address problems can still occur. Online availability mitigates some failures but cannot fully replace paper for people with limited digital access.

The system also does not encompass the whole campaign. Social-media posts, rally speeches, commercial printing, party leaflets, interviews and many other communications operate under campaign law but are not all handled as article 17 or 18 official material. Analysts should ask two separate questions: whether communication is legally regulated as electoral propaganda, and whether it belongs to the narrow official production-and-distribution operation.

## Why it matters for 2027

The official packet and panels will offer a uniquely comparable record of the final authorized candidates' self-presentation. Because each declaration is national and each candidate receives the same document category, researchers can compare priorities, rhetorical framing and second-round repositioning without confusing regional variants. The audio, machine-readable and FALC files may also preserve wording that is easier to search and analyze than photographed posters.

The decisive 2027 checkpoints will come late. The list of official candidates determines whose materials enter the process. The summoning decree activates the national commission. Deposit deadlines compress review, correction, printing and distribution. Between rounds, the two surviving candidates must produce material on a far tighter timetable. A dispute about a poster or statement should be read through article 18-1's correction-and-observation procedure, not automatically described as censorship or approval.

For voters, the practical rule is simple: treat the *profession de foi* as the candidate speaking through an equalized public delivery channel. Use its accessible versions where helpful, but verify contested factual claims independently. At the polling station, use only the official ballot supplied by the administration. For campaign-finance analysis, separate the regulated direct state coverage of official material from the campaign-account reimbursement regime.
