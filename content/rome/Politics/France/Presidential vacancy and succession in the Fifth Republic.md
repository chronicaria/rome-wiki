France answers a presidential vacancy with a restricted interim presidency and a rapid new national election, not with an heir who completes the departed president's term.

Up: [[2027 French presidential election]] · Related: [[Presidential powers in the Fifth Republic]] · [[How the French presidential election works]]

## The central design

Article 7 of the 1958 Constitution combines continuity with democratic replacement. If the presidency becomes vacant “for whatever cause,” presidential functions pass temporarily to the president of the Senate. If that officeholder is also unable to act, the Government exercises the functions. The interim authority keeps the state operating, but it cannot use the president's Article 11 referendum power or Article 12 power to dissolve the National Assembly. A new president must then be elected within a constitutionally compressed window.

This is not succession in the American sense. France has no vice-president elected as part of a presidential ticket and no constitutional officer who inherits the remainder of the five-year mandate. The Senate president is an interim custodian. The electorate supplies the successor through a fresh two-round election. That distinction matters for 2027 because a vacancy before the ordinary election would replace the published calendar with Article 7's emergency timetable, while a disruption involving a candidate can postpone or restart electoral operations without necessarily creating a vacancy in the presidency.

The system separates four questions that are often blurred in crisis reporting:

1. Has the presidential office become vacant?
2. Is the serving president temporarily or permanently unable to exercise the office?
3. Who may exercise presidential functions during the interruption?
4. Must an election be held, postponed, or begun again?

Each answer comes from a different part of Article 7. None should be inferred merely from illness, political weakness, absence from France, or controversy.

## Vacancy is different from incapacity

A *vacance* means that the office itself is empty. Article 7 deliberately says that vacancy may arise “for whatever cause.” The two Fifth Republic examples are the resignation of Charles de Gaulle in April 1969 and the death of Georges Pompidou in April 1974. Removal under Article 68 would likewise produce a vacancy: destitution ends the incumbent's tenure rather than merely suspending the exercise of a power. Expiration of the normal term is different because Article 7 ordinarily schedules the replacement election before the incumbent's powers expire.

An *empêchement* is an incapacity while a person remains president. The Constitution does not supply a medical checklist or allow journalists, Parliament, a court, or the Senate president to pronounce it informally. The Government must refer the question to the Conseil constitutionnel, and the Council must decide by an absolute majority of its members. This institutional gate protects continuity against self-appointed successors and makes a consequential finding depend on a defined constitutional procedure.

Article 7 distinguishes the finding of incapacity from a finding that it is definitive. Once an incapacity is formally found, the interim arrangement can operate. The accelerated replacement election is triggered when the incapacity is declared definitive. A temporary incapacity therefore need not end the mandate or immediately send voters to the polls. The text leaves difficult judgment to the Government and Council: whether circumstances justify referral, what evidence establishes inability, and when inability has become definitive.

That silence is functional but uncertain. A rigid diagnosis list could fail in an unforeseen crisis; an open constitutional standard can adapt. Yet flexibility also means that predictions about an untested case must be labelled as interpretation. No Fifth Republic precedent has required the Council to declare a serving president incapacitated. The historical record establishes how vacancy works more clearly than it establishes how evidence for incapacity would be evaluated.

## The interim authority

The president of the Senate is first in line to exercise presidential functions provisionally. The choice favors institutional continuity. Unlike the National Assembly, the Senate cannot be dissolved, and it renews only part of its membership at a time. Its president is therefore likely to remain identifiable even if a presidential crisis coincides with an Assembly dissolution.

If the Senate president is also unable to exercise the functions, they pass to the Government. The constitutional wording points to the Government collectively, not to an automatic promotion of the prime minister as a new president. France would still have an interim exercise of presidential functions pending resolution of temporary incapacity or election of a successor.

The interim president has broad continuity powers but two express presidential tools are unavailable. Article 11 cannot be used to submit the covered legislative questions to referendum, and Article 12 cannot be used to dissolve the National Assembly. The restriction prevents a temporary custodian from remaking the political arena through the two most direct instruments of presidential appeal over or against Parliament.

Article 7 adds system-wide restraints during vacancy, and during the period from a declaration of definitive incapacity until the successor's election. Articles 49 and 50—the principal constitutional provisions governing Government responsibility before the National Assembly and the consequences of censure or loss of confidence—cannot be applied. Article 89 constitutional revision is also disabled. These freezes reduce the risk that the parliamentary majority, Government, or interim authority exploits a short presidential interruption to force a simultaneous change of Government or constitutional settlement.

The text does not say that every other presidential power disappears. The interim office exists precisely so that acts necessary to state continuity can occur. Alain Poher presided over the Council of Ministers while acting as interim president in 1974. Nevertheless, legal availability is not the same as a political mandate. An interim president is unelected to that office and appointed by constitutional sequence for a short bridging period. Prudence, duration, parliamentary conditions, and the purpose of the interim all constrain what can responsibly be inferred from the absence of an express prohibition.

## The rapid replacement election

When a vacancy opens, or when the Council declares incapacity definitive, the new presidential election must ordinarily occur no fewer than twenty and no more than thirty-five days after the triggering event. The Government must convene voters without delay by decree under the organic provisions of the law of 6 November 1962. This is much shorter than the ordinary rule requiring the convocation decree at least ten weeks before the first round.

The twenty-to-thirty-five-day language refers to the election for the new president within Article 7's emergency scheme. The two-round structure still applies: if no one receives an absolute majority of valid votes in the first round, the second occurs on the fourteenth day thereafter. Administrators and candidates must therefore fit presentations, eligibility review, campaigning, voting logistics, and possible runoff operations into a schedule designed for constitutional necessity rather than convenience.

Organic rules compress ballot access accordingly. In the version of Article 3 of the 1962 law in force on the research date, presentations in a vacancy or definitive-incapacity election must arrive by 6 p.m. on the third Tuesday before the first round, rather than the sixth Friday used for an ordinary election. The candidate list must be published at least fifteen days before the first round, rather than no later than the fourth Friday before it. The precise version in force when a future crisis occurs would control, so these implementation rules must be rechecked rather than treated as permanently frozen.

The Conseil constitutionnel may recognize *force majeure*. Article 7 allows it to extend the relevant deadlines, but polling may not take place more than thirty-five days after the Council's decision. Force majeure is not a free political option to lengthen a difficult campaign. It is a constitutional finding by the institution assigned to manage an exceptional obstruction.

An emergency election produces a full new presidential mandate. The successful candidate does not merely serve the months or years remaining in the predecessor's term. That consequence follows from the structure of Articles 6 and 7: the vacancy triggers “the election of the new President,” and Article 6 gives the president a five-year term. The interim office ends when the constitutionally selected successor takes over; it is not a truncated elected presidency of its own.

## Disruption before or during an election

Article 7 also anticipates the death or incapacity of a potential or confirmed candidate. These provisions protect electoral choice, but their triggers and consequences differ by stage.

In the seven days before the presentation deadline, the Council may postpone the election if a person dies or becomes incapacitated after publicly announcing a candidacy fewer than thirty days before that deadline. The rule is discretionary—“may”—and is limited by its timing and public-announcement conditions. It is not a general power to postpone whenever a politically discussed figure cannot run.

After the candidate list is established but before the first round, death or incapacity of a candidate requires the Council to postpone the election. Once the first round has occurred, death or incapacity of either of the two best-placed candidates before possible withdrawals requires the electoral operations to begin again. The same restart follows if one of the two candidates remaining for the runoff dies or becomes incapacitated.

These rules avoid a ballot or runoff whose competitive structure has been fundamentally altered. A simple withdrawal is not automatically equivalent. Article 7's ordinary runoff rule permits the two highest candidates to be identified after any withdrawals by better-placed candidates, while its crisis clauses specifically address death and incapacity. Analysts should therefore identify the stage, the person's legal status, and the Council's decision before claiming that an election “must” be restarted.

If a postponement pushes the election beyond expiration of the sitting president's powers, Article 7 keeps that president in office until the successor is proclaimed. This continuity rule concerns an incumbent whose ordinary term would otherwise end while an election is delayed. It is distinct from the Senate president's interim role after an actual vacancy.

## The two precedents

France has used the vacancy mechanism twice under the Fifth Republic, with Alain Poher, then president of the Senate, serving as interim president both times.

De Gaulle resigned after voters rejected the April 27, 1969 referendum. The vacancy opened and an early presidential election followed. Poher did more than maintain paperwork: he stood as a candidate, reached the runoff, and lost to Georges Pompidou. That sequence demonstrates a structural tension the Constitution permits. The interim custodian may also participate in the contest for the permanent office, even though the express restrictions on referendum and dissolution and the short timetable limit the office's leverage.

Pompidou died in office on April 2, 1974. Poher again exercised the presidential functions during the accelerated election that brought Valéry Giscard d'Estaing to office. An archived April 5 account records Poher presiding over an exceptional Council of Ministers as interim president. The example illustrates why continuity requires affirmative capacity, not merely prohibitions: ministers, appointments, diplomacy, and administration cannot all pause until polling is complete.

Two precedents are enough to show that resignation and death activate the mechanism immediately, but not enough to answer every modern scenario. Neither required a definitive-incapacity ruling. Neither occurred amid the present media system, campaign-finance framework, public presentation rules, or a fragmented Assembly like the one elected after the 2024 dissolution. Historical analogy should establish the constitutional sequence, not manufacture certainty about contemporary political behavior.

## Relationship to Article 68 and Article 16

Three exceptional constitutional ideas must be kept separate. Article 7 addresses vacancy and incapacity. Article 68 permits removal of a president for a breach of duties manifestly incompatible with continuation in office; actual removal would create a vacancy and then activate Article 7. Article 16 gives an incumbent exceptional powers when the Republic's institutions, independence, territory, or international commitments face grave and immediate threat and normal constitutional public powers are interrupted. It does not itself declare the president incapacitated or appoint a successor.

The distinction is causal. Article 68 supplies one possible route by which the office becomes empty. Article 7 supplies continuity and election rules after vacancy, and a separate procedure for incapacity. Article 16 presupposes a president able to exercise extraordinary authority. Treating all three as a generic “emergency succession” power obscures who initiates each procedure, what fact must be established, and what legal consequence follows.

## What a pre-2027 vacancy would change

The Interior Ministry currently publishes April 18 and May 2, 2027 as the expected ordinary rounds. Those dates remain the baseline only while the presidency reaches its normal electoral transition. If a vacancy or definitive incapacity occurred early enough, Article 7 would require an earlier election on its own twenty-to-thirty-five-day clock. The Government's immediate convocation decree and the Council's compressed presentation calendar would become controlling sources.

The candidate field could change sharply. Five hundred presentations would still be required, but campaigns would have much less time to secure and transmit them. Party nomination plans designed around an ordinary calendar could be overtaken by the constitutional timetable. Polls testing hypothetical 2027 slates would not establish who could organize, qualify, and campaign under emergency conditions.

A vacancy would also freeze dissolution, Government-responsibility procedures, and constitutional revision at a moment when the Assembly's composition could matter greatly. The new president would still face the separate problem explained in [[From the Élysée to a National Assembly majority]]: a presidential victory does not itself create a parliamentary majority. Article 7 ensures a president is chosen quickly; it does not resolve legislative fragmentation for that president.

## Why it matters

Succession law is insurance against both a power vacuum and an unelected long-term replacement. The Senate president or Government can keep constitutional functions operating, but cannot use the interval to call a referendum, dissolve the Assembly, revise the Constitution, or trigger the specified Government-confidence machinery. Voters then confer a fresh mandate within weeks.

For election analysis, the practical rule is to avoid premature constitutional storytelling. Illness is not a formal incapacity. Political calls for resignation do not create a vacancy. A candidate's withdrawal is not necessarily death or incapacity. A published ordinary calendar is not immutable after an Article 7 event. Only the responsible institution's act—the president's resignation, the fact of death or removal, a Government referral and Council finding, a convocation decree, or a Council postponement decision—changes the legal state.
