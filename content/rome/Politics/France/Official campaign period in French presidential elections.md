France's *campagne électorale officielle* is a short, equality-centered legal phase immediately before each presidential ballot; it must not be confused with the much longer campaign-finance period, Arcom's pre-campaign media monitoring, or ordinary political campaigning.

## The phrase “the campaign” hides several clocks

In ordinary political speech, a French presidential campaign can last for months or years. A prospective contender tours the country, publishes a book, seeks a party nomination, recruits staff, raises money, and pursues the 500 presentations well before the official candidate list exists. None of that is made unreal by the fact that the *official* campaign has not opened. It simply belongs to a different legal phase.

For legal and journalistic purposes, at least five clocks should be kept separate:

| Clock | What it regulates | 2027 position as of 10 July 2026 |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Political campaigning | Declarations, meetings, party selection, organization, persuasion | Already occurring as a political fact; no single statutory opening day |
| Campaign-finance accounting | Election-directed receipts and expenses, the financial agent, account and ceilings | Opened **1 April 2026**, according to the 2027 CNCCFP guide |
| Pre-official audiovisual phases | Equitable access and exposure under an election-specific Arcom recommendation | 2027 recommendation and exact phases not yet issued |
| Official electoral campaign | Strict equality, official broadcasts, state facilities and the final regulated phase before each ballot | First-round opening is legally derivable as **5 April 2027**; operational instruments remain future |
| State propaganda operation | Approved posters, candidate statements, audio versions, printing and delivery | Detailed 2027 timetable, specifications, draws and submission deadlines not yet issued |

This separation prevents a common false binary. It is wrong both to say that candidates cannot campaign before April 2027 and to treat every activity since 2026 as part of the formal campaign. The law attaches different consequences to different periods.

## The formal campaign's legal boundaries

Article 10 of Decree no. 2001-213, the presidential implementing decree, says that the campaign for the first round opens on the **second Monday preceding the ballot** and closes at **zero hours on the eve of the ballot**. Article L. 47 A of the Electoral Code supplies the general statutory formulation. For a first round scheduled on Sunday 18 April 2027, the opening is therefore Monday **5 April 2027**. Closure at zero hours on Saturday 17 April means the campaign activity governed by the closing rule stops as Friday turns into Saturday; it does not continue through Saturday daytime.

The between-round rule needs more care. The Electoral Code generally says that a second-round campaign opens the day after the first round. The presidential implementing decree states more specifically that it opens upon publication in the *Journal officiel* of the names of the two candidates authorized to stand. The Conseil constitutionnel establishes the first-round result and the two-person field. For 2027, the exact publication event cannot be supplied in advance. Coverage should identify the actual Council decision and *Journal officiel* publication rather than inventing a date from the 2022 sequence.

The second-round campaign closes at zero hours on the eve of the second ballot. With the second round scheduled for Sunday 2 May 2027, that closing point is Saturday **1 May at 00:00**. Again, the practical silence period occupies the Saturday before Sunday voting.

The formal period is short because it is not intended to contain the whole democratic contest. Its distinctive purpose is to place the officially qualified candidates within a final common framework: equal state facilities, controlled official materials, equal audiovisual treatment, and a common cessation point before voting.

## Qualification comes before official equality

The formal campaign is built around a settled candidate list. Under Article 3 of the 1962 law, the Government publishes the list no later than the fourth Friday before the first round, after the [[Conseil constitutionnel]] has handled presentations and the relevant eligibility process. The list is therefore a legal gateway: equality during the official phase applies to the people who have actually qualified, not to every declared, party-selected, or widely polled contender.

That does not mean media pluralism begins only after qualification. It means the regulator must use different principles at different stages. Before a final field exists, mechanically equal treatment of every possible contender would be both difficult and potentially distortive. Once the field is fixed and the official campaign opens, strict equality becomes administrable and legally required.

The [[French presidential nomination mechanisms|qualification system]] and the official campaign consequently answer different questions. Presentations and consent establish who may appear on the ballot. The official campaign establishes the common facilities and treatment available to those qualified candidates in the final days.

## The national control commission and equal state facilities

The 1962 law requires all candidates to receive the same facilities from the state for the presidential campaign. Decree no. 2001-213 creates the **Commission nationale de contrôle de la campagne électorale en vue de l'élection présidentielle** to help enforce that equality and oversee the campaign's proper conduct.

The commission is an election-specific body, not a continuously operating campaign regulator. The decree provides for it to be installed around the election and gives it a high-level institutional composition. Its work sits alongside, rather than replaces, other authorities. Arcom supervises the audiovisual rules. The CNCCFP later examines campaign accounts. The Conseil constitutionnel supervises the regularity of the presidential election and proclaims results. Interior and local administrations carry out logistics. Treating “the commission” as a single all-purpose electoral authority obscures this divided architecture.

The national control commission's practical center is official candidate communication. It verifies that state-provided facilities are equal and examines official materials under the decree. Past commission reports are valuable because they show how rules are operationalized—format reviews, deadlines, printing problems, distribution, online accessibility, disputes and coordination—but a prior report is precedent and evidence, not the 2027 instruction sheet.

This distinction also clarifies the meaning of “official propaganda.” In French election administration, *propagande électorale* is a neutral legal term for campaign communication. It does not necessarily imply deception. The official operation covers specified materials and facilities; it does not certify every political claim as true or convert the state into the author of a candidate's program.

## Posters, candidate statements, and delivery

Articles 16–18 of the presidential decree organize the principal official physical materials. Authorities provide reserved poster locations. During the campaign before each round, each candidate may use the assigned space for a poster presenting declarations and another announcing electoral meetings; the decree permits specified additional information such as the time of official broadcasts and campaign internet or social-media identifiers.

Each candidate may also have a text of declarations sent to voters. This is commonly called the *profession de foi* or electoral statement. The presidential system is national: the statement is a common candidate document rather than a patchwork of locally tailored leaflets. The commission examines conformity with the governing rules. An audio recording accompanies the accessibility system. The Interior Ministry has also made candidate statements available through its official online program portal in recent elections.

State involvement should be described precisely. The candidate writes and politically owns the message. The control and administrative bodies set formats, examine compliance, coordinate production or receipt, and arrange equal distribution under the applicable instructions. Delivery to a household is not a governmental endorsement. Nor does failure of a particular envelope to arrive erase the election; distribution quality is an administrative issue whose legal consequence depends on scale, cause, unequal effect and the competent authority's findings.

The controlled mailing is only one channel. Candidates and supporters may communicate through meetings, leaflets, websites, social platforms, interviews and other lawful means outside the official packet. Those channels remain subject to applicable propaganda, finance, advertising, data-protection, defamation and silence-period rules. “Official material” therefore means a state-facilitated category, not the universe of legitimate campaign speech.

For 2027, the exact paper dimensions, grammage, electronic-file requirements, accessibility specifications, quantities, delivery addresses and deadlines should be taken from the future commission, ministry and prefectural instruments. Copying a 2022 technical deadline into a 2027 calendar would turn a precedent into a false fact.

## Official radio and television broadcasts

Article 15 of Decree no. 2001-213 guarantees each candidate equal duration in official radio and television broadcasts carried by the national public-service program companies. Arcom fixes the duration after consulting all candidates. The decree sets a floor of **15 minutes per candidate for the first round**. For the second round, the floor is **one hour per candidate**, unless both finalists agree to a shorter duration.

These candidate broadcasts are a distinct object from news coverage. They are designated campaign programs produced and transmitted within rules set by Arcom. Equality of their allocated duration does not itself describe how every news bulletin, interview or debate is counted. Conversely, a candidate's appearance in editorial programming is not automatically part of that candidate's official broadcast allotment.

The distinction matters operationally. Arcom normally issues one or more decisions dealing with duration, sequence, production conditions, use of archives or third-party material, technical delivery, draws, recordings, accessibility and schedules. None of the detailed 2027 broadcast grid existed in the official materials reviewed as of 10 July 2026. The statutory and decree-based guarantee is already knowable; the actual slots are future facts.

## Arcom's media phases are broader than the formal campaign

[[Media pluralism and speaking time in French elections]] begins before the official campaign. For 2022, Arcom's election-specific recommendation created three periods. From 1 January until the eve of publication of the candidate list, broadcasters had to provide equitable speaking and airtime to candidates and supporters. From publication of the official list until the eve of the formal campaign, equity continued in comparable programming conditions. During the official campaign, equality replaced equity for speaking time and airtime, again in comparable programming conditions.

**Equity** is not a euphemism for arbitrary favoritism. Arcom considers indicators such as political representativeness and demonstrated involvement in the campaign. **Equality** is the stricter presidential rule once the official campaign is underway. Arcom distinguishes *temps de parole*—candidate and supporter speech—from *temps d'antenne*, which additionally captures editorial sequences devoted to a candidate when they are not explicitly unfavorable. Equal raw minutes at 3 a.m. and prime time would not necessarily satisfy comparable programming conditions, so the regulator also examines scheduling bands.

The 2022 chronology illustrates the mechanism but does not fix the 2027 calendar. The statutory equality requirement during the official campaign is durable. The date on which Arcom begins a pre-campaign equity count, the services covered, reporting intervals, attribution conventions and treatment of new audiovisual formats depend on a future 2027 recommendation. Until that text is issued, an analyst may use 2022 as a labeled precedent but should not announce “1 January 2027” as an appointed regulatory opening.

Between rounds, the relationship between the Council's result process, formal campaign language and Arcom's equality count also deserves exact sourcing. In 2022, Arcom applied equality to the two finalists from the Monday after the first round. The eventual 2027 recommendation will govern the regulator's counting practice; the presidential decree governs the formal campaign opening tied to publication of the authorized names. These rules can be coordinated without being identical in wording.

## The silence period and prohibited last-minute acts

The close of the formal campaign activates concrete restrictions. Article L. 49 of the Electoral Code prohibits, from zero hours on the eve of the ballot, distribution of ballots, circulars and other documents; electronic dissemination of messages constituting electoral propaganda; automated mass telephone appeals; and meetings of an electoral character. Article L. 48-2 bars candidates from bringing a new polemical electoral argument to public attention so late that opponents cannot usefully answer before the campaign ends.

The rule is broader than a ban on purchasing new advertisements and narrower than a command to erase the internet. The key questions are who disseminated what, when, through which channel, and whether the act had the character of electoral propaganda. A pre-existing webpage remaining accessible is not analytically identical to a new campaign post pushed during the silence period. News organizations also retain their own legal and editorial responsibilities; the campaign-closing rules should not be paraphrased as a total news blackout.

Separate rules restrict premature communication of election results and estimates before the legal closing time, while polling publication has its own statutory framework. Those subjects should not be collapsed into the official-campaign definition merely because they become especially salient on election weekend.

Longer restrictions begin still earlier. Article L. 52-1 prohibits electoral use of commercial advertising through the press or audiovisual communication during the six months preceding the first day of the election month and through the decisive ballot. Article L. 51 restricts election posters outside authorized locations during the same general period. These rules demonstrate again why the formal two-week campaign is not the only regulated interval.

## Campaign finance runs on a different logic

The [[French presidential campaign finance|campaign account]] exists to trace resources and election-directed expenditure, not to declare when public persuasion becomes politically legitimate. For 2027, the CNCCFP says the financing period began on **1 April 2026**—more than a year before the first ballot and a full year before the formal campaign.

During that accounting period, the financial agent receives campaign funds and pays election expenses under the statutory framework. A cost can be relevant to the future account even though it was incurred before qualification and before the official campaign. Conversely, opening day on 5 April 2027 does not make every expense automatically reimbursable; purpose, payment route, documentation, timing and the substantive finance rules still matter.

The CNCCFP does not pre-approve campaign rhetoric or run the official broadcast schedule. It reviews the filed financial record after the election and can approve, reform or reject an account and determine reimbursement. Arcom's equality finding and the CNCCFP's accounting classification are therefore different legal determinations. The national control commission's approval of the format of a *profession de foi* likewise does not settle whether every related invoice is properly included or reimbursable.

## What is known—and not yet known—for 2027

Given the officially published ballot dates, current law supports several firm statements:

- the finance-accounting period opened on **1 April 2026**;
- the first-round formal campaign is scheduled by law to open **5 April 2027**;
- it closes **17 April 2027 at 00:00**;
- the second-round formal campaign opens through the legally required post-first-round publication process, whose exact 2027 timestamp is not yet known;
- the second-round campaign closes **1 May 2027 at 00:00**;
- qualified candidates receive equal state facilities and equal treatment in the official audiovisual phase.

But current law does not by itself provide every operational fact. Future 2027 instruments must establish or confirm the candidate list, the two finalists, the Arcom pre-campaign periods, monitoring and reporting method, official-broadcast durations above the minimum if any, production schedule, order, commission instructions, poster and statement specifications, delivery timetable, overseas adjustments and administrative contacts.

The election could also be affected by later legislation, litigation or emergency constitutional circumstances. Any 2027 calendar should therefore label a date as statutory, derived, officially appointed, or still pending. That vocabulary is more informative than calling the entire year either “the campaign” or “pre-campaign.”

## Why it matters

The clocks determine what claims are legally meaningful. Saying that the “official campaign began” implies strict equality and access to state facilities. Saying that the “financial campaign began” implies accounting and intermediary obligations, not equal broadcast time. Saying that Arcom is monitoring equity implies a regulator-defined audiovisual phase, not ballot qualification. Saying that propaganda was “official” identifies a controlled distribution channel, not official agreement with its content.

For reporting on [[2027 French presidential election]], these distinctions prevent four recurring errors: calling a politically active person an official candidate; treating equity as equality; assuming the state mailing covers all campaign speech; and using the formal opening date as the start of financial accountability. They also identify the evidence needed to verify each claim: the Conseil constitutionnel and *Journal officiel* for the field, Legifrance for the legal window, Arcom for media phases and broadcasts, the control commission and Interior Ministry for official materials, and the CNCCFP for money.
