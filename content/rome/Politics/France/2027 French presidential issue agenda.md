France enters the 2027 presidential pre-campaign with a broad cost-of-living concern, sharply polarized security and immigration concerns, a newly salient crisis of political competence, and no settled hierarchy that can be read straight from one poll.

Up: [[2027 French presidential election]]

Related: [[2027 French presidential candidate field]] · [[2027 French presidential coalition map]] · [[How to read French presidential polls]] · [[2027 French presidential territorial coalitions]]

## The direct reading

As of 10 July 2026, four conclusions are defensible.

First, **purchasing power and the cost of living form the widest common pressure**. In a June 2026 YouGov forced-choice question, purchasing power led at 24%; an open-ended CEVIPOF–L'ObSoCo survey in January put inflation/cost of living at 27%, tied with security and political life. The European Parliament's spring 2026 Eurobarometer adds intensity: 44% of French respondents expected their living standard to fall, the highest national result reported in its release.

Second, **security and immigration are more politically owned than purchasing power**. They are not synonymous: security can mean crime, narcotics, disorder, terrorism, policing, or justice, while immigration can mean borders, asylum, integration, nationality, or labor. Both are disproportionately mentioned on the right, especially the radical right. That gives candidates there a ready-made interpretive frame, but not necessarily exclusive electoral ownership.

Third, **political dysfunction is itself an issue** rather than background noise. Open responses put “political life” among the three leading concerns in January 2026. CEVIPOF's February confidence barometer found only 22% trusted politics, 15% trusted parties, and 18% trusted the president; 80% said the government was “navigating by sight.” A presidential contender therefore competes not only over taxes, borders, schools, or climate, but over whether the state can decide and execute at all.

Fourth, **the agenda remains unusually available for reframing**. Purchasing power is widely felt but weakly owned; environmental concern looks modest in single-choice national rankings but can become intense through energy, transport, food, housing, heat, or disaster; debt is salient enough to constrain promises but can be narrated as either fiscal responsibility or austerity. A campaign event, budget shock, security crisis, climate disaster, international conflict, or candidate breakthrough could reorder the hierarchy.

These claims describe the pre-campaign, not the final 2027 ballot. They do not identify a nominee, predict a winner, or assume that a concern automatically produces a vote.

## Five quantities that must not be collapsed

An “issue agenda” sounds like a ranked list. It is better understood as five related but distinct measurements.

### Salience: what comes to mind

Salience is the prominence of a problem when respondents are asked what matters. It depends heavily on the question. An open prompt records what people retrieve without cues. A closed list makes every supplied category available. A single-choice question forces trade-offs; a multiple-choice question records breadth. “For you personally,” “for France,” “for the EU,” and “for your vote” are different objects.

This explains why CEVIPOF–L'ObSoCo and YouGov should not be treated as a trend line. The former used open-ended responses and coded themes; the latter asked respondents to select one principal political concern from a list. January's three-way 27% tie and June's 24–15–13 ranking can both be true. They answer different questions with different instruments.

### Ownership: who is trusted or associated with an issue

Ownership asks which party or candidate voters associate with handling a problem. High salience without ownership creates an opportunity; ownership without salience creates a niche. CEVIPOF–L'ObSoCo's January interpretation is revealing: security was mentioned by 49% of Reconquête sympathizers, 41% of LR sympathizers, and 34% of RN sympathizers, against 9% among LFI sympathizers. Immigration reached 65% among Reconquête and 44% among RN sympathizers. The survey therefore shows partisan association and constituency intensity—not proof that all voters trust those parties to govern well.

Purchasing power is the opposite case. Respondents across camps use similar language—prices, wages, and daily life—but no single diagnosis dominates. The left can frame it through wages, benefits, rents, public services, and price regulation; the center through employment, investment, targeted relief, and European coordination; the right through taxes, energy, work incentives, and spending control; the radical right through tax cuts, protection, energy sovereignty, and immigration-linked distribution. Shared pain does not produce shared causation.

### Differentiation: what choice the candidates make visible

An issue can be salient but electorally inert if every candidate offers indistinguishable language. Differentiation concerns the policy conflict voters can actually perceive. On purchasing power, useful dividing lines include universal versus targeted relief, wage-setting versus tax reduction, deficit-financed support versus fiscal consolidation, regulated prices versus market adjustment, and national discretion versus EU constraints. On security, the choices include police and prison capacity, prevention, judicial procedure, local policing, and civil-liberties safeguards. On immigration, they include asylum administration, legal migration, removal capacity, nationality law, integration, and France's relationship to European law.

No complete official 2027 programs existed across the field by the as-of date. Existing party texts are directional evidence, not a substitute for future manifestos. LFI's evolving *Avenir en commun*, reopened to contributions in May 2026, retains social redistribution and ecological planning as organizing principles. Other party families continue to develop their offers and selection mechanisms. Candidate rhetoric should not be promoted into a settled program merely because a campaign has begun.

### Intensity: how strongly the issue can move behavior

A respondent may mention an issue without changing vote choice over it. Intensity includes personal exposure, emotional force, perceived threat, willingness to trade off other goals, and capacity to mobilize or demobilize. Eurobarometer's June release helps distinguish intensity from ranking: 44% in France expected their living standard to decline, while financial circumstances were one of the two leading components of quality of life EU-wide. The 2025 CEVIPOF–Ipsos *Fractures françaises* survey likewise reported that more than half in France had difficulty making ends meet.

Intensity is often uneven. Housing costs bite renters and young adults differently; fuel and car dependence divide territories; pension reform affects age cohorts differently; public-service closure has a spatial pattern; immigration and insecurity perceptions vary by ideology and local experience. See [[2027 French presidential territorial coalitions]]. National averages can conceal electorates for whom a lower-ranked issue is nearly dispositive.

### Uncertainty: what the measurement cannot yet tell us

Uncertainty is substantive as well as statistical. The YouGov notice reports a sample of 1,002 and illustrates margins of error around percentages, but sampling error is only one limit. Category wording, mode, coding, field dates, nonresponse, news context, and the difference between sympathizers and prospective voters matter too. A two-point movement across differently worded polls is usually less meaningful than it looks.

The largest uncertainty is temporal. Eleven months before the expected election, candidacies and coalitions are unsettled, and many voters are not processing detailed platforms. Issues become electoral through campaigns: contenders select dimensions, attach causes and villains, demonstrate competence, and link several concerns into a story. The eventual agenda will be co-produced by events, media, institutions, and candidate supply.

## The leading issue complexes

### Purchasing power, prices, and living standards

Purchasing power is the safest candidate for the agenda's broad base. YouGov's June single-choice result put it first at 24%, ahead of insecurity at 15%, immigration at 13%, debt/public finance at 12%, and social protection at 9%. The European Parliament's winter 2025 survey found 56% in France wanted inflation, rising prices, and cost of living prioritized; its spring 2026 survey reported especially strong French pessimism about future living standards.

But “purchasing power” is a container, not a policy. It can absorb food, energy, fuel, rent, mortgage rates, wages, pensions, benefits, taxes, and service charges. That breadth makes it resilient and politically difficult. A promise that benefits one group may be financed by another; short-term price relief can conflict with deficit reduction or climate investment; nominal wage gains may not address housing scarcity. The candidate who owns the issue will need a credible distributional account: who gains, who pays, through which institution, and on what timetable.

### Political life, competence, and democratic control

The unusual feature of the 2026 agenda is that political life itself ranks beside material and security concerns in spontaneous responses. This category plausibly combines disgust with parties, instability, blocked governing, ethical complaints, institutional reform, and a desire for clearer authority. It should not be reduced to a generic anti-incumbent mood.

CEVIPOF's February evidence indicates a competence and accountability crisis: 20% thought political leaders tried to keep promises, 80% believed the government improvised, and 88% believed failed elected officials did not accept responsibility. Yet 82% remained attached to democracy; 79% wanted more frequent referendums, and 77% supported citizens' conventions. The demand is therefore not simply withdrawal. It mixes rejection of national political performance with interest in direct participation, local proximity, and visible control.

This issue is weakly owned because nearly every challenger can denounce the status quo. Differentiation requires specifying whether reform means a Sixth Republic, proportional representation, referendums, decentralization, stronger executive authority, parliamentary coalition-building, or merely a change of personnel. Institutional promises also interact with [[Presidential powers in the Fifth Republic]] and the possibility that the president lacks a compatible Assembly majority.

### Security, justice, and order

Security has high spontaneous salience and strong right ownership. It also connects to unusually trusted institutions: CEVIPOF found 77% confidence in the gendarmerie and 73% in the police in February 2026. That combination—concern about safety alongside trust in coercive institutions—can favor offers emphasizing capacity and authority.

Still, the category is unstable. A survey label may merge everyday crime with organized narcotics, public disorder, terrorism, or judicial delay. Policy ownership can also be contested: a candidate may agree that security matters while arguing that housing, schools, mental health, prevention, customs, or court resources are the missing mechanisms. The campaign's decisive question is less whether candidates say “security” than which diagnosis becomes dominant and whether voters believe the proposed instruments can be delivered.

### Immigration, borders, and national membership

Immigration remains below purchasing power in the June single-choice national ranking but is much more intense among radical-right sympathizers. This gives RN and Reconquête a mobilizing axis and pressures LR and parts of the center to respond. It also means national salience can understate campaign impact: an issue concentrated among highly motivated voters may shape turnout, candidate selection, and agenda-setting beyond its average percentage.

Ownership is not the same as agreement on implementation. Border control, asylum adjudication, expulsions, legal labor migration, family reunification, integration, secularism, and nationality raise different administrative and legal questions. Proposals may encounter constitutional, European, fiscal, or capacity constraints. A credible comparison must test concrete measures rather than code every reference to immigration as one position.

### Debt, social protection, and public services

Debt and public finances reached 12% in YouGov's June redressed result, level with immigration within ordinary sampling uncertainty. Social protection followed at 9%, while education was 3% and health 1% under the forced single-choice design. It would be a mistake to conclude that schools and hospitals do not matter. Forced choice compresses service concerns into whichever immediate category respondents prioritize, while trust in hospitals remained 79% and in social security 68% in CEVIPOF's February barometer.

Fiscal constraint can become the bridge connecting the whole agenda. Tax cuts, pension commitments, defense spending, ecological investment, police recruitment, and public-service restoration compete for resources. The campaign can frame debt as a threat to sovereignty and future generations, or frame consolidation as a threat to protection and demand. This is a differentiation engine: detailed financing may expose conflicts that broad valence language conceals.

### Climate, energy, housing, and adaptation

Environment registered only 6% in YouGov's single-choice June list and 7% in the 2025 open-ended CEVIPOF–L'ObSoCo wave. That is evidence of low top-of-mind ranking under those instruments, not evidence that ecological policy is electorally irrelevant. Climate is often experienced through other categories: electricity and fuel prices, farm incomes, housing renovation, transport, insurance, water, heat, and industrial employment.

The issue's campaign strength will depend on bundling. An abstract emissions target may lose a forced choice against prices; a proposal framed as cheaper energy, thermal comfort, resilience, or industrial sovereignty can enter through a higher-salience door. Conversely, opponents can link climate policy to taxes, restrictions, or territorial unfairness. This makes climate a prime example of differentiation exceeding measured salience.

### Europe, defense, and the international environment

Foreign and European affairs scored 3% in YouGov's June single-choice result. Yet EU-level surveys show strong concern about war, irregular migration, security, and defense. National presidential power over diplomacy and defense also gives external shocks unusual agenda-setting potential. Low routine salience therefore carries high event risk.

Candidates may connect international issues to domestic ones through energy prices, industrial policy, agricultural trade, migration, defense budgets, or European fiscal rules. The analytical rule is to preserve levels: an answer about what the EU should prioritize is not directly a forecast of a French presidential vote.

## How issue competition may shape the blocs

The radical right benefits when immigration and insecurity dominate and when living costs can be integrated into a protectionist or national-preference story. Its challenge is to convert ownership of grievance into confidence about governing, financing, and legal execution.

The conventional right has potential ownership of security and debt but faces differentiation pressure from the radical right and competition over economic competence. Its strategic problem is to make fiscal restraint compatible with public demand for protection.

The left has strong programmatic differentiation on wages, redistribution, services, and ecological planning, but coalition divisions can blur ownership. Its opportunity is to connect material hardship to a coherent social-ecological diagnosis; its risk is that security and immigration split its electorate or displace its chosen terrain.

The center and post-Macron space can claim administrative experience, Europe, and economic modernization, but the incumbent record makes competence a liability as well as an asset. With trust in the government and economic management low in June's YouGov barometer, a continuity-adjacent contender must explain both what was achieved and why a new governing method would work.

These are strategic inferences, not voter-transfer estimates. They should be read alongside [[2027 French presidential first-to-second-round transfers]] and [[2027 French presidential candidate field]].

## What to watch through the campaign

A useful tracker should preserve the exact question and measure at least five series separately: open-ended national concern; forced single-choice concern; multiple-choice personal concern; party or candidate competence; and issue-specific vote importance. It should also record field dates, sponsor, mode, sample, population, response options, coding, and original notice.

Substantively, watch whether purchasing power acquires a dominant explanation; whether political dissatisfaction attaches to a concrete institutional remedy; whether security and immigration remain distinct; whether debt rises when budgets become more specific; whether climate is activated by events or bundled into energy and prices; and whether international conflict shifts attention toward presidential competence.

The key campaign signal will not be a one-point change in a generic ranking. It will be a durable alignment of **high salience, strong intensity, recognizable ownership, and visible policy differentiation**. That is when an issue becomes capable of reorganizing the race.

## Why it matters

Candidate polls show who respondents select in hypothetical slates; the issue agenda helps explain why those coalitions might hold or move. It also guards against two recurring errors: assuming the most-mentioned problem determines every vote, and assuming the party most associated with a problem has already persuaded the electorate about its solution.

For 2027, the central contest is not simply “economy versus immigration.” It is whether contenders can link material insecurity, public order, national membership, ecological transition, fiscal constraint, and democratic control into a credible account of state capacity. Because purchasing power and political dysfunction are broad but weakly owned, the agenda remains more open than a static ranking suggests.

## Open questions

- Will a common 2027 survey measure open salience, issue competence, vote importance, and policy trade-offs in the same sample?
- Which contenders will publish sufficiently costed programs to distinguish symbolic ownership from deliverable policy?
- Does “political life” resolve into institutional reform, anti-incumbency, corruption, instability, or a broader demand for state competence?
- How much do issue intensities differ by age, income, education, territory, and likelihood of voting once identical questions are used?
- Which shock—budget, security, climate, war, or government collapse—is most likely to reorder the agenda before the official campaign?
