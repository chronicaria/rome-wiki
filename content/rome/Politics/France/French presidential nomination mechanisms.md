French presidential contenders pass through several politically important but legally distinct gates: self-declaration, party or coalition selection, elected-official presentations, statutory declarations, and final inclusion on the Constitutional Council's candidate list.

## Five acts that should not be collapsed into “the nomination”

France has no single, state-administered presidential nomination process comparable to one nationwide primary system. Political parties and alliances decide whether and how to choose a standard-bearer. The state decides later whether a person satisfies the rules for appearing on the presidential ballot. Between those two processes, a politician can publicly declare, seek endorsements, build a campaign-finance structure, and collect the formal presentations of elected officeholders. Each act supplies a different kind of evidence.

The distinctions are easiest to preserve as a sequence:

1. **Declaration:** a person says that they intend to run. This is a political speech act, not a filing that creates ballot status.
2. **Party or coalition selection:** an organization chooses, invests, supports, or recognizes a contender under its own statutes or an ad hoc agreement. The electorate may be a leadership body, dues-paying members, registered sympathizers, or anyone meeting the rules of an open primary.
3. **Political endorsements:** politicians, parties, unions, associations, or public figures announce support. These endorsements can affect legitimacy and coordination but ordinarily have no direct ballot-access effect.
4. **Presentation and required declarations:** eligible officeholders transmit at least 500 valid *présentations* to the Conseil constitutionnel under geographic-distribution rules; the prospective candidate consents and supplies the legally required declarations.
5. **Qualification:** the Conseil constitutionnel establishes and publishes the official candidate list. Only this last act confirms a place on the ballot.

These gates can occur in different orders. A person may declare before a party decides its procedure. A party may select someone who has not yet secured 500 presentations. An independent contender may never seek a conventional party nomination. A presenter may enable ballot pluralism while denying that the act is an endorsement. A campaign can appoint a financial agent and incur regulated election expenses before its principal is officially qualified. The word “candidate” is therefore context-dependent; for live [[2027 French presidential election]] coverage, “declared,” “party-selected,” and “qualified” are safer labels.

## Declaration is political, not constitutive

French law does not require a prospective contender to win a primary or receive a party label before seeking the presidency. A public declaration tells voters and potential supporters that the person intends to compete. It can activate news coverage, fundraising, staff recruitment, policy production, and pressure on adjacent candidates. It does not bind the Constitutional Council and does not reserve a ballot line.

Nor is there a general official register of declarations maintained throughout the pre-campaign. Journalists establish declaration status from the person's own dated statement. Phrases such as “is considering,” “is preparing,” and “has been urged to run” do not equal a declaration. Conversely, a clear personal announcement can establish declared status even if a party later refuses its nomination.

The regulatory consequences of campaigning do not wait neatly for a ceremonial declaration. The CNCCFP's 2027 presidential guide says that the statutory campaign-finance period began on 1 April 2026. Election-directed receipts and expenses during that period must be traced under the applicable rules. A prospective candidate who raises funds must work through a single declared financial agent—either an individual *mandataire financier* or a campaign-finance association—and a campaign account. That administrative machinery is evidence of organization, but it is still not legal qualification.

## Parties choose their own selectors

Political parties are associations whose internal rules and political agreements determine how they award their support. There is no permanent public-law template requiring every party to hold the same presidential primary. The available mechanisms form a spectrum from leadership designation to mass participation.

At one end, a party can treat its established leader or congress-backed figure as the natural presidential standard-bearer and formalize the choice through the competent party organ. This minimizes an internal campaign and can project unity. It also gives members or sympathizers little direct opportunity to compare alternatives and can turn a leadership succession into an opaque negotiation.

A second model is a **closed member ballot**. Only party members who meet a membership-date or dues rule vote. This gives the organization a verifiable electorate and makes the nominee accountable to activists who sustain it. The selectorate is nevertheless much smaller and often more ideological than the eventual presidential electorate. Membership cutoffs, validation of dues, sponsorship thresholds for would-be nominees, electronic-voting rules, and runoff provisions can all shape the result.

A third model is a **supporter consultation**. A party can invite registered sympathizers or construct a temporarily expanded electorate without opening the vote to every voter. This seeks a broader mandate while preserving some organizational boundary. The label “consultation” matters: the governing resolution must say whether the outcome is binding, advisory, or a choice among procedures rather than among candidates.

A fourth model is an **open primary**. Voters outside formal party membership may participate after meeting stated conditions, such as signing a declaration of values, appearing on the electoral roll, registering, or making a small contribution toward costs. An open primary can generate attention, voter data, volunteers, and a visible winner. But it is organized by parties or an association, not by the French state as an official stage of the presidential election. Its legitimacy reaches only as far as the organizations and candidates that accept its rules and result.

Finally, a **coalition selection** can be negotiated across parties or created through a bespoke primary. The difficult questions are not merely who wins. The agreement must define candidate eligibility, the selectorate, financing, adjudication of disputes, programmatic commitments, withdrawal duties, use of party brands, and whether presidential unity carries into legislative nominations. If a major adjacent party stays outside, calling the event “the primary of the left,” “of the right,” or “of the center” overstates its jurisdiction. It is a primary of its participating coalition.

## Statutes create a procedure, not a ballot entitlement

Party statutes matter because they allocate authority inside an organization. They may empower a congress, national council, political bureau, investiture commission, or membership vote; they may also allow the competent body to choose the method later. This flexibility explains why a party can use different mechanisms in successive elections without the state changing presidential law.

Les Républicains provide a current example. Article 21 of the movement's 2025 statutes states that members assembled as the Congress decide, by vote and on a proposal from the party president after consultation of the political bureau, the method for choosing the movement's presidential candidate. The rule does not permanently command an open primary. In April 2026, LR reported that members chose among three procedural options: a members-only primary, a primary restricted to LR candidates but open to sympathizers, or designation of party president Bruno Retailleau. The party reported 73.8 percent for the third option among those choosing a method. That made Retailleau **party-selected** under LR's procedure. It did not make him constitutionally qualified.

The Parti socialiste illustrates a different institutional tradition and the importance of checking the operative text. Its reference documents contain detailed rules for candidate designations and distinguish party membership votes from broader primary arrangements. The PS has alternated between member selection and wider procedures: a statute can authorize a primary while leaving the national council to set crucial modalities, and a later party decision can delimit participation. Reporters should therefore cite the actual statutes, national-council resolution, electoral circular, and certified result for the election at issue—not infer the 2027 process from the better-known 2011 experience.

Movement parties can be less conventionally structured. La France insoumise's presidential offer has historically been centered on a movement program, its coordinating bodies, and Jean-Luc Mélenchon's ability to establish a mass campaign outside a classic membership primary. En Marche in 2017 likewise demonstrated that a candidate can build a new political vehicle around a declared presidential project instead of first winning an established party's nomination. The constitutional ballot-access rule treats neither route as intrinsically superior: both still confront presentations and the Council's final list.

## Historical examples show that “primary” is not one institution

The Socialist-organized open primary of October 2011 is the canonical modern example. It admitted a much broader electorate than PS members, held two rounds, and selected François Hollande over Martine Aubry. Its political effects extended beyond choosing a name: millions of participants gave the winner visibility and a claim to lead the wider left. Yet the primary remained a privately organized political process. Hollande still had to traverse the legal candidacy process before appearing on the 2012 ballot.

In November 2016, the right and center held an open primary that selected François Fillon over Alain Juppé after eliminating former president Nicolas Sarkozy. The event showed both the coordinating power and the limits of a primary. It concentrated a fragmented party family behind one winner and gave him a large participatory mandate. It did not immunize the coalition against later crisis, nor create a legal mechanism for replacing the winner without political conflict. Fillon's later campaign difficulties were handled through party politics; the state did not rerun the primary.

The Socialist-led *Belle Alliance populaire* organized the 2017 *primaire citoyenne*, won by Benoît Hamon. But Emmanuel Macron did not enter it and Jean-Luc Mélenchon remained outside it. The contest therefore selected a candidate for participating organizations rather than a universally binding candidate of every left current. The first round of the presidential election exposed that boundary: several candidates appealed to electorates commonly grouped on the left or center-left.

For 2022, Les Républicains used a members' congress to select Valérie Pécresse, while the PS selected Anne Hidalgo through a member vote. Europe Écologie–Les Verts and partners used the *primaire des écologistes*, a registered supporter electorate that selected Yannick Jadot. The unofficial *Primaire populaire* then attempted a citizen-driven ranking of left candidates, but several highly placed figures had not agreed to be bound by it. Its result could express participant preference and exert political pressure; it could not nominate unwilling people on behalf of their parties or qualify anyone for the state ballot.

These episodes support three general conclusions. “Open” and “closed” refer to the selectorate, not whether the result has legal force. High turnout strengthens a political mandate but cannot enlarge the organizers' jurisdiction to nonparticipants. And a selection method that works in one cycle need not become a party's permanent rule.

## Endorsement, investiture, and presentation answer different questions

French political vocabulary can conceal several distinct relationships. *Investiture* usually means that a party confers its official label and organizational support. *Soutien* may indicate support without full investiture or may be used where a candidate comes from an allied organization. A public *ralliement* is a political rallying or endorsement. None of these automatically counts toward the 500 presentations.

The presentation is a personal, legally regulated act available only to specified officeholders. The governing 1962 organic law requires at least 500 valid presenters from at least 30 departments or overseas collectivities and limits geographic concentration. Each eligible presenter may present only one person; the act is transmitted to and validated by the Conseil constitutionnel and is public. [[The 500 presentations in French presidential elections]] explains the mechanics in detail.

Calling these acts *parrainages*—sponsorships or endorsements—is common but analytically risky. A mayor may support a candidate's program and submit a presentation. Another may present a minor candidate because the mayor believes a real current of opinion deserves ballot access, while intending to vote for someone else. A party endorsement can deliver activists and funds without controlling enough eligible officeholders. A party-selected contender can therefore lack 500 presentations, while an unendorsed or dissident contender can qualify through a sufficiently broad network.

The categories should be reported separately. “Endorsed by 200 mayors” is ambiguous unless the source says whether these are political endorsements, promises to present, forms mailed, or presentations validated by the Council. Only the Council's rolling publication establishes valid presentations, and only its final list establishes qualification.

## The legal candidacy package

Presentations are necessary but not the entire legal package. Under Article 3 of the law of 6 November 1962, the Council must obtain the person's consent to candidacy. The prospective candidate must submit a declaration of assets and a declaration of interests and activities in the prescribed sealed form. The person also undertakes, if elected, to submit a new end-of-term assets declaration under the statutory timetable. The Council checks the relevant materials before establishing the list.

The implementing decree governs transmission, deadlines, review, and publication. In an ordinary election, valid presentations must reach the Council by 6 p.m. on the sixth Friday before the first round. The official candidate list is published in the *Journal officiel* no later than the fourth Friday before the first round. This creates a short but decisive interval in which campaign claims yield to institutional verification.

The official list is therefore not a certification of party representativeness, ideological seriousness, polling strength, or campaign viability. It establishes that the legal conditions for candidacy have been met. Conversely, exclusion from a party primary does not itself bar a person from the presidential ballot if the person can independently meet the legal requirements.

## A status and verification protocol

For every purported 2027 contender, record the following evidence independently:

| Question | Best evidence | Safe status language |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Did the person personally announce an intention to run? | Dated first-person statement or full speech | **Declared** |
| Did a party choose the person? | Operative statute or resolution plus certified party result | **Party-selected by X** |
| Did allied organizations join? | Each organization's authorized decision | **Supported by X and Y** |
| Are presentations secured? | Council's rolling validated list, with date and geography | **Has N validated presentations as of date** |
| Is the person on the ballot? | Council decision and *Journal officiel* list | **Qualified** |

Avoid “official candidate” before the last row. Avoid “won the primary of the left/right” unless the participating organizations actually span the asserted family, and name important nonparticipants. Distinguish a binding vote from an advisory consultation. When party rules are amended or an ad hoc coalition is created, archive the version and date: a generic party webpage may silently change.

Also separate political and financial status. Declaring a mandataire, opening a campaign account, or entering regulated expenditure is significant evidence that a project is real. It is not a declaration if the person has not spoken, a nomination if no organization has decided, or qualification if the Council has not acted.

## Relevance for 2027

Nomination mechanics are unusually consequential in 2027 because several political families face genuine coordination problems. The outgoing president cannot serve a third consecutive term, leaving the post-Macron center without an inherited incumbent nominee. Its parties must decide whether to accept multiple first-round candidacies, negotiate a single figure, or build a primary whose electorate and participants are mutually acceptable.

On the conventional right, LR's April 2026 member consultation already demonstrates how a party can settle its own label while leaving wider right-and-center coordination unresolved. A valid internal designation answers “who represents LR?” It does not answer whether neighboring parties withdraw, whether their voters transfer, or whether the selected person reaches the Council's list.

On the left and among ecologists, proposals for a common selection mechanism raise questions of scope. A primary can bind participating parties and candidates who accept its rules. It cannot, by branding alone, bind LFI, an independent social-democratic candidacy, or any other nonparticipant. Eligibility rules, voter registration, adjudication, programmatic commitments, and withdrawal pledges will determine whether the mechanism consolidates a coalition or merely adds another source of legitimacy claims.

The RN's more centralized structure presents a different problem: allocation between prominent leaders and the effect of any legal constraint may matter more than designing an open primary. Yet the same state-law gates remain. Leadership control can settle the party label quickly; it cannot substitute for the 500 presentations or the Council's decision.

These distinctions also discipline polling. A pollster may test a declared figure, a party-selected figure, a person who has not declared, or alternative nominees from the same family. The resulting percentages are scenario evidence, not votes in a nomination contest and not proof of ballot access. [[2027 French presidential coalition map]] should therefore track selection capacity and qualification capacity separately.

## Why it matters

Conflating nomination with qualification creates predictable errors. It turns a private party result into state recognition, treats elected officials' presentations as ideological endorsements, and describes speculative figures as official candidates. It also hides the real strategic choices. Parties select for internal legitimacy and coalition control; contenders seek endorsements for political momentum; campaigns build financial and territorial organizations; the Constitutional Council applies a legal gate.

The layered system permits organizational diversity. A mass party, a leader-centered movement, a coalition primary, and an independent campaign can all attempt to reach the same national ballot. Its cost is that legitimacy is plural: a contender may claim member support, primary turnout, polling strength, elected-official reach, or formal qualification, and those claims are not interchangeable. Accurate 2027 analysis asks which claim has actually been earned.
