France's Interior Ministry publishes 18 April and 2 May 2027 for the presidential ballots, but many other dates are only statutory calculations from that first-round date, while the convocation decree and election-specific decisions that will operationally settle the calendar have not yet been issued.

## The calendar at a glance

The safest way to read the 2027 timetable is to separate five kinds of date that are often blurred together.

| Date or window | Event | Status as of 2026-07-10 |
|---|---|---|
| 1 April 2026 | Presidential campaign-finance period began | **Officially confirmed** by the CNCCFP and its 2027 guide |
| 18 April 2027 | First round | **Published by the Interior Ministry**; the election-specific convocation decree is still awaited |
| 2 May 2027 | Second round, if required | **Published by the Interior Ministry** and also follows constitutionally from a first round on 18 April |
| No later than 7 February 2027 | Ordinary convocation decree must be published | **Derived legal deadline**, assuming the first round remains 18 April |
| 5 March 2027, 18:00 | Presentations must reach the Conseil constitutionnel | **Derived legal deadline**, subject to the law in force in 2027 and future instructions |
| No later than 26 March 2027 | Government publication of the candidate list | **Derived legal deadline**, not a scheduled announcement date |
| 5 April 2027 | Official first-round campaign opens | **Derived legal date** under the current implementing decree |
| 17 April 2027, 00:00 | Official first-round campaign ends | **Derived legal date**: campaigning closes when Friday 16 April ends |
| 18 April 2027 | Ordinary Sunday vote in metropolitan France and most territories | **Published ballot date**; some overseas and Americas voting normally occurs the preceding Saturday |
| No later than 21 April 2027, 20:00 | Conseil makes first-round totals known if no one wins outright | **Derived outer deadline** under the implementing decree |
| 22 April 2027, midnight | Deadline for possible withdrawals by runoff-eligible candidates | **Derived legal deadline** |
| 1 May 2027, 00:00 | Official second-round campaign ends | **Derived legal date**: campaigning closes when Friday 30 April ends |
| 2 May 2027 | Runoff | **Published ballot date** |
| No later than 12 May 2027 | Final result must be proclaimed | **Derived outer deadline** if the election is won on 2 May |
| 2 July 2027, 18:00 | Campaign accounts due | **Derived legal deadline**, the eleventh Friday after the first round |

This table is not an election notice. A derived deadline is a transparent calculation from current law and the ministry's published first-round date. It can be useful for planning without being mistaken for a date that the Conseil constitutionnel, Arcom, or the Government has formally appointed.

## What is actually official now

The Interior Ministry's presidential-election portal says the next first round will take place on **18 April 2027** and the second on **2 May 2027**. That is a current official government publication, stronger than a press estimate or a date inferred from past practice. The CNCCFP has independently moved from generic preparation to an election-specific 2027 finance regime: it states that the financing period began on **1 April 2026**, and it published a candidate-and-mandataire guide adopted on 26 March 2026 after an opinion from the Conseil constitutionnel.

Those facts do not mean that every instrument for the election already exists. Article 7 of the Constitution says that voting opens on the Government's convocation. Article 1 bis of the 6 November 1962 law now requires the decree convoking electors for an ordinary end-of-term election to be published at least ten weeks before the first round. The decree is therefore not ceremonial. It is the election-specific legal act around which administrations, candidates, courts, broadcasters, and electors can organize.

As of this note's cutoff, the ministry page supplies the ballot dates, but a search of the cited official election and legal sources did not identify the 2027 convocation decree, a 2027 Conseil constitutionnel presentation decision, a 2027 Arcom recommendation, or the final administrative circulars and orders. Their absence is expected this far from voting. It nevertheless matters: an article should not say that all 2027 deadlines are “officially announced” merely because they can be calculated.

## The constitutional skeleton

Article 7 provides the durable sequence. In an ordinary election, the new president is elected between **twenty and thirty-five days before** the incumbent president's powers expire. The Government convokes the electorate. If nobody wins an absolute majority of valid votes in the first round, the second round occurs on the **fourteenth day following** the first.

This makes the ministry's two published dates internally coherent: 2 May is exactly fourteen days after 18 April. It does not by itself explain why 18 April was selected inside the constitutional window. That choice depends on the end of the current presidential powers and the Government's calendar judgment. The future convocation decree should be treated as the definitive election-specific reference for the ordinary schedule.

The Constitution also makes the calendar conditional in a deeper sense. Candidate death or incapacity, a vacancy in the presidency, definitive presidential incapacity, or force majeure can activate different clocks. The ordinary April–May calendar is therefore the baseline, not an unalterable natural fact.

## From the ballot date to candidate qualification

Assuming the first round remains Sunday 18 April 2027, the ten-week convocation rule yields **Sunday 7 February 2027** as the latest permissible publication date for the decree. “At least ten weeks” is an outer deadline: the Government may publish earlier. Describing 7 February as the decree date would be wrong until a decree actually carries that date.

The current version of article 3 of the 1962 law establishes two further relative rules for an ordinary election. Presentations—the formal acts commonly called the *500 parrainages*—must reach the Conseil constitutionnel by **18:00 on the sixth Friday preceding the first round**. Counting backwards from 18 April gives **5 March 2027 at 18:00**. The Government must publish the list established by the Conseil no later than the **fourth Friday preceding** the first round, which gives **26 March 2027**.

These dates need an explicit legal-version warning. The Légifrance consolidation viewed in 2026 displays article 3 in a version effective only through 31 December 2026, with enacted changes scheduled afterward. The correct operational rule is the version in force during the 2027 presentation window, plus the Conseil's election-specific decision and forms. The March dates are sound calculations from the currently displayed relative rules, but campaign teams should not substitute this note for the 2027 legal package.

Publication “no later than” the fourth Friday also does not promise that the candidate list will appear precisely on 26 March. In 2022 the list appeared earlier than the outer limit. Once it appears in the *Journal officiel*, a person who was the subject of at least one presentation has only the specialized, very short challenge period described by the implementing decree. A party nomination, declaration, poll showing, or claimed tally of promised signatures does not start this clock and does not confer [[The 500 presentations in French presidential elections|ballot qualification]].

## The official campaign clock

The official campaign is much shorter than the political campaign. Under article 10 of the 8 March 2001 implementing decree, it opens on the **second Monday preceding the first round**. For an 18 April vote, that is **5 April 2027**. It ends on the eve of voting at zero hour, meaning **00:00 on Saturday 17 April**—in ordinary language, at the end of Friday 16 April. The phrasing matters because “through Saturday” would add a day that the rule does not allow.

If a runoff is required, its official campaign opens when the names of the two eligible candidates are published in the *Journal officiel*. Possible withdrawals must reach the Conseil constitutionnel by midnight on the Thursday after the first round: **22 April 2027** on the present assumptions. The second-round campaign closes at **00:00 on Saturday 1 May**, when Friday 30 April ends.

This official-campaign window governs a concentrated set of propaganda and equality rules. It should not be confused with campaign-finance accounting, which began a year earlier, or with audiovisual pluralism, which operates before the official campaign. During the official period, qualified candidates receive equality of speaking time and airtime in comparable programming conditions; before it, the presidential regime uses equity. [[Media pluralism and speaking time in French elections]] explains why those terms are not interchangeable.

The exact **2027 media calendar remains partly unissued**. Arcom will need an election-specific recommendation establishing its monitoring periods, reporting rhythm, services covered, attribution rules, and practical method. The 2022 recommendation began an anticipatory equity regime on 1 January 2022, but that precedent does not itself appoint 1 January 2027 or any other date for the coming contest. The legally derived April equality window is firmer than any attempt to copy Arcom's entire 2022 timetable forward by five years.

Likewise, the official broadcasts made available on public-service radio and television depend on Arcom decisions after consultation with candidates. The implementing framework guarantees equal durations and minimum totals, but it does not yet provide a 2027 broadcast grid. Dates for clips, recordings, draws, submissions, and transmission are future operational facts.

## Voting, counting, and proclamation

The national labels “18 April” and “2 May” conceal territory-specific voting times. The 1962 framework provides Saturday voting for specified overseas territories and for polling in the Americas so that local electors do not vote after metropolitan results have begun to circulate. If the ordinary scheme applies, affected electors would vote on **17 April** and, if necessary, **1 May**, local time. Election-specific instruments must still be checked for the exact 2027 territorial implementation; [[Overseas France in presidential elections]] should own the full territorial explanation.

After each round, departmental or equivalent recensement must be completed by midnight on the Monday following the vote, and records are sent to the Conseil constitutionnel. If nobody wins in the first round, article 29 of the implementing decree requires the Conseil to make each candidate's totals known by **20:00 on Wednesday** at the latest—**21 April 2027** under the ordinary calendar. This is an outer deadline, not a forecast of the exact announcement hour.

The Conseil constitutionnel, not an election-night television projection, decides the authoritative totals. It also informs the Government of the two candidates eligible to remain after the withdrawal window, leading to immediate publication in the *Journal officiel*. After the round in which an absolute majority is obtained, the Conseil must proclaim the election's result within ten days. If the runoff on 2 May decides the election, the outer limit is therefore **12 May 2027**. The actual proclamation may come earlier, as past practice suggests; “by 12 May” is the legally derived claim.

The handover date should not be guessed from the ballot date alone. The elected person's term and the incumbent's expiration of powers interact with the Conseil's proclamation and the constitutional continuity rules. The proclamation decision and later official announcements will establish the operational transition.

## Campaign finance is a different calendar

The CNCCFP's 2027 guide makes the earliest major election-specific date unusually clear: the finance period opened on **1 April 2026**. From this period, funds collected and electoral expenses incurred for the campaign must be handled and recorded under the applicable rules. A prospective candidate may need a financial agent long before the Conseil constitutionnel decides whether that person appears on the ballot.

The end point also differs from the media and propaganda clocks. Presidential candidates must deposit their accounts by 18:00 on the **eleventh Friday following the first round**. Starting with the first Friday after 18 April and counting eleven yields **2 July 2027 at 18:00**. This deadline applies to presidential candidates under the organic regime even though the winner may have been known for two months and even though eliminated candidates stopped competing after the first round.

The CNCCFP then examines the accounts through an adversarial procedure and decides whether to approve, reform, or reject them and what reimbursement is due. The 1962 law gives the commission six months from deposit to decide. Publication of accounts and decisions follows statutory rules; those later dates depend on actual deposits, decisions, possible proceedings, and the precise law then in force. They should not be compressed into a single fictional “campaign ends” date. See [[French presidential campaign finance]].

## Dates still awaiting election-specific instruments

At least six operational layers remain open as of 10 July 2026:

1. the Government's decree convoking electors and any associated territory-specific provisions;
2. the Conseil constitutionnel's 2027 decision and instructions for presentations, electronic or paper transmission, validation, publication, and challenges;
3. the final candidate list and its actual publication date;
4. Arcom's presidential recommendation, its pre-campaign start date, measurement phases, and reporting deadlines;
5. decisions for official public-service broadcasts and the campaign-control commission's practical calendar; and
6. ministry circulars, polling-station arrangements, overseas and expatriate voting details, and administrative deadlines not fixed directly by the relative rules above.

Future instruments can add detail without changing the constitutional skeleton. A legal amendment, however, could change a calculated deadline. Every live calendar should therefore store three fields: the source instrument, the date on which that source was checked, and whether the displayed date is quoted or derived.

## Vacancy, incapacity, and candidate contingencies

An early presidential vacancy or definitive incapacity does not simply move every ordinary date forward by the same amount. Article 7 requires a new election between **twenty and thirty-five days after** the vacancy opens or incapacity is declared definitive, unless the Conseil constitutionnel finds force majeure. The Government convokes electors without delay. The candidacy timetable is compressed: under the current organic framework, presentations arrive by 18:00 on the third Tuesday before the first round, and the candidate list is published at least fifteen days before it. The Senate president normally performs interim presidential functions, but the point for the calendar is that the ten-week ordinary convocation rule no longer applies.

Separate rules protect the integrity of an election already underway. In the seven days before the presentation deadline, the death or incapacity of a person who publicly announced a candidacy within the preceding thirty days permits the Conseil to postpone the election. Death or incapacity of a qualified candidate before the first round requires postponement. If one of the two best-placed first-round candidates is affected before possible withdrawals, or one of the two remaining candidates is affected before the runoff, the entire electoral operation begins again.

The Conseil may extend constitutional deadlines in these cases, but article 7 caps the rescheduled vote at thirty-five days after its decision. If postponement carries voting beyond the normal expiration of the incumbent's powers, the incumbent remains until a successor is proclaimed. Thus “18 April and 2 May” describes the ordinary scheduled path, not every constitutionally possible 2027 path.

## Why it matters

Calendar errors change political analysis. Calling someone an “official candidate” before the candidate list confuses aspiration with legal qualification. Treating the finance period as beginning with the official campaign erases a year of regulated fundraising and spending. Copying the 2022 Arcom dates into 2027 turns precedent into a nonexistent decision. Calling every calculated deadline “official” hides which authority can still change or implement it.

The sequence also determines incentives. Presentation gathering must culminate a month and a half before voting; strict audiovisual equality arrives only during the final two weeks; and finance accountability continues after the result. A candidate can be politically prominent but legally unqualified, legally qualified but weakly covered during an equity phase, or eliminated while still owing a full campaign account. The calendar is therefore the connective tissue among [[2027 French presidential candidate field]], campaign finance, media rules, voting, adjudication, and succession.
