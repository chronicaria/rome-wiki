French presidential abstention is a round-specific share of registered electors, not a synonym for political disengagement: registration gaps, intermittent participation, blank and null ballots, and survey-reported transfers require separate denominators and evidence.

## The denominator comes first

In official French election results, an *abstention* is a registered elector who does not vote in a particular round. If $I$ is the number registered and $V$ the number recorded as voting, then:

$$\text{abstention rate}=\frac{I-V}{I}.$$

This administrative measure is exact for that electorate and that round, subject to the normal certification and corrections of the result. It does not include French citizens of voting age who are not registered. It does include registered people whose practical connection to their polling place may be weak. Nor does it tell us whether a nonvoter stayed away from indifference, protest, illness, mobility, administrative friction, inability to arrange a proxy, or dissatisfaction with the available candidates.

That distinction matters because several quantities are often compressed into the word “turnout.” A clear account keeps at least five populations separate:

1. **Eligible citizens** are French citizens old enough to vote and possessing civil and political rights. A person can be eligible but absent from the electoral register.
2. **Registered electors** (*inscrits*) form the official denominator for turnout and abstention. Registration is therefore a gate before the choice to vote in a given round.
3. **Voters** (*votants*) are registered electors who take part. They include people who deposit a blank or ultimately invalid ballot.
4. **Valid candidate votes** (*suffrages exprimés*) exclude blank and null ballots. Candidate percentages and the presidential majority rule use this narrower denominator.
5. **Participation trajectories** describe whether the same registered person votes across several rounds. These require linked records, not one aggregate result.

In the 2022 first round, the Interior Ministry reported 48,747,876 registered electors, 12,824,169 abstentions, and 35,923,707 voters: abstention was 26.31%. But only 35,132,947 ballots were valid candidate votes. The 543,609 blank ballots and 247,151 null ballots belonged among the voters, not among the abstainers. Emmanuel Macron's 27.85% was a share of valid votes; his 9,783,058 votes were 20.07% of all registered electors. Each percentage answers a different question.

The 2022 second round makes the denominator problem even more visible. Official results recorded 48,752,339 registered electors, 13,655,861 abstentions (28.01%), 2,233,904 blank ballots, 805,249 null ballots, and 32,057,325 valid candidate votes. Macron won 58.55% of valid votes, but his 18,768,639 votes represented 38.50% of registered electors. Saying that “58.55% of France” endorsed him silently replaces both the registered electorate and the adult citizen population with the valid-vote denominator.

## Registration is analytically prior to abstention

Official abstention cannot measure people who are outside its denominator. INSEE estimated that 48.8 million electors were registered for the 2022 presidential election outside New Caledonia, while 95% of French citizens of voting age in metropolitan France and the overseas departments were on a principal communal list. In a separate linkage of census and register data for France excluding Mayotte, it counted 49.9 million adult French citizens, of whom 47.0 million were registered and 2.9 million—5.8%—were not.

Even formal registration does not guarantee convenient access. INSEE found that 7.7 million registered adult citizens, 16.5% of registrants in that resident-population study, were registered outside the commune of their principal residence. This “mal-registration” category is not the same as legal ineligibility or a defective register entry: a person may retain a valid attachment to a former or family commune. But distance can raise the cost of voting or of arranging a *procuration*. Among 18–25-year-olds, 38.7% were not registered in their principal-residence commune, illustrating how mobility and life stage can sit behind an apparently behavioral abstention statistic.

Registration and voting also compound social inequalities. INSEE's 2022 participation study concluded that, per 100 adult French citizens in the lowest living-standard group, 62 participated in at least one of the four presidential and legislative rounds, compared with 88 among the most affluent. That gap reflects both lower registration and lower participation once registered. Reporting only abstention among registrants removes the first step and can understate unequal inclusion.

The practical lesson for [[2027 French presidential election|2027]] is to publish registration measures beside, not inside, turnout estimates. The electorate can change between rounds because of administrative corrections and the legal calendar, while the population eligible in a broader civic sense is a different statistical universe. A campaign claim about “millions who do not vote” should state whether it means nonregistrants, round abstainers, or people who participated in none of a sequence of elections.

## A round rate is not a stable type of person

Aggregate returns cannot identify whether the people abstaining in round one are the same people who abstain in round two. INSEE can study this because its electoral-participation survey links a representative sample to actual attendance records. For the four national rounds in spring 2022—two presidential and two legislative—it classified registered residents in its scope into **systematic voters**, **intermittent voters**, and **systematic abstainers**.

Only 36% voted in all four rounds; 48% voted in at least one but not all four; and 16% voted in none. Intermittent participation, not permanent exit, was therefore the modal trajectory. Of the intermittent voters, 54% participated only in the presidential election: 38% voted in both presidential rounds and 16% in one presidential round. Another 31% voted in both presidential rounds but only one legislative round. These figures cannot be substituted for the Interior Ministry's single-round rates because INSEE's scope excludes some electors included in the national administrative total and asks a longitudinal question.

The trajectory evidence changes how abstention should be interpreted. A first-round nonvoter may return when the runoff creates a stark choice; a first-round voter may withdraw because neither finalist is acceptable. Presidential voters may skip lower-salience legislative rounds. “Abstainers” are consequently not a single bloc available for one candidate to mobilize. Even *systematic abstention* is defined relative to the observed sequence: four missed rounds in 2022 does not prove lifelong nonparticipation.

Age patterns also resist a one-dimensional account. INSEE found systematic abstention across the four 2022 rounds among 24% of registered 18–34-year-olds, while more than half of younger electors participated intermittently. Systematic voting rose with age through roughly age 80, but systematic abstention increased sharply after 85. For younger adults, mobility, weaker registration alignment, and selective engagement matter; for the oldest, health, dependency, or access may matter. Age gradients should not be translated automatically into political alienation.

Education and resources are strong correlates. Thirty percent of registered people without a diploma abstained in every 2022 national round, compared with 10% of higher-education graduates. Participation also increased with living standards, and residence in a priority urban neighborhood was associated with both registration and participation disadvantages. These are descriptive relationships. They do not establish that education itself causes voting or that every low-turnout territory contains the same motives.

Over a longer horizon, INSEE reports a structural shift in the four-round national-election sequence. Systematic voting fell from 48% in 2002 to 37% in 2022 in its comparable metropolitan-resident field, while intermittent voting became more common than voting at every round from 2017 onward. Systematic abstention fell from 12% in 2002 to 9% in 2007, then rose by roughly two or three points per sequence to 16% in 2022. The pattern is not a simple linear flight from all elections: selective participation expanded while faithful all-round voting declined.

## First and second rounds mobilize differently

The presidential rounds are separate elections with different candidate sets and political meanings. Their abstention rates should never be averaged as though they were repeated measurements of the same proposition.

In 2002, first-round abstention reached 28.40%, amid a field of sixteen candidates; the fragmented result put incumbent Jacques Chirac and Jean-Marie Le Pen into the runoff and eliminated Socialist prime minister Lionel Jospin. Second-round abstention then fell to 20.29% as a broad anti-Le Pen mobilization developed. This is a clear case in which the runoff increased participation.

The direction reversed in the next two elections for which the contrast is especially relevant. In 2017, abstention rose from 22.23% in the first round to 25.44% in the Macron–Marine Le Pen runoff. In 2022 it rose from 26.31% to 28.01% in their rematch. Yet non-selection among voters rose more sharply: in 2017 the second round produced 3,021,499 blank and 1,064,225 null ballots, together 11.52% of voters, versus 949,334 combined in round one (2.56% of voters). In 2022, blank and null ballots together were 8.66% of second-round voters, against 2.20% in round one.

These contrasts show why a runoff can change along two margins. Some registered electors exit, increasing abstention. Others enter the polling booth but refuse or fail to select either finalist, increasing blank or null voting. A binary candidate menu therefore does not force every participant into one of two political camps.

Historical comparison also requires attention to classification. Before the 2014 legal recognition and separate counting of blank ballots, official tables often grouped *blancs ou nuls*. The 2002 result accordingly reports one combined category, while 2017 and 2022 report blank and null ballots separately. A long-run series that treats the old combined category as “blank votes” overstates deliberate blank voting because invalid ballots are included.

Blank voting itself has a precise status. It proves participation and expresses no valid candidate preference; it is counted separately but excluded from *suffrages exprimés*. A null ballot is also excluded, but may result from intentional spoiling or an error. Administrative totals cannot recover motive from the paper. Abstention is even more motivationally ambiguous. Analysts can describe the act recorded by election administration; explaining the act requires additional evidence.

## Surveys illuminate motives but introduce artifacts

Representative surveys are necessary for questions that official returns cannot answer: who intended to vote, why someone abstained, which first-round voters backed a finalist, and how social attitudes relate to participation. They are not a replacement for certified totals.

Turnout is particularly vulnerable to measurement error. Voting is socially approved, so respondents may overreport it. People who answer political surveys are often more engaged than those who do not. A pre-election “certainty to vote” scale measures stated intention under the conditions of the fieldwork, not later attendance. Weighting and likely-voter filters can reduce some bias, but their thresholds become part of the estimate. A poll that presents candidate shares only among likely voters has already modeled participation before reporting preference.

Transfer tables add a second layer. A post-election survey may ask a respondent to recall a first-round vote and report a second-round action. The resulting cells are estimates conditioned on recalled origin, not linked secret ballots. Sampling error grows within smaller first-round electorates. Recall can shift toward winners or toward choices that now seem socially acceptable. Panel attrition can change the composition of respondents between waves.

Pre-election runoff tables measure something else again. Pollsters may combine abstention, blank/null voting, refusal, and indecision under *non exprimé*. That category is not an administrative forecast of abstention. A person declining a hypothetical choice months before the election may vote after the finalists and stakes are known. Conversely, someone naming a finalist may stay home. If one release distinguishes “would abstain” from “would vote blank or null” and another combines them, their transfer percentages are not directly comparable.

The 2022 election-weekend Ipsos/Sopra Steria survey illustrates both the value and the limit. It estimated that among recalled first-round Jean-Luc Mélenchon voters, 42% chose Macron, 17% Le Pen, 24% abstained, and 17% cast a blank or null ballot. Those categories correctly preserve nonparticipation and non-selection instead of allocating every voter to a finalist. But they remain survey estimates. The official totals can verify aggregate abstention and blank/null voting; no authority can certify an individual transfer matrix because ballot secrecy prevents linking a person's first- and second-round choices.

Survey language should therefore track the instrument. Write “respondents who recalled voting for Mélenchon reported…” rather than “Mélenchon transferred 42% of his votes.” Parties and eliminated candidates do not own electorates, and the apparent precision of a transfer percentage does not remove sampling, recall, timing, and category error.

## How to read turnout claims for 2027

A credible 2027 turnout analysis should pass six checks.

**Name the universe.** Is the number a share of adult French citizens, registered electors, respondents, likely voters, actual voters, or valid votes? “The French” is almost never a sufficient denominator.

**Name the round and candidate set.** First-round mobilization depends on the breadth and competitiveness of the field. Runoff mobilization depends on the actual pairing, rejection levels, and the inter-round campaign. A hypothetical pairing is conditional evidence, not a forecast of a realized election.

**Separate acts from motives.** Official returns establish registration, abstention, voting, blank/null ballots, and candidate votes. Motives such as protest, indifference, logistical constraint, or strategic abstention require carefully designed surveys or qualitative work.

**Preserve non-expression categories.** Do not redistribute undecided respondents, abstainers, or blank/null voters between candidates unless the source explicitly models that operation and it is labeled. In a runoff, these outcomes are part of the coalition arithmetic.

**Compare compatible fields.** Interior results cover the certified electorate; INSEE participation studies can use resident samples and exclude consular or other populations. Survey samples have their own eligibility and likely-voter screens. Differences may reflect scope rather than error.

**Treat turnout as an outcome, not a fixed input.** Campaign competitiveness, candidate supply, perceived stakes, registration alignment, and access all influence it. Applying 2022 abstention mechanically to 2027 would assume away the political event being analyzed.

## Why it matters

Participation determines which social and territorial preferences become votes. Because lower registration and lower all-round participation are socially patterned, the valid electorate is not a neutral miniature of all eligible citizens. Yet abstention does not move uniformly toward one party if mobilized: intermittent voters are heterogeneous, and every candidate's potential coalition includes people who might instead stay home or vote blank.

For [[2027 French presidential territorial coalitions]], candidate percentages should be shown with turnout because an apparent advance can arise from opponent demobilization or a shrinking valid-vote denominator. For [[2027 French presidential first-to-second-round transfers]], abstention and blank/null voting belong inside the matrix, with survey uncertainty visible. For [[How to read French presidential polls]], likely-voter selection is part of the model, not a neutral preprocessing step.

The safest summary is therefore not that abstention automatically delegitimizes a result or conceals a predetermined electoral reserve. It is that French presidential outcomes are produced through successive filters—eligibility, registration, round-specific attendance, ballot validity, and candidate choice. Each filter has its own data and mechanism. Analysis becomes misleading when one is used as a proxy for another.
