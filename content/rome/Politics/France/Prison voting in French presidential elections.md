Most people held in French prisons retain the right to vote, and a prison-based postal ballot introduced for the 2022 presidential election turned a formally available right into a substantially more usable one—without eliminating the administrative, informational, and representational barriers that still depress participation.

## Imprisonment does not itself cancel the vote

The first point is easy to state but politically important: being in prison in France is not, by itself, a disqualification from voting. The prison population includes people held in pre-trial detention, who have not been convicted, as well as people serving sentences. A convicted prisoner also remains an elector unless a court has imposed an electoral incapacity or an applicable older judgment still produces that effect.

Article 131-26 of the Penal Code allows a court to prohibit some or all civic, civil, and family rights as an additional penalty. The listed rights include the right to vote and eligibility for office. The court may distinguish among them rather than treating civic exclusion as an indivisible package. Under that article, the maximum duration is ordinarily ten years for a felony (*crime*) conviction and five years for a misdemeanor (*délit*) conviction. This legal structure matters because it makes disenfranchisement a judicially specified consequence, not an automatic incident of incarceration.

That principle separates two questions that public debate often collapses:

1. **Does the person legally possess the franchise?** Most adult French prisoners do.
2. **Can the person actually exercise it from detention?** That depends on registration, information, deadlines, prison administration, and an available voting channel.

The modern history of prison voting is largely the history of closing the gap between those two questions. Before postal voting, the right frequently existed on paper but was difficult to use. A detainee normally had to arrange a proxy or receive temporary leave to vote at a polling station. Both channels remain available in appropriate circumstances, but neither is an easy general solution for a population living under confinement.

## Why the older routes produced very low participation

A voting proxy (*procuration*) lets another elector cast the detainee's ballot. It requires paperwork, identity verification, and a trusted person willing and able to act as proxy. The Justice Ministry's 2022 guidance directed a detainee seeking this route to obtain from the prison registry an extract of the detention record establishing the inability to attend a polling place and to arrange certification of the proxy by an authorized officer. Even when officials facilitate the procedure, the detainee must still have a suitable person outside.

Temporary leave to vote is more direct but necessarily limited. It depends on the person's legal and custodial situation and on a judicial authorization. Pre-trial detainees, prisoners not yet eligible for leave, people held far from their electoral commune, and people whose requests cannot be processed in time cannot treat it as a dependable voting mechanism.

Registration added another barrier. A prisoner might remain registered in a commune with which the person had a qualifying connection, yet confinement made voting there impractical. Institutional mobility also complicates election administration: people enter custody, leave custody, or transfer between establishments while lists and ballot materials are being prepared. These are not exceptional events in a prison system; they are ordinary features of detention that a workable franchise must absorb.

The participation figures show the practical effect. An official parliamentary report records turnout among eligible prisoners at about 3.9 percent in the 2012 presidential election and 2 percent in 2017. Those percentages should not be read as a clean measurement of political indifference. They describe participation under a system whose principal voting routes required outside social connections or exceptional physical access to a polling station. They therefore measure institutional accessibility as well as electoral motivation.

## The 2019 experiment and the permanent reform

The decisive change began with the 2019 European Parliament election. Article 87 of the 23 March 2019 justice programming law created an exceptional postal-voting experiment for people in detention. Prisoners could place their ballot in a sealed envelope within the institution; a national commission established the list of admitted postal voters and centralized the count. The Justice Ministry deployed election coordinators and local referents to explain registration and organize the operation.

The experiment did not solve every problem. The ministry later reported that roughly 40 percent of prisoners who chose postal voting could not use it because they were not registered on an electoral list. But participation reached about 8 percent, or 4,550 voters—roughly four times the 2017 presidential rate even though European elections usually mobilize fewer voters than presidential elections. The comparison is not a controlled experiment, but it supplied strong operational evidence that the method of voting, not merely interest in politics, constrained prison turnout.

The 27 December 2019 Engagement and Proximity Act then embedded prison postal voting in the Electoral Code. It also created a detention-linked registration route. Article L. 12-1 permits eligible prisoners, under specified conditions, to register in the commune that is the departmental capital for the prison in which they are held. Articles L. 79–L. 82 govern sealed postal voting for people registered through that route, with procedures designed to protect secrecy, personal voting, and the sincerity of the election.

The presidential election required a distinct mechanism because its rules rest on the 6 November 1962 law. Parliament extended postal voting to the presidency through the reform applied in 2021. Section VI of article 3 of the 1962 law covers people in pre-trial detention and sentenced prisoners whose sentence does not entail electoral incapacity, provided they are incarcerated in France and registered to vote. It establishes an electoral commission responsible for the personal and secret character of the vote, the list of admitted voters, and the count. A voter admitted to this channel cannot also vote at a polling station or by proxy, except under the statutory safeguard for release before election day.

The presidential design was deliberately centralized. A national presidential ballot presents the same candidates everywhere, unlike legislative or local elections in which the correct ballot depends on a territorial constituency. The Conseil d'État observed during review of the reform that centralization allowed prisoners to retain registration in a commune with personal ties rather than forcing every presidential postal voter onto a special local roll solely for logistical purposes. Votes could be collected from prisons and counted through a dedicated national structure.

## How the presidential postal ballot works

For the 2022 election, the head of each prison had to inform eligible detainees of postal voting and provide the required option form. People registered through the detention-specific provision of article L. 12-1 were treated as having opted for postal voting; other registered detainees had to make an affirmative choice by the regulatory deadline. Once admitted, a voter ordinarily could not switch to a proxy or polling-station ballot. This exclusivity protects against double voting.

The vote itself took place inside the prison before the ordinary election day. The system separated the ballot envelope from an identification envelope so eligibility could be checked without associating a named voter with a chosen candidate. The relevant correspondence was legally protected. Candidate representatives could designate delegates to observe committee and commission operations, providing a partisan scrutiny mechanism familiar from ordinary election administration.

In 2022 the sealed votes were transported for central processing, and the dedicated ballot operation at the Justice Ministry's Place Vendôme headquarters became, in the ministry's description, France's largest polling station. More than 190 volunteer officials from prison administration, judicial services, and youth judicial protection assisted with registration and counting on the two election Sundays. The postal votes were attached electorally to Paris through a specific polling bureau rather than reported as a freestanding national “prison vote.”

That last feature has two consequences. It preserves the secrecy of small groups within individual institutions: official results do not expose how the residents of a particular prison voted. But it can also distort casual geographic analysis. Anyone interpreting a Paris bureau or aggregating local results must know that a centralized prison electorate is present. Prisoners are citizens of electoral communes across France, not a naturally Parisian voting bloc.

## What changed in 2022

The 2022 presidential election was the first presidential use of prison postal voting. According to the Justice Ministry, more than 10,000 detained people voted in each round, producing participation a little above 20 percent in both rounds. More than 93 percent of participating prisoners used the postal channel. Compared with the approximately 2 percent participation reported in 2017, the increase was dramatic.

The strongest conclusion is institutional, not partisan: bringing a secure ballot process into prisons materially increased the number of eligible detainees able to vote. It did not bring participation close to the national rate, and it does not show that every remaining non-voter faced an administrative obstacle. But a roughly tenfold rise between presidential elections, combined with the dominance of postal ballots among those who participated, is powerful evidence that access design matters.

The result also changes how analysts should discuss abstention. A prisoner who did not vote in 2017 may have lacked a workable proxy or leave from custody; a non-voter in 2022 had access to a more realistic channel, but could still have encountered registration failures, missed deadlines, transfers, limited campaign information, language or literacy difficulties, or a deliberate decision to abstain. The reform makes prison abstention somewhat more politically interpretable, but not equivalent to abstention outside prison.

## Integrity safeguards and the 2022 problems

Postal voting must solve a genuine democratic tension. The state must facilitate an elector's vote while ensuring that custodial authority, other prisoners, or informal pressure do not compromise secrecy and personal choice. French law therefore emphasizes a sealed ballot, an electoral commission, controlled lists, official transport, observation by candidate delegates, and reconciliation between envelopes and signatures.

The Constitutional Council's formal observations after the 2022 presidential election judged the new channel successful but identified implementation defects. Transfers between prisons caused small discrepancies between the number of signatures and the number of ballot envelopes received at the first-round count. Administrators also had to manage two systems and two kinds of electoral list: the centralized presidential arrangement and the decentralized system then used for other elections. Some town halls sent prisons a decentralized list containing people who had not formally opted into presidential postal voting and therefore were not on the national list of admitted presidential postal voters.

The Council concluded that these marginal problems did not affect the sincerity of the election. That finding is reassuring but should not erase the lesson. A prison election system is unusually exposed to changes of address that are controlled by the state itself. If a detainee is transferred after the option deadline or after materials are assigned, database coordination must keep the voter from being omitted, duplicated, or sent the wrong materials. The Council recommended improving implementation and reconsidering how prison results are aggregated with other electors' results.

These are administrative risks rather than evidence of widespread fraud. The relevant policy question for 2027 is whether the institutions have converted the first-use observations into tested procedures: accurate list reconciliation, rapid transfer updates, clear instructions to municipalities and prisons, protected delivery, contingency planning for release, and auditable reconciliation of every envelope.

## The law after the 2025 restriction

France narrowed prison postal voting in 2025, but the restriction did **not** remove it from presidential elections. The 18 July 2025 law limited the general Electoral Code mechanism by removing postal voting for territorial elections. The policy rationale centered on local representation: registering prison postal voters at a departmental capital and counting their ballots there could give them influence in a municipality with which they had no chosen connection, while the host municipality bears the presence of a prison. Whatever the merits of that argument for local contests, it does not apply in the same way to a single national presidential constituency.

As of July 2026, the dedicated presidential provisions of the 1962 law remain in force. Prisoners eligible and registered to vote can use the centralized sealed postal process for the presidency. The 2025 change is nevertheless relevant to 2027 for two reasons. First, public descriptions that say “postal voting for prisoners was abolished” are overbroad and risk deterring eligible voters. Second, administrative familiarity can erode if staff and detainees encounter different rules across successive elections. Clear election-specific communication will be essential.

The contrast with the March 2026 municipal elections is instructive. A May 2026 government answer to the National Assembly reported that, without postal voting, 2,681 detained people voted in the first round and 2,554 in the second, mostly by proxy; participation among the stated potential electorate was about 4.01 and 3.82 percent. The figures are not directly comparable to a presidential election, which normally attracts more attention, but they reinforce the accessibility problem that postal voting was designed to address.

## What the prison electorate can and cannot tell us

There is a recurring temptation to treat “the prison vote” as a sociological poll or a verdict on criminal-justice policy. The available evidence does not justify that simplification. People in detention vary by age, class, origin, legal status, sentence, geography, and political belief. Some are awaiting trial. The participating electorate is also selected by registration, awareness, deadline compliance, and willingness to vote under institutional conditions. It is not a representative sample of all prisoners, still less a proxy for residents of disadvantaged communities.

Centralized results may permit a broad account of how admitted postal ballots were distributed among candidates, but interpretation requires care. Small changes in registration and turnout can alter the composition of voters substantially. The public result also excludes eligible detainees who used proxies or temporary leave, whose votes are counted in ordinary local bureaus. Therefore even a correctly identified central bureau does not encompass every ballot cast by an incarcerated person.

For candidates in [[2027 French presidential election]], the democratic stakes exceed the likely effect on the national margin. Prison voting tests several campaign commitments simultaneously: equal citizenship, punishment and rehabilitation, electoral integrity, administrative competence, and access to political information. A candidate can support punishment while accepting that loss of liberty does not automatically mean civil death. Conversely, defending the formal franchise without addressing registration and usable voting routes leaves the central practical problem unresolved.

## A 2027 evaluation framework

The 2027 process should be judged against observable criteria rather than celebratory or alarmist rhetoric.

- **Eligibility and registration:** How many detained French adults retain the vote, how many are registered, and how many registration requests fail or remain unresolved?
- **Information:** Are detainees told promptly, in accessible language, about the presidential postal option, deadlines, alternatives, and the consequences of opting in?
- **Campaign access:** Do voters receive official candidate materials in time, and do prison rules allow reasonable access to news and civic education without privileging a candidate?
- **Transfer resilience:** Can the system track admission, release, and inter-prison transfer without disenfranchisement or duplicate admission?
- **Secrecy and freedom:** Are voting spaces, envelope procedures, and staff instructions sufficient to protect voters from observation or pressure?
- **Auditability:** Do the commission and departmental committees reconcile admitted voters, signatures, envelopes, rejected ballots, and delivered ballots, with candidate delegates able to observe?
- **Transparent reporting:** Do official post-election reports publish participation, channel use, rejected-ballot figures, and operational incidents while protecting individual and institution-level secrecy?
- **Legal clarity:** Do official communications clearly distinguish the preserved presidential postal ballot from the 2025 restriction affecting other contests?

Turnout is an important outcome measure, but it is not the only one. A high rate achieved through coercive mobilization would be unacceptable; a lower rate following genuine access and free choice may be democratically legitimate. The proper aim is not to make prisoners vote. It is to make an eligible prisoner's decision to vote or abstain meaningfully their own.

- Will aggregation of centralized prison ballots change before 2027, and can reporting become more transparent without exposing the choices of small prison populations?
