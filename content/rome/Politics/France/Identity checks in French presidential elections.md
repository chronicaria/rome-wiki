Identity at a French presidential polling station is established through the voter list, a public name check, and—mandatorily in communes of at least 1,000 inhabitants—an approved identity document; the electoral card helps locate the record but is neither compulsory nor proof of identity.

Up: [[2027 French presidential election]] · Related: [[Voter registration for French presidential elections]] · [[French presidential polling-station operations]] · [[Ballot secrecy and proxy voting in French presidential elections]]

## The check answers two different questions

An identity control at a polling station is easy to describe as “showing ID,” but French election law separates two propositions. First, is this person entered on the electoral list used by this *bureau de vote*? Second, is the person standing before the officials the elector whose name appears there? Registration answers the first question; the polling-station identity process answers the second. Neither alone is sufficient.

Article L.62 of the Electoral Code makes the sequence visible. On entering the room, the elector’s identity or right to vote is verified. The elector then takes an envelope and at least two different ballots, goes alone through the voting booth, presents at the table, has identity checked, places the envelope in the transparent ballot box, and signs the *liste d’émargement*. The public declaration of the elector’s name and the signature are not substitutes for secrecy: they document participation, while the booth and envelope hide the choice.

The distinction matters for 2027 analysis. An expired electoral card does not extinguish a valid registration, and the absence of the card is not itself disfranchisement. Conversely, possessing a card does not establish a current right to vote if the person is not on the polling station’s list. A registration dispute belongs to the procedures described in [[Voter registration for French presidential elections]], not to ad hoc discretion over an otherwise valid identity document.

## The electoral card is useful but not an identity document

The *carte électorale* records practical information: the elector, commune, polling station, and national electoral number. It helps officials find the correct list entry and helps voters arrive at the correct bureau. It is not a national identity card, and official guidance says it is not mandatory in order to vote.

This produces three common cases:

1. An elector brings both the electoral card and an approved identity title. In a commune of at least 1,000 inhabitants, this is the ordinary and fastest route.
2. An elector has no electoral card but appears on the bureau’s list. The missing card does not bar the ballot. Where Article R.60 applies, the elector still must present an approved identity title.
3. A person presents an electoral card but is not on the relevant list. The card does not override the list. Officials must determine whether the person is at the wrong bureau, has been removed, is covered by a judicial or administrative correction, or lacks a current registration.

This is why “ID requirement” is an incomplete description. The legal object is not a free-standing photographic-ID test. It is the authentication of a listed elector at a specified polling station, followed by an auditable signature.

## The 1,000-inhabitant threshold

Article R.60 establishes a bright administrative distinction. In communes with **1,000 inhabitants or more**, electors must present to the polling-station president, at the time of voting, an identity title alongside the electoral card or an attestation that replaces it. The permissible titles are set by an Interior Ministry order. Assessors may participate in the identity control if they ask to do so.

In communes with **fewer than 1,000 inhabitants**, official Interior Ministry and Service-Public guidance says that no particular identity document is compulsory. This is not an exemption from identity verification. The president of the bureau must still be able to verify who the elector is and, where doubt persists, may require identity to be established by any means. Familiarity in a small commune can therefore perform an evidentiary role that the prescribed-document list performs in a larger one, but it does not authorize voting under an unverified name.

The threshold attaches to the commune, not to the size of an individual polling station or to whether local officials personally recognize the voter. A small bureau inside a large commune remains subject to Article R.60. The rule should also not be confused with municipal-election voting systems; it is an administrative identity rule incorporated into national elections, including the presidential election.

The asymmetry embodies a tradeoff. A uniform documentary rule is easier to administer and audit in more populous places where officials cannot be expected to know electors personally. A flexible rule in very small communes reduces exclusion when local knowledge can establish identity. Yet flexibility also makes the quality of the bureau president’s judgment important, which is why the public nature of the operation, assessors, signatures, minutes, and later review remain part of the safeguard.

## Which documents count

The controlling list is regulatory, not a matter of what seems persuasive to an individual official. The Interior Ministry’s order of November 16, 2018 lists documents usable to prove identity when voting in a commune of at least 1,000 inhabitants. Official public guidance includes, among others, a national identity card, passport, health-insurance card with photograph, driving licence, military identity or circulation card with photograph, state civil-servant identity card with photograph, disability or mobility-inclusion card with photograph, combatant’s card with photograph, and hunting permit with photograph issued by the competent public body. The order itself, rather than a campaign’s checklist or a polling-place improvisation, is authoritative.

French nationals may use a national identity card or passport that is current or that expired less than five years earlier, according to Service-Public’s voting guidance. Most other listed documents must be current. This limited acceptance of recently expired national documents should not be generalized to every title on the list.

The voter must present the **original**. A photocopy or a photograph displayed on a telephone is not accepted as the document. This is an authentication rule: a recognisable image of a document lacks the physical and administrative characteristics on which the prescribed control relies.

The name on a document and the electoral list may require ordinary reconciliation—for example, after a change in civil status—but officials should not invent an extra eligibility condition. The material question is whether the prescribed evidence establishes that the person present is the listed elector. Difficult cases should be recorded rather than resolved by partisan intuition.

## What happens at the bureau

The polling-station president has responsibility for order and regularity, assisted by assessors and the secretary. Identity is checked publicly enough for the bureau and representatives to observe the operation, but personal data should not be turned into campaign material. Article R.60 expressly allows assessors to join the control on request, distributing scrutiny beyond a single official.

Once identity and list entry are established, the ordinary sequence continues. The elector votes personally, even when another elector has appointed that person as proxy for a separate ballot. The official says the person’s name aloud; after the envelope is placed in the box, the elector signs beside the list entry. If the elector cannot sign, the Electoral Code provides a notation and assistance procedure rather than making handwriting ability a condition of suffrage. [[Accessibility in French presidential elections]] treats assistance and accessible voting more fully.

For a proxy vote, the proxy voter proves their own identity at the polling station assigned to the principal and states the principal’s name. Officials verify the valid proxy notation and the applicable proxy limits. The principal’s electoral card is not required. Identity checking therefore authenticates the person physically casting the ballot, while the proxy register supplies legal authority to cast it for someone else. The proxy never gains a right to reveal or replace the principal’s political choice.

Officials cannot cure a missing registration simply because a person’s identity is obvious, nor refuse a registered elector merely because the electoral card is absent. These opposite errors—letting identity substitute for enrolment and letting the card substitute for identity—are the core conceptual traps.

## Disputes, refusals, and documentation

If a voter is directed to the wrong bureau, the first response should be to identify the correct registration location through the electoral list and official lookup tools. If the dispute concerns a recent omission or removal, statutory administrative and judicial remedies may allow correction, sometimes through a court order presented at the bureau. The polling station is not free to rewrite the list on oral assurance.

If an elector in a commune of at least 1,000 inhabitants cannot present an approved title, the bureau cannot waive Article R.60 because the person is locally known or because turnout is high. In a smaller commune, by contrast, the president evaluates proof by any means. The different rules make the commune’s population category a fact that election administrators must establish before polling day.

A disagreement should be entered in the *procès-verbal* when it bears on the regularity of voting. Candidate delegates and electors may make observations through the legally available channels. The record permits later review by recensement bodies and ultimately the Constitutional Council, whose Article 58 function is explained in [[Constitutional Council review of presidential elections]] and [[Presidential election complaints and annulments]]. A court assessing an irregularity will care not only that a rule was breached but also about scale, circumstances, corrective action, and whether the integrity or result could have been affected.

Identity controls should therefore be strict in content and neutral in application. Selectively demanding extra evidence from some electors, accepting non-listed substitutes for allies, or using the process to intimidate would undermine equality even if the underlying document rule is valid. The best protection is a rehearsed, published rule applied consistently, with edge cases documented.

## Identity is one layer of election security

Documentary control is sometimes presented as the entire answer to impersonation. In the French system it is one linked layer. Registration assigns each elector to a list and bureau. Identity control authenticates the person. The public call of the name allows observation. The signature records that the vote was cast. The ballot-box counter, reconciliation of envelopes and signatures, count, minutes, and higher-level recensement expose discrepancies. Complaints and constitutional review provide remedies.

No single layer proves that the whole election is sound. A perfectly checked document cannot correct an erroneous list; a correct list cannot prevent someone from voting twice without signature and reconciliation controls; a numerical reconciliation cannot protect secrecy without the booth. The architecture works because checks answer different questions.

For journalists and campaigns, this means reports of an “ID problem” should specify the precise failure: absent electoral-card convenience, absent list entry, missing prescribed identity title, doubt in a sub-1,000 commune, proxy-record problem, civil-status mismatch, unequal treatment, or a wrongly located bureau. These have different legal consequences and different remedies.

## Special voting channels do not erase authentication

The ordinary commune rule should not be projected mechanically onto every French presidential elector. Citizens voting at consular polling stations abroad are registered on a consular electoral list and follow the identification rules administered through the diplomatic network. Electors in overseas territories may be governed by locally applicable adaptations. Detained electors using the presidential postal-voting mechanism are authenticated and reconciled through a different custodial and electoral chain, described in [[Prison voting in French presidential elections]]. These are adaptations of the control environment, not identity-free voting.

Proxy voting is likewise a change in who appears at the bureau, not a suspension of verification. The principal has established the proxy through an authorized procedure, the administration transmits or records that authority, and the proxy appears in person and proves their own identity. Officials then connect three records: the person present, the valid proxy, and the principal's list entry. Treating a proxy ballot as though the principal had simply lent an electoral card would miss the legal chain.

The French framework also distinguishes identity at **registration** from identity at **voting**. An applicant registering online or at a mairie supplies identity and nationality evidence under registration rules. That earlier check does not eliminate polling-day authentication because circumstances and records can change and because the bureau must establish who is physically casting this particular ballot. Conversely, a strong identity document at the bureau cannot repair failure to register by the legal deadline. The two controls occur at different times and guard against different errors.

These distinctions matter when comparing turnout channels. A consular voter, detained postal voter, proxy principal, and ordinary in-person voter should not be placed in a single “showed ID” count unless the underlying authentication events are defined. For public evaluation, the better question is whether each channel established eligibility, identity, authorization, uniqueness, secrecy, and an auditable record under its applicable law.

## Why it matters for 2027

The presidential electorate will encounter a nationally important ballot through locally operated polling stations. Small variations in training or communication can generate dramatic anecdotes without establishing systematic fraud or exclusion. The proper denominator is essential: how many electors were affected, in which commune category, under which document rule, whether they were registered, whether they returned with valid proof, and whether the incident was entered in the minutes.

Election-specific instructions issued before April 2027 may update operational details, and the Interior Ministry may amend the approved-document order. The durable structure is nonetheless clear under current law: enrolment and identity are distinct; the electoral card is useful but optional; prescribed identity evidence is mandatory at the 1,000-inhabitant threshold; and smaller communes still must establish identity.

That structure also limits premature political claims. A queue caused by slow document checks is not itself disenfranchisement. An elector turned away for lack of registration is not evidence of an identity-document rule. A documented refusal of an approved title, however, is a concrete administrative failure worthy of immediate correction and possible complaint. Precise classification is more informative than importing a generic international argument over “voter ID.”
