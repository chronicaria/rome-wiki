Accessibility in a French presidential election is a legal requirement across the electoral chain, but its practical effect depends on local administration, accessible campaign information, and procedures that preserve both autonomy and ballot secrecy.

The word *accessibility* can sound like a narrow question about ramps. French election law sets a broader standard. It covers the polling place and the techniques used to vote, and it expressly includes physical, sensory, mental, and psychological disability. An accessible presidential election therefore requires more than entry to a building. An elector must be able to learn about the candidates, reach the assigned polling station, move through it, use the booth and voting equipment, complete or lawfully delegate physical acts, and cast a secret ballot on terms comparable to other citizens.

This makes accessibility a useful test of the difference between a formal right and an effective one. Universal suffrage establishes who is entitled to vote; accessible administration determines whether that entitlement can be exercised independently and with dignity. The relevant rules overlap with [[French presidential polling-station operations]], [[Ballot secrecy and proxy voting in French presidential elections]], [[Official campaign period in French presidential elections]], and [[Administration of French presidential elections]], but accessibility has its own explanatory center: it asks whether the whole system works for electors with different impairments rather than assuming a standard voter.

## The governing principle is universal, not impairment-specific

Article L. 62-2 of the Electoral Code states that polling stations and voting techniques must be accessible to disabled people regardless of the kind of disability, specifically naming physical, sensory, mental, and psychological disability. The provision was created by the disability equality law of 11 February 2005. Two implementing articles translate that broad rule into operational duties. Article D. 56-1 requires polling-place premises to be accessible on election day and requires disabled people—expressly including wheelchair users—to be able to enter, circulate, and leave under normal operating conditions, using permanent or temporary adaptations where necessary. Article D. 61-1 requires voting techniques to be accessible and directs the president of the polling station to take any useful measure to facilitate autonomous voting.

These provisions matter in three ways. First, accessibility is an obligation, not a favor offered at the discretion of polling staff. Second, the law does not confine disability to reduced mobility. A step-free entrance cannot by itself satisfy a rule that also encompasses visual, hearing, intellectual, and psychological disability. Third, the statutory objective is autonomy. Assistance remains available when a person cannot perform a physical act, but the initial design duty is to make independent voting possible.

The standard also reaches beyond a presidential-election-specific code. Article 29 of the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities, ratified by France in 2010, requires states to guarantee political participation on equal terms. It calls for electoral procedures, facilities, and materials that are appropriate, accessible, and easy to understand and use, while protecting the right to a secret ballot without intimidation. The Convention supplies the rights-based frame; the Electoral Code assigns concrete duties within the French voting process.

The practical consequence is that election accessibility should be assessed as a chain. A nominally accessible voting room may remain unusable if the route from accessible parking or public transport is blocked, if signage cannot be understood, if the booth is too narrow, if the table and ballot box are too high, or if staff do not know how lawful assistance works. Conversely, a temporary ramp does not excuse inaccessible campaign information. Each link can determine whether the elector has a meaningful choice.

## The polling place must support an ordinary voting sequence

French presidential voting ordinarily involves several distinct movements: entering the station, identifying oneself where required, taking an official envelope and candidate ballots, entering an *isoloir*, selecting and enclosing a ballot in secret, presenting at the voting table, depositing the envelope in the transparent urn, and signing the *liste d'émargement*. Accessibility applies to this sequence rather than only to the front door.

For reduced-mobility electors, relevant details include the path to the room, thresholds, door width, turning space, the stability and slope of temporary ramps, booth dimensions, writing surfaces, and the height of the ballot box and signature table. The legal phrase “in normal operating conditions” is important: an elector should not have to use a freight entrance, be carried, or disclose a choice because ordinary equipment cannot be reached. Temporary adaptations are permitted, which helps communes use buildings that are not permanently configured as polling places, but the adaptation still has to work safely on election day.

Sensory accessibility requires different measures. Clear, high-contrast signs and an intelligible layout can assist people with low vision. Polling staff may need to communicate in writing or use other appropriate methods with a deaf or hard-of-hearing elector. The official government guidance for organizers emphasizes the whole journey and suitable equipment rather than treating every impairment as identical. The president of the bureau's duty under Article D. 61-1 gives local officials room to solve a particular obstacle, but that discretion operates inside a duty to facilitate autonomy; it is not permission to improvise rules about eligibility, secrecy, or ballot validity.

The paper-ballot system poses a specific autonomy problem for blind and visually impaired electors because candidate ballots are standardized paper objects whose visual text normally identifies the choice. Practical arrangements can help an elector locate materials, but any aid must preserve reliable selection and secrecy. A helper chosen under Article L. 64 may perform specified physical acts when the statutory threshold is met. That accommodation is valuable, but assisted voting is not equivalent to autonomous voting: it requires another person to learn or potentially influence the choice. This is why accessible technique and trustworthy assistance should be analyzed separately.

Voting machines, where lawfully used, must under Article L. 57-1 allow disabled electors to vote autonomously whatever their disability. This is one of several statutory machine requirements, alongside secrecy, prevention of multiple votes, and auditable counters. The provision establishes a design condition; it does not prove that every installed interface is equally usable in practice. Nor are machines the general solution for 2027: French presidential voting remains predominantly paper-based, and use of approved machines is limited to authorized communes. Accessibility analysis must therefore examine the actual equipment in each bureau rather than infer nationwide practice from the Code's machine standard.

## Assistance is lawful, but bounded by secrecy and safeguarding rules

Article L. 64 addresses an elector whose established impairment makes it impossible to place the ballot in the envelope, put the envelope into the urn, or operate a voting machine. The elector may be assisted by another elector of their choice. If the elector cannot sign the register, the chosen elector signs and adds the prescribed statement that the elector cannot sign personally. An elector cannot simply make a cross instead of a signature; the formal substitute is the helper's signed notation.

This mechanism protects participation without pretending that every physical act can always be made accessible through equipment alone. It should not, however, be described as a general transfer of the vote. The disabled elector retains the political choice. The helper assists with acts that the elector cannot perform. Polling staff should neither demand unnecessary proof beyond the governing rules nor choose the helper for the voter.

Special safeguards apply to adults under guardianship. The 23 March 2019 justice reform repealed former Article L. 5, under which a guardianship judge could remove an adult's right to vote. A protected adult now exercises the right personally. But for a person under guardianship, the helper or proxy cannot be the judicial protection representative, certain staff or volunteers of the institution providing care or residence, or a person working at the protected adult's home within the statutory categories. These exclusions are anti-coercion safeguards: restoration of universal voting rights is paired with limits on assistance by people who may hold institutional or personal power over the elector.

This history is central to intellectual and psychological accessibility. Before the 2019 reform, the system treated some protected adults' political capacity as something a judge could remove. The modern rule starts from personal suffrage and asks what information, communication, and procedural support makes it usable. The official use of *Facile à lire et à comprendre* (FALC, Easy to Read and Understand) materials follows the same direction, although accessible information and independent decision-making are not identical. FALC should make content easier to understand; it should not simplify away genuine political differences or substitute another person's judgment.

## Proxy voting solves some mobility barriers, not accessibility as a whole

An elector may establish a proxy under the ordinary rules described in [[Ballot secrecy and proxy voting in French presidential elections]]. For a person who manifestly cannot appear before the authorized police or judicial officer because of serious illness or infirmity, Article R. 72-1 requires the relevant officer or agent to travel on request. Current Interior guidance says that the request is made in writing and accompanied by an attestation on honor stating the manifest inability to appear.

Home travel can remove a serious barrier at the authorization stage. It does not turn proxy voting into an adequate substitute for accessible in-person voting. A proxy necessarily asks another elector to cast the ballot and depends on trust that the instruction will be followed. The constitutional and statutory commitment is to accessible, autonomous participation where possible, not to route disabled people away from the polling station. Proxy voting is an option for an elector, not a cure for an inaccessible bureau.

There is also an important distinction between inability to travel and disability as such. A disabled elector who wants to vote in person retains that right. Administrators should not assume that a visible impairment implies incapacity, a need for help, or a preference for proxy voting. Accessibility is best understood as expanding available modes of participation while leaving control with the elector.

## Candidate information has its own accessibility rules

An elector cannot make an informed choice if campaign materials are formally available but unusable. For presidential elections, the state-managed official campaign includes candidates' electoral declarations, commonly called *professions de foi*. In 2022, the national campaign-control commission placed each candidate's declaration online in its standard digital version, an audio recording, and a FALC version. The commission described the FALC requirement as a new obligation resulting from the 22 December 2021 decree and Article R. 38-1 of the Electoral Code.

The Interior Ministry's current voter guidance likewise states that, for presidential, legislative, and regional elections, candidates have been required since 1 January 2022 to provide digital propaganda in FALC. It also says candidates may provide an audio version of their digital declaration for people with visual impairments. The distinction between required and optional formats must be preserved: the government page identifies FALC as obligatory for the covered elections while describing audio as something candidates may provide. The 2022 presidential commission did in fact publish audio for the candidates, but that historical implementation should not be converted into a claim about the exact 2027 specification before the new instruments are issued.

FALC is not merely “shorter French.” Article R. 38-1 describes language aimed at disabled people or people with comprehension difficulties, favoring common words and short sentences combined with pictograms. Good FALC production involves choices about structure, vocabulary, images, layout, and testing. It makes official statements more navigable for people with intellectual disabilities and can help other readers, including people with limited literacy or cognitive fatigue. Its existence does not establish its quality: a 2027 audit should ask whether every candidate supplied it on time, whether versions accurately reflect the standard declaration, whether files are technically accessible, and whether voters can find them.

Digital publication introduces another layer. A PDF may exist yet remain hard to use with a screen reader because of missing document structure, poor reading order, unlabeled images, or an inaccessible website interface. The Interior election portal publishes an accessibility declaration and a route for reporting barriers, but site-level compliance and document-level usability require separate verification. Analysts should test the actual 2027 candidate portal and files rather than infer accessibility from a label or format name.

## Broadcast accessibility affects the common campaign

Arcom regulates audiovisual campaign conditions. For the 2022 presidential election, it required official campaign clips to be subtitled, interpreted in French Sign Language through an on-screen inset, and audio-described for blind or visually impaired audiences. Arcom also explained that major television services had broader accessibility duties and that other broadcasters were expected to facilitate access by subtitling or sign language to principal election programs at high-audience times, with particular attention to candidate debates.

These are distinct layers. Official campaign broadcasts are specially produced messages within the state-regulated campaign. News programs, interviews, and debates sit within the wider audiovisual environment and may be governed by service-specific statutory, convention, and Arcom requirements. A presidential campaign can therefore have fully accessible official clips while important editorial coverage remains unevenly accessible. Real political equality depends on both because voters form judgments through much more than official statements.

For 2027, the durable point is that Arcom has regulatory responsibility and that 2022 provides a strong precedent. The exact 2027 production decision, schedules, technical standards, and monitoring results were not available in the official materials reviewed as of 10 July 2026. They are future facts. A responsible election tracker should update this article when Arcom issues the 2027 decision and should record actual compliance rather than merely repeat the announced standard.

## The implementation gap is local and measurable

National law is clear enough to make accessibility enforceable, but presidential voting is delivered through thousands of local polling stations. Communes select and prepare premises; polling-station presidents manage election-day operations. This distributed implementation creates variation in buildings, staff training, equipment, transport, signage, and the quality of temporary adaptations.

The Défenseur des droits' 2015 study recommended assessing the entire chain of movement—transport, streets, paths, parking, and the bureau itself—and supplied an evaluation grid for communes. It also recommended reminders and training for polling-station officials. A 2017 amicable settlement shows why those details matter: a mobility-impaired woman reported that she could not use an adapted booth and could not reach the ballot box during the first round of that year's presidential election; after intervention, the commune undertook to correct conditions for the second round and legislative elections. One case cannot estimate national prevalence, but it demonstrates that a legally accessible building can still fail at the decisive equipment stage.

Measurement should therefore avoid a single yes-or-no accessibility field. A stronger 2027 assessment would separately record approach and transport, entry, interior circulation, booth access, ballot-material access, urn and signature access, communication support, staff knowledge, digital program accessibility, and broadcast formats. It should also record whether a workaround preserved independence and secrecy. Counting complaints alone would understate barriers because an elector who leaves, uses a proxy, or accepts unwanted assistance may never file one.

The appropriate evidentiary hierarchy is also important. The Code states legal duties. Ministry and disability-secretariat guidance explains recommended practice. Arcom and the national campaign commission specify campaign arrangements. The Défenseur des droits documents rights problems and remedies. User and association reports can reveal failures that national institutions do not systematically measure, but individual reports must be verified and should not be generalized without representative evidence.

## What should be watched for 2027

Accessibility should be integrated into 2027 election monitoring before polling day. The main checkpoints are:

- whether official 2027 instructions preserve or strengthen the broad physical, sensory, cognitive, and psychological standard;
- whether communes assess the full route and every stage of the voting sequence, including temporary premises;
- whether polling-station staff understand autonomous voting, Article L. 64 assistance, the signature substitute, and safeguards for protected adults;
- whether every candidate's official declaration appears in accurate, findable, technically usable standard and FALC formats, with the status of audio versions stated precisely;
- whether Arcom's 2027 decision requires subtitling, French Sign Language, and audio description for official broadcasts, and whether broadcasters deliver them in practice;
- whether government portals and documents work with assistive technologies rather than merely carrying an accessibility label;
- whether complaints, election observations, and remedies reveal recurring local failures.

The central distinction is between **availability**, **usability**, and **autonomy**. A ramp or PDF can be available but unusable. A helper can make voting possible but reduce autonomy. An accessible system aims to let the elector complete the ordinary democratic act independently and secretly, while providing bounded assistance when independence is physically impossible. That standard makes accessibility part of election integrity: a ballot is not equally available if some eligible citizens must overcome avoidable barriers or surrender secrecy to cast it.
