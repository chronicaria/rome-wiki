The French president is powerful because a limited set of constitutional powers combines with electoral legitimacy and majority leadership, but the office does not legally direct the whole executive in every political configuration.

The familiar description of the Fifth Republic as “presidential” is useful only if it is separated into three questions: what the constitutional text assigns to the president; which presidential acts require another officeholder's countersignature; and whether the president commands a loyal majority in the National Assembly. The first changes rarely, the second structures legal authority, and the third can transform political practice without amending a word of the Constitution. That distinction is essential for interpreting the [[2027 French presidential election]]. Winning the Élysée confers real powers, but it does not itself guarantee control of the Government or a legislative program.

## The president's constitutional function

Article 5 does not make the president the ordinary head of government. It says that the president ensures respect for the Constitution, safeguards the regular functioning and continuity of public powers through arbitration, and guarantees national independence, territorial integrity, and respect for treaties. These broad clauses describe a constitutional role; they are not a free-standing authorization to legislate or administer.

The allocation of day-to-day executive responsibility appears elsewhere. Article 20 says the Government “determines and conducts the policy of the Nation,” has the administration and armed force at its disposal, and is responsible to Parliament. Article 21 says the prime minister directs Government action, bears responsibility for national defence, ensures execution of statutes, and—subject to presidential appointment powers—exercises regulatory authority. France therefore has a *dual executive*: a president with important constitutional functions and a Government led by a prime minister whose survival depends on the National Assembly.

This architecture explains why the same constitutional text can sustain both presidential predominance and [[Cohabitation in the Fifth Republic]]. When the president leads the parliamentary majority, partisan authority and electoral sequencing make the president the system's political center. When an opposing Assembly majority can support a Government and defeat one it rejects, the constitutional responsibilities of articles 20 and 21 become much harder for the president to overshadow.

## Article 19 and the map of powers

Article 19 supplies the cleanest legal map. Presidential acts generally require countersignature by the prime minister and, where appropriate, the responsible ministers. It then lists exceptions: the first paragraph of article 8 and articles 11, 12, 16, 18, 54, 56, and 61. These exceptions are conventionally called *pouvoirs propres*, or the president's own powers. The label does not mean unlimited personal discretion. Each power still has its own procedural conditions, and some require a proposal, consultation, or a particular institutional situation.

Everything outside the exception list is often described as a shared power because countersignature makes another executive officeholder legally participate in the act. Countersignature is not merely ceremonial in an adversarial cohabitation. A president cannot convert a countersigned competence into a unilateral one by invoking electoral legitimacy. Conversely, under a unified majority, political agreement can make a formally shared power look presidential in everyday practice.

## Appointing the prime minister and ministers

Under article 8's first paragraph, the president appoints the prime minister without countersignature. The text says the president terminates the prime minister's functions when the prime minister submits the Government's resignation; it does not give the president an explicit at-will dismissal power. Under stable presidential majorities, prime ministers have ordinarily departed when the president required it because party hierarchy supplied the political leverage that the text did not state. A prime minister backed by an opposing Assembly majority occupies a different position.

The president appoints and dismisses other ministers on the prime minister's proposal. This is therefore not a unilateral cabinet-building power. More fundamentally, article 49 allows the National Assembly to hold the Government politically accountable. The appointment power must operate in the shadow of a possible censure motion and the practical need for budgets and legislation. In a fragmented Assembly, choosing a prime minister can become bargaining over toleration rather than the simple designation of a presidential subordinate.

## Referendum under article 11

Article 11 permits the president, on a Government proposal during parliamentary sessions or on a joint proposal of the two assemblies, to submit certain bills to referendum. The permitted subjects include organization of public powers, reforms concerning national economic, social, or environmental policy and the public services contributing to it, and authorization to ratify certain treaties affecting institutions without violating the Constitution.

This is an article 19 “own power” because the presidential act is not countersigned, but it is not a power to invent and submit any referendum question alone. The required initiating proposal and the subject-matter limits matter. Article 11 also contains the separate shared-initiative referendum procedure involving one fifth of Parliament supported by one tenth of registered voters. That mechanism is not a plebiscitary tool held exclusively by the president.

Referendum politics can strengthen a president by bypassing ordinary legislative blockage, but the vote remains politically risky and the legal path constrained. A campaign promise to “hold a referendum” is incomplete unless it identifies the constitutional route, permissible subject, initiating actors, and possible constitutional review.

## Dissolving the National Assembly

Article 12 allows the president to dissolve the National Assembly after consulting the prime minister and the presidents of both assemblies. Their agreement is not required. A general election follows within twenty to forty days, and the newly elected Assembly cannot be dissolved again during the following year. This power can seek a governing majority, resolve a conflict, or gamble that voters will change the parliamentary balance.

Dissolution does not command the electorate. Its outcome may reinforce the president, impose cohabitation, or produce another hung Assembly. The power is therefore constitutionally unilateral but politically contingent. It also illustrates why presidential and legislative mandates must be analyzed separately in [[From the Élysée to a National Assembly majority]]. A 2027 winner could dissolve an existing Assembly, work with it, or face the ordinary legislative timetable, but none of those choices guarantees a supportive majority.

## Emergency powers under article 16

Article 16 is the exceptional concentration of authority, not a general emergency clause. Two cumulative conditions must exist: a grave and immediate threat to the Republic's institutions, national independence, territorial integrity, or international commitments; and interruption of the regular functioning of constitutional public powers. The president must formally consult the prime minister, the assembly presidents, and the Constitutional Council, then inform the nation.

Measures must aim to restore constitutional public authorities to their mission as quickly as possible. Parliament sits as of right, and the National Assembly cannot be dissolved while article 16 operates. Since the 2008 revision, the Constitutional Council may examine after thirty days, on referral by specified parliamentary authorities, whether the conditions remain satisfied; after sixty days it conducts that examination automatically and may do so again later.

These safeguards do not make article 16 ordinary. Its breadth is precisely why analysis should not merge it with states of emergency created by statute or the state of siege in article 36. A candidate's broad invocation of “full powers” is not a statement of the constitutional test, and ordinary security or political difficulty does not itself satisfy both article 16 conditions.

## Parliament, constitutional review, and communication

Article 18 lets the president send messages to the two houses and speak before Parliament convened in Congress. Debate may follow outside the president's presence, but no vote is taken on the presidential declaration. The power gives the president a national platform, not legislative command.

The president may refer an international commitment to the Constitutional Council under article 54 and may refer a statute before promulgation under article 61. The president appoints three of the nine appointed members of the Constitutional Council, including its president, under article 56. These are significant agenda and appointment powers, but constitutional review is performed by the Council, not by the president.

Article 10 requires the president to promulgate statutes within fifteen days after transmission of a definitively adopted law, while permitting one request for renewed parliamentary deliberation. That request cannot be refused. It delays and reopens consideration; it is not a permanent veto. The contrast with a veto-based presidential model is important: the French president's leverage comes through several tailored mechanisms rather than one general power to reject legislation.

## Appointments, decrees, and the Council of Ministers

Article 9 makes the president chair the Council of Ministers. Article 13 says the president signs ordinances and decrees deliberated there and makes specified civil and military appointments. An organic statute subjects certain appointments important to rights, freedoms, economic and social life, or national sovereignty to parliamentary committee review; appointment is blocked if negative votes reach the constitutionally defined three-fifths threshold.

These powers are generally countersigned. In a unified executive, control of the Council's agenda and agreement with the Government can make them instruments of presidential direction. In cohabitation, they become points of negotiation. Historical disputes over signing ordinances demonstrate that terse constitutional verbs do not eliminate political conflict over timing and responsibility.

The president also accredits ambassadors, receives foreign ambassadors, negotiates and ratifies treaties under article 52, and is commander-in-chief of the armed forces and chair of higher defence councils under article 15. Yet the Government has the armed force at its disposal under article 20, the prime minister is responsible for national defence under article 21, declarations of war require parliamentary authorization, and overseas military interventions trigger information and duration controls under article 35. “Reserved domain” in foreign affairs and defence describes a strong convention and political practice, not a constitutional transfer of all relevant powers away from Government and Parliament.

## Pardon, legal responsibility, and removal

Article 17 grants the president power to issue individual pardons. The restriction to individual rather than collective pardons dates from the 2008 revision. The act is countersigned, illustrating that a personally recognizable presidential function need not be a *pouvoir propre* under article 19.

Article 67 protects the president from being required to account, during the term, for official acts except through specified international-criminal and removal procedures, and temporarily bars ordinary proceedings against the officeholder. Suspended limitation periods resume after the term. This is not a declaration that every presidential act is lawful or that immunity lasts forever.

Article 68 permits removal for a breach of duties manifestly incompatible with continuation in office. Parliament acts as a High Court and must meet demanding supermajority rules. Removal is a constitutional safeguard distinct from the Government's ordinary political responsibility to the National Assembly. The president does not lose office merely because a legislative election is unfavorable or a policy becomes unpopular.

## Formal authority versus the presidential majority

The Fifth Republic's practical hierarchy cannot be read from articles 5–19 alone. Direct election gives the president a distinct democratic mandate. The five-year term and the post-presidential legislative calendar have often encouraged voters to give the newly elected president a majority. Party leadership then lets the president influence candidate selection, the prime minister, ministers, and the parliamentary agenda. Much of what observers call “presidential power” is this alignment of institutions and politics.

That alignment is contingent. The National Assembly can censure the Government; Parliament legislates; courts and the Constitutional Council review within their jurisdictions; local and European institutions constrain policy; and a prime minister with an independent majority controls the Government's survival. A president may remain dominant within a fragmented camp, but dominance is a bargaining outcome, not an additional constitutional article.

For 2027, three scenarios should therefore be kept distinct. A president with a disciplined majority can usually implement a program through the Government while using the office's formal powers. A president with a negotiated or minority arrangement must bargain bill by bill and protect the Government from censure. A president facing an opposing coherent majority retains the own powers but cannot ordinarily determine and conduct national policy against the Government charged with doing so. Election analysis that reports only the identity of the presidential winner misses the institutional question that follows immediately.

## How to assess 2027 campaign claims

A useful audit of any presidential promise asks five questions:

1. **What is the legal instrument?** Constitution, statute, decree, appointment, treaty, referendum, or political persuasion are not interchangeable.
2. **Is the act an article 19 own power?** If not, who must countersign it?
3. **Does another actor initiate or authorize it?** A Government proposal, parliamentary vote, Constitutional Council decision, or treaty partner may be indispensable.
4. **Is an Assembly majority required?** Ordinary legislation, budgets, and constitutional revision have different thresholds and failure modes.
5. **What happens under cohabitation or a hung Assembly?** A promise premised on unified control should be labeled as such.

This framework avoids two opposite errors. The first treats the president as an elected monarch capable of enacting a manifesto by command. The second sees textual constraints and concludes that the presidency is mostly symbolic. The office has unusually consequential tools—appointment of the prime minister, dissolution, referendum referral, constitutional review referrals, senior appointments, defence and diplomatic functions, and, in an extreme breakdown, article 16. Their use, however, is structured by conditions, countersignature, institutional response, and the parliamentary balance.
