France elects its president in a nationwide two-round contest, but getting onto the ballot, validating the vote, and replacing a president are constitutional processes rather than ordinary party decisions.

## The short version

The French president is elected directly for five years. Every qualified candidate competes in the first round. A candidate who wins an absolute majority of valid votes cast is elected immediately; otherwise, a second round is held fourteen days later between the two candidates with the largest first-round totals, subject to the Constitution's unusual allowance for withdrawals by better-placed candidates. The candidate with an absolute majority of valid votes in the runoff wins.

This simple description conceals three filters. A would-be candidate must be legally eligible, must consent to candidacy and make the required declarations, and must receive at least 500 valid *présentations* from designated elected officeholders, spread across the territory. The Conseil constitutionnel—not a party, pollster, television network, or ministry—then establishes the ballot. It also supervises the regularity of the election, rules on claims, corrects or annuls tainted local returns where necessary, certifies the totals, and proclaims the winner.

That produces an important vocabulary rule for [[2027 French presidential election]] coverage: a person can be discussed, declared, or selected by a party without being a legally qualified candidate. In French public debate, *candidat* is often used loosely before the ballot is settled. In a precise institutional account, “qualified” should be reserved for someone included in the Conseil constitutionnel's published list.

## A national, personal, two-round election

The constitutional core lies in articles 6 and 7 of the 1958 Constitution. Article 6 provides for direct universal suffrage, a five-year term, and a limit of two consecutive terms. Article 7 specifies an absolute-majority election and the sequence of rounds. The 1962 organic law and its implementing decree supply much of the operational machinery; provisions of the electoral code also apply through cross-reference.

The presidential election is *uninominal*: the voter selects one individual, not a party list or a presidential–vice-presidential ticket. France has no separately elected vice president. It is also one national election. Votes are administered and counted through local polling stations and aggregated through territorial stages, but the decisive totals are nationwide. A candidate does not win departments, regions, or an electoral college as intermediate prizes.

“Majority of votes” here means a majority of *suffrages exprimés*, or valid votes cast for candidates. Blank ballots and invalid ballots are counted and reported separately but do not enter the denominator used to decide whether a candidate has an absolute majority. Abstention likewise matters politically but not arithmetically to the majority threshold. A president can therefore win more than half of valid candidate votes while receiving support from substantially fewer than half of registered electors.

At the first round, every person on the official list competes. France does not ordinarily use one ballot paper listing all the names: polling places provide a separate printed *bulletin de vote* for each candidate, and the voter places the chosen bulletin in the official envelope. If one candidate receives more than half of valid votes cast, the election ends. That has never occurred in a direct presidential election under the Fifth Republic. If nobody does, the Constitution schedules a second round on the fourteenth day after the first.

Normally the two highest first-round finishers advance. Article 7 is drafted more carefully: only the two candidates who, “where appropriate after the withdrawal of better-placed candidates,” have received the greatest first-round totals may stand. The clause can permit a qualified finisher to withdraw and thereby change who remains eligible for the runoff. It is not a general rule letting any first-round candidate negotiate entry into round two. Once two candidates remain, the one receiving an absolute majority of valid votes is elected; with only two names, barring an exact tie or an exceptional disruption, one will have it.

The two-round system therefore asks different questions in quick succession. The first round sorts a plural field and reveals the relative strength of candidacies. The runoff makes a binary choice. Endorsements and *front républicain* appeals between rounds do not mechanically transfer votes: supporters can follow an endorsement, switch for other reasons, vote blank, spoil a ballot, or abstain. Polling that treats first-round shares as owned blocs misses this institutional and behavioral break.

## Who may vote

Presidential suffrage is for French citizens registered on an electoral list and possessing electoral rights. Citizens vote through the polling arrangements applicable to their registration, including municipal lists in France and consular lists abroad. French citizens living outside France are part of the same national choice; they do not elect a separate presidential representative. Overseas France is also included. By statutory exception to ordinary Sunday voting, Guadeloupe, Guyane, Martinique, Saint-Barthélemy, Saint-Martin, Saint-Pierre-et-Miquelon, French Polynesia, and embassies and consulates in the Americas vote on Saturday. The sequence limits premature exposure to Sunday results, but the legally decisive point is the territory-specific voting day fixed by the 1962 law.

Ordinary French voting is personal and secret, using official ballots and an envelope deposited at a polling station after identity and registration checks. A registered elector who cannot attend can arrange a *procuration*, authorizing another elector to vote on the principal's behalf under the governing rules. The 1962 law also provides postal voting under safeguards for eligible voters held in French prisons. These mechanisms do not create extra votes: they are alternative ways for an elector to exercise one vote.

Local polling stations conduct the initial count publicly under statutory procedures. Their minutes record totals and incidents. Results then move through official tabulation, while delegates and magistrates involved in constitutional supervision can record irregularities. Election-night ministry totals are operationally authoritative reporting, but the legal endpoint is the Conseil constitutionnel's proclamation, not a media projection or a candidate's concession.

## Who may seek the office

The legal baseline is broader than the political reality. A presidential contender must be French, have reached the age required to be an elector, enjoy civil and political rights, and not be legally ineligible. The candidate must consent. The process also requires declarations concerning assets and interests under the applicable transparency regime. These requirements concern legal capacity and disclosure; they do not demonstrate political viability.

The decisive ballot-access hurdle is commonly called the *500 parrainages*. The organic law's own term is *présentations*: presentations of a candidate by eligible elected officials. The distinction matters. A presentation is a formal institutional act that enables candidacy; it need not mean the presenting official endorses the candidate's program or intends to vote for that person.

At least 500 valid presentations must reach the Conseil constitutionnel by the statutory deadline. Presenters come from defined categories of officeholders: parliamentarians, French members of the European Parliament elected in France, mayors and certain intercommunal leaders, regional and departmental councillors, members of specified overseas assemblies, and other offices enumerated by law. Each eligible presenter may present only one person. Presentations must also satisfy a territorial distribution rule: they must come from at least thirty departments or equivalent overseas collectivities, and no more than one tenth of the required total may come from any one such territory. Five hundred signatures concentrated in a single political stronghold would not suffice.

The Conseil constitutionnel receives and validates the forms, publishes presenters' names under the statutory transparency regime, verifies the threshold and territorial spread, and checks the other candidacy materials. The Government formally publishes the resulting list, but the Conseil establishes it. A party nomination, a campaign launch, abundant polling support, or even more than 500 publicly promised presentations cannot substitute for the valid documents received and accepted before the deadline.

For an election at the normal end of a term, the consolidated 1962 law sets two useful relative deadlines. Presentations must arrive by 6 p.m. on the sixth Friday before the first round, and the candidate list is published no later than the fourth Friday before that round. Different compressed deadlines apply after a vacancy or definitive incapacity. Exact calendar dates should therefore be taken from the election decree and contemporary Conseil instructions, not inferred casually from campaign reporting.

The official list can be contested only through the specialized route set by article 8 of the implementing decree and constitutional case law. A person who was the subject of at least one presentation may lodge a claim, which must reach the Conseil before the end of the day after publication of the list in the *Journal officiel*. The Conseil rules without delay. This is not an open-ended popular appeal enabling any voter to reorder the ballot. The Conseil's candidate-list jurisdiction is part of why “official candidate” should be used cautiously before its decision.

## The ordinary calendar

The Constitution anchors a scheduled election to the end of the incumbent's powers. The new election must take place no fewer than twenty and no more than thirty-five days before those powers expire. The Government convokes the electorate. Since the 2021 organic reform, the convocation decree for a normal end-of-term election must be published at least ten weeks before the first round.

Within that envelope, the legal and administrative calendar layers several distinct moments:

1. The convocation decree fixes the voting dates and activates election-specific deadlines.
2. Eligible officeholders submit presentations to the Conseil constitutionnel.
3. The Conseil establishes, and the Government publishes, the qualified candidate list.
4. The official campaign runs under election-specific finance, propaganda, and audiovisual rules.
5. Voters cast first-round ballots.
6. The Conseil verifies and proclaims first-round totals; if required, two candidates proceed.
7. The runoff occurs fourteen days after the first round.
8. After examining records and claims, the Conseil proclaims the final result and the president-elect takes office at the constitutional handover.

The Interior Ministry currently gives April 18 and May 2 as the 2027 first- and second-round dates. Those are the government's published current dates as of this article's cutoff; the election-specific convocation decree remains the primary instrument that legally fixes the ordinary election calendar. An evergreen explanation should preserve the governing relative rules because constitutional contingencies can alter a calendar.

## What “the campaign” means legally

Political campaigning begins long before the official campaign period. French law nevertheless attaches different obligations to different periods. Campaign finance accounting, donation and spending rules, official electoral propaganda, and audiovisual pluralism do not all begin on the same day or use the same clock. The legally defined campaign is therefore narrower than the sociological campaign and intersects several regulatory calendars.

Once the ballot is established, the state provides a regulated channel for official campaign material. Candidate treatment on radio and television is overseen under rules applied by Arcom, while campaign accounts are reviewed through the finance regime associated with the CNCCFP and, for presidential accounts, ultimate adjudicative consequences involving the Conseil constitutionnel. Those systems deserve their own articles because “equal time” and “public funding” are often oversimplified. For election mechanics, the key point is that ballot equality does not erase differences in parties, money, organization, or popularity; it creates a common legal contest among the qualified names.

## Counting, certification, and disputes

Article 58 of the Constitution gives the Conseil constitutionnel three connected functions: it ensures the regularity of the presidential election, examines claims, and proclaims the results. Its involvement begins before election day. Under the organic law and implementing decree, it establishes the candidate list, appoints delegates to monitor operations, and receives official records. After voting, it does not merely add numbers supplied by the ministry. It reviews polling-station minutes, incidents, observations, and formal complaints, and can determine whether irregularities impaired the sincerity of the vote.

Challenges follow specific channels and tight deadlines. A voter can have a claim entered in the polling station's official minutes; certain authorities and candidates can also use the procedures provided by the implementing rules. A complaint is not self-proving, and not every procedural mistake changes the result. Electoral adjudication asks what occurred, whether the governing rule was breached, and whether the defect affected the integrity or outcome of the vote. Depending on the evidence, the Conseil may reject a claim, correct figures, or annul voting operations in particular polling stations.

Past presidential proclamations show why the distinction between national annulment and local correction matters. Where polling-station conduct compromised the required controls—for example, failures affecting ballot custody or the ability to verify operations—the Conseil has excluded that station's votes from the national totals. This is a serious remedy, but it does not automatically rerun the entire national election. The effect of an irregularity, its scale, and the vote margins all matter to the legally appropriate response.

The Conseil first proclaims the first-round result and identifies the candidates permitted to remain. It later proclaims the final result after the runoff. Provisional ministry figures and media calls are invaluable for public knowledge, but only the proclamation completes the constitutional determination. This also means that close-result coverage should distinguish reported totals, validated totals, corrections, and the proclamation rather than treating them as one event.

## Death, incapacity, vacancy, and an interrupted election

Article 7 contains contingency rules unusually detailed for a constitution. They address two different problems: interruption of an election already approaching, and absence or incapacity of the sitting president.

If, in the seven days before the deadline for presentations, a publicly announced prospective candidate dies or becomes incapacitated—and had announced within the preceding thirty days—the Conseil constitutionnel may postpone the election. If an already qualified candidate dies or becomes incapacitated before the first round, the Conseil must postpone it. If one of the two best-placed first-round candidates dies or is incapacitated before possible withdrawals, or if either of the final two is affected before the runoff, the entire electoral operation begins again. These rules protect real choice and prevent an accidental victory produced by a ballot that no longer contains the constitutionally relevant competitors.

A vacancy in the presidency “for whatever cause,” or an incapacity formally found by the Conseil constitutionnel on a Government referral, activates interim arrangements. The president of the Senate temporarily exercises presidential functions; if the Senate president is also unable, the Government does so collectively. The interim authority cannot exercise the presidential powers to call an article 11 referendum or dissolve the National Assembly under article 12. During the vacancy or definitive-incapacity period, the Constitution also disables specified government-responsibility and constitutional-amendment procedures.

After a vacancy opens or incapacity is declared definitive, a new presidential election normally must occur between twenty and thirty-five days later. The Conseil can recognize *force majeure* and extend constitutional deadlines within the limits article 7 sets. The Government must issue the convocation without delay, and the organic law compresses the presentation and candidate-list timetable. France therefore does not automatically elevate an interim officeholder for the remainder of the five-year term; it organizes a fresh direct election.

An election postponement can also carry the incumbent beyond the originally expected end of the term. Article 7 expressly provides that when the constitutional postponement rules place the vote after the incumbent's powers would otherwise expire, the incumbent remains in office until the successor is proclaimed. Continuity is supplied by constitutional rule, not an improvised extension.

## Terms that prevent common mistakes

- **Scrutin uninominal majoritaire à deux tours:** a vote for one person under an absolute-majority, two-round rule. It is not proportional representation.
- **Suffrages exprimés:** valid ballots cast for candidates. Blank and invalid ballots are reported but excluded from the majority denominator.
- **Présentation / parrainage:** the formal act by an eligible officeholder putting a person forward. “Sponsorship” is a useful translation only if it is not confused with personal endorsement.
- **Candidat déclaré:** someone who says they are running. Declaration alone does not place a name on the ballot.
- **Candidat qualifié:** a useful English analytical label for a person on the Conseil constitutionnel's official list.
- **Premier tour / second tour:** first round and runoff. The second is a new national vote, not a redistribution formula applied to first-round ballots.
- **Conseil constitutionnel:** the constitutional authority that establishes the candidate list, supervises regularity, decides claims, and proclaims results. It is not simply France's analogue of an election commission or an ordinary supreme court.
- **Proclamation:** the authoritative constitutional declaration of results, distinct from preliminary reporting.
- **Empêchement:** incapacity preventing the president or a candidate from performing the relevant role; its constitutional consequences depend on when it occurs and, for the presidency, on a formal Conseil determination.

## What remains uncertain for 2027

The institutional skeleton is settled enough to explain, but not every 2027 operational detail is fixed forever. The consolidated 1962 law displayed on July 9, 2026 contains an article 3 version marked effective only through December 31, 2026, reflecting already enacted institutional changes with later effective dates. The rule applicable during the 2027 presentation period must therefore be checked in Légifrance's version in force on the relevant 2027 date rather than copied from a 2026 snapshot. Election-specific decrees and institutional instructions will also supply exact forms, deadlines, polling arrangements, and campaign procedures. The Interior Ministry's current dates are April 18 and May 2, 2027; calendar-sensitive notes should continue to recheck them.

More importantly, the rules cannot identify the field in advance. Public declarations, party nominations, and opinion polls are evidence about politics, not ballot qualification. Nor can the two-round formula determine transfer behavior: the identities of the finalists change the meaning of the runoff, and abstention or blank voting can be a substantive choice. A sound 2027 analysis should therefore separate four questions: who seeks to run, who can assemble valid presentations, whom the Conseil qualifies, and how voters behave in each actual round.
