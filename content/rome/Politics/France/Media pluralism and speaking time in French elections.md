French audiovisual law applies a permanent pluralism regime to political expression, then overlays special presidential-election rules that move from equity to strict equality as polling day approaches.

Up: [[2027 French presidential election]]

## The short version

“Speaking time” is not one rule. France uses several related controls, principally for radio and television:

- **Outside an election-specific period**, Arcom supervises political pluralism continuously. Government, parliamentary majority, opposition, and other political forces do not receive identical shares; exposure is assessed against institutional position and political representativeness. Since a 2024 Conseil d’État judgment and Arcom’s July 2024 deliberation, the regulator’s broader pluralism review also considers the range of viewpoints expressed by all on-air participants, not only politicians whose seconds can be counted.
- **During the early presidential-election period**, likely candidates and then declared candidates receive **equitable**, not equal, speaking time and airtime. Equity permits different quantities, but the differences must be justified by measurable political weight and participation in the campaign.
- **From the start of the official campaign**, every candidate receives **equal** speaking time and **equal** airtime, in comparable scheduling conditions. The same strict equality governs the two finalists from the Monday after the first round through the Friday before the second.

The decisive distinction is therefore not “regulated” versus “unregulated.” Political broadcasting is regulated throughout. What changes is the unit being measured, the people being compared, the permissible justification for differences, and eventually the governing principle: pluralism, then electoral equity, then electoral equality.

## Four concepts that should not be collapsed

### Pluralism

Pluralism is the durable constitutional and statutory objective. The Constitutional Council has treated pluralism of sociocultural currents of expression as a constitutional objective and a condition of democracy. The 30 September 1986 freedom-of-communication law charges Arcom with guaranteeing pluralism of information and ensuring pluralistic expression of currents of thought and opinion in audiovisual programming, especially political and general information.

This is an **internal pluralism** obligation: within a regulated radio or television service, the range and balance of political expression must not become manifestly and durably one-sided. It is different from **external pluralism**, meaning diversity among separately owned media outlets. Ownership concentration matters to democratic debate, but it is not what a speaking-time table directly measures.

Arcom’s 17 July 2024 deliberation broadened the operational lens after the Conseil d’État held on 13 February 2024 that the regulator could not assess pluralism solely from airtime granted to political personalities. Arcom must also consider the currents of thought and opinion expressed by presenters, journalists, columnists, experts, and other participants. The deliberation calls for a holistic assessment of the diversity of participants, the variety of subjects, and the plurality of viewpoints, looking for a **manifest and lasting imbalance**. It does not require mechanically labeling every journalist’s ideology or imposing second-for-second balance among commentators.

### Speaking time

**Temps de parole** is the duration for which a candidate, political figure, or qualifying supporter is actually heard speaking. A candidate’s interview answers count; so may a supporter’s advocacy. A journalist describing the candidate normally does not become the candidate’s speaking time.

The category matters because access to direct speech gives a politician control over wording, emphasis, and emotional presentation. But speaking time alone can mislead. A candidate might speak briefly while a channel devotes hours of favorable or hostile discussion to that person.

### Airtime

**Temps d’antenne** is broader. In a presidential campaign it includes speaking time plus editorial material devoted to a candidate and their supporters: reports, commentary, presentation, images, and other treatment of the candidacy. This is why the presidential rules demand equity or equality in both speaking time **and** airtime. A broadcaster cannot satisfy equality by giving every candidate the same interview duration while surrounding some with dramatically more campaign coverage.

The distinction also explains why tone cannot be reduced to a stopwatch. Equal airtime can include critical reporting; the rules do not create a right to favorable journalism. Separate duties of honesty, independence, and rigor continue to apply.

### Comparable programming conditions

Equality at 3 a.m. is not equivalent to equality in prime time. The organic presidential-election law therefore requires equity and equality in **comparable programming conditions**, which Arcom specifies in its election recommendation. In 2022 it divided the day into morning (6–9), daytime (9–18), evening (18–24), and night (0–6) bands. Broadcasters had to balance exposure within meaningful scheduling contexts, not merely reach an aggregate total across the entire day.

Comparable conditions also concern the nature and audience of programs. Exact arrangements for 2027 will depend on Arcom’s future recommendation, so the 2022 bands are a strong precedent rather than a legally fixed 2027 calendar.

## The routine regime outside the presidential sequence

The permanent framework rests mainly on articles 1, 3-1, and 13 of the 1986 law and Arcom’s general pluralism deliberations. It applies whether or not an official campaign has begun. Broadcasters themselves make editorial choices, keep relevant records, and transmit political speaking-time statements where required; Arcom examines the results and can seek corrections or use its enforcement powers.

Routine political speaking-time accounting has historically distinguished the executive from other political forces. The governing framework does not promise each party an identical fraction. Instead, it seeks a politically intelligible distribution that recognizes the government’s institutional role while preserving meaningful access for the parliamentary majority, opposition, and non-parliamentary currents. Representativeness can be inferred from election results, numbers of elected officials, polling, and contribution to political debate.

This ordinary regime remains important before the presidential recommendation starts. A person may behave like a candidate long before becoming legally nominated, but not every appearance is automatically counted under a presidential-candidate ledger. Arcom must decide when an election-specific framework should begin and whom it covers; meanwhile ordinary pluralism still constrains the channel.

The 2024 expansion adds a second analytic layer. Quantified political speaking times remain a central tool, but Arcom also assesses whether the overall information service persistently excludes or overwhelms certain currents through its choice of guests, themes, and opinions. This qualitative review is deliberately longer-horizon: isolated programs or individual statements do not alone establish a manifest and durable imbalance. Nor does the broader test replace the precise presidential stopwatch once election rules apply. Both can operate together.

Print newspapers are not subject to Arcom’s radio-and-television speaking-time allocation. Online political debate is also not generally converted into candidate time ledgers merely because it is influential. The relevant election recommendation defines which audiovisual services and programs fall within its scope. Digital platforms may face other transparency, systemic-risk, or electoral-integrity duties, but those are not the same as French broadcast equality.

## The presidential clock

The exact dates for 2027 are not yet available as of 9 July 2026. They will depend on the election calendar, the Constitutional Council’s candidate-list publication, the statutory start of the official campaign, and Arcom’s election-specific recommendation. The 2022 sequence shows how the legal machinery works.

### 1. An anticipatory equity period

For 2022, the CSA—the predecessor whose recommendation Arcom administered from January 2022—made its presidential recommendation effective on **1 January 2022**. From 1 January through 7 March, before publication of the official candidate list, broadcasters had to provide prospective candidates and their supporters equitable speaking time and airtime.

Equity was assessed using two families of indicators:

1. **Representativeness**, including results obtained by the candidate or supporting political formations in the most recent elections, indications from opinion polls, and the political weight of supporters.
2. **Contribution to the animation of the electoral debate**, including campaign activity, meetings, field visits, public exposure of the person or program—including through social networks—and participation in debates.

The second criterion prevents equity from becoming a frozen entitlement based only on past results. A new candidacy that demonstrably structures the current contest may deserve substantial exposure. Conversely, declaring an intention to run does not by itself compel a broadcaster to provide the same time as a nationally established and active campaign.

### 2. Equity after the official candidate list

Article 3, I bis of the 1962 organic-law framework creates a statutory phase from publication of the Constitutional Council’s list of candidates until the eve of the official campaign. In 2022 this ran from **8 March through 27 March**. The same principle—equity—continued, but now the comparison was among legally established candidates.

This phase matters because obtaining 500 valid presentations and appearing on the Constitutional Council’s list does **not** immediately produce equal broadcast time. The 2016 reform replaced the former longer equality phase with equity until the official campaign. Its practical purpose was to reduce the programming burden created by strict equality among numerous candidates while preserving justified access for all.

Equity is not editorial discretion without reasons. Arcom examines quantitative discrepancies against representativeness and campaign activity, across speaking time, airtime, and comparable schedule bands. In March 2022 it publicly identified overexposure on particular channels and demanded correction. Broadcasters therefore must monitor balances while there is still time to repair them; a mathematically improved final week may not erase prolonged earlier distortion in every context.

### 3. Equality during the official first-round campaign

For 2022 the official campaign began on **28 March** and ended on **8 April**, the Friday before the Sunday vote, because campaigning and electoral propaganda stop before polling day. From the campaign’s start, article 3, I bis requires equality in the reproduction and commentary of candidates’ declarations and writings and in presentation of their person. Arcom operationalizes this as equality of both speaking time and airtime in comparable programming conditions.

Equality means each legally qualified candidate is owed the same quantity, irrespective of polls, previous results, campaign budget, or perceived newsworthiness. A broadcaster may choose formats and retain journalistic freedom, but its overall allocation must satisfy the rule. This is the sharpest departure from routine pluralism and early equity.

Separately, official campaign broadcasts on national public-service channels are allocated under article 15 of the 8 March 2001 decree. Each first-round candidate receives an equal duration, fixed by Arcom after consultation and no less than fifteen minutes. These candidate-produced official broadcasts are not the whole media-equality regime; they sit alongside equality across ordinary audiovisual coverage.

### 4. Equality between the two rounds

If no candidate wins an absolute majority on the first ballot, equality resumes for the finalists from the **Monday after the first round through the Friday before the second**. In 2022 Arcom applied equality from 11 through 22 April. The two finalists and their supporters had to receive equal speaking time and airtime in comparable conditions.

Public-service official broadcasts are again separately guaranteed. Under the 2001 decree each finalist is allocated at least one hour, unless both agree to a shorter duration. The televised debate is politically pivotal but not itself the complete legal balance sheet; other interviews, reports, and commentary throughout the inter-round period count as well.

## Who and what gets counted

The candidate’s own political expression is straightforward. Supporters are harder. A minister, party official, elected representative, celebrity, or civil-society figure may be attributed to a candidate when their intervention supports that candidacy. Broadcasters must classify context, not merely identity: the same person can speak in an institutional capacity at one moment and campaign at another.

The incumbent president poses the most visible classification problem. Arcom distinguishes speech arising from presidential duties from speech belonging to political debate or the campaign. In 2022, interventions linked to constitutional or diplomatic functions were not automatically charged to the president-candidate, while statements entering partisan debate were. The label attached by the broadcaster is not conclusive; substance and context govern. This boundary is inevitably contestable because incumbency itself produces newsworthy images and authority that rivals cannot reproduce.

Editorial comments about a candidate enter airtime even when the candidate is silent. Ordinary factual news may therefore affect the ledger. However, the system does not demand identical tone, identical questions, or identical sequences of favorable and unfavorable treatment. It regulates access and exposure while leaving room for journalism, subject to honesty and pluralism obligations.

Broadcasters do much of the first-line measurement. Services designated by Arcom submit statements at specified intervals, and Arcom publishes or audits relevant totals, contacts editors about imbalances, and can require corrective action. The regulator’s leverage is partly preventive: frequent reporting makes it possible to rebalance before a phase closes. Formal enforcement can include notices and sanctions under the 1986 law, but public scrutiny and rapid compliance are especially important during a short campaign.

## Why it matters for 2027

First, the rule change alters campaign strategy. During equity, campaigns have incentives to demonstrate activity, polling relevance, elected support, and contribution to debate because those facts can justify more exposure. Once equality begins, marginal and leading candidates receive the same quantitative entitlement. Candidates who qualify late therefore acquire a scarce public resource that money alone cannot buy.

Second, dates shape the information voters receive. A large portion of opinion formation occurs before strict equality. The later equality phase cannot fully reverse months of agenda setting under ordinary pluralism or equity. For analysis of [[2027 French presidential election]], every claim that a candidate was “excluded” or “favored” should identify the applicable period and compare speaking time, airtime, and schedule placement—not cite a single aggregate number.

Third, the 2024 broadened pluralism doctrine may matter more in 2027 than it did in 2022. A channel could distribute candidates’ seconds correctly yet surround election coverage with a stable set of hosts and commentators expressing a narrow range of views. The quantitative presidential rules and qualitative general-pluralism assessment address different pathways to imbalance. How Arcom combines them in a high-intensity presidential campaign will be an important regulatory test.

Fourth, equality can conflict with ordinary news judgment. A breakthrough, scandal, war, or incumbent presidential act may objectively make one candidate more newsworthy. The French system accepts that tension to protect the voter’s opportunity to encounter every qualified candidacy during the short official campaign. “Equal” does not mean a broadcaster endorses equivalence among programs; it means that editorial gatekeeping temporarily yields to a democratic allocation rule.

Finally, television and radio no longer monopolize political attention. Candidates can build enormous audiences on platforms outside the traditional time-counting regime. Broadcast equality therefore protects access to regulated mass media but cannot equalize total visibility across the information environment. A rigorous 2027 assessment should place Arcom’s ledgers beside platform reach, paid and organic digital campaigning, and media-ownership patterns without pretending those are governed by the same rule.

## A practical checklist for reading 2027 coverage

When a speaking-time controversy arises, ask:

1. What exact dates does the claim cover?
2. Was the governing rule routine pluralism, presidential equity, or presidential equality?
3. Is the number speaking time or the broader airtime?
4. Does it include supporters, and how were ambiguous interventions classified?
5. Were comparable morning, daytime, evening, and night conditions respected?
6. Is the comparison made service by service and phase by phase, or is it an aggregate that hides imbalance?
7. Is the complaint about candidate allocation, or about the broader mix of hosts, guests, themes, and viewpoints under the 2024 pluralism doctrine?
8. Is the medium actually within Arcom’s election recommendation?

Without those answers, “equal time” is usually rhetoric rather than a legal diagnosis.
