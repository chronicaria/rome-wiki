First-to-second-round “transfers” are conditional choices made after the presidential field collapses to two candidates; they are not votes owned by eliminated candidates, and abstention and blank or null ballots are part of the transfer matrix rather than statistical leftovers.

Up: [[2027 French presidential election]] · Related: [[2027 French presidential coalition map]] · [[How to read French presidential polls]] · [[How the French presidential election works]]

The French phrase *reports de voix* invites a misleading picture: votes collected by an eliminated candidate in the first round appear to be poured into one of two runoff containers. No institution performs that operation. Each voter faces a new ballot, on a new day, after endorsements, conflict, campaign events, and information about the first-round result. Some choose finalist A, some finalist B, some abstain, and some cast a blank or null ballot. Others had already voted strategically in round one, so even the first-round label may imperfectly describe their political attachment.

For 2027, transfer analysis should therefore answer a conditional question: given a specified pair of finalists, how might the electorates observed or measured in round one divide among the two names and non-expression? It should not answer “which party’s votes belong to whom?” No 2027 first-round electorate exists yet, the qualified field is not settled, and hypothetical runoff surveys remain scenario tests. Historical evidence can identify mechanisms and plausible constraints, but it cannot supply a fixed 2027 matrix.

## The transfer matrix

For an electorate that chose candidate $i$ in round one, a complete runoff accounting has at least four destinations:

$$
p_{iA}+p_{iB}+p_{i,\mathrm{abstain}}+p_{i,\mathrm{blank/null}}=1.
$$

Poll releases often combine the last two destinations with refusal or uncertainty into *non exprimé*. That is useful for showing how much of the first-round group has not selected a finalist, but it is not identical to eventual abstention. A respondent who declines to state a runoff choice may later vote; a respondent who currently says “blank” may abstain; and a late campaign event may move someone between the finalists. An election-night survey can separate reported abstention from blank/null voting more directly, but it remains a survey estimate rather than a count of linked individual ballots.

The final runoff total can be represented as the sum of each first-round electorate multiplied by its destination rate, plus the behavior of people who did not cast an expressed first-round vote but participate in the runoff. That last term matters. Published “transfer” tables centered on named first-round candidates can omit first-round abstainers who mobilize in round two, while some first-round voters demobilize. Turnout is not a conserved quantity.

Official results establish aggregate flows, not individual ones. France uses a secret ballot; the Ministry of the Interior and the Conseil constitutionnel publish totals by round and territory, but those totals do not reveal how the same person voted twice. A transfer matrix is consequently estimated from surveys, panel reinterviews, ecological patterns, or a combination. The method must be named because each supports a different claim.

## Four mechanisms that produce a runoff choice

### Proximity and program

Some voters choose the finalist they regard as closer on ideology, program, social interests, or governing style. This is the most intuitive mechanism, but “distance” is multidimensional. A first-round voter may place economic redistribution, immigration, Europe, climate, institutional reform, or presidential temperament at different weights. Two voters who chose the same first-round candidate can therefore rank the finalists differently.

Candidate identity matters independently of party family. The [[2027 French presidential coalition map]] is best read as a set of coalition projects rather than permanent blocs precisely because adjacent parties do not create identical electorates. A runoff featuring a conventional conservative, a post-Macron centrist, a left candidate, or an RN candidate would activate different dimensions and produce different transfer patterns. Substituting one figure for another inside the same broad family changes the counterfactual.

### Blocking and the republican front

A voter may choose primarily to prevent the other finalist from winning. In France this is often discussed as a *front républicain*, a *barrage*, or a vote “against” rather than “for.” These expressions should not be treated as synonyms for an automatic anti-RN majority. The strength and direction of blocking depend on who the two finalists are, how each is perceived, the conduct of the inter-round campaign, and whether voters consider the recommended alternative acceptable.

Elite endorsements can provide information and moral or strategic cues, but they do not bind voters. An eliminated candidate can recommend a vote for one finalist, say that not one vote should go to another, refuse to endorse, or consult supporters. Each message leaves multiple compliant behaviors. “Do not vote for B” can mean vote for A, abstain, or cast a blank ballot. Reporting the instruction as a guaranteed transfer erases that choice.

The intensity of rejection matters as much as left–right placement. A voter can dislike both finalists but perceive one as uniquely dangerous; another can see them as different versions of an unacceptable system and opt out. Campaigns try to convert negative motivation into participation, yet harsh first-round attacks can make a later unity appeal less credible.

### Strategic first-round voting

Transfer tables classify voters by what they did in round one, not necessarily by their preferred candidate. A *vote utile* can concentrate voters behind the contender thought most able to qualify. In that case the apparent first-round electorate is already a coalition of core supporters, late strategic voters, and people voting against exclusion. Its runoff behavior may be unusually heterogeneous.

This creates a selection problem. Suppose voters from several left parties converge late on one candidate to try to reach the runoff. A table labeled with that candidate’s name may be read as the behavior of a coherent party electorate even though part of it arrived for strategic reasons. The same logic can operate among right, center, or nationalist contenders. Stable candidate labels do not guarantee stable electorate composition.

### Mobilization and non-expression

The choice is not only between finalists. Participation responds to closeness, perceived stakes, campaign legitimacy, practical access, and whether either option feels tolerable. Blank voting communicates that the voter participated but selected neither finalist; a null ballot is invalid, whether intentionally or accidentally. Official statistics distinguish abstentions, blank ballots, null ballots, and expressed votes. A poll that collapses them should say so.

Non-expression is often concentrated among the supporters of eliminated candidates whose preferences do not map neatly onto the runoff. It can decide the result without appearing as a “transfer” to either finalist. The proper question is not merely who wins a plurality within an eliminated electorate, but how much of that electorate supplies expressed votes at all.

## What 2022 demonstrates—and what it does not

The 2022 Macron–Le Pen rematch offers an observed case, not a template for 2027. The Conseil constitutionnel proclaimed 18,768,639 votes for Emmanuel Macron and 13,288,686 for Marine Le Pen in the second round, or 58.55% and 41.45% of expressed votes. It also recorded 13,655,861 abstentions, 2,233,904 blank ballots, and 805,249 null ballots. The distinction is substantive: the winner’s percentage of expressed votes does not mean 58.55% of registered voters endorsed him, and millions participated without selecting either finalist.

Ipsos and Sopra Steria’s election-weekend survey, fielded April 21–23 among 4,000 registered adults, estimated how first-round electorates divided. Among people identified as first-round Jean-Luc Mélenchon voters, 42% reported choosing Macron and 17% Le Pen; 24% abstained and 17% reported a blank or null ballot. Among Yannick Jadot voters, the estimated destinations were 65% Macron and 6% Le Pen, with 16% abstention and 13% blank/null. Among Valérie Pécresse voters, they were 53% Macron and 18% Le Pen, with 15% abstention and 14% blank/null. Among Éric Zemmour voters, 73% reported Le Pen. These estimates illustrate fragmentation within every eliminated electorate; they are not administrative counts or linked-ballot observations.

Pre-election measures also moved. In the Ipsos daily barometer published through the Commission des sondages, estimated intentions among Mélenchon’s first-round voters varied during the inter-round period. On 15 April, the table showed 28% Macron, 16% Le Pen, and 56% non-expressed; by 22 April it showed 33%, 19%, and 48%. The election-weekend survey later estimated 42%, 17%, and 41% split between abstention and blank/null. Differences in timing, design, and the meaning of “non-expressed” prohibit a clean claim that nine percentage points mechanically “switched” to Macron. They do show why an early matrix is not a final allocation.

The 2022 history supplies several durable lessons. The Mélenchon electorate was pivotal but not unitary. The Jadot and Pécresse electorates leaned toward Macron while retaining meaningful non-expression. The Zemmour electorate leaned strongly toward Le Pen, yet even a strongly aligned adjacent electorate did not transfer unanimously. Macron’s and Le Pen’s own first-round electorates also had different retention and participation rates. A runoff coalition is assembled through both expansion and core remobilization.

The history does **not** establish stable coefficients such as “a left voter transfers to the center at 42%” or “a nationalist-right voter transfers to RN at 73%.” In 2027 the candidate identities, records, issue agenda, party endorsements, first-round strategic composition, and governing context will differ. Even a repeated Macron–Le Pen pairing would not be the same experiment; Macron cannot seek a third consecutive term, and the alternatives inherit neither his electorate nor his rejection profile automatically.

## How to read a 2027 transfer poll

Start with the pairing. “Second-round voting intention” is meaningless without two names. A poll testing several alternative finalists contains several separate counterfactual elections, not interchangeable measurements of party blocs. A respondent can choose the center against one opponent and abstain against another.

Next identify the base. Before the first round, a table may cross a hypothetical first-round intention with a hypothetical second-round intention in the same interview. That is a scenario matrix, not a transfer from an observed vote. After the first round, the base can be reported first-round vote, which is more concrete but still subject to recall and sample size. A late-campaign or election-weekend survey samples reported behavior close to voting; a panel can recontact the same people and observe within-person changes, but attrition and panel conditioning require attention.

Then preserve the non-expressed category. A topline such as 52–48 is normally calculated among respondents who select a finalist. If 20% of the relevant base is non-expressed, the displayed gap describes the other 80%, not the full group. For a first-round electorate of 100 respondents, a 60–40 split among expressers could mean 48 for A, 32 for B, and 20 non-expressed. Reporting only 60–40 makes the transfer look more complete and certain than it is.

Inspect sample sizes. Even a large national survey can contain small subgroups for minor first-round candidates. If 8% of a 1,500-person relevant sample supports a candidate, the unweighted subgroup is roughly 120 before further filters; weighted effective size may be smaller. A five-point change inside that subgroup can reflect few interviews. The notice should provide the total sample, relevant voting-intention base, quotas or recruitment, field dates, mode, adjustment criteria, and question wording. [[How to read French presidential polls]] gives the full audit routine.

Finally, distinguish three products:

1. **Stated intention:** what respondents say under a named pairing at a particular time.
2. **Estimated behavioral transfer:** an inference about how first-round groups may divide, with sampling and modelling uncertainty.
3. **Forecast:** a prediction of the actual runoff result, requiring assumptions about qualification, late change, turnout, and error.

A poll directly observes only the first product. A transfer model and forecast add assumptions. They should be labeled rather than smuggled into a confident verb such as “will go.”

## A practical 2027 ledger

A useful transfer tracker should keep one row per institute, fieldwork window, and named pairing. It should record:

- sponsor and exact notice URL;
- interview dates and publication date;
- mode, total sample, and subgroup bases where disclosed;
- whether the first-round category is a hypothetical intention, recalled vote, or observed panel response;
- shares for finalist A, finalist B, abstention, blank/null, undecided, and refusal, without silently redistributing missing categories;
- exact candidate slate and question wording;
- whether figures are among all eligible respondents or only expressed choices;
- endorsements or major events that occurred during fieldwork;
- revisions to weighting, turnout filters, or candidate configuration.

Comparisons should remain within the same institute and design when possible. Cross-institute convergence can strengthen an inference, but averaging incompatible “non-expressed” definitions or candidate slates produces false precision. Territorial results can test whether a proposed story is plausible, yet aggregate departmental change cannot prove individual movement: different people may enter and leave the electorate, an error known as ecological inference.

## Uncertainty specific to 2027

The largest uncertainty comes before any transfer percentage: who qualifies for round two. As of 10 July 2026, no official 2027 candidate list exists. Prospective coalitions are still choosing candidates and selection methods. A hypothetical matrix can be informative about acceptability or rejection, but its political meaning is conditional on a first round that may never occur.

The second uncertainty is compositional. A candidate who becomes the focal *vote utile* can absorb voters from adjacent first-round options. Their apparent electorate then changes just as analysts begin estimating its transfers. Poll-to-poll variation can reflect this changing mixture rather than persuasion between finalists.

The third is turnout. Early surveys ask people to imagine abstention many months ahead. Expressed certainty at that horizon measures present engagement as much as eventual behavior. Runoff turnout also depends on the first-round outcome and the inter-round campaign, neither of which can be observed in advance.

The fourth is coalition asymmetry. Formal party support may secure activists, elected officials, finance, and legitimacy while moving relatively few voters; an endorsement-free strategic convergence can move many voters without creating a governing coalition. The presidential transfer matrix therefore should not be used as a shortcut to predict the subsequent National Assembly majority. [[From the Élysée to a National Assembly majority]] is a separate question.

## Why it matters

Qualification is only the first coordination test in a two-round election. A candidate can reach the runoff with a cohesive minority and then fail to expand; another can enter with a less enthusiastic base but become the acceptable blocking choice for several eliminated electorates. Analysts who add first-round party percentages miss abstention, blank voting, candidate-specific rejection, and the fact that strategic voters have already crossed party lines.

Transfer evidence also shapes the first round. Claims that one contender has larger *réserves de voix* can influence endorsements and *vote utile*, thereby changing the electorate whose later transfer is being forecast. This feedback makes methodological restraint politically important. A noisy subgroup table can become a narrative of “electability,” while a large non-expressed share can be hidden behind a crisp two-candidate topline.

For 2027, the defensible approach is conditional and plural: publish matrices for exact pairings, retain every non-expression category, identify whether the first-round base is observed or hypothetical, and treat endorsements as inputs rather than commands. Historical patterns establish possibilities, not entitlements. Voters transfer themselves.

## Open questions

- Which institutes will publish stable 2027 pairing series with subgroup bases large enough to interpret first-round electorates?
- Will notices distinguish abstention, blank/null voting, undecidedness, and refusal, or combine them as *non exprimé*?
- How much will late *vote utile* change the composition of the leading first-round electorates before transfer estimates become politically salient?
- Can a high-quality recontact panel distinguish genuine individual movement from changing cross-sectional composition without excessive attrition?
- Which endorsements will affect expressed runoff choice, and which will mainly affect turnout or blank voting?
