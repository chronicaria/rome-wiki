French presidential-election registration is a continuously maintained administrative status with a pre-election cutoff, narrow statutory exceptions, and judicial correction routes—not a measure of whether a registered person will actually vote.

Up: [[2027 French presidential election]]

## The registration question

To vote in a French presidential election, a person must be an eligible French elector and appear on the relevant electoral list. The presidential franchise therefore begins with two distinct questions: does the person possess the right to vote, and is that right attached to the correct list and polling station? Registration answers the second question. It does not show that the elector will turn out, cast a valid ballot, or support any candidate.

This distinction matters in interpreting election statistics. The registered electorate is the denominator used for official turnout and abstention rates. People who are legally eligible but not registered are outside that denominator; people registered at an obsolete address may face practical obstacles or vote in their former commune; people who abstain remain registered. Analysts should therefore keep **eligibility**, **registration**, **access on election day**, and **turnout** separate.

The ordinary rules for municipal electoral lists also structure the presidential election. Under Article 3 of the Constitution, voters are adult French nationals of both sexes enjoying civil and political rights. Citizens of other European Union states may vote in French municipal and European Parliament elections under separate complementary lists, but not in a French presidential election. A foreign EU citizen's presence on a complementary list is consequently not presidential registration.

## A permanent register, locally administered

Since 2019, France has used the *répertoire électoral unique* (REU), a single permanent electoral register managed by INSEE. Municipalities and consulates still decide many applications and removals, but the national register propagates changes and helps prevent a person from remaining on multiple ordinary lists. Each voter has a national electoral number. The institutional division is useful: the mayor handles local decisions; INSEE manages the REU and certain automatic entries and removals; a municipal control commission reviews the list; and the judicial court resolves disputes.

An elector normally registers in a commune on the basis of a legally recognized connection. The usual ground is actual domicile. Other grounds include at least six months' residence, prescribed local-tax connections, a qualifying managerial or ownership connection to a locally taxed company, or compulsory residence as a public official. The detailed proof depends on the claimed ground. For most applicants the practical file is simpler: proof of identity and nationality, proof of connection to the commune—often a recent residence document—and the application itself.

French citizens abroad face a real choice of list, not an extra vote. They may register on a consular electoral list to vote abroad in presidential elections, or retain or seek a qualifying communal registration in France and vote there, potentially by proxy. The REU is designed to prevent simultaneous effective registration on both a consular list and a communal list. Moving abroad, returning to France, or changing consular district therefore calls for a status check rather than an assumption that a separate consular administrative registration has automatically placed the person on the right electoral list.

## The ordinary deadline

Electoral lists are permanent, so an eligible person can submit an application throughout the year. But an application must be effective before a particular election. Article L.17 of the Electoral Code sets the ordinary deadline at the **sixth Friday before the first round**. The implementing rule gives online applicants a slightly earlier operational deadline: the teleprocedure closes at midnight on the **sixth Wednesday before the election**. A paper application may be filed at the mairie, personally or through a representative holding a written mandate, or sent by post; the sixth-Friday rule governs the ordinary statutory cutoff.

The Interior Ministry currently schedules the 2027 first round for Sunday, April 18. On that published date and under current law, the relative rules yield an online deadline of Wednesday, March 10 and a general deadline of Friday, March 12. These are calculated dates rather than an election-specific administrative notice, so they must be rechecked against the convocation decree and official guidance. Public messaging should state both deadlines because simply saying “six weeks before” can be wrong by several days and can obscure the difference between channels.

The application date is not itself proof of acceptance. The mayor verifies eligibility and the link to the commune and generally decides within five days after receiving a complete request. An applicant should use the official electoral-status service to confirm the commune and polling station after processing. The electoral card is useful but is not what creates the right: appearance on the list is decisive, and the official status lookup is more reliable than possession of an old card.

## Application routes

The standard routes are:

1. **Online.** The Interior Ministry's registration service accepts digitized supporting documents. For a general election the online cutoff is the sixth Wednesday at midnight, local legal time.
2. **At the mairie.** The applicant presents the French electoral-registration form (Cerfa 12669), identity evidence, and proof of the claimed municipal connection.
3. **By post.** The same form and documentary evidence can be sent to the mairie. The elector should not treat dispatch at the last minute as equivalent to confirmed timely receipt.
4. **Through an authorized representative.** Article R.5 permits personal filing or filing through a representative with a written mandate.
5. **At a competent consulate or embassy for a consular list.** French citizens abroad can register through consular channels; registration with the register of French citizens abroad and electoral-list status are related administrative processes but should still be checked separately.

For an ordinary domicile application, the recurring evidentiary pair is identity plus a recent address document. Applicants relying on tax, residence, company, official-residence, homelessness, or expatriate provisions need the documents matching that legal basis. The registration service and mairie determine sufficiency; campaign organizations can explain the process but cannot adjudicate an application.

## Automatic registration is limited

The Interior Ministry's general page warns that registration normally requires an application. Two large categories qualify that statement.

First, a French citizen reaching 18 is normally registered automatically if the authorities hold the necessary information from the compulsory citizen census completed from age 16. The young person should nevertheless verify the result. A missing census, late census, move after census, inaccurate civil-status record, or processing problem can prevent the expected entry or place it in the wrong commune. Automatic registration is therefore a legal-administrative mechanism, not a reason to skip verification.

The age rule accommodates an election between birthdays. A person who turns 18 no later than the day before the first round can vote in both rounds if otherwise qualified and registered. A person who turns 18 between rounds may vote in the second round. This is why official guidance expresses majority by reference to the eve of the relevant round rather than by saying all voters had to be 18 when applications closed.

Second, a person who acquires French nationality after 2018 is generally entered automatically through the information transmitted to INSEE. Again, the person should verify list placement. Naturalization does not make a non-French resident's prior complementary-list registration equivalent to presidential registration; the presidential entitlement follows French nationality and the REU entry.

Automatic entry should not be generalized to movers. An already registered elector who changes address must report the move and, when the move is to another commune, apply in the new commune. Even a move within one commune should be reported because it may change the assigned polling station. Updating a general government address service, tax file, postal forwarding, or identity document does not safely substitute for checking the electoral record.

## Address changes and the old-list problem

A move does not create a free choice of polling station. Before the ordinary deadline, the elector applies in the new commune using the new qualifying address. Registration there normally causes removal from the former list through the REU. If the elector remains validly registered in the former commune and has not been removed, voting may still be possible there, in person or by proxy, but this is not a guarantee: once the former connection is lost, the former municipality can initiate removal after an adversarial process.

The correct response to a move is therefore: check current status, apply or update where legally connected, and check again after processing. An old electoral card or knowledge of the old polling station is not enough. Nor does a proxy repair non-registration: a proxy lets another registered elector cast the principal's ballot at the principal's polling station; it cannot create an absent entry on the list.

Article L.30 creates a late-registration path for certain people whose move occurred after the ordinary cutoff because of a professional transfer or specified related circumstances. This is an exception, not a general moving grace period. A purely personal move after the sixth Friday ordinarily does not qualify merely because returning to the old polling place is inconvenient.

## Narrow late-registration exceptions

Article L.30 permits specified applicants to register after the ordinary cutoff, with the request generally due no later than the **tenth day before the election**. The categories must be read precisely. They include:

- public officials and agents of public administrations transferred or compulsorily retired after the ordinary deadline, and members of their household domiciled with them;
- military personnel returning to civilian life after the deadline;
- people who move for professional reasons after the deadline, together with household members domiciled with them;
- French citizens who acquire nationality after the deadline;
- people who recover the exercise of voting rights after the deadline;
- young French citizens who reach voting age after the ordinary deadline, including those becoming eligible only for the second round under the age rule.

The statutory text and current form should be consulted for the exact category and evidence. “I missed the deadline,” an ordinary late personal move, lack of awareness, travel, illness, or loss of an electoral card is not by itself an L.30 ground. The late route remains an application: evidence must connect the applicant to one of the enumerated events, and timing matters.

Under Article L.31, the mayor decides a special application within three days and immediately notifies the elector and INSEE; the mayor publishes the resulting registration decisions no later than five days before polling. A refusal can be brought before the judicial court under the accelerated Electoral Code procedure. Because the route is compressed, an affected person should contact the mairie promptly and retain proof of submission and of the qualifying event.

## Correcting the list and appealing decisions

Several different correction paths coexist. Conflating them can send an elector to the wrong institution.

### Challenge to a mayor's refusal or removal

The mayor examines applications and may remove an elector who no longer satisfies the local attachment condition, but removal normally requires notice and an opportunity to respond. An interested elector contesting the mayor's refusal to register or decision to remove must first make the mandatory prior administrative appeal to the municipal **control commission**. Under Article L.18, that appeal is lodged within five days of notification. The commission can overturn the mayor's decision.

If the commission rejects the appeal, or does not decide within the prescribed period, the elector may go to the judicial court under the accelerated Electoral Code procedure. The official Cerfa 16038 and its notice structure this challenge. The short deadlines mean that retaining the decision notice and its date is important.

### Commission review and third-party challenges

The control commission also supervises the list and meets before an election. It can, on its own initiative and by the required majority, reform mayoral registration or removal decisions and register an omitted elector or remove an improperly listed one, subject to procedural safeguards. An elector registered in the commune may also challenge another elector's registration before the judicial court under Article L.20; the challenged elector must be able to present observations. This third-party route is distinct from an applicant's appeal against their own refusal.

### Material omission or procedurally defective removal

Article L.20 provides a direct judicial safeguard for a person omitted because of a purely material error or removed without observance of required formalities. The person can seek judicial registration up to election day. This is not a blanket judicial extension of the application deadline. The applicant must show the kind of administrative omission or defective removal contemplated by the statute, rather than simply an untimely ordinary application. The official Cerfa 15878 addresses this route.

Courts can also correct certain civil-status or list errors. On election day, polling-station officials apply the list available to them; they do not have a general power to add a person on the strength of an old card or an assertion that registration should have occurred. A qualifying court order is therefore materially different from an informal complaint at the desk.

## Why it matters for 2027

Registration administration can affect participation without explaining all abstention. Young adults, frequent movers, newly naturalized citizens, people returning from abroad, and citizens with unstable housing are more exposed to records that lag their circumstances. Digital registration reduces transaction costs for some voters, while documentary requirements, online access, and compressed appeals can still impose friction. These are access mechanisms; they should not be translated automatically into claims about partisan intent.

For the [[2027 French presidential election]], three dates deserve prominent publication and later confirmation in election-specific guidance: the April 18 first round, the calculated March 10 online deadline, and the calculated March 12 ordinary deadline. A fourth calculated date—April 8, the tenth day before polling—matters only for valid L.30 cases and should always be labeled exceptional. Neutral public information should also link directly to the official status checker, because checking catches both non-registration and wrong polling-station assignment.

Campaign reporting should distinguish the stock of registered voters from additions, removals, and corrections to the REU. A rise in registered electors may reflect demography, automatic registration, nationality acquisition, or administrative cleanup; it is not itself mobilization. Likewise, turnout is calculated from the registered list and measures ballots cast relative to registrants, while non-registration describes eligible citizens absent from that list. Both can depress participation relative to the eligible population, but through different mechanisms.

The most reliable practical sequence is simple: verify early; register or update through an official channel; preserve the receipt; verify again; and use the statutory correction route quickly if the status is wrong. That sequence recognizes both the convenience and the fallibility of a permanent national register.
