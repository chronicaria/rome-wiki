France enters 2027 with durable electoral geographies, but territory is a context in which social coalitions form—not a collection of fixed regional vote banks.

## Why it matters

The French presidency is decided by one nationwide total. A candidate receives no electoral prize for “winning” a region or *département*. Geography nevertheless determines how a national percentage is assembled: which electorates are available in the first round, where turnout can expand or contract, which adjacent candidacies compete for the same voters, and how a finalist can—or cannot—grow in the runoff. It also reveals whether a presidential coalition has the local depth needed for a later legislative contest.

For 2027, the useful question is therefore not “which colour will each department be?” It is whether the coalitions visible in the 2017 and 2022 returns endure after changes in candidates, participation, alliances, economic conditions, and campaign issues. The map supplies a baseline, not a forecast.

## Read territory at the right scale

Three cautions should come before any map.

First, administrative units are containers, not explanations. A *département* can include a large city, commuter municipalities, industrial towns, affluent suburbs, and remote rural communes. Its average may conceal more than it reveals. Commune-level returns recover local variation, but very small communes produce volatile percentages from few ballots. Polling-station results offer still greater resolution while increasing the danger of reading neighbourhood composition as individual behaviour.

Second, “urban” and “rural” are not binary social identities. INSEE's 2020 **aires d'attraction des villes** classify functional urban areas around poles of population and employment. Their surrounding *couronnes* comprise communes from which at least 15 percent of employed residents work in the pole. This distinguishes a city centre from its commuting belt and separates both from communes outside a city's attraction. It is more informative than treating every low-density commune as isolated countryside. Yet it still does not measure a voter's occupation, education, income, age, housing tenure, or attitude.

Third, aggregate returns do not establish why individuals voted. If a candidate scores strongly in a poorer department, it does not follow that every poor resident supported that candidate. This is the ecological fallacy. Territorial analysis should be paired with representative surveys and individual-level participation studies, while remembering that survey vote recall and small subgroup estimates have their own uncertainty. Official returns tell us **where** votes were counted; they do not alone tell us **who** cast them or **why**.

## The three large first-round coalitions visible in 2022

The 2022 first round concentrated around Emmanuel Macron (27.85 percent of valid votes), Marine Le Pen (23.15 percent), and Jean-Luc Mélenchon (21.95 percent). Those close national totals were produced by sharply different territorial combinations.

### Macron: metropolitan cores, the prosperous west, and older moderate electorates

Macron's coalition joined several geographies that should not be collapsed into “the cities.” He performed strongly in Paris and many affluent or highly educated metropolitan municipalities, where pro-European and professional electorates were prominent. He also retained a broad western base, particularly across Brittany and Pays de la Loire, areas historically less favourable to the far right than the northeast and Mediterranean southeast. In addition, prosperous suburbs, some border regions, and many older electorates supplied support beyond metropolitan centres.

This coalition was geographically broad enough to lead the first round nationally, but breadth did not mean uniformity. The social basis of a wealthy inner suburb is not the same as that of an ageing rural commune in the west. Both could produce a high Macron share through different mixtures of policy preference, incumbent approval, strategic voting, attachment to European integration, or rejection of alternatives. A successor to Macron cannot simply inherit those votes by occupying an abstract “centre.” Candidate credibility and the menu of rivals will determine how much of each component remains coordinated.

The comparison with 2017 also matters. Macron had already built an unusually dispersed first-round coalition in his first campaign, drawing former centre-left, centrist, and centre-right voters while the traditional governing parties weakened. By 2022 the coalition had become more clearly associated with incumbency and attracted substantial older and more affluent support. This consolidation improved first-round efficiency but also created a vulnerability for 2027: without an eligible incumbent, several candidates may claim different territorial pieces of the same inheritance.

### Le Pen: deindustrialized northeast, Mediterranean arc, small towns, and expanding rural reach

Le Pen's strongest metropolitan pattern remained anchored in two long-standing zones: the northern and northeastern belt shaped by industrial decline and working-class political realignment, and the Mediterranean southeast where the far right has deeper roots connected to immigration politics, security, and the post-Algerian-war settlement. These are not identical electorates. Their shared ballot choice arises from distinct regional histories.

By 2022 the RN coalition extended beyond those historic bastions. Le Pen advanced across many small towns, lower-density communes, and outer belts where dependence on cars, distance from services, constrained household budgets, and distrust of governing institutions can combine. But “rural France votes RN” is inaccurate. Rural territories differ by region, economic structure, religious and political history, age, and proximity to dynamic employment centres. Western rural France remained more resistant in many places; some southern and central rural areas were competitive among Le Pen, Macron, and the radical left.

The change from 2017 to 2022 was not merely a darker shade on the same map. Le Pen increased her first-round national score from 21.30 to 23.15 percent despite competition from Éric Zemmour, and her second-round share rose from 33.90 to 41.45 percent. Her normalization strategy and emphasis on purchasing power helped widen the coalition, while Zemmour drew a more affluent and ideologically hard-line segment in parts of the southeast and major cities. The 2027 territorial question is whether the RN can recombine these right-populist electorates while continuing to penetrate areas once protected by traditional right or left local networks.

### Mélenchon: large-city popular districts, younger urban voters, and overseas strength

Mélenchon's 2022 coalition was unusually concentrated but nationally consequential. He dominated or performed strongly in many dense, younger, diverse, and working-class municipalities in the Paris region and other large metropolitan areas. Seine-Saint-Denis was emblematic, but the pattern extended to popular districts and university-centred electorates in multiple cities. His late strategic rise also compressed much of the left vote around the candidate viewed as most capable of reaching the runoff.

This was not simply an educated metropolitan-left map. Mélenchon combined highly educated younger voters with residents of lower-income, immigrant-origin, and working-class urban neighbourhoods. Those groups can share concerns about inequality, discrimination, public services, climate, or opposition to the far right without constituting one stable sociological bloc. Their participation rates and relationships to party organizations differ substantially.

Overseas France formed another indispensable component. Mélenchon led the 2022 first round in several overseas territories, including Guadeloupe, Martinique, and Guyane. In the runoff, many of these same territories gave large majorities among valid votes to Le Pen. That reversal is not evidence that a coherent Mélenchon-to-Le Pen ideological bloc exists. It is consistent with a runoff shaped by rejection of Macron, local anger, candidate elimination, and high abstention. Transfers must be measured among actual first-round voters and nonvoters; territorial totals cannot reveal individual paths.

## Overseas France is not one electorate

Any account confined to metropolitan France is incomplete. The overseas departments and collectivities differ in legal status, economic structure, demography, relations with the state, and party system. Cost of living, public services, unemployment, health policy, institutional autonomy, and memories of colonial rule can carry different weight than in metropolitan debate. A national candidate's standard left–right position is therefore an unreliable guide to local support.

Participation is also central. INSEE's study of all four presidential and legislative rounds in 2022 found systematic abstention of about 30 percent or more among registered residents in La Réunion, Guadeloupe, and Martinique, about twice the France-wide level in the study's resident population. That measure is not the same as abstention at a single presidential round, but it shows why percentages of valid votes must never be presented without turnout. A dramatic overseas majority may represent a smaller share of registered voters than its headline score suggests.

The lesson for 2027 is twofold. Campaigns need territory-specific organization and credible positions rather than late symbolic visits. Analysts need to report each territory separately, state the denominator, and resist interpreting an overseas runoff protest vote as a permanent attachment to the finalist who received it.

## The west, the northeast, and the Mediterranean remain real—but not immutable

French electoral geography contains durable historical layers. Much of the west retained comparatively strong Christian-democratic, moderate, and anti-RN traditions even as religious practice and party labels changed. The industrial north and northeast experienced the collapse of older Communist and Socialist structures alongside factory closure and labour-market insecurity, enabling a long RN advance. The Mediterranean far right developed through a different history involving the Algerian war, migration, repatriate settlement, and local party implantation.

Durability does not mean territorial destiny. Party organizations dissolve; populations move; metropolitan labour and housing markets spread; cohorts replace one another; crises reorder issue priorities. The collapse of the Parti socialiste and Les Républicains in the 2022 presidential first round showed how rapidly established territorial networks can cease to deliver presidential votes, even when local elected officials and municipal institutions remain. Conversely, presidential support built around one personality may lack candidates and activists beneath the national level.

The 2017–2022 comparison is therefore best understood as realignment within inherited landscapes. Macron stabilized a centre spanning metropolitan winners, much of the west, and older moderate voters. Le Pen broadened geographically beyond the RN's oldest strongholds. Mélenchon consolidated a left pole in major urban popular districts and overseas territories. These were coalitions assembled under a particular candidate slate. None is guaranteed to survive the removal, replacement, or multiplication of its leading candidate.

## Participation is part of the coalition

Maps of valid votes omit citizens who abstain, cast blank ballots, or are not registered where they live. That omission can reverse the interpretation of a territory. A candidate can increase a percentage because more supporters mobilized, because opponents demobilized, or because the valid-vote denominator contracted.

INSEE's linked-register study found that across the presidential and legislative elections of 2022, 36 percent of registered voters in its scope voted in every round, 48 percent voted intermittently, and 16 percent voted in none. Participation rose with education and standard of living; systematic abstention was 30 percent among people without a diploma and 10 percent among the highly educated. Younger adults voted much more intermittently than older voters. These are participation patterns across four rounds, not candidate preferences, but they identify a key territorial mechanism: places with younger, poorer, and less credentialed populations often contain larger pools of episodic voters.

For a 2027 coalition, mobilizing those pools may matter as much as transferring votes from another candidate. This is especially true for the left in popular urban districts and overseas France, but it also applies to RN-leaning working-class areas. A poll of stated first-round preferences does not automatically capture who will register correctly, turn out, or return two weeks later.

## What could change the map before 2027

Five mechanisms deserve monitoring.

1. **Candidate substitution.** Macron cannot seek a third consecutive term under the Constitution's two-consecutive-term limit. A centrist successor may preserve affluent metropolitan support but lose older, western, centre-left, or centre-right components in different directions.
2. **Right-wing coordination.** The RN's territorial ceiling depends partly on whether a distinct conventional-right or Zemmour-aligned candidacy remains competitive. Elite alliance does not guarantee voter addition: local histories and candidate evaluations shape transfers.
3. **Left selection and turnout.** A single left candidate could reduce first-round fragmentation, but different nominees would mobilize metropolitan graduates, popular suburbs, smaller cities, and overseas voters unevenly. “Unity” changes both arithmetic and participation.
4. **Material conditions.** Purchasing power, pensions, farming, industrial employment, housing, transport, health access, and public services have geographically uneven exposure. National issue salience becomes territorial through local circumstances; no issue belongs permanently to one bloc.
5. **Post-2024 party implantation.** European, legislative, and municipal contests can strengthen candidate networks, normalize alliances, or expose weak local supply. Their electoral formulas differ from the presidency, so results are evidence about organization and mood, not direct presidential forecasts.

## A disciplined 2027 territorial dashboard

A credible dashboard should preserve several denominators and scales rather than publishing a single winner map.

- Compare 2027 with both 2022 and 2017 using the same round, candidate-family definition, and territorial boundaries wherever possible.
- Report registered voters, turnout, blank and invalid ballots, valid votes, and candidate shares. Separate percentage change from raw-vote change.
- Use commune-level returns joined to INSEE's density grid and *aires d'attraction des villes*, distinguishing poles, crowns, and areas outside urban attraction. Do not label all crowns “rural.”
- Treat overseas territories and French voters abroad separately before reintegrating them into the national total.
- Pair aggregate results with high-quality individual surveys. Mark any inference that combines the two.
- Test whether a pattern survives controls for age, education, occupation, income, and regional history. A descriptive map is not a causal model.
- In the runoff, distinguish endorsement, reported transfer, turnout, and final territorial result. They answer different questions.

The most informative sign of change will not necessarily be a department switching its leading candidate. It may be a turnout increase in Seine-Saint-Denis, a centrist loss across western small towns, an RN advance in previously resistant Atlantic communes, a conventional-right recovery in affluent crowns, or a different overseas first-to-second-round pattern. These shifts can move the national total before the broad regional map looks different.

## Limits and implications

Territorial coalitions are durable because residential sorting, economic structure, institutions, and political memory change slowly. They are mutable because the presidential ballot offers a changing set of individuals and because participation is episodic. The 2017 and 2022 maps identify where each coalition has recently been efficient; they do not allocate 2027 votes in advance.

Nor does territorial breadth automatically produce a governing mandate. The presidential contest aggregates one national vote, while a legislative election divides France into 577 constituencies. A coalition concentrated in major cities can be efficient nationally but poorly distributed for legislative runoffs; a coalition spread through small towns can lead many communes without assembling an Assembly majority. Local candidates, withdrawals, and alliances matter. See [[From the Élysée to a National Assembly majority]].

The safest 2027 interpretation is consequently conditional: ask which components a candidate can mobilize in round one, which territories contain expandable turnout, which rival's elimination opens space, and whether second-round growth reflects support or rejection. Geography narrows plausible stories. It does not replace evidence about voters.

- Can a reproducible commune-level model separate regional history, urban-functional position, and population composition using official returns and INSEE data?
- Which original post-election surveys provide adequately powered estimates of first-to-second-round transfers in each overseas territory?
- How will the 2026 municipal results alter local organization and turnout capacity without being mistaken for a presidential vote forecast?
