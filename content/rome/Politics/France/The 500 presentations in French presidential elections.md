To appear on a French presidential ballot, a prospective candidate must obtain at least 500 valid, public presentations from eligible officeholders, distributed across at least 30 departments or overseas collectivities.

Up: [[2027 French presidential election]]

## A qualification rule, not a primary

The familiar phrase *les 500 parrainages*—the 500 endorsements—can mislead an English-speaking reader. The governing law calls them *présentations*: formal presentations of a prospective candidate by specified public officeholders. A presenter does not cast a vote in a party primary, and the process does not measure mass public support. It is a legal gateway between political aspiration and inclusion on the official presidential ballot.

The distinction matters especially when following the [[2027 French presidential election]]. A person can announce a campaign, lead polls, win a party nomination, assemble staff, or receive extensive media coverage without yet being an official candidate. The legally operative list is established by the *Conseil constitutionnel* (Constitutional Council) after it has checked the presentations and other candidacy requirements. Until that decision, “candidate” may be descriptively convenient but is not the same as formally qualified.

Article 3 of the organic provisions in the law of 6 November 1962 supplies the basic test. A person needs presentations from at least 500 eligible citizens holding one of the listed mandates or offices. Among the signatories must be elected officials from at least 30 *départements* or overseas collectivities, and more than one tenth of the valid signatories may not be elected from the same department or equivalent collectivity. A campaign cannot qualify by concentrating its support in a small number of strongholds.

This is therefore a compound threshold:

1. at least 500 presentations must be valid;
2. they must include presenters from at least 30 departments or overseas collectivities; and
3. no single department or equivalent collectivity may account for more than one tenth of the valid signatories being assessed.

The last rule is often compressed into “a maximum of 50 per department.” Fifty is the maximum when exactly 500 valid presentations are considered. It is not a free-standing statutory cap: with more than 500 valid signatories, the one-tenth ceiling changes with the denominator. The Conseil constitutionnel's 2012 commentary describes the rule as applying to the signatories, not merely to an arbitrarily selected block of 500. Campaigns seek more than 500 forms because invalidations, postal delay, and an unbalanced geographic distribution can make a nominal total insufficient. The law sets the threshold; prudent collection strategy supplies a margin.

## Who can make a presentation

The eligible body is broad but not universal. Ordinary voters do not sign these forms merely in their capacity as citizens. The current consolidated text names members of Parliament; members of regional and departmental bodies; members of several territorial and overseas assemblies; mayors and certain delegated or arrondissement mayors; Paris councillors; councillors in the Assembly of French Citizens Abroad; and presidents of consular councils. It also includes specified presidents of intercommunal deliberative bodies and executives, the heads of certain overseas institutions, and French members of the European Parliament elected in France.

Mayotte requires a transition note rather than a timeless label. The 2025 organic law creates an Assembly of Mayotte and amends the presidential-presentation list to name its councillors, but article 5 postpones that amendment until the first general renewal of departmental councils after the reform enters into force. That renewal is expected after the 2027 presidential election. For 2027 verification, a reporter should inspect the version of Article 3 legally in force on the date of presentation rather than assuming that the future “Assembly of Mayotte” wording already governs. The older rule refers to members of Mayotte's departmental council.

This list is technical because French territorial government is technical. A *département* is an intermediate territorial unit with an elected departmental council; it is neither exactly a U.S. county nor a province. A *collectivité d'outre-mer* is an overseas collectivity with a constitutionally and statutorily defined status. An *établissement public de coopération intercommunale*, often encountered through its president, is an intermunicipal cooperation structure rather than another general-purpose layer identical to a commune. The statute enumerates mandates instead of creating a loose category of “local politicians.”

Mayors are central in practice because France has thousands of communes and therefore a very large pool of municipal officeholders. But eligibility should not be confused with partisanship. Many mayors, particularly in small communes, have no national party label. Their institutional role gives them the capacity to present someone; it does not mean that the state treats them as party delegates.

Each eligible person may present only one prospective candidate. That exclusivity raises the stakes of timing and outreach. A signed presentation cannot be reassigned to a second contender, and Article 3 states that a presentation, once sent, cannot be withdrawn. A campaign that persuades an officeholder early has secured a scarce legal act, although only the Constitutional Council can validate it.

## How the process works

The operational machinery is set out in Decree no. 2001-213 of 8 March 2001. The administration prints official forms and matching postal envelopes in models determined by the Constitutional Council. In a regularly scheduled election, the relevant public authorities distribute them to eligible presenters after the decree convening the electors is published. The presenter completes and personally signs the form, identifies the mandate relied upon, and sends it to the Constitutional Council through an authorized postal operator.

The form is not supposed to pass through a campaign as a bearer instrument. The 2016 reform made direct postal transmission the normal rule, responding to the earlier practice in which campaigns sometimes collected completed forms and delivered batches. Direct transmission strengthens the idea that presentation is the personal act of the officeholder and reduces a campaign's physical control over completed forms. The Constitutional Council's review of the 2016 law nevertheless preserved room for force-majeure circumstances that gravely disrupt dispatch or delivery near the deadline.

Timing is unforgiving. Under the current Article 3, presentations in an ordinary election must reach the Council by 6 p.m. Paris time on the sixth Friday before the first round. Mailing by the deadline is not enough: the presentation must arrive by it. The implementing decree expressly tells presenters to allow for normal postal transit. Special, compressed deadlines apply if the election follows a presidential vacancy or a definitive declaration of incapacity under Article 7 of the Constitution.

The Council may conduct whatever checks it considers useful. It verifies the presenter's eligibility, the singularity and regularity of the act, the prospective candidate's consent, and the other candidacy conditions before establishing the list. A form that a campaign counts internally is therefore not necessarily a valid presentation. Conversely, public reporting should not treat a campaign's claimed tally as the Council's certified tally.

The Council publishes the names and official capacities of people whose presentations have been validated at least twice a week as forms arrive. The complete public record makes the procedure unusually visible. The official candidate list is then published in the *Journal officiel de la République française* no later than the fourth Friday before the first round. A person who has received a presentation has a tightly limited right to challenge establishment of the list: under the decree, a complaint must reach the Council before the end of the day following publication, and the Council rules without delay.

## A verification checklist for 2027 claims

Claims about “having the 500” should be decomposed before publication. The following checks prevent a campaign's organizational estimate from being mistaken for legal qualification:

1. **Identify the category.** Is the number a count of verbal promises, signed forms, forms reportedly mailed, or presentations validated and published by the Conseil constitutionnel? Only the last category is official evidence that a presentation has passed review.
2. **Use the live Council publication.** Record the publication date and exact validated count. Do not substitute a campaign dashboard, press interview, or media reconstruction for the Council's list.
3. **Check territorial spread.** Confirm at least 30 qualifying departments or overseas units and test the one-tenth rule against the valid signatories being assessed; do not impose a fixed 50 ceiling when the total exceeds 500.
4. **Check the presenter's mandate.** Use the version of Article 3 in force at the time. Territorial reorganizations—especially the deferred Mayotte transition—can make a familiar office label misleading.
5. **Check singularity and finality.** Each authorized officeholder can present one person, and a transmitted presentation cannot be withdrawn. A later political disclaimer does not erase a valid form.
6. **Check receipt before the deadline.** In an ordinary election, the Council must receive the form by 6 p.m. on the sixth Friday before the first round. A postmark or campaign possession is not enough.
7. **Wait for the candidate list.** Passing 500 is necessary but not identical to being included on the ballot; consent and the other candidacy requirements still matter, and the Conseil establishes the official list.

The checklist also disciplines negative claims. A contender below 500 in an interim publication has not necessarily failed, because more valid forms may arrive before the deadline. A person above 500 has not necessarily demonstrated broad voter support, because presentations are institutional acts by officeholders rather than a representative public sample.

## Why France uses this filter

The presentations system predates the present 500 threshold. The 1962 framework initially required 100 presentations from a smaller set of officeholders, with representation from at least ten departments, and kept presenters' names confidential. In its formal observations after the 1974 election, the Constitutional Council said two objectives had to be held together: candidacies should have a genuinely national base, and every real current of opinion should still be able to produce a candidate. It recommended more presentations, signatories from at least one quarter of departments and territories, official forms, and publication of presenters.

Organic Law no. 76-528 of 18 June 1976 implemented a legislative version of that program, not the Council's wording verbatim. It increased the threshold from 100 to 500, required representation across at least 30 departments or overseas territories, imposed the one-tenth concentration rule, broadened the eligible-presenter pool, and introduced publication of presenters. This chronology matters: “1974 created the 500 rule” is wrong. The 1974 observations recommended reform; Parliament enacted the new threshold in 1976, before the 1981 election.

Its official logic is not that elected presenters should select the president. Voters do that by universal suffrage. Rather, the filter is meant to require a minimum measure of institutional seriousness and national reach before a contender enters a first round with major legal, administrative, media, and financial consequences. It aims to deter purely fanciful candidacies while allowing significant currents of opinion to compete.

That objective is more subtle than limiting the number of candidates. French presidential elections have often had crowded first rounds. The mechanism asks whether a campaign can build a geographically dispersed network among officeholders; it does not demand proof that the campaign can win, cross a polling threshold, or command a large party. A small party with disciplined local relationships may qualify. A nationally famous figure without an effective territorial organization may struggle.

The presentations consequently connect the presidential contest to France's local political fabric. Direct election makes the president a national office, but ballot access depends partly on thousands of holders of subnational mandates. Outreach to mayors is not peripheral campaign theater: it is organizational work necessary to transform a political project into a legally receivable candidacy.

## Publicity and the meaning of support

The French legal term *présentation* is also important because *parrainage* sounds like political sponsorship. A valid presentation plainly helps a person gain access to the ballot, and presenters know their choices will be public. Yet the act is not legally identical to endorsement of the prospective candidate's program, party, or fitness for office. An elected official may present someone out of substantive support, a belief that a current deserves representation, a commitment to pluralism, a local relationship, or some combination of these.

This ambiguity is not a defect that can be defined away. Campaigns reasonably advertise presentation totals as evidence of organization. Journalists reasonably scrutinize who enabled a candidacy. Presenters may reasonably insist that their act protected electoral choice rather than announced how they will vote. The most accurate English phrasing is therefore “presentation,” followed by an explanation that the act is commonly called a sponsorship or endorsement but is a distinct legal instrument.

Publicity has changed over time. Before the 2016 reform, the regime did not publish every valid presentation in the same way. The organic law of 25 April 2016 required publication, as validation proceeds, of the name and capacity of every valid presenter. The Constitutional Council upheld this greater transparency, reasoning that voters could be informed about the process and that the legislature's choices did not unconstitutionally violate pluralism or equality.

The reform makes informal assurances harder to keep private. It also prevents a misleading impression created under the former system, when only a selected subset could be publicized for candidates who received more than 500 presentations. Today, a searchable public trail can reveal the breadth, concentration, and local composition of a campaign's institutional network.

## The recurring controversy

Criticism focuses on whether a gatekeeping system operated by officeholders fairly preserves political pluralism. A contender with measurable voter support can fail if eligible officials refuse to be publicly associated with the candidacy. Public attribution may create concern among anti-establishment, stigmatized, or organizationally weak movements that potential presenters will be treated as supporters. A mayor who regards presentation as a neutral democratic service may still anticipate that others will read it as endorsement.

The evidence supports a narrower claim about pressure than campaign rhetoric often makes. In its post-2012 observations, the Constitutional Council said campaign collection of forms could compromise the personal and voluntary character of presentation and referred to sometimes strong pressure on mayors of rural communes; the 2016 Senate report cited that concern when supporting direct postal transmission by presenters. Parliamentary debates also record elected officials arguing both that publicity creates pressure and that public responsibility requires transparency. These are official records of an institutional problem and competing judgments, not a causal estimate of how many small-town mayors declined to present a candidate. The public lists alone cannot establish that rate or identify why an individual abstained.

The opposing concern is that secrecy would reduce accountability for an official act and obscure how a presidential candidacy gained access. Presenters act because they hold public mandates, not because they are a random sample of private citizens. From this perspective, publication allows voters to judge the conduct of their representatives and makes campaign networks visible. It also reduces the scope for campaigns or presenters to claim different things privately and publicly.

The Constitutional Council has generally treated the balance as one for the legislature, subject to constitutional limits. In its 2012 decision on the older publication rules, it found no violation of pluralism or equality. In reviewing the 2016 modernization, it accepted publication of all valid presentations and the direct-mail system, while attaching the force-majeure qualification to postal transmission. These decisions establish constitutionality; they do not prove that the system is politically optimal.

Alternative proposals recur. One family would replace or supplement elected-official presentations with a large number of citizen signatures. That could give ballot access a more directly democratic basis, but it would require robust identity checking, duplicate prevention, privacy rules, and a defensible threshold. It might also favor movements with money, digital reach, or intense national followings rather than territorial institutional depth. Another approach would anonymize presenters, reducing interpersonal pressure at the cost of transparency. A third would automatically qualify parties or candidates meeting a prior electoral-performance test, which would stabilize access for established forces but could entrench them against new ones.

No alternative eliminates gatekeeping; it relocates it. A citizen-signature rule gates through mobilization and verification. A polling rule gates through survey design and media attention. A party-performance rule gates through previous elections. The French system's distinctive choice is to make current officeholders, geographically dispersed, the initial institutional screen.

## What the numbers do and do not show

The Council's rolling publication offers unusually good data, but interpretation requires care. A published presentation is valid as of the Council's review; a campaign press release is a claim. A high tally may show a strong organization, party control over local mandates, a deliberate pluralist effort by unaligned mayors, or all three. It does not translate mechanically into votes.

Nor should the race to 500 be read only as a binary countdown. Geographic diversity can become the binding constraint. So can the composition of the eligible pool, changes in local offices, mail timing, and the willingness of officeholders to make a public act. Campaigns may report pledges, completed forms, mailed forms, and validated presentations as if they were interchangeable, but they occupy different stages. Only the final category is authoritative for qualification.

Surplus presentations are useful insurance and political signaling. They reduce the danger that invalid forms or territorial concentration leave a contender below the threshold. They can also display institutional strength. But after qualification, an additional presentation does not give a candidate extra ballot status, speaking time, votes, or campaign reimbursement. The rule is a gateway, not a weighted electoral college.

For the 2027 contest, the best reporting discipline is to maintain separate labels: *discussed*, *declared*, *party-selected*, *collecting presentations*, and *qualified by the Constitutional Council*. Before the formal collection period, statements that someone “has 500 endorsements” usually refer to pledges or political assurances rather than forms validly received and certified under the statutory procedure. The difference is not pedantry. It is the line between campaign organization and official ballot access.

## Limits of the current account

This article describes the law consolidated as of July 9, 2026 and the procedure used in the most recent presidential cycle. The exact 2027 administrative calendar, form instructions, publication interface, and any election-specific Council decisions will need to be checked once issued. The eligible-office list can also change when territorial institutions are reorganized; the current consolidated Article 3, rather than an older explainer, should control.

Official materials are strongest on legal rules and institutional reasoning. They document concern about pressure on rural mayors but do not measure its prevalence, its change after full publication, or its effect independently of party organization and candidate outreach. Strategic bargaining and whether a presenter acts for pluralism rather than support require careful interviews or systematic research and should not be inferred from the public list alone.
