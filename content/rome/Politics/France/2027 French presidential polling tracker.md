As of July 10, 2026, the defensible polling signal is that the tested Rassemblement national option leads every published first-round scenario, while the identity and number of center and center-left candidates materially change the contest behind it; no poll yet measures the eventual official ballot.

## What this tracker does

This is a scenario ledger, not a polling average. It records original institute releases only when the electorate, candidate slate, field dates, question, and published denominator can be recovered. A row represents a counterfactual ballot offered to respondents. It does not establish that everyone named will run, qualify, or appear together on the ballot established by the Conseil constitutionnel.

The central finding is robust but narrower than a forecast. Across the original first-round intention surveys examined here from late May through July 8, the tested RN name received roughly the low-to-high thirties and led each tested configuration. The second-place ordering was much less settled. Édouard Philippe generally performed better when Gabriel Attal was absent; Attal likewise rose when Philippe was absent. Jean-Luc Mélenchon was comparatively stable across many slate changes. Raphaël Glucksmann polled above François Hollande when each was tested as the non-LFI center-left option in otherwise similar Ipsos configurations. These are conditional contrasts, not predictions of nominations or transfers.

The tracker deliberately does not blend Marine Le Pen and Jordan Bardella. Nor does it average a poll containing Philippe and Attal with one containing only one of them. Those substitutions change the choice set. [[2027 French presidential candidate field]] separately records verified political status, which a poll cannot confer.

## What can be compared

The cleanest within-study experiment is the substitution or removal of one candidate while the institute, fieldwork, sample construction, question, and most of the slate remain fixed. In Toluna Harris Interactive's July survey, Philippe moved from 14% when Attal was also offered to 20% when Philippe was the sole tested center option. Attal moved from 8% with Philippe present to 16% without him. The RN line stayed between 34% and 36%, while Mélenchon remained at 16%. This supports an inference about fragmentation in the tested center: offering two adjacent names divided expressed intentions much more than it changed the leading line.

Ipsos showed the same mechanism in May, though its levels differed. With Glucksmann and Bardella held constant, Philippe measured 13% alongside Attal and 17.5% without him; Attal measured 8.5% alongside Philippe and 14.5% without him. Ipsos also showed Glucksmann moving from 11% with both center names present to 13–14% when only one was present. Choice-set effects therefore can cross bloc boundaries: removing a center candidate does not mechanically send every supporter to the remaining center candidate.

The Glucksmann–Hollande comparison is also meaningful only within matched configurations. In the eight Ipsos scenarios, Glucksmann received 11–14%, whereas Hollande received 7–8%; Mélenchon stayed at 13–13.5%. In July Toluna Harris measured Glucksmann at 11% and Hollande at 8% in the corresponding one-center scenarios, with Mélenchon at 16% in both. This is evidence that respondents reacted differently to the two named center-left options under those survey conditions. It is not a primary, a forecast of a common-left selection process, or evidence that either has qualified.

Cross-institute changes require greater caution. Ipsos screened registered respondents who said they were certain to vote and reported results among expressed choices, with scenario bases around 1,037–1,079 out of a 1,500-person registered sample. Toluna Harris began with 1,592 registered respondents from a broader 1,837-person adult sample and published expressed-vote shares while separately offering blank, null, and abstention responses. Ifop interviewed 984 registered respondents drawn from 1,095 adults and calculated published scores using its turnout and political adjustments; only about 624–625 certain voters expressed a first-round choice in the two scenarios. Differences of a few points across those designs may reflect screening, adjustment, timing, and slate—not opinion change alone.

## The strongest current signals

### A leading RN line, not a certified finalist

The most persistent result is the RN's first-round lead. Ipsos measured Bardella at 33.5–36% across its six Bardella scenarios and Le Pen at 31–32% across two Le Pen scenarios in late May. Toluna Harris measured Le Pen at 34–36% across five July scenarios. Ifop measured Le Pen at 36% in both July scenarios. An OpinionWay survey fielded June 30–July 1 and published July 3 also placed Bardella at 36% in a broad slate, according to its original Commission-filed report.

Persistence across different slates and institutes is more informative than any single decimal. Yet “leading the tested first round” is not “will reach the runoff.” The eventual candidate, legal eligibility, valid presentations, campaign, turnout, and official ballot remain unresolved. The distinction is especially important when institutes switch between Le Pen and Bardella around political or judicial events. Combining their lines would conceal the very contingency being tested.

### The center's candidate count changes its apparent strength

The center is not measured as a fixed bloc. A Philippe-only scenario produces a different object from a Philippe-plus-Attal scenario. In July Toluna Harris, their combined score with both present was 22%, compared with Philippe's 20% alone or Attal's 16% alone. In May Ipsos, the pair combined for 21.5% in the matched Glucksmann/Bardella configuration, versus Philippe at 17.5% or Attal at 14.5% alone. Adding individual scores therefore overstates what a unified candidacy automatically inherits, while citing only the stronger one-center scenario understates the cost if both run.

The within-survey recall tables explain the structure without proving future behavior. In July Toluna Harris's Glucksmann/Philippe-only scenario, 55% of respondents recalling a Macron first-round vote in 2022 chose Philippe among those expressing a 2027 choice; 12% chose Glucksmann, 12% Le Pen, and 10% Retailleau. When Philippe and Attal were both offered, those recalled Macron voters divided 40% Philippe and 21% Attal. Because vote recall is weighted and self-reported, these are modeled subgroup estimates, not a voter file. They nevertheless show why nomination structure is central to polling interpretation.

### The left is plural, and stability can be deceptive

Mélenchon's 13–13.5% across the May Ipsos permutations and 16% across all five July Toluna Harris permutations looks stable. But stability under a limited set of substitutions does not mean his electorate, turnout, or transfer prospects are fixed. The surveys retained several left options, and the population expressing a choice can change between designs. Glucksmann's higher result than Hollande in matched tests is a named-candidate contrast, not the result of a shared selection mechanism.

No first-round table should be converted into a single “left total” without defining the political proposition. Mélenchon, Glucksmann or Hollande, Roussel, Tondelier, and Arthaud represent distinct candidacies and organizations. Their voters may coordinate, fragment, abstain, or reconsider depending on the actual field. [[2027 French presidential coalition map]] owns those organizational questions.

## Runoff polls belong in a separate lane

First-round support and second-round performance answer different questions. Ifop's July 7–8 survey tested Le Pen at 54% to Philippe's 46%, 55% to Attal's 45%, and 70% to Mélenchon's 30%, among expressed choices. Non-expression was substantial: 28.2%, 29.2%, and 38.9% of registered respondents respectively did not express a choice in those pairings. Toluna Harris separately offered abstention and blank/null responses; in its July survey, blank/null plus abstention amounted to 22% for a Philippe–Le Pen pairing, 25% for Attal–Le Pen, and 34% for Mélenchon–Le Pen before the reported expressed-vote normalization.

Those figures are scenario stress tests. They do not show that Le Pen will qualify, that the named opponent will qualify, or that today's non-expression will persist through a real two-week runoff campaign. They also cannot be compared directly with a table that excludes abstention from the question or groups blank and null choices differently. Three rules govern future updates. First, preserve the exact slate. Second, record the denominator and non-expression categories. Third, treat repeated waves as comparable only when the institute confirms the same method and the underlying question has not changed. A chart should break—not bridge—when one of those conditions fails.

## What the tracker cannot tell us

Polls do not observe qualification, campaign finance, media equality, tactical withdrawals, or a governing majority. They also do not eliminate ordinary survey uncertainty. The institutes' illustrative 95% intervals are roughly two to three points for many reported national shares, before adding design effects, turnout-model error, political weighting, and uncertainty created by a hypothetical slate. Subgroup estimates are less precise.

Nor should publication timing be confused with causal identification. The July surveys were fielded immediately after a major legal and political event involving Le Pen. They measure opinion after that event, but without a clean experiment they cannot isolate how much any result was caused by the judgment, her declaration, news coverage, other contemporaneous events, or sampling variation.

The right reading as of July 10 is therefore layered: the RN line leads persistently; the competition behind it is highly sensitive to who is offered; Philippe has generally been the strongest tested center option when only one center name is present; Mélenchon remains competitive for second place in some broad fields; and runoff resistance varies sharply by opponent and non-expression. Every clause remains conditional on a ballot France does not yet have.

## Open questions

- Will future institute waves retain an identical slate long enough to establish a genuine trend rather than a sequence of counterfactuals?
- How will the legally operative candidate field change after party selection, presentations, and eligibility decisions?
- Which institutes will publish effective subgroup bases and clearer turnout-model documentation for 2027?
- Can changes in blank, null, abstention, and undecided responses be tracked without collapsing categories that carry different political meanings?
