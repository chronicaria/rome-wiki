French presidential voting combines a tightly staged secret-ballot procedure with a limited form of proxy voting: the proxy casts a physical ballot in the principal’s polling station, under the same secrecy rules, but the law cannot technologically verify that the proxy followed the principal’s private instruction.

Up: [[2027 French presidential election]]

The distinction matters. France does **not** generally allow an ordinary presidential elector to send a ballot from home, vote online, or hand a completed ballot to another person. Voting is personal and takes place at the ballot box. A *procuration* changes who performs the physical act when an elector cannot attend: the absent elector is the **mandant** (principal), and the chosen elector is the **mandataire** (proxy). The mandate is recorded before polling day; the proxy then goes to the principal’s assigned polling station and votes there.

This note describes the law and official procedure in force on **10 July 2026**. It is a baseline for the [[2027 French presidential election]], not a guarantee that every administrative or digital rule will remain unchanged by polling day.

## The ordinary physical voting sequence

The legal architecture begins with a terse rule in article L59 of the Electoral Code: the ballot is secret. Article L62 and the Interior Ministry’s voter guidance turn that principle into a physical sequence designed to separate three facts: who is entitled to vote, whether only one ballot envelope enters the box, and which candidate the elector chose.

1. **The elector arrives at the assigned polling station.** Registration on that station’s electoral roll is checked. Identity is controlled under the applicable rules. The electoral card is useful but is not itself always indispensable; in communes of 1,000 inhabitants or more, an accepted identity document is required, while in smaller communes the presiding officer must still be able to establish identity.
2. **The elector takes an official envelope and ballots.** Interior Ministry guidance tells the elector to take several candidates’ ballots, because visibly taking only one could disclose the choice. An elector may instead use a ballot received with the official election materials at home, but the envelope used at the station remains the controlled vehicle placed in the box.
3. **The elector enters the voting booth alone.** Passage through the *isoloir* is compulsory. Article L62 requires the elector, without leaving the polling room, to go alone to the screened area and place the chosen ballot in the envelope out of sight. The Code requires one booth per 300 registered electors or fraction thereof. Booths must protect the elector’s choice without concealing the voting operations themselves from public observation.
4. **Identity is confirmed at the ballot box.** Official guidance describes the presiding officer or deputy reading the presented identity document or electoral card aloud. The point is to connect the eligible person to the next act without learning the content of the vote.
5. **The single-envelope rule is checked without contact.** The elector shows that only one envelope is being carried. Article L62 expressly says the president observes this without touching the envelope. The elector personally inserts it into the transparent ballot box.
6. **The elector signs the roll.** The signature on the *liste d’émargement* records that the voting entitlement has been used. It records participation, not choice. If the elector cannot sign, another elector chosen by that person may sign with the prescribed handwritten indication that the elector cannot sign personally.

These steps deliberately create a chain of public controls around a private decision. Eligibility, identity, the single envelope, deposit in the box, and the signature are observable. The selection and enclosure of the ballot are not. The transparent box lets participants and observers see that envelopes enter the box while leaving their contents hidden. Public counting after the close of polls then converts anonymous envelopes into totals; the voter roll and ballots are never designed to be linkable voter by voter.

## What protects secrecy—and what secrecy means

Secrecy is more than permission not to tell anyone one’s choice. The system tries to make a provable vote difficult. Taking several ballots and entering the booth interrupt visual inference. The standardized opaque envelope prevents observers from reading the ballot. The ban on the president touching the envelope limits discretionary handling at the decisive moment. Personal insertion prevents an official from substituting or withholding the envelope. Finally, the signature roll and the ballots are distinct records: one proves that a named elector voted; the other contributes anonymously to the count.

This design protects both privacy and freedom. If a voter cannot reliably prove how they voted, a buyer, employer, family member, party organizer, or coercer has less ability to enforce a demanded choice. The booth therefore serves the collective integrity of the election even when an individual says they do not care about privacy.

Accessibility rules qualify, rather than abandon, personal voting. Article L64 permits an elector with a disability that makes it impossible to introduce the ballot or operate a voting machine to be assisted by an elector of their choice; if the elector cannot sign, the chosen person may sign with the statutory notation. That helper is not a proxy exercising an absent person’s franchise. The voter is present and chooses assistance for the acts they cannot perform. Because assistance necessarily creates some knowledge or opportunity for influence, it is a carefully bounded exception and one reason the Code penalizes fraudulent attempts to circumvent both article L64 and the proxy rules.

## Creating a proxy

Article L71 now states that any elector may, on request, vote by proxy. There is no longer a need to prove a work, health, travel, or similar reason. The essential legal and administrative controls instead concern the identities of the principal and proxy, the proxy’s electoral capacity, the duration and election covered, and formal validation.

The principal chooses a proxy who enjoys electoral rights and is registered on an electoral roll. Since 1 January 2022, the proxy need not be registered in the same commune as the principal. This makes selection easier, but it does not move the vote: on polling day the proxy must travel to **the principal’s** polling station.

Official guidance offers two principal routes:

- Through **Maprocuration**, the principal enters the request online and ordinarily validates identity in person at a police station, gendarmerie brigade, or consulate, presenting the registration reference and identity document.
- Through the **paper Cerfa process**, the principal completes and validates the form before an authorized authority, including specified police, gendarmerie, judicial, consular, or prefecturally designated public locations, with identity evidence.

The principal must identify the proxy accurately. The online route accepts the proxy’s national elector number and date of birth, or the required civil-status details and voting commune. The official electoral-situation service can be used to check whether a proxy has been given or received.

For electors unable to travel because of serious illness or disability, authorized police officers or agents may collect the request at home. The request for travel must be in writing and accompanied by a sworn statement describing the manifest inability to appear. This accommodation concerns creation of the proxy; it does not turn the home into a polling station.

Fully digital creation and cancellation have been expanding. As of the 2026 official guidance, a completely dematerialized route is available in defined circumstances to users with a high-assurance France Identité identity whose certification involved an in-person municipal check. The 2024 European and legislative elections served as an experiment, and rules were expanded for the 2026 municipal elections. Because the exact eligibility and scope of this route have changed recently, a 2027 voter should check Maprocuration and Interior Ministry instructions rather than assume the 2026 implementation will carry over unchanged.

## Timing and duration

A request can legally be made close to polling day, but there is an operational difference between submitting it and having it recorded in time. The Interior Ministry urges early action because validation, transmission, and municipal processing take time. If the polling station’s records have not been updated when the proxy appears, the proxy may be unable to cast the principal’s vote. A request received too late for the first round can still operate in the second round if its stated validity includes that round.

The principal can establish a proxy for a particular election—both rounds or only one—or for a stated period, normally up to one year from creation. For French citizens resident abroad, a consularly established proxy may last up to three years. These duration options do not give the proxy an unlimited stock of ballots; use remains tied to elections for which the principal is eligible and to the statutory cap on mandates held on the day.

## Limits on how many proxies one elector may hold

Article L73 provides the core rule: one proxy may hold no more than two mandates, only one of which may have been established in France. If the limits are exceeded, the earliest mandates remain valid and later ones are automatically void.

In ordinary domestic terms, the practical message is simpler: on election day an elector may hold only **one proxy established in France**. That person may alternatively or additionally hold mandates established abroad within the legal combinations. Interior Ministry guidance lists: one French-established mandate; one foreign-established mandate; one of each; or two foreign-established mandates.

There is a consular-list exception in current official guidance: an elector may hold as many as three mandates if at least one comes from an elector registered on a consular electoral roll, but only one of the three may have been established in France. This is relevant to a presidential electorate that includes French citizens abroad, yet it should not be generalized into a three-proxy domestic rule.

The cap is an anti-concentration safeguard. It makes it harder for a party operative, employer, care provider, or local broker to aggregate many votes in one person’s hands. Article L72-1 adds specific protections for adults under legal protection: they vote personally and cannot appoint their court-appointed guardian or defined categories of owners, managers, staff, volunteers, and personal-service workers connected to institutions or services caring for them. Those exclusions target relationships where dependency can undermine free choice.

## Cancelling, replacing, and arriving in person

Article L75 gives the principal a continuing right to revoke the mandate and to issue a new one. Official guidance says cancellation uses the same competent authorities and form framework as creation, and Maprocuration can also be used. Recent high-assurance digital procedures may permit fully online cancellation where eligibility conditions are met.

Revocation should still be completed early enough to reach the electoral records. Merely telling the proxy privately not to vote is not the same as formally cancelling the recorded mandate. Likewise, appointing someone new must be regularized through the official process; personal messages do not change the polling station’s authorization record.

There is also a polling-day priority rule. Under article L76, the principal may vote personally if they arrive before the proxy has used the mandate. If the principal votes first, the proxy cannot later use it. If the proxy has already voted for the principal, the principal cannot cast a second ballot. This is not a recall of an already cast vote: once the proxy’s envelope has entered the anonymous ballot box, secrecy makes it impossible to identify and retrieve.

Death or loss of civic rights by the proxy automatically cancels the mandate under article L77. A change in the principal’s commune of registration does not, according to Interior Ministry guidance, itself invalidate an otherwise valid proxy, though the voter should verify current registration and polling-station information.

## How the proxy vote is executed

The proxy goes to the principal’s polling station and presents only the proxy’s own identity document. The proxy does not need the principal’s electoral card or another document belonging to the principal. Poll workers check the recorded mandate.

Article L74 then brings the act back inside article L62’s ordinary voting process. After the mandate is confirmed, the proxy takes an electoral envelope, uses the booth, presents the single envelope, and personally places it in the ballot box. The proxy signs the roll **opposite the principal’s name**, in ink, recording use of that principal’s entitlement. If the proxy also votes personally at the same station, that is a separate exercise of the proxy’s own entitlement and a separate signature.

The proxy is expected to follow the principal’s instructions, but the secret ballot produces an unavoidable limit: neither the principal nor polling officials can observe the marked or selected ballot inside the booth, and the deposited envelope cannot later be linked to the proxy. The state verifies authorization and prevents double voting; it does not verify obedience to a candidate instruction. Proxy voting therefore rests on personal trust. The principal should choose someone reliable and communicate clearly, but no lawful receipt can prove performance.

## Fraud controls and residual risks

Controls operate at several layers. Identity validation at creation reduces impersonation. The electoral register and national elector identifiers reduce mistaken attribution. Formal transmission to the municipality puts the mandate on the polling record. Capacity and number limits constrain accumulation. At the station, the proxy’s identity and recorded mandate are checked, and the signature opposite the principal’s name creates an auditable record that the authorization was used. The first-vote rule blocks a second ballot by the principal or proxy.

Criminal law backs those controls. Article L111 punishes fraudulent maneuvers intended to violate the disability-assistance rule or articles L71–L77 on proxies by reference to the penalties in article L107. More broadly, article L116 reaches fraudulent maneuvers that violate or attempt to violate ballot secrecy, impair the sincerity of the poll, obstruct operations, or change results. These provisions do not make manipulation impossible, but they make proxy abuse an electoral offense rather than a private disagreement.

The main residual risk lies before the booth: coercion or deception in choosing a proxy, pressure on a dependent principal, unauthorized creation using compromised identity credentials, or a trusted proxy deliberately voting differently. The system’s answer is not surveillance inside the booth, because that would destroy secrecy. It uses identity assurance, restricted proxy capacity, vulnerable-person exclusions, revocation, the principal’s right to vote first, auditable use of the mandate, and penalties. This is a deliberate tradeoff: a secret ballot can verify that an authorized proxy cast **a** ballot, not which ballot was cast.

## Why it matters for 2027

In a close two-round presidential election, proxy voting can preserve participation for travelers, ill or disabled voters, people with work or family constraints, and citizens whose trusted proxy lives in another commune. The reform removing the need to justify an absence and the growth of Maprocuration lower procedural barriers. But late processing, misunderstanding about where the proxy must vote, and misplaced trust can still disenfranchise an elector in practice.

The subject is also fertile ground for misleading claims. A digital **request** is not an online presidential ballot. A proxy registered elsewhere cannot vote for the principal at the proxy’s own station. Holding a mandate does not allow the proxy to bypass the booth or carry in a completed envelope. And a proxy vote does not create two votes for the proxy: it uses the principal’s distinct entitlement and is signed against the principal’s name.

For election-integrity analysis, secrecy and auditability should not be treated as opposites. France makes the *use of an entitlement* auditable through identity checks and signatures, while deliberately making the *content of the choice* unlinkable. Proxy voting preserves that same boundary. The hard policy question is whether identity and authorization controls remain strong and accessible as creation becomes more digital—without creating a trace that would reveal the vote itself.
