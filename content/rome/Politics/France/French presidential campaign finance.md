French presidential campaign finance combines an early accounting perimeter, a mandatory financial intermediary, spending ceilings, conditional public reimbursement, and retrospective review by the CNCCFP rather than giving candidates either a purely private market or a fully state-funded campaign.

Up: [[2027 French presidential election]] · Related: [[The 500 presentations in French presidential elections]] · [[How the French presidential election works]]

## The system in one view

France regulates presidential money through a chain rather than a single cap. A prospective candidate must appoint a **mandataire financier**—either an individual financial agent or a campaign-finance association—to collect campaign funds and pay election expenses. Receipts and expenses within the legal campaign period enter a **compte de campagne**, a campaign account supported by documentation and presented by a chartered accountant. Spending cannot exceed the presidential ceiling. After the election, every candidate on the official first-round list must file, and the **Commission nationale des comptes de campagne et des financements politiques** (CNCCFP) approves, reforms, or rejects the account and fixes the public reimbursement. Appeals go to the [[Conseil constitutionnel]].

Each link in that chain does different work. The mandataire separates campaign money from the candidate's ordinary finances. The account makes the campaign reconstructable. The ceiling limits electoral expenditure but does not guarantee equal resources. Reimbursement reduces candidates' ultimate cost but arrives only after review. Publication makes both the account and the regulator's judgment available for later scrutiny. Treating “the spending cap” as the whole system therefore misses the strongest mechanisms.

For the 2027 election, the CNCCFP issued a candidate-and-mandataire guide and a formal presentation decision in 2026. Those documents operationalize the statutory framework for this cycle. The consolidated text of the 6 November 1962 law remains the legal anchor, but the current guide matters for dates, account categories, supporting documents, and the Commission's administrative expectations.

## When campaign finance begins

The accounting perimeter begins before France has an official candidate list. For 2027, the CNCCFP guide fixes the opening of the one-year financing period at **1 April 2026**. Electoral expenses are counted through midnight at the start of the day before the round in which the candidate participates: the period therefore ends before the first round for an eliminated candidate and before the second round for a finalist. Receipts may continue until the account is filed. This creates an important distinction between political status and financial obligation. A person may still be “discussed,” “exploring,” or merely seeking support under the status vocabulary of [[2027 French presidential candidate field]], yet spending undertaken to obtain votes can already fall inside the eventual campaign account.

That is why the legal question is not simply whether an expense occurred after a declaration. The regulator asks whether it was **engaged or incurred with a view to the election**. Ordinary party activity, an officeholder's institutional communication, a book tour, or a policy organization can overlap with a future campaign without every euro automatically becoming electoral expenditure. Conversely, an organization cannot necessarily remove an expense from the account by paying it before a formal announcement or routing it through a supportive party. Purpose, timing, beneficiary, coordination, and the character of the activity all matter.

This functional test creates a recurring evidence problem for public analysis. Media estimates of a campaign's “war chest” or organizational scale are not equivalent to the legally recognized account. The definitive classification comes through documented filing and CNCCFP review, often long after the political effect has occurred. Before that review, precise claims about whether a disputed expense belongs in the account should be labeled as interpretations, not findings.

## The mandataire financier as a gatekeeper

The mandataire is the campaign's legal financial channel. Once appointed, the mandataire opens the dedicated bank account, receives permitted resources, issues donation receipts where appropriate, and pays the expenses that must pass through the campaign account. The role may be held by an individual or by an **association de financement électorale**. It is not simply a treasurer chosen for convenience: the separation is designed to establish traceability and keep the candidate from personally mixing campaign receipts and payments with other funds.

Parties remain central to presidential campaigns, but their participation must be visible. The consolidated law requires the published campaign account to include a detailed annex of expenses borne by parties and political groups that support the candidate, together with direct or indirect advantages, services, and contributions in kind. The CNCCFP can demand the accounting records needed to assess that annex. This responds to a structural difficulty in French presidential politics: a candidate is legally an individual contender, while the campaign may draw on several parties, parliamentary groups, associations, and pre-existing organizations.

The mandataire requirement also changes the practical meaning of fundraising. A promise of support, access to a donor network, or a party's endorsement is not itself a campaign receipt. Money and in-kind support must be legally eligible, documented, valued where necessary, and assigned to the correct channel. The account is therefore an accounting model of the campaign, not a diary of every politically helpful action.

## Where money may come from

French rules sharply distinguish individual participation from organizational money. Only French nationals and people residing in France may donate. Their donations are capped at **€4,600 for the same election across all candidates combined**, not €4,600 per candidate. Any donation above €150 must be made by cheque, transfer, direct debit, card, or an eligible payment platform; total cash donations received by a candidate may not exceed 20 percent of the spending ceiling. Donations from legal persons are prohibited except those from French political parties or groups complying with the 11 March 1988 political-finance law. Companies, ordinary associations, public bodies, and foreign states therefore cannot fund a presidential candidate as commercial or philanthropic sponsors.

Loans require separate attention from donations. A loan creates an apparent resource during the campaign but a liability afterward, so its lender, terms, duration, interest, and realistic repayment path affect whether it is permissible and how it is recorded. The presidential provisions are stricter than the general election rules: individuals may not grant a presidential candidate loans or repayable advances. A candidate may borrow from credit institutions or financing companies headquartered in an EU member state or a state party to the European Economic Area agreement, under a written repayment schedule. A qualifying political party may also lend: a loan from its own funds cannot carry interest, while a documented “mirror loan” financed by the party's own borrowing may pass interest through to the candidate. Each route has distinct accounting evidence and reimbursement consequences.

Personal contributions by the candidate are another category. They are not donations from a third party, but they still enter the financing plan and may depend on borrowing. The political headline “candidate invests personal money” does not answer whether the source was savings, a qualifying loan, or funds transmitted through another entity. A useful comparison between candidates therefore separates donations, party contributions, bank borrowing, candidate contribution, public advance, and contributions in kind.

Foreign financing is a particularly sensitive area. The safe analytical rule is to rely on the statutory text, the CNCCFP guide, and eventual account decisions rather than infer illegality from nationality, residence, a vendor relationship, or an international event. Campaign services purchased from a foreign supplier and prohibited foreign funding are not the same legal category. Allegations require the exact transaction and governing provision.

## Spending ceilings and what they do not mean

The operative 2027 spending ceilings stated by the CNCCFP are **€16.851 million** for every candidate present in the first round and **€22.509 million** for each of the two second-round candidates. The lower figures of €13.7 million and €18.3 million remain visible as statutory base amounts in the 1962 law, but they are not the amounts campaigns should budget against. The CNCCFP guide attributes the operative amounts to the adjustment made by Decree no. 2009-1730 of 30 December 2009 and describes them as the figures applicable under the texts in force when its corrected June 2026 edition was issued.

The ceiling applies to election expenditure attributed to the campaign account, not to every political resource that may affect the race. News coverage, official activity conducted within lawful boundaries, ordinary parliamentary speech, volunteer time that does not require valuation, and independent political expression do not all become candidate expenditure. The difficult work lies at the boundaries: party payroll, shared offices, pre-campaign events, social-media production, polling, travel, security, and communications that serve both electoral and non-electoral purposes.

A cap also does not create equality of opportunity by itself. Candidates differ in access to credit, party infrastructure, donor lists, experienced staff, existing name recognition, volunteers, and the ability to risk expenses that may not be reimbursed. Equal legal ceilings can coexist with unequal capacity to reach them. Public reimbursement reduces this inequality but cannot eliminate the need for bridge financing, nor does it assign every candidate the same operational apparatus.

The proper comparison is therefore not “candidate A can spend the same as candidate B.” It is: which expenses count, what resources can legally finance them, how quickly the campaign can obtain liquidity, how much reimbursement is at risk, and which supporting-party resources must be integrated into the account.

## Public reimbursement is conditional

French public reimbursement is often summarized by the first-round 5 percent threshold, but the mechanism has several conditions. A first-round candidate receiving less than 5 percent of expressed votes has a statutory maximum equal to **4.75 percent of the €16.851 million ceiling**, or **€800,422.50** by direct multiplication. A first-round candidate reaching at least 5 percent has a maximum of **47.5 percent**, or **€8,004,225**. Each finalist has a maximum of **47.5 percent of €22.509 million**, or **€10,691,775**. These are ceilings on reimbursement, not automatic awards.

The actual payment cannot exceed the smallest of three quantities: the reimbursable expenditure accepted by the CNCCFP after any reformation; the candidate's personal contribution minus the campaign account's surplus; and the applicable statutory percentage maximum. It is therefore not a prize paid simply for appearing on the ballot or crossing a vote-share line.

Most importantly, final reimbursement depends on approval of the campaign account. A candidate must file on time, use the required accounting presentation, provide supporting evidence, respect the financial rules, and survive substantive review. The CNCCFP may reform the account by removing ineligible expenses or correcting valuations. It may reduce reimbursement according to the number and seriousness of irregularities. In more serious cases it may reject the account, which ordinarily removes the reimbursement and can produce additional legal consequences.

The state provides a **€200,000 advance** when the Constitutional Council's official candidate list is published. It is recorded within the candidate's personal contribution and deducted from the reimbursement ultimately paid. If final entitlement is below the advance, the excess must be returned. This matters for small campaigns: public assistance offers some early liquidity, yet it does not transfer the regulatory risk to the state.

The threshold also creates a strategic cliff. A candidate polling near 5 percent may be politically relevant even when the tested slate is unstable, but the difference between finishing just below and just above the threshold can materially alter the maximum reimbursement. Campaigns and lenders may respond to that risk. Still, poll percentages must not be converted directly into expected reimbursement: polls have sampling and scenario uncertainty, the threshold uses actual expressed votes, and reimbursement remains constrained by eligible spending and account approval.

## Filing, review, publication, and appeal

All official presidential candidates must file a campaign account, not only those seeking the larger reimbursement. It must be filed digitally through the CNCCFP service by **6 p.m. on the eleventh Friday after the first round**. Given the officially scheduled first round on 18 April 2027, that rule yields **Friday 2 July 2027 at 6 p.m.**; the election-specific portal should still be checked when filing opens. The account must be presented by a member of the order of chartered accountants. The accountant's statutory presentation role is not the same as the CNCCFP's regulatory approval: organizing the records and checking the file's formal completeness does not pre-judge whether every expense is electoral, eligible, correctly valued, or lawfully financed.

Review is adversarial in the limited administrative sense that the candidate can answer questions and proposed corrections before the Commission decides. The CNCCFP may approve, reform, or reject. It also determines the reimbursement and, where the ceiling has been exceeded, fixes a payment to the Treasury equal to the excess under the statutory rule. The Commission must decide within six months of the legal filing deadline. Candidates may challenge its decision before the Conseil constitutionnel within one month of notification, which is why a preliminary CNCCFP outcome and a final legal outcome must be distinguished.

Publication serves a different purpose from review. The Commission publishes accounts in the *Journal officiel* and in an open, reusable format, along with decisions. The record permits journalists, scholars, opponents, and voters to reconstruct spending categories and compare the Commission's treatment across campaigns. But publication occurs after the vote. Transparency therefore strengthens retrospective accountability and future deterrence more than it gives voters a complete audited picture during the campaign.

That delay should shape Rome's language. Before filing, use “campaign reports,” “the candidate says,” or “the party disclosed” for voluntary figures. After filing but before decision, say “declared in the account” rather than “approved.” After a CNCCFP decision, record whether the account was approved, reformed, or rejected and whether an appeal remains. Only after the appeal window or Conseil constitutionnel judgment should a contested account be called final.

## Enforcement is graduated, not automatic

An irregularity does not produce one universal penalty. The response depends on the rule breached, intent, materiality, amount, and the legal powers attached to that provision. Possible outcomes include reformation of the account, reduction or loss of reimbursement, payment to the Treasury for overspending, referral or separate proceedings, and potential ineligibility in circumstances defined by electoral law. Campaign-finance reporting should name the actual decision rather than collapse all outcomes into “sanctioned.”

The distinction between accounting disagreement and unlawful financing is especially important. The CNCCFP may exclude an expense from reimbursement because it was not sufficiently electoral or documented without finding a corrupt funding scheme. Conversely, a formally recorded receipt can still be prohibited. Readers need the decision's operative language, amount, and reasoning.

For 2027, enforcement claims are necessarily prospective as of this note's date. The current guide and presentation decision tell campaigns how accounts should be built; they do not establish that a named contender has complied or violated the rules. Candidate-specific allegations belong in a dated status record only when supported by authoritative evidence and, where consequential, independent reporting.

## Campaign finance and the 500 presentations

Financial organization and ballot qualification are separate barriers. The [[The 500 presentations in French presidential elections|500 presentations]] are submissions by eligible elected officials to the Conseil constitutionnel under geographic-distribution rules. They are not donations, and a person with substantial fundraising capacity does not become qualified by spending money. Conversely, a figure who can assemble presentations may still lack the credit, party support, documentation, or risk tolerance to operate near the spending ceiling.

The two systems interact indirectly. Building a national network to solicit presentations requires staff, travel, communications, and legal organization, some of which may fall within campaign expenditure. A party's local elected base can reduce the political difficulty of qualification and provide organizational capacity, but it cannot sell or transfer presentations as financial assets. Analytical models should therefore maintain separate columns for political support, presentation path, cash and credit, party infrastructure, and official qualification.

## Why it matters for the 2027 race

Campaign finance is a test of organizational viability, not a forecast of vote share. The rules reveal whether a prospective campaign can establish a compliant financial vehicle, obtain lawful liquidity, coordinate support from parties without hiding their contributions, document expenditures, and bear the risk that reimbursement will be reduced. Those capacities may affect who can sustain a national effort long before the official list is published.

The system also cautions against easy comparisons with American campaigns. France does not use the same candidate-committee, political-action-committee, and independent-expenditure architecture; the presidency is governed by French organic law, the Electoral Code, CNCCFP administration, and Conseil constitutionnel review. Terms such as “super PAC,” “matching funds,” or “campaign war chest” can obscure rather than clarify unless translated into the actual French categories.

For the [[2027 French presidential election]], a durable tracker should record only verified structural facts before accounts exist: appointment of a mandataire when publicly documented, official party financing decisions, the applicable ceiling and reimbursement rules, and authoritative regulatory changes. It should resist estimates that combine party budgets, candidate cash, loans, and hoped-for reimbursement into a single unsourced number.

## A source-critical checklist

When a campaign-finance claim appears, ask:

1. Is the source the consolidated law, the 2027 CNCCFP guide, a CNCCFP decision, a Conseil constitutionnel judgment, a filed account, or merely campaign commentary?
2. Does the number describe money raised, cash available, expenditure incurred, expenditure declared, expenditure accepted, or reimbursement awarded?
3. Is the entity the candidate's mandataire, a supporting political party, another association, a bank, or an individual?
4. Does the statement concern the legal accounting period, and is the expense actually electoral in purpose?
5. Has the account only been announced, filed, reviewed, decided, or finally resolved after appeal?
6. Is a penalty a reimbursement reduction, account rejection, excess-spending payment, ineligibility decision, or another proceeding?

These distinctions prevent a common error: turning an incomplete financial fact into a claim about candidate legitimacy, legality, or electoral strength.
