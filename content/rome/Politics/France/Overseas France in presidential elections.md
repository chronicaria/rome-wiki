Overseas France participates in the same nationwide presidential election as metropolitan France, but its territories have different constitutional statuses, political histories, turnout patterns, and local issue agendas—and must never be treated as one electoral bloc or confused with French citizens voting abroad.

## Why it matters

The French president is chosen from one national total of valid votes. An elector in Pointe-à-Pitre, Mamoudzou, Papeete, Nouméa, or Saint-Pierre contributes to that total under the same constitutional two-round choice as an elector in Paris. There is no overseas electoral college, territorial weighting, or separate overseas presidency. Yet the simplicity of that national rule can produce bad analysis. A map may colour all overseas territories alike, a broadcaster may quote only candidate percentages among valid ballots, or a campaign may speak of *les outre-mer* as though one policy package addressed a single constituency.

The 2022 election shows why those shortcuts fail. In the official Interior Ministry overseas aggregate, Jean-Luc Mélenchon led the first round with 39.99 percent of valid votes; Marine Le Pen had 21.30 percent and Emmanuel Macron 20.56 percent. In the runoff, Le Pen won 58.26 percent of overseas valid votes. But the aggregate combined very different territorial outcomes. Mélenchon received 56.16 percent in Guadeloupe's first round and 53.10 percent in Martinique, while Macron led New Caledonia with 40.51 percent and French Polynesia with 40.16 percent; Le Pen led Mayotte with 42.68 percent. In round two Le Pen won Guadeloupe, Martinique, Guyane, Réunion, and Mayotte, while Macron won New Caledonia, Wallis and Futuna, and French Polynesia. Saint-Pierre-et-Miquelon was nearly even.

Those returns matter nationally, but their deeper value is diagnostic. They reveal how presidential coalitions interact with local cost-of-living pressures, public services, migration and citizenship debates, institutional autonomy, memories of colonization, relations with the state, and participation. They also warn against treating a runoff vote against an incumbent as proof of durable attachment to the finalist who benefited.

## What counts as overseas France

“Overseas France” is a geographic umbrella, not a single constitutional category. Article 72-3 of the Constitution names several groups.

The territories governed under **article 73** are Guadeloupe, Guyane, Martinique, La Réunion, and Mayotte. INSEE calls these the *départements et régions d'outre-mer* (DROM), though Guyane and Martinique each have a single territorial collectivity rather than separate departmental and regional institutions. Article 73 begins from legislative identity: national laws and regulations apply as of right, while adaptations may be made for local characteristics and constraints within constitutional limits.

The **article 74 collectivities** are Saint-Barthélemy, Saint-Martin, Saint-Pierre-et-Miquelon, Wallis and Futuna, and French Polynesia. Each has an organic-law status designed around its particular interests, institutions, competences, and rules for applying national law. “COM” therefore does not imply that all five have the same autonomy or political system.

**New Caledonia** is separate again. Article 72-3 sends its status to title XIII of the Constitution, and its institutions and allocation of powers derive from the Nouméa framework and organic law. Whatever institutional changes are debated before 2027, its voters' participation in the presidency must be checked against the law actually in force at election time rather than inferred from a generic DROM–COM formula.

The French Southern and Antarctic Lands and Clipperton also fall under article 72-3, but neither supplies an ordinary resident territorial electorate comparable to Guadeloupe or Réunion. Their presence on a list of French overseas possessions does not justify treating them as presidential electoral units.

These status distinctions affect institutions and the application of law; they do **not** create votes of different national value. For the presidency, the relevant elector is a French citizen with electoral rights registered on the applicable electoral list. Local autonomy does not turn a presidential ballot into a territorial delegation.

## Overseas residents are not French citizens abroad

Overseas France must also be separated from **French citizens established outside France** (*Français établis hors de France*). A French citizen registered in Martinique or French Polynesia votes in a French overseas territory. A French citizen registered on a consular electoral list in Canada, Senegal, Japan, or Brazil votes abroad through an embassy or consulate. Both ballots enter the nationwide presidential total, but they are administered and reported through different geographic systems.

The distinction is substantively important. Overseas territories are parts of the French Republic with their own resident populations, local governments, public-service systems, and histories of incorporation or decolonization. The expatriate electorate is organized through consular lists across foreign states. Its social composition, mobility, tax position, exposure to French public services, and campaign access cannot be assumed to resemble any overseas territory. An “overseas and abroad” aggregate might be operationally convenient, but it erases the political question being studied.

It is also possible for a person born overseas to live and vote through a consulate abroad, or for a metropolitan-born citizen to live and register in an overseas territory. Birthplace, residence, registration, and constitutional status are different variables. Official election returns describe where a ballot was registered and counted, not an essential identity of the voter.

## One national ballot, adjusted voting times

The legal machinery is national but adapted to geography and time zones. The 1962 organic law governing presidential elections provides that voters normally vote on Sunday. To prevent some western territories from voting after Sunday results from metropolitan France have begun circulating, Guadeloupe, Guyane, Martinique, Saint-Barthélemy, Saint-Martin, Saint-Pierre-et-Miquelon, and French Polynesia vote on the preceding Saturday. The same Saturday rule applies at embassies and consulates located in the Americas. This schedule is one more reason not to conflate territorial and expatriate voters: they can share a voting day for time-zone reasons without belonging to one legal or political electorate.

New Caledonia, Wallis and Futuna, Réunion, and Mayotte lie east of metropolitan France and follow the election's applicable Sunday timetable. Exact polling hours and operational instructions are set through election-specific rules and local orders. Analysts should verify the 2027 instruments rather than recycle a 2022 polling-hours table.

The adjusted day does not create an early result that campaigns may freely exploit. Counting, transmission, and publication remain governed by election law, and the Conseil constitutionnel ultimately proclaims the national result. A territory's ballot is neither a pre-election poll nor an independent contest.

## How to read the official results

The Interior Ministry's definitive 2022 dataset offers a useful baseline. Its “Outre-mer” aggregate reported 1,954,672 registered electors in round one. Only 861,418 voted, a turnout of 44.07 percent. After 19,319 blank and 19,430 invalid ballots, 822,669 valid votes remained. In round two, the registered total was 1,955,508, turnout rose to 48.70 percent, and 90,652 ballots were blank or invalid, leaving 861,586 valid votes.

Three denominators answer three different questions:

- **Share of valid votes** measures the contest among candidates and determines the result. Mélenchon's 328,960 overseas first-round votes were 39.99 percent of valid votes.
- **Share of registered electors** measures territorial reach through both preference and participation. The same votes were 16.83 percent of those registered.
- **Turnout** measures participation, not support for any candidate. High abstention cannot be allocated to parties without individual-level evidence.

The runoff makes this distinction especially sharp. Le Pen's 501,991 votes were 58.26 percent of valid overseas votes but 25.67 percent of registered electors. Macron's 359,595 were 41.74 percent of valid votes and 18.39 percent of registered electors. The decisive legal denominator is valid votes, but the registered-voter denominator is necessary for interpreting coalition depth. Calling the result an unqualified “overseas landslide” hides that a majority of registered electors did not cast a valid vote for either finalist.

The aggregate then needs to be disassembled. First-round abstention ranged from 43.29 percent in Wallis and Futuna and 45.03 percent in Saint-Pierre-et-Miquelon to 66.65 percent in New Caledonia and 69.13 percent in French Polynesia. Candidate order differed as well. Even neighbouring or jointly reported islands can be politically distinct: the ministry's department-level file combines Saint-Martin and Saint-Barthélemy, so claims about either collectivity individually require a finer official table rather than assigning the combined result to both.

## What the 2022 reversal does—and does not—show

Guadeloupe, Martinique, and Guyane provide the most striking first-to-second-round contrast. Mélenchon won an absolute majority of valid first-round votes in all three: 56.16, 53.10, and 50.59 percent respectively. Two weeks later, Le Pen won 69.60 percent in Guadeloupe, 60.87 percent in Martinique, and 60.78 percent in Guyane. Réunion moved from a Mélenchon first-round lead of 40.26 percent to Le Pen at 59.56 percent in the runoff. Mayotte was different: Le Pen already led its first round and then won 59.10 percent in round two.

These territorial totals cannot identify individual transfers. The people who voted for Mélenchon in round one, voted for Le Pen in round two, abstained, or entered the electorate between rounds are not linked in the published aggregate returns. The reversal is compatible with a large Mélenchon-to-Le Pen transfer, but also with differential turnout, blank or invalid voting, and movements from other first-round candidates. Survey evidence may estimate flows, yet official totals alone cannot prove them.

Nor does the reversal make these territories ideologically “far right.” A presidential runoff is a constrained binary choice. In 2022 it occurred after sharp overseas conflict over prices, inequality, public authority, and the state's handling of the Covid-19 health pass and vaccination policies. A ballot for Le Pen could express support for her program, rejection of Macron, protest against the state, or some combination. Those motives require evidence rather than inference from the winner's label.

The Pacific results reinforce the heterogeneity. Macron led and won New Caledonia, where the institutional future and the politics of loyalist and pro-independence participation shaped a distinct environment. He also led French Polynesia's first round and narrowly won its runoff, while Wallis and Futuna gave him 67.45 percent in round two. These outcomes cannot be generalized into an “Atlantic protest, Pacific moderation” law: candidate organization, local endorsements, participation, institutional alignments, and election-specific events can all change.

## The recurring issue agenda

National candidates operate within a common campaign, but overseas issues enter it through territory-specific mechanisms.

**Cost of living and market structure** are recurrent because distance, shipping, import dependence, taxation, and concentrated distribution can make prices and consumer choice unlike metropolitan conditions. A promise framed as national purchasing-power relief may therefore be judged by whether it addresses local formation of prices.

**Employment and public services** also have different baselines. INSEE reports persistently higher unemployment in the DROM than France as a whole, with particularly severe conditions in Mayotte. Health care, water, housing, schools, transport, and administrative capacity are experienced locally. It is unsafe to infer one territory's priorities from another's headline indicators, but equally unsafe to treat national service provision as territorially uniform.

**Migration, citizenship, and security** take different forms. Guyane and Mayotte face border and migration pressures unlike island territories with different population trajectories. New Caledonia's citizenship and electoral questions belong to its sui generis institutional order. A presidential proposal can therefore have sharply unequal legal and political meanings across territories.

**Autonomy, independence, and relations with the state** are not interchangeable. Some elections turn partly on demands for adaptation within the Republic; others feature strong autonomy or independence currents; still others centre on the quality of departmental equality. A candidate cannot infer presidential support from a local party's position on institutional status alone.

**Colonial memory and state trust** shape how national authority is received. That does not mean every overseas vote is a referendum on France. It means that policy conflicts can acquire historical meanings absent from a metropolitan-only account, while local party systems mediate national labels.

## A disciplined framework for 2027

Analysis of [[2027 French presidential territorial coalitions]] should keep the following layers separate.

First, identify the territory and its constitutional status. Do not use “department,” “collectivity,” and “territory” as synonyms. Second, report registered voters, turnout, blank and invalid ballots, valid votes, and candidate votes. Third, compare like with like: same territorial boundary, same round, and the actual candidate slate. Fourth, distinguish an official result from a survey estimate or interpretation. Fifth, connect local developments to credible territorial sources rather than translating a national party press release into local support.

Campaign visits and endorsements need the same discipline. A mayor, assembly president, parliamentarian, customary authority, independence leader, or local party can be influential without “delivering” a territory. Presidential presentations are formal ballot-access acts and are not necessarily endorsements; endorsements are not votes; territorial results are not individual transfers. See [[The 500 presentations in French presidential elections]].

For 2027, the highest-value indicators will include registration and turnout changes, the territorial credibility of candidates replacing Macron, the ability of left and RN candidates to retain their different 2022 overseas components, local responses to cost-of-living and service crises, and the effect of institutional debates in New Caledonia and elsewhere. Any forecast should remain conditional on the actual qualified field.

## Place in the map

This article supplies the overseas layer beneath [[2027 French presidential territorial coalitions]]. It complements [[Abstention in French presidential elections]] by showing why a valid-vote majority can coexist with shallow reach among registered electors, and it constrains [[2027 French presidential first-to-second-round transfers]] by separating aggregate reversal from individual movement. Its central rule is simple: integrate overseas votes into the national election, but disaggregate the territories before explaining them.
