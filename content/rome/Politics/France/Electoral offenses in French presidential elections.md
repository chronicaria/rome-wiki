French electoral criminal law separates particular offenses—false registration, impersonation, multiple voting, ballot tampering, bribery, coercion, obstruction, and attacks on secrecy—from the separate question whether the Constitutional Council should correct or annul a presidential vote.

## The offense catalogue is not a synonym for “fraud”

The *Code électoral* contains a criminal chapter, articles L.86 to L.117-2, that applies a sequence of specific offenses to conduct before, during, and after voting. It is better read as a catalogue of protected interests than as one general crime called “election fraud.” The provisions protect the accuracy of electoral registration, the rule of one personal vote per eligible elector, free choice, secrecy, the physical and documentary integrity of the poll, public scrutiny, and the truthful establishment of results.

This page owns that criminal-law map: prohibited conduct, the actors it can reach, attempts, penalties, and the relationship between prosecution and the presidential result. [[Electoral fraud controls in French presidential elections]] instead explains the institutional safeguards that make misconduct harder and leave evidence when it occurs. [[Presidential election complaints and annulments]] explains how an elector, candidate, official, or supervisory body can place an irregularity before the election judge and what the Conseil constitutionnel can do to the count. The three questions overlap, but they are not interchangeable.

An *irrégularité* can exist without criminal intent. A polling board may be improperly composed, an identity check may be omitted, or a required record may be incomplete because officials misunderstood a rule. Such a defect can still make votes unreliable. A criminal offense ordinarily requires conduct and a mental element defined by its article: fraud, knowledge, violence, threat, a corrupt offer, or an intentional maneuver. Conversely, an attempted offense may be punishable even when controls stop it and every lawful vote remains in the final total.

The code cited here is the version in force on **10 July 2026**. Penalties and cross-references can change before the 2027 election. Reporting should therefore name the article, check its version on the date of the alleged conduct, and avoid turning this map into case-specific legal advice.

## Registration offenses protect the electorate before polling day

Articles L.86–L.88 address manipulation of electoral lists. Article L.86 covers obtaining registration under a false name or false status, concealing a statutory incapacity, or obtaining registration on two or more lists. The current maximum stated in that article is one year's imprisonment and a €15,000 fine. Article L.88 reaches fraudulent declarations or false certificates used to obtain or attempt an improper registration, or to cause or attempt the improper registration or removal of another citizen; it also reaches accomplices and states the same maximum.

Article L.87 separately concerns fraud in issuing or producing a certificate of registration or removal and incorporates the penalties in article L.113. Article L.88 also imposes an institutional bridge: when an administrative court's final decision finds electoral-fraud facts, the court transmits the file to the competent public prosecutor. That transmission does not itself convict anyone. It places possible criminal conduct before the authority responsible for deciding whether investigation and prosecution are warranted.

These provisions show why list litigation and criminal liability must remain distinct. A court can correct an elector's status without finding that anyone lied. A duplicate or erroneous record can arise administratively. To call a registration problem a crime, coverage needs evidence matching the article—false identity or status, concealed incapacity, multiple registrations knowingly obtained, fraudulent declarations or certificates, an attempt, or complicity—not merely the discovery that a list required correction.

## Personation and multiple voting attack personal suffrage

Articles L.91 and L.92 protect the rule that the ballot is cast by the entitled elector and only as law permits. Article L.91 punishes a person who, having lost the right to vote because of a judicial decision, votes in an election. Article L.92 addresses voting either by using a false name and status or by taking the name of a registered elector, as well as voting more than once after taking advantage of multiple registration. The present text provides two years' imprisonment and a €15,000 fine for the conduct in article L.92.

The legal categories are narrower than casual claims of “suspicious identity.” A clerical mismatch, an elector sent to the wrong bureau, or an official's mistaken document demand is not automatically personation. The relevant proof may include the electoral list, identity documents, the *liste d’émargement*, the polling-station minutes, witness statements, and evidence connecting the defendant to the act. [[Identity checks in French presidential elections]] explains the ordinary verification sequence; the criminal provision addresses deliberate substitution or repeat voting.

Proxy voting has its own criminal exposure. Article L.111 reaches fraudulent maneuvers intended to violate the rules governing who may act as a proxy, how proxy voting is exercised, the permitted number of proxies, and the termination of a proxy. This matters because a proxy ballot is still legally the principal elector's vote, cast through a regulated mandate. Assistance with paperwork, political canvassing, and an unlawful scheme cannot be collapsed into one category. The evidence must establish the maneuver and the rule it was meant to evade. [[Ballot secrecy and proxy voting in French presidential elections]] owns the underlying proxy system and its safeguards.

## Ballot handling and counting offenses protect the documentary result

The code addresses misconduct by voters, scrutineers, polling-board members, and custodians of election materials. Articles L.93 and following cover introducing ballots into the poll through false names, signatures, or other fraudulent operations, and removing or adding ballots. Article L.94 aggravates specified conduct when committed by a polling-station official. The architecture recognizes a practical reality: someone entrusted with a formal role can have access or authority unavailable to an ordinary elector.

Article L.95 is directed at a scrutineer who knowingly reads a name other than the one on a ballot, or who removes, adds, or alters a ballot. Article L.104 reaches a polling-board member or custodian who fraudulently violates the ballot, while articles L.103–L.104 address grave physical attacks involving a ballot box or the poll. Article L.101 treats breaking into the poll with violence in order to prevent electoral choice as a crime, with aggravated treatment when committed by an armed group; the surrounding provisions distinguish threats, violence, disruption, and removal of an urn.

These offenses should be reported with the physical voting process in view. French presidential votes are locally cast on paper, reconciled against signatures, publicly counted, recorded on tally sheets and minutes, and transmitted through canvassing. [[Vote counting in French presidential elections]] describes that chain. A numerical discrepancy is evidence of a problem requiring explanation, not by itself proof of who acted or with what intent. A criminal allegation requires the additional step from anomaly to attributable prohibited conduct.

Article L.113 supplies an important general provision for fraudulent acts committed in a commission or polling station. It covers intentionally violating voting law, or using fraudulent acts before, during, or after the poll, to violate secrecy, impair the sincerity of the vote, obstruct operations, or change or attempt to change the result. Its base penalty is one year's imprisonment and a €15,000 fine. The penalty is doubled when the perpetrator falls within the public or electoral roles specified by the article, including certain public officials, public-service personnel, judicial or administrative officers, and polling-station presidents.

Article L.116 extends comparable protection beyond the rooms and commissions named in L.113. It reaches fraudulent maneuvers, even elsewhere, that attack or attempt to attack sincerity or secrecy, prevent or attempt to prevent operations, or change or attempt to change results. It also addresses interference with a voting machine intended to stop operations or falsify results, and unjustified expulsion of an assessor or delegate or prevention of that person's prerogatives. Article L.117-2 confirms that the criminal chapter applies to machine voting and electronic remote voting where those methods lawfully operate. That extension should not be misreported as proof that France uses internet voting for the ordinary metropolitan presidential poll.

## Bribery, coercion, and collective inducements protect free choice

Article L.106 criminalizes obtaining or attempting to obtain a vote—or an abstention—through gifts, donations, promises of benefits, public or private employment, or other particular advantages. It also reaches a person who solicits or accepts such gifts, donations, or promises. The article's current penalty is two years' imprisonment and a €15,000 fine. The protected choice includes abstention: paying someone not to vote can be as legally significant as buying support for a candidate.

Article L.107 addresses threats, violence, or coercive maneuvers used to influence an elector's vote or obtain abstention. Article L.108 deals with donations or promises made to a commune, community, or citizens collectively in order to influence an electoral body or part of it. Article L.109 provides aggravated treatment when specified public officials commit conduct covered by articles L.106–L.108.

These articles demand care in campaign coverage. Ordinary policy promises are the substance of democratic competition; the offense catalogue is not a shortcut for calling any targeted public proposal “vote buying.” The statutory concern is a corrupt or coercive transaction or inducement of the defined kind. Reporters should identify who allegedly offered what, to whom, on what condition, through which evidence, and whether prosecutors or a court have adopted that characterization. A viral quotation stripped of context cannot establish the offense.

Secrecy is also a freedom guarantee. Article L.113 protects secrecy within election operations, while L.116 reaches fraudulent attacks more broadly. Coercion can occur privately and leave a perfectly balanced count. The absence of a counting discrepancy therefore does not disprove pressure; equally, political disagreement or social influence does not itself establish a threat or fraudulent maneuver.

## Obstruction offenses protect plural supervision

Candidate representatives, assessors, scrutineers, electors, polling officials, control commissions, and Conseil constitutionnel delegates do different jobs. Their overlapping presence is part of the integrity system. The criminal chapter protects that system against violence, obstruction, and fraudulent exclusion.

Article L.100 addresses disrupting electoral operations through an assembled group, demonstrations, or threats; articles L.102–L.104 treat violence, violation of the poll, and attacks on the urn or its custody. Article L.116 expressly covers causing an assessor or candidate delegate to be expelled without legitimate reason or preventing that person from exercising legal prerogatives. This is more than workplace disorder: excluding an authorized observer can remove an independent source of challenge and documentation.

Not every removal is criminal. The president of a polling station has authority to maintain order, and disruptive conduct can justify intervention. The statutory wording therefore makes legitimate reason and interference with protected functions important. The polling-station minutes, designation papers, orders given, warnings, witness accounts, and any police record would matter to distinguishing lawful maintenance of order from unlawful exclusion.

## Penalties are article-specific, and conviction can affect civic status

The chapter does not impose one uniform punishment. Depending on the provision, it contains *délits* with imprisonment and fines, aggravated penalties for entrusted actors, and serious crimes for violent collective attacks on the poll. Article L.117 provides that natural persons convicted of a listed group of offenses may also incur deprivation of specified civic rights and ineligibility under Penal Code articles 131-26 and 131-26-1. For the crime in article L.101, it identifies additional possible occupational prohibitions under article 131-27.

Three cautions follow. First, “incurs” a complementary penalty does not mean that every allegation instantly removes a person's rights. The legally operative consequence depends on charge, conviction, the applicable text, and the sentencing decision. Second, the maximum stated in an offense is not a prediction of sentence. Third, old articles, summaries, and database pages may display a historical version. Exact penalty reporting should link the current article and specify the relevant date.

The code sometimes expressly punishes attempts, and several provisions use formulas covering conduct that “attempts” to alter sincerity, secrecy, operations, or results. That matters even when a transparent box, an assessor, or reconciliation catches the act. But it is unsafe to assume that every incomplete act under every article is punishable without checking the article and the Penal Code rules that govern its classification.

## Criminal prosecution and presidential adjudication run on different tracks

For the presidency, article 58 of the Constitution assigns the Conseil constitutionnel supervision of the election, examination of complaints, and proclamation of results. That jurisdiction protects the lawful presidential result. Criminal courts determine individual guilt and punishment. The same facts can engage both tracks, but neither track is a substitute for the other.

The Conseil can exclude votes from a polling station because legal safeguards failed and sincerity cannot be assured without first identifying or convicting an offender. A prosecutor can investigate an unsuccessful attempt even though no votes are removed. A criminal conviction after proclamation does not automatically rewrite the national result. Conversely, exclusion or annulment is not a declaration that named people committed an electoral offense.

This division also governs language. “The Conseil annulled this bureau's votes” states an electoral remedy. “The prosecutor opened an investigation” states a procedural fact, not guilt. “The court convicted X under article L.92” states criminal adjudication. “Officials recorded a discrepancy” states evidence that may have several explanations. Combining these into “fraud was proved” erases the institutions and standards that make the claim testable.

Potential criminal conduct may reach police or prosecutors through a complaint, a report by officials, materials generated during canvassing, or a statutory transmission such as the one in article L.88. The public prosecutor controls the criminal response subject to French criminal procedure. An elector preserving a problem in the polling-station minutes is valuable for the electoral record but does not by itself commence or complete a criminal case.

## Why this matters for 2027

The 2027 presidential contest will invite fast claims about false registrations, proxy schemes, intimidation, ballot stuffing, observer exclusion, and altered totals. The offense map supplies a disciplined set of questions. What exact conduct is alleged? Which article plausibly describes it? Does the provision require fraud, knowledge, an offer, pressure, violence, or an attempt? Who had access and what role did that person hold? Which contemporaneous records exist? Has any competent authority verified the facts, and on which legal track?

This method neither dismisses misconduct nor amplifies it prematurely. It preserves the difference between a detected anomaly, an electoral-law violation, a suspected offense, a charge, and a conviction. It also keeps scale separate from classification. One attempted impersonation can be a real offense without establishing a national scheme; a widespread administrative failure can threaten confidence without proving coordinated criminal intent.

For each 2027 allegation, coverage should record the as-of date and the procedural posture, link primary documents, quantify the maximum number of ballots plausibly affected, and revisit the claim when an investigation or judgment changes its status. Claims involving campaign finance, media rules, or forbidden propaganda may engage other offense and sanction regimes and should be routed to [[French presidential campaign finance]], [[Media pluralism and speaking time in French elections]], or [[Official campaign period in French presidential elections]] rather than forced into polling-day fraud categories.
