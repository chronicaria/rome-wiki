France's presidential election is national in its choice and result but municipal in much of its physical execution: mayors and communes turn uniform law into registration decisions, polling places, staffed bureaux, counts, records, and—through eligible officeholders' presentations—access to the ballot itself.

## A national election built from communes

The president is elected in one national constituency. A ballot cast in Lille and one cast in a village in the Creuse enter the same nationwide calculation; a commune does not award presidential electors or a territorial block of votes. Yet the voter normally experiences the election through a commune: registration is attached to a communal electoral list, the voter is assigned to a local *bureau de vote*, the mayor designates polling premises, municipal personnel and local electors help operate them, and a communal result is recorded before results travel upward.

That combination is not a contradiction. France separates political decision from administrative production. The state and the organic law establish the presidential rules; the Constitutional Council supervises the regularity of the election, examines claims, conducts the national canvass, and proclaims the result. Prefects transmit instructions and supervise implementation territorially. Communes supply the last-mile institution. The election is therefore neither simply centralized nor a federation of local elections. It is a nationally governed operation executed through a highly dispersed municipal network.

France had 34,875 communes on 1 January 2026 according to the official geographic code. Their scale varies radically, from Paris and other large cities to communes with only a few dozen residents. The common legal framework must work across that range. This makes the commune both an administrative strength and a recurrent source of operational asymmetry: the same duties are easier to staff, specialize, and audit in a large city than in a tiny rural municipality, while small communes may possess local knowledge and dense civic ties that large administrations cannot reproduce.

The mayor's role also needs careful definition. A mayor is an elected municipal executive, but certain election tasks are performed as an agent of the state. The General Code of Local Authorities places the mayor under the administrative authority of the prefect when exercising state functions, including functions assigned by electoral law. A mayor organizing a presidential polling operation is therefore not free to redesign it according to a local political mandate. Municipal autonomy supplies premises, personnel, and practical judgment within nationally prescribed rules; prefectural and judicial supervision constrain that judgment.

## The electoral register begins locally

The modern registration system joins a national database to local decisions. INSEE maintains the *répertoire électoral unique* (REU), while mayors decide ordinary applications for entry and certain removals from their commune's list. Consular authorities perform corresponding functions abroad. The national register helps prevent fragmented local lists from quietly retaining duplicate registrations, but it does not eliminate municipal responsibility. An applicant's connection to the commune—residence, domicile, or another statutory basis—still has to be assessed, and decisions can be contested through the statutory commission and courts.

Each commune has a control commission for its electoral list. Its composition depends partly on municipal size and political configuration, but its broad purpose is stable: review the regularity of mayoral registration and removal decisions, decide specified administrative appeals, and ensure publication of the list. This distributes checking rather than leaving a politically interested mayor as the final local judge of who votes. Judicial remedies add another layer. The REU, commission review, public-list procedures, and court access should be understood as a system; none alone guarantees a flawless roll.

Registration illustrates why “the mayor runs the election” is too loose. The mayor makes legally defined decisions, INSEE manages the national infrastructure, a commission controls the list, and judges can correct errors. Before the 2027 contest, official election-specific instructions will specify deadlines and operations. Analysts should cite the then-current guidance rather than infer every detail from the 2022 cycle.

## From buildings to bureaux de vote

The prefect fixes the number and perimeter of polling stations on the mayor's proposal, under Article R.40 of the Electoral Code. The mayor then assigns polling places. The premises must permit regular, accessible, and secret voting. In practice, town halls, schools, community rooms, and other municipal buildings are common. A polling place is not merely a room with a ballot box: it needs voter-list materials, an identity-control position, ballot papers and envelopes, booths, a signature register, public circulation, counting tables, posted legal information, and a route usable by disabled voters.

Municipal preparation begins well before polling day. Communes receive and sort election material, prepare voter lists and signature registers, notify voters, arrange equipment, recruit or schedule staff, and coordinate with prefectural services. They must anticipate shortages and incidents without altering the equal rules of the contest. A missing ballot pile, inaccessible booth, late-opening station, or improperly composed bureau is a local fact with national constitutional significance because it can affect equal suffrage.

The mayor, deputy mayors, and municipal councillors are central to constituting bureaux. Under the Electoral Code, each bureau has a president, at least two assessors, and a secretary. Candidates may appoint assessors and alternates; when those appointments do not fill the statutory places, municipal mechanisms supply members. The president has authority to maintain order inside the polling station, but that authority is procedural, not partisan. Candidate delegates may monitor operations and require observations to be entered in the minutes. Voting and counting are public, allowing electors to observe subject to order and space.

The municipal institution thus performs two roles that must remain distinct. It supplies the organization, but it does not own the process. Competing candidates receive representation rights; electors can watch; commissions may inspect; prefectural services receive records; and the Constitutional Council can annul votes or take incidents into account. A well-run commune makes these controls possible rather than treating them as interference.

## Municipal scale changes the practical problem

Uniform legal rights do not imply identical local conditions. In a very small commune, officials and voters may know one another, a single polling station may cover the whole electorate, and recruiting a full team for a long Sunday can be difficult. In a major city, there are many bureaux, more professional administrative capacity, more candidate delegates, and greater logistical complexity. Neither setting is inherently more reliable. Familiarity can help establish identity in a small commune, but it can also make social pressure more visible; scale can support specialization in a city, but it multiplies handoffs and coordination points.

The law sometimes recognizes scale expressly. In communes of at least 1,000 inhabitants, voters must present an approved identity document; in smaller communes, no particular listed document is categorically compulsory, although the polling president must still be able to establish identity. In communes with more than 20,000 inhabitants, a commission controlling voting operations monitors the regular composition and conduct of polling bureaux. These thresholds solve different problems and should not be conflated. They do not create a general division between “secure” large communes and informal small ones.

Accessibility duties apply everywhere. Article L.62-2 requires polling places and voting techniques to be accessible to disabled people, whatever the disability. A commune may use temporary adaptations, but formal availability is not enough: the route, booth, writing surface, ballot box, and signature table must work in normal conditions. Small municipal budgets may make adaptation harder, yet the legal right does not shrink with the tax base. Prefectures and national guidance matter because local implementation capacity is unequal.

Polling hours similarly join national baseline and territorial adjustment. Presidential voting ordinarily runs from 8:00 to 19:00, with authorized local extensions no later than 20:00. The prefectural order, not a mayor's convenience, determines an extension. Communes then bear its staffing consequences. Researchers comparing turnout should remember that differently timed communes have unequal opportunity windows and different social profiles.

## Counting locally, deciding nationally

After the last admitted elector votes, the bureau establishes signatures, opens the ballot box, and organizes the count. Scrutineers work at public counting tables. Envelopes and ballots are reconciled, doubtful ballots are treated under legal categories, and results are announced and entered in the official minutes. The commune aggregates its bureaux where necessary and transmits records through the prefectural chain.

Municipal results are therefore provisional components, not autonomous declarations of the presidential outcome. Departmental canvassing commissions review and total them; records and complaints go onward; the Constitutional Council has the authoritative final role. This distinction matters during election-night reporting. A commune's published number may be accurate and politically revealing, but media aggregation does not replace legal canvassing, and a mayor cannot certify the national winner.

The minutes are a crucial bridge between local incident and national review. Bureau members, candidate representatives, or electors can preserve observations. Numerical discrepancies, improper ballots, disruptions, or failures to follow procedure then become reviewable evidence rather than anecdote. The Constitutional Council may cancel votes from a bureau or commune when irregularities warrant it, taking account of their nature and potential effect. Local administration is consequently part of constitutional adjudication: the quality of the paper trail determines what can later be established.

Commune-level returns are also an important public dataset. They allow territorial comparison below departments and reveal differences between central cities, suburbs, small towns, tourist areas, industrial basins, and rural territories. But a commune is not a voter type. Its result aggregates people with different occupations, incomes, ages, and motivations. In tiny communes, a handful of votes can produce dramatic percentage changes; in large communes, an average can conceal sharp neighbourhood divisions. Commune returns are evidence about places, not proof of individual behavior.

## Mayors as presenters of candidates

Mayors connect local government to the presidential field before election day through the system commonly called the *500 parrainages*. The legal act is a *présentation*. Mayors, delegated mayors, certain arrondissement mayors, and specified intercommunal and other officeholders belong to the eligible body. Each eligible official can present only one prospective candidate. The Constitutional Council receives, validates, and publishes presentations under the organic rules.

Mayors matter numerically because the communal map creates a very large body of eligible officeholders, many from small municipalities and many without a strong national party label. Campaigns lacking a dense parliamentary or regional network therefore devote substantial effort to contacting them. Yet a presentation must not automatically be reported as an ideological endorsement. An official may present a candidate to support pluralism or ballot access while intending to vote differently. Conversely, a public political endorsement is not necessarily a valid presentation received by the Council.

The rule's territorial design prevents qualification through a single fortress. A candidate needs at least 500 valid presentations from at least thirty departments or equivalent territories, with no more than one tenth of the required total—effectively fifty—coming from any one territory. This turns municipal reach into a test of organizational breadth. For 2027, claims that a campaign “has its signatures” remain claims until the Council's published validation.

Publicity is contested. Since the 2016 reform, the Council publishes all valid presentations rather than only a selected 500. Transparency makes official responsibility visible and permits auditing. Critics argue that publication can expose small-commune mayors to party, voter, or social pressure and deter presentations made only to broaden debate. Official reports document the concern, but public lists alone cannot establish its size or causal effect. Reporting should separate established rules, officials' testimony, and inference.

## Mayors as political intermediaries

Beyond formal duties, mayors are political actors. Their endorsements can signal local legitimacy, provide venues and networks, and help national candidates demonstrate territorial breadth. Associations of mayors articulate municipal priorities such as local finance, public services, housing, ecological transition, security, and decentralization. Presidential contenders use mayoral congresses and local visits to frame themselves as attentive to “the territories.”

The phrase can obscure more than it explains. France's mayors do not form a single rural bloc. They govern dense suburbs, wealthy metropolitan communes, shrinking villages, overseas municipalities, industrial towns, and fast-growing peri-urban areas. Party labels are unevenly meaningful, particularly in small communes. A list categorized *sans étiquette* or *divers* is not politically empty, but neither can it safely be assigned to a national camp without evidence.

Municipal elections also create organizational capital. Parties and movements with councillors, mayors, and intercommunal executives possess experienced officeholders, local voter knowledge, venues, volunteer networks, and eligible presenters. But municipal implantation does not translate mechanically into presidential votes. A popular mayor can endorse a candidate whom residents reject nationally; local coalitions often combine actors who divide in presidential politics; and the two-round national contest activates different issues and strategic choices.

For the 2027 election, mayoral support should therefore be measured in multiple columns: valid presentations, explicit endorsements, campaign committee participation, municipal-party control, and actual commune results. Combining them into one “mayor support” number invites false precision. The most defensible claim is functional: communes administer the vote, while mayors also supply one important network through which candidates qualify and seek territorial credibility.

## Accountability and failure modes

Because mayors are political figures administering a contest, neutrality safeguards matter. Municipal premises and communications cannot lawfully be converted into unequal campaign resources. Polling-station composition, candidate representation, public counting, written minutes, control commissions, prefectural oversight, and Constitutional Council review distribute authority. The point is not to assume mayoral bad faith; it is to make regularity demonstrable even when local political interests are strong.

Operational fragility can arise without fraud. Volunteer shortages, storms, inaccessible buildings, mistaken registers, material-delivery failures, cybersecurity or transmission problems, and inconsistent training can interrupt an otherwise honest operation. The response should distinguish remediation from accusation. A late opening documented and corrected is an incident; evidence that it selectively prevented voting is a more consequential claim. The Council evaluates concrete circumstances and effect rather than treating every procedural defect as proof of manipulation.

Mayoral nonperformance is not the end of the chain. The mayor's state functions are performed under prefectural authority, and election law supplies substitution and supervision mechanisms. The exact remedy depends on the duty and circumstances. Before 2027, prefectures will issue detailed instructions, and communes will update plans, staffing, accessible premises, and transmission procedures. Those documents will be better evidence of readiness than general assurances.

## Why this matters for 2027

The 2027 field will be narrated through leaders, polls, parties, and national television. The communal layer determines whether those national choices become an accessible and auditable vote. It also shapes the candidate field through presentations and supplies the finest official geography for interpreting the result. Ignoring communes means missing both the administrative mechanism and a major source of political organization.

Three distinctions should guide coverage. First, administration is not endorsement: a mayor who flawlessly operates a bureau may campaign for one candidate, oppose all of them, or remain publicly neutral. Second, presentation is not necessarily support: only the Council certifies the legal act, and the official's motive cannot be read from the list. Third, territory is not demography: a commune result describes an aggregate place, not the reasons each resident voted.

The best 2027 reporting will test preparedness locally while keeping the national legal chain in view. It will check prefectural orders, accessible premises, staffing, candidate representation, incidents recorded in minutes, validated presentations, and official results. It will avoid romanticizing village familiarity or assuming metropolitan professionalism. France's commune system works through redundancy: local knowledge and labor, standardized state rules, partisan scrutiny, public observation, documentary records, and judicial review reinforce one another.
