France's 2027 presidential field contains declared and party-selected contenders, but no one is yet a legally qualified candidate; every name must be read through dated evidence, coalition control, and the later 500-presentation process.

Up: [[2027 French presidential election]] · Related: [[2027 French presidential coalition map]] · [[The 500 presentations in French presidential elections]] · [[French presidential nomination mechanisms]]

## Status is evidence, not prominence

French pre-campaign coverage often uses *candidat* loosely for anyone expected to run. This ledger uses narrower labels because they answer different questions. **Discussed** means that media, allies, or pollsters mention a person without a personal declaration. **Exploring** means that the person is taking identifiable organizational steps while withholding a declaration. **Declared** means that the person has personally and unambiguously said that they intend to contest the presidency. **Party-selected** means that a party or participating alliance has completed a procedure that gives the person its nomination. **Qualified** is reserved for the list later established by the Conseil constitutionnel after the presentation and eligibility process.

These labels are cumulative only in a limited sense. A party-selected person has normally also declared, but selection does not create legal ballot access. A declared person can later withdraw. A court judgment, party vote, or coalition agreement can materially change a path without moving the person into the next legal category. No one should be described as an “official candidate” merely because a poll includes the name, a party leader endorses it, or a campaign says it has promises from 500 elected officials.

The table is deliberately asymmetric. It records stronger labels only when the underlying act is direct and dated. It does not attempt to reward prominence with a higher status. It also does not treat every name tested by an institute as a candidate. Pollsters construct hypothetical slates to answer survey questions; they do not register candidacies.

## The nationalist-right branch

Marine Le Pen is the declared RN path as of the research cutoff. Her status requires two qualifications. First, her declaration followed an appellate ruling whose legal consequences must be described precisely. Reuters reported that the Paris Court of Appeal upheld the conviction in the European Parliament assistants case, reduced the unsuspended ineligibility period to time already served, and left further cassation proceedings. The correct inference is that the appellate disposition presently leaves an electoral path. It is not that the conviction vanished or that later legal process is irrelevant.

Second, Le Pen's declaration does not make Jordan Bardella a co-candidate or successor candidate. Her stated plan assigns Bardella a possible governing role. Under the Fifth Republic, however, a president appoints the prime minister in a parliamentary environment shaped by the National Assembly; a campaign announcement cannot guarantee a durable majority or appointment under every post-election configuration. [[From the Élysée to a National Assembly majority]] explains that separate test.

Bardella remains important because party leadership, public profile, and previous polling make him the most obvious RN contingency. That is exactly why the label **discussed** matters: it can capture genuine strategic relevance without inventing a declaration. Poll scenarios substituting Bardella for Le Pen test a counterfactual field. They should not be averaged with Le Pen scenarios or reported as evidence that the substitution has occurred.

The rest of the nationalist and sovereignist right cannot be compressed into the RN line. Éric Zemmour, Marion Maréchal, Nicolas Dupont-Aignan, and other figures or parties may be discussed, organize, endorse, or contest independently. Each needs its own direct evidence before entering this canonical ledger at exploring or declared status. Ideological adjacency does not create shared nomination authority, and an eventual endorsement would not guarantee complete voter transfer.

## The post-Macron succession branch

Emmanuel Macron cannot serve a third consecutive term under Article 6 of the Constitution, so the outgoing governing space has no incumbent nomination to inherit. Gabriel Attal and Édouard Philippe are declared contenders with different organizational bases and different theories of legitimacy. Attal leads Renaissance and has advocated a selection process capable of consolidating the center. Philippe leads Horizons and has resisted making his candidacy dependent on such a primary.

That disagreement is substantive. A primary could create a shared result only for organizations and candidates that agree on its electorate, eligibility rules, timetable, and binding force. If Philippe does not participate, an Attal victory would not automatically demote Philippe from declared status. Conversely, Philippe's earlier declaration does not give Horizons authority over Renaissance, MoDem, or the wider center-right. The field can therefore contain more than one declared successor even when commentators speak of a single *bloc central*.

Other figures associated with the outgoing majority or center-right should be handled cautiously. Holding a senior office, creating a political association, publishing a program, or appearing in a poll may support **exploring** if it is tied to identifiable presidential organization. Otherwise the person remains **discussed**. The threshold prevents a succession story from becoming a list of every prominent minister.

The institutional resource behind this branch is broader than its polling percentage. Renaissance, Horizons, MoDem, and allied networks contain parliamentarians, local officeholders, donors, and campaign professionals. Those assets can assist with presentations and national organization. They do not settle which declared person receives them, and they cannot turn a coalition label into a legal nomination.

## Les Républicains: declaration plus selection

Bruno Retailleau currently has the strongest party-procedural label in the field. His 12 February statement plainly meets the declaration test. Les Républicains then asked members about the method of choosing the party's candidate. The party's 20 April report says turnout was 60.01% and, among members choosing between the proposed methods, 73.8% favored designating the party president; smaller shares preferred closed or sympathizer-open primaries. LR described the result as designating Retailleau as the movement's candidate.

That is sufficient for **party-selected** in this ledger, even though the consultation asked members to choose a procedure rather than staging a conventional multicandidate primary. The label records the party's own completed act and stated effect. It does not settle whether other right-wing organizations accept the result or whether a later bargain alters the field.

Retailleau's selection also demonstrates why the ledger separates organization from public support. A party can confer a real nomination while its candidate remains weaker in hypothetical national surveys. Conversely, a highly placed person can poll strongly without controlling a party procedure. Both facts matter, but they answer different questions.

## The left: selection disputes before status inflation

The left's central uncertainty is not just which personality is most popular. It is whether any process can bind organizations with different programs and strategic goals. LFI can argue that Mélenchon's three prior candidacies, activist infrastructure, and 2022 vote establish a focal point. Ecologists and other participants can argue for an open or common selection. Social democrats can reject both an LFI-led convergence and a procedure whose boundaries they do not control.

Jean-Luc Mélenchon now meets the **declared** threshold. LFI's 3 May proposal of his candidacy to a 150,000-citizen investiture, his own 10 May acceptance of the candidacy framing, and the movement's June campaign launch are stronger than observer expectation. The investiture's completion remains a separate party-procedural event; declaration does not permit the ledger to assume it.

Marine Tondelier is **declared** and is the Ecologists' selected representative in the proposed wider primary. This ledger reserves **party-selected** for the person chosen as a party's or alliance's presidential nominee, rather than a party representative entering a still-unfinished coalition selection. If she wins that binding wider vote—or if the Ecologists formally convert their representative designation into an autonomous presidential nomination—the stronger label becomes appropriate.

François Ruffin likewise meets the **declared** threshold through his January announcement. Raphaël Glucksmann, Fabien Roussel, and other relevant left figures should be treated in the same disciplined way. European-election performance, an association, speaking tours, programmatic positioning, or allies urging a run can establish political relevance. Only direct acts establish the stronger categories. A common primary, if held, would select a candidate only within the scope of participating organizations; it could not erase a nonparticipant's declaration.

## Qualification is a later official event

No person in this ledger is **qualified**. Under the legal framework explained in [[The 500 presentations in French presidential elections]], a prospective candidate needs at least 500 valid presentations from eligible officeholders, distributed across at least 30 departments or overseas units and respecting the concentration rule. Forms must be received and validated through the election-specific process. The Conseil constitutionnel then establishes the candidate list.

Before that window, campaigns may announce pledges or claim that they have enough *parrainages*. Such claims can be useful evidence of organization, but they are not certified totals. Even a party-selected contender with a dense local network must complete the legal process. Conversely, a smaller party can qualify through disciplined territorial work despite modest polling.

The same caution applies to eligibility litigation. A court ruling may remove or restore a plausible route, but the canonical status should describe what the ruling actually does, any suspensive appeal, and the remaining official steps. Legal possibility is not qualification; unresolved risk is not automatic disqualification.

## How this ledger should change

Update a row only for a traceable status event: a personal declaration, an explicit withdrawal, a completed party or alliance procedure, a consequential final or operative court ruling, or the Conseil constitutionnel's official list. Preserve the old status and transition date in prose rather than silently rewriting history. Add a new person when direct evidence establishes at least exploring status or when the person is essential to understanding a live succession or selection contingency.

Do not update for an anonymous report alone, a poll's tested slate, a television host's prediction, an ally's wish, or a campaign appearance that never states presidential intent. Multiple articles repeating the same anonymous briefing remain one unconfirmed claim. When French wording is ambiguous—*candidat naturel*, *prêt*, *disponible*, or *vocation à*—quote or translate narrowly and wait for an unmistakable act.

The ledger also should not become a forecast table. Status describes verified procedural position. Viability requires separate analysis of first-round support, ballot access, coalition breadth, runoff expansion, finance, and capacity to govern. [[2027 French presidential coalition map]] owns that larger structure, while [[How to read French presidential polls]] explains why tested scenarios cannot be collapsed into a ranking of actual candidates.

## Why it matters

Candidate-status discipline prevents three recurrent errors. It stops hypothetical polling from manufacturing candidacies. It stops party declarations from being mistaken for official ballot access. And it stops political relevance from being translated into a false claim that a person has personally chosen to run.

The result is not merely semantic caution. Different statuses imply different failure modes. A discussed figure may never enter. An exploring figure may lose or abandon a selection process. A declared contender may fail to consolidate a party or collect valid presentations. A party-selected contender may still confront litigation, finance, or qualification barriers. A qualified candidate may still lack a plausible runoff or governing coalition. Keeping the categories separate makes later changes legible instead of forcing every development into “in” or “out.”
