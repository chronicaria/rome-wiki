# What is String Theory

**One line:** A framework proposing that nature's fundamental constituents aren't point particles but tiny vibrating one-dimensional *strings*, whose different vibration modes appear to us as the different particles.

## The core idea
In the Standard Model, an electron or a quark is a mathematical *point* — zero size. String theory replaces each point with a string roughly the Planck length (~10⁻³⁵ m), far too small to see directly. Like a violin string, it can vibrate in different modes, and **each mode looks like a different particle** with a particular mass and charge. So one kind of object, vibrating differently, could in principle reproduce the whole particle zoo.

## Why physicists care
- A string's vibration spectrum *automatically* includes a massless spin-2 mode — and a massless spin-2 particle is exactly what a **graviton** (the quantum of gravity) must be. Gravity falls out of the theory rather than being bolted on, which is why string theory is the leading candidate for [[The graviton and quantum gravity|quantum gravity]] — uniting general relativity with quantum mechanics.
- It is highly constrained: mathematical consistency forces specific features (see below) rather than letting you tune anything.

## The strings attached (the hard parts)
- **Extra dimensions.** Consistency requires more than the 3+1 spacetime dimensions we observe — 26 for the early *bosonic* string, **10** for *superstring* theory. The extra ones are presumed curled up too small to notice → [[Why string theory needs extra dimensions]].
- **Five theories, then M-theory.** There were five consistent superstring theories; in the 1990s they were shown to be limits of a single 11-dimensional framework, **M-theory** → [[M-theory and the five string theories]].
- **No experimental confirmation.** The natural energy scale is the Planck scale, hopelessly beyond current colliders. As of now there is *no* direct experimental evidence, which is the central criticism of the program.
- **The landscape.** The extra dimensions can curl up in a staggering number of ways (~10⁵⁰⁰ estimates), each giving different physics — so the theory struggles to predict *our* universe uniquely → [[The landscape problem]].
