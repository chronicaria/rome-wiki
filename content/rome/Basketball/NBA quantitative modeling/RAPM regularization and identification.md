Ridge regularization makes the lineup problem numerically stable, but it does not erase the identification limits created by shared minutes, selective roles, and an additive observational design.

## Why the unregularized problem is unstable

In [[RAPM lineup model]], player columns are generated by actual coaching choices rather than an experiment. Starters share many possessions. Bench units appear in particular contexts. A traded player may be observed under only two schemes. Some low-minute teammates never separate.

These patterns make columns of the lineup matrix nearly dependent. Ordinary least squares can fit the possession outcomes with implausibly large positive and negative coefficients whose sum is stable but whose individual values are not. Small changes to the sample can flip credit between correlated players.

Ridge addresses this by paying a quadratic cost for coefficient magnitude. With a nonzero prior mean $\mu$, the player part of the objective is

$$
\lambda_o\lVert\beta_o-\mu_o\rVert^2
+
\lambda_d\lVert\beta_d-\mu_d\rVert^2.
$$

This chooses a stable solution among many nearly equivalent lineup explanations. In Bayesian language, the penalty corresponds to a Gaussian prior centered on $\mu$; the pure model sets $\mu=0$.

## Component-specific shrinkage

The production configuration does not pretend every target has the same signal-to-noise ratio. It uses separate offense and defense penalties for the eight underlying components. Current penalties span roughly $750$ to $6{,}000$ on offense and $750$ to $24{,}000$ on defense. Noisier defensive three-point attribution receives much stronger shrinkage than better-identified components.

A larger $\lambda$ has a concrete meaning: the data need more effective possession evidence to move a player far from the prior center. This improves stability but compresses the tails. A ranking can change because the evidence changed, the penalty changed, or the scale calibration changed; model comparisons must hold those layers straight.

The garbage-time nuisance block uses a much smaller penalty, currently $\lambda_{rb}=10$, so context columns can absorb systematic noncompetitive-game effects rather than forcing player coefficients to explain them.

## Centering and scale

Each solve removes a decay-weighted target mean before estimating player effects. The ridge penalty then selects a particular offensive/defensive allocation from a design with weak location identification. After solving, component offsets calibrate the NBA output onto the project's DataBallR reference scale. Under the exact [[Six-factor RAPM decomposition|six-factor closure]], `net_offset` is zero and the six component offsets carry the scale alignment.

This calibration is a coordinate choice, not new basketball evidence. It lets the model reproduce a familiar public range and compare versions, while the lineup contrasts determine relative player values.

## Informative priors

The prior-informed path supplies player-day component means derived from causal box and predictive EPM features. The implementation subtracts the design's contribution at $\mu$, solves the zero-centered residual problem, then adds $\mu$ back. For a simplified orthogonal column with effective exposure $n$, the posterior mean behaves like

$$
\hat\beta \approx \frac{n}{n+\lambda}\hat\beta_{lineup}
+\frac{\lambda}{n+\lambda}\mu.
$$

Real lineup columns are not orthogonal, but the expression captures the intended behavior: thin samples lean on the prior and rich samples lean on possession evidence. The prior is helpful only to the degree that it is causal, well calibrated, and predictive for the factor in question. The project therefore keeps a pure series as an essential control.

## Identification limits that remain

Regularization answers “which stable coefficient vector should this design prefer?” It does not turn observational lineup data into randomized causal effects.

- **Persistent pairs:** teammates who rarely separate still share credit according to the penalty and surrounding lineups.
- **Role selection:** a player's coefficient includes the environments coaches choose for him.
- **Scheme and interaction:** an additive model can distribute synergy or incompatibility across individual terms.
- **Opponent targeting:** the response to a player may alter which actions opponents attempt, complicating the factor label.
- **Data coverage:** structural zeros and missing play-by-play weaken particular eras and targets.

The safe interpretation is a regularized adjusted association, conditional on the design and scale.

## Standard errors are a known approximation

The current per-component standard error is exposure based:

$$
se_j \approx 100\frac{\hat\sigma}{\sqrt{Weff_j}}.
$$

This correctly widens bands for thin samples, but it is not the diagonal of the full ridge covariance matrix. It ignores the geometry of $(X^\top W X+\Lambda)^{-1}$. Two teammates with the same exposure can receive the same reported standard error even when one is much more collinear and therefore less separately identified.

The net standard error also requires a factor-covariance adjustment; simply summing six variances assumes independent component errors. The project uses empirical calibration and inflation artifacts, but [[RAPM evaluation and uncertainty]] treats the full ridge diagonal and factor covariance as correctness work still worth doing.

## Open questions

- How much would full ridge-covariance standard errors widen bands for persistent teammate pairs?
- Should scale calibration be refit on a larger reference panel rather than a small set of public targets?
- Can partial pooling across positions or roles improve identification without smuggling box-score bias into the pure model?
- Which interactions are stable enough out of sample to justify moving beyond additivity?
