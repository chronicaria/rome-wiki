Dallas can distribute ball security, passing, and secondary scoring across several players, but the post-turnover roster still has no verified replacement for Kyrie Irving's ability to create the first advantage and a credible late-clock shot against a set defense.

**Current state — as of July 9, 2026:** the verified Dallas guard-creation group is built around Irving, Cooper Flagg, Ryan Nembhard, newly acquired Marcus Sasser, and secondary handlers such as Max Christie and Naji Marshall. Tyus Jones is not a current Maverick: Denver officially signed him on March 5 after his eight-game Dallas stop, and the NBA's July offseason tracker lists his agreement to return to Denver. Any Dallas depth chart that still assigns Jones minutes is using a stale roster state.

## Creation is four different jobs

Calling every player who can dribble a “creator” hides the Mavericks' actual problem. Half-court offense asks for at least four separable functions:

1. **Organizing:** advancing the ball safely, calling the action, delivering passes on time, and preventing empty possessions.
2. **Creating the first advantage:** forcing the on-ball defender or a helper to move far enough that the defense enters rotation.
3. **Extending an advantage:** attacking a closeout, making the next pass, or converting a four-on-three after someone else bends the defense.
4. **Producing scoring pressure:** threatening a shot or drive strongly enough that a defender cannot simply stay home, especially late in the clock.

The first and third jobs can be shared relatively well. The second and fourth become scarce against good defenses. A low-turnover point guard can organize without forcing help. A wing can punish a tilted floor without being able to tilt it himself. A shooter can create gravity without being able to navigate a trap. Conversely, a high-volume scorer can bend the defense while making unstable reads. Dallas's task is therefore not to identify one nominal “backup point guard.” It is to assemble lineups in which the four jobs coexist.

Irving is the only current Maverick who has already demonstrated the full package at a high NBA level. He can bring the ball up against pressure, manipulate a screen defender, score from every level, make the help defender commit, and manufacture a contested attempt when the planned action dies. His ACL recovery makes the question unusually urgent: the team must prepare both for ordinary non-Irving minutes and for a branch in which his availability or effectiveness is limited. Those are different workloads, but the same functional shortage appears in each.

## The turnover removed the easy veteran answer

Dallas's guard room has changed twice in a few months. The February 2026 Anthony Davis transaction sent D'Angelo Russell, Jaden Hardy, and Dante Exum to Washington while returning several players and assets. Dallas separately acquired Tyus Jones at the deadline, but his stay lasted only eight games before Denver signed him in March. The July 8 six-team transaction then sent AJ Johnson out and brought Marcus Sasser in from Detroit.

The net effect is important. Dallas did not merely exchange one bench guard for another. It lost or moved several players who could absorb possessions, then entered July without Jones, the veteran organizer whom some local summaries still treat as part of the roster. The current fallback hierarchy is younger and more specialized: Nembhard supplies organization; Sasser supplies pull-up and shooting possibility; Flagg supplies large-scale wing initiation; Christie and Marshall are better suited to attacking advantages than creating them from neutral; and Irving supplies the only proven elite primary package.

That distinction also corrects the apparent depth-chart comfort. “Irving, Jones, Sasser, Nembhard” looks like four point guards. The verified roster is “Irving, Sasser, Nembhard,” with Flagg performing major initiation and wings contributing selective handling. Removing the stale Jones entry does not make Dallas devoid of passers. It does make the gap between orderly possession management and playoff-grade advantage creation harder to ignore.

## Ryan Nembhard is the clearest organizer

Nembhard's rookie line establishes a real NBA skill rather than a theoretical one. NBA.com credits him with 6.6 points and 5.3 assists in 19.5 minutes across 60 games, with 1.4 turnovers per game and a 3.72 assist-to-turnover ratio. In 27 starts he averaged 9.4 points, 6.8 assists, and 2.9 rebounds while shooting 40.4 percent from three, according to the Mavericks' own offseason material. Dallas converted his two-way deal to a multiyear standard contract in March and exercised his 2026-27 team option in June.

Those facts support a specific claim: Nembhard can organize NBA possessions. He sees passing windows, keeps the ball moving, and can preserve structure when the first option is unavailable. The season-ending 23-assist game against Chicago—the NBA's highest single-game assist total of 2025-26 and a Mavericks rookie record—shows his upper-end passing vision, even though one April game cannot establish a normal offensive level.

Organization is not the same as pressure. Nembhard used only 17.3 percent of possessions in his minutes, and NBA.com lists a 49.2 true-shooting percentage for the season. His 5-foot-11 frame gives opponents an obvious place to test Dallas defensively and may make it harder for him to finish or pass over switching size. A defense that does not fear his pull-up or rim attack can go under screens, keep the weak-side defender attached, and invite him to become the scorer. In that response, a clean assist-to-turnover profile does not by itself create a quality shot.

The best Nembhard role is therefore as a possession steward beside a player who generates pressure. With Irving, he can reduce the burden of ball advancement and let Irving begin actions from more dangerous locations. With Flagg, he can call the set, deliver the entry, relocate, and make the next read while Flagg attacks the defense's strongest wing. In bench groups, Nembhard needs either Sasser's pull-up threat, Flagg's downhill size, or enough movement shooting that the scheme creates the first opening for him. Asking Nembhard to be Irving would misuse evidence of one skill as proof of another.

## Marcus Sasser adds scoring possibility, not certainty

The July trade adds a different type of small guard. Sasser has played 166 regular-season games across three Detroit seasons. NBA.com lists career averages of 7.0 points and 2.7 assists in 15.8 minutes, with 38.6 percent three-point shooting. In 2025-26 he played 38 games, averaged 5.2 points and 2.0 assists in 12.0 minutes, and shot 41.5 percent from three on 2.8 attempts per game.

That profile matters because shooting and pull-up willingness can force a different coverage than pure organization. A guard who will punish a defender for going under a screen can begin to create an advantage even before reaching the paint. Sasser can also attack a second side after Flagg or Irving draws two defenders. Compared with a low-usage caretaker, he offers a more plausible route to self-generated points.

But the evidence has strict limits. Sasser's NBA role has been small, he has started 17 career games, and his 2025-26 sample was only 456 minutes. His career scoring average and free-throw volume do not describe a guard who has repeatedly lived at the rim or carried a primary burden. At 6-foot-1, he may face the same defensive size questions as Nembhard, particularly if Dallas plays them together. Three-point percentage in a reserve sample does not prove that the shot will hold when attempts become more difficult, the best perimeter defender changes assignments, or a playoff opponent switches length onto him.

Sasser should initially be treated as a **scoring-pressure candidate** and an **advantage extender**, not a verified lead creator. Camp and preseason should test whether he can reject screens, reach the paint, manipulate the low man, and pass accurately after drawing a second defender. If he only converts open threes and takes pull-ups without shifting help, he can still be a useful rotation scorer. That outcome simply would not solve the non-Irving creation problem.

## Cooper Flagg is the highest-upside secondary engine

Flagg is the roster's best internal path to reducing dependence on Irving. As a rookie he averaged 21.0 points, 6.7 rebounds, and 4.5 assists in 70 starts, leading Dallas in total points, rebounds, assists, and steals. After the All-Star break, the Mavericks reported 5.4 assists against 2.5 turnovers per game across 21 appearances. Dallas had already used him as a point forward, and his size lets him see and deliver passes that the smaller guards cannot.

Flagg creates pressure in a different way from Irving. He can drive through a smaller defender, attack a mismatch from the elbow, grab-and-go in transition, and draw help with force rather than with Irving's change of pace and handle. That allows Dallas to invert the offense: Nembhard can technically occupy point guard while Flagg performs the possession's decisive creation. It also makes Flagg a natural hub for split cuts, handoffs, and short-roll decisions in a passing-oriented Dusty May system.

The unresolved part is half-court repeatability. Flagg shot about 29 percent from three as a rookie. If defenders can go under his ball screens or station a strong wing in the driving lane, his first move may not create the same rotation against a prepared defense. Increasing his initiation also raises the difficulty of his reads and may reduce the energy available for demanding defensive assignments. His strong counting production proves he can carry substantial offense; it does not yet prove he can solve every late-clock or postseason coverage.

The developmental target should be **co-creation**, not immediate substitution for Irving. Flagg should receive possessions in which he initiates against a set defense, but lineups should give him an organizer, shooting, and a vertical target. Nembhard can get the ball to the right place. Sasser or Klay Thompson can punish under-help. Dereck Lively II or Daniel Gafford can screen and finish above the rim. Santi Aldama can space, pass, or run a second action. These supports allow Dallas to learn whether Flagg can bend the first defender without forcing every possession into a referendum on his jumper.

## Christie, Marshall, and the frontcourt are advantage extenders

Christie's 2025-26 scoring growth does not automatically make him a primary guard creator. He averaged 12.3 points but only 2.0 assists. NBA tracking data offer a sharper role clue: on attempts after touches shorter than two seconds, he shot 46.1 percent from the floor and 43.5 percent from three; after holding the ball six seconds or longer, he shot 34.1 percent overall and 12.9 percent from three. Those splits are descriptive, not causal, and difficult late-clock attempts likely populate the long-touch group. Still, they strongly favor using Christie as a quick-decision finisher—catch, shoot, drive, or move it—rather than asking him to dominate the ball.

Marshall fits a similar conceptual bucket. He can handle in transition, drive a closeout, punish a mismatch, and make connective passes. Aldama can receive at the elbow, attack a slow big, and keep a possession flowing. Lively and Gafford create vertical pressure as screeners. Thompson creates off-ball gravity. Each can make the offense harder to guard, but most require the initial matchup displacement to come from Irving, Flagg, Sasser, or the scheme.

This is not a semantic demotion. Advantage extension is how good offenses turn one opening into a high-value shot. It becomes a problem only when Dallas mistakes a roster full of capable second-side players for a roster full of first-side creators. Against ordinary regular-season defense, pace, passing, and talent may blur the difference. Against switching size or a playoff opponent that has removed transition, the distinction becomes the offense.

## Functional lineup architecture

The cleanest way to allocate creation is by complementary pairs rather than by nominal position:

| Pairing | Organization | First advantage | Scoring pressure | Principal risk |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Irving + Flagg | High | Elite/proven from Irving; high-upside from Flagg | High | Irving workload and Flagg shooting |
| Nembhard + Flagg | High | Primarily Flagg | Medium | Defense can load toward Flagg and ignore Nembhard |
| Sasser + Flagg | Medium | Shared, still unverified at lead scale | Medium-to-high if Sasser's pull-up translates | Turnovers, size, difficult decision load |
| Nembhard + Sasser | High | Uncertain | Medium | Two small guards and no large primary initiator |
| Irving + Christie | Medium-to-high | Irving | High | Christie must stay in quick-decision role |
| Flagg + Christie/Marshall | Medium | Flagg | Medium | Too much burden on Flagg if spacing slips |

These pairings suggest three rotation principles. First, stagger Irving and Flagg whenever health permits so that at least one meaningful advantage creator is on the floor. Second, pair Nembhard with pressure rather than asking his organization to masquerade as pressure. Third, give Sasser controlled primary repetitions to determine whether his scoring translates upward, but preserve a second organizer or passing hub while the answer is unknown.

May's stated interest in pace and passing can improve the environment. Early offense reduces the number of possessions in which Dallas must beat five organized defenders. Multiple initiators can keep Irving from laboring through every ball advance. Movement can make Christie's quick decisions and Aldama's passing more valuable. Yet scheme redistributes hard possessions; it does not abolish them. Every close game still produces possessions after the opponent switches the first action, denies the preferred entry, and leaves eight seconds on the clock. Dallas needs evidence about who owns those possessions when Irving sits.

## What would count as an answer

Summer League cannot answer the entire question because opponent quality, roster continuity, and defensive game planning differ from the NBA. It can clarify Nembhard's command and whether young guards create separation. Training camp can reveal May's preferred functional pairings. Preseason can show who initiates against NBA size. The strongest evidence will come in regular-season non-Irving minutes and, eventually, against defenses that deliberately take away the first option.

The useful indicators are role-based rather than merely scoring-based:

- Can Nembhard preserve his decision quality when opponents pressure him full court or switch size onto him?
- Does Sasser force screen defenders above the level, reach the paint, and create passes after help—not merely make open threes?
- Can Flagg create efficient first advantages when defenders go under screens or load the nail?
- Do Christie and Marshall remain efficient when limited to fast decisions, and does the offense deteriorate when their touches lengthen?
- Can Dallas keep one of Irving or Flagg on court without overloading Irving's return or depriving Flagg of developmental repetitions?
- Which lineups survive defensively if two small guards play together?

Until those questions are tested, the most defensible conclusion is asymmetric. Dallas has credible organization behind Irving because Nembhard proved he can pass and protect possessions. It has plausible secondary scoring because Sasser can shoot and Flagg can carry offense. It has abundant advantage extension through Christie, Marshall, Aldama, and its finishers. What it does not yet have is a verified second player who combines advantage creation, scoring pressure, and late-clock reliability against high-level set defense.

That is why Irving's health matters beyond his box score and why Flagg's year-two development is the roster's most important offensive swing. If Flagg becomes a dependable primary or co-primary initiator and either Nembhard or Sasser complements him, Dallas can cover non-Irving minutes by committee. If not, the offense may look orderly without being forceful: clean entries, low turnovers, and competent passing that still end in difficult shots once the defense refuses to rotate.

## Open questions

- What workload and effectiveness can Irving sustain after ACL reconstruction, separately from whether he is active?
- Does Sasser's pull-up shooting force NBA defenses to change coverage when he receives more primary possessions?
- Can Nembhard's organization survive playoff-style pressure and switching size without another elite creator beside him?
- Which half-court actions does May use to create the first advantage when both Irving and Flagg sit?
- Does Flagg's jumper improve enough that defenders must chase him over screens rather than loading the lane?
- Can any two-small-guard pairing create enough offense to offset the defensive matchup cost?
