Dallas cannot solve spacing by counting nominal shooters; it must allocate shot volume, locations, movement, screening, and creation so that each lineup forces defenders to make costly choices.

## Current state — as of July 10, 2026

Dallas officially acquired Santi Aldama and Marcus Sasser on July 8, but its public roster grid still lagged that completed transaction on July 10. This analysis therefore uses the official transaction announcement for personnel identity and treats exact minutes as unsettled. It does not assign roles to departed players merely because the roster display has not updated.

The relevant evidence points in different directions. Cooper Flagg produced 21.0 points and 4.5 assists per game as a rookie, but made 29.5 percent of 3.5 three-point attempts per game. Aldama made 35.0 percent on 4.7 attempts in 27.9 minutes in 2025-26. Sasser made 41.5 percent on 2.8 attempts in 12.0 minutes, but that was a 38-game, 456-minute reserve sample. Klay Thompson remains the roster's clearest established movement-shooting archetype, while P. J. Washington, Max Christie, Naji Marshall, Ryan Nembhard, Kyrie Irving, and the centers supply different combinations of accuracy, willingness, handling, and interior pressure.

Those facts do not add up to one team percentage. Dallas's spacing will be the product of five simultaneous assignments: who bends the defense, who occupies the rim, who pins help away from the ball, who receives the conceded attempt, and who can preserve the advantage after the first closeout. A lineup can contain four respectable career shooters and still be cramped if two are reluctant, one cannot shoot on the move, and the only creator is crowded by a non-shooting center. It can contain two below-average percentages and still create room if the defense respects the attempts, the screen angle, and the threat behind the play.

## Spacing is defender behavior, not a roster label

The useful definition of spacing is how far and how urgently defenders must travel. A 40-percent shooter who attempts only uncontested corner threes may not pull a center above the level of a screen. A 35-percent big who will immediately fire above the break can change a coverage because conceding that shot repeatedly is expensive. A poor shooter can still create temporary space by screening, cutting behind a helper, or occupying the dunker spot at the right moment. Conversely, two players standing in opposite corners are not producing good geometry if both defenders can stunt at the ball and recover without urgency.

Dallas should therefore classify shooting roles along at least four axes:

1. **Accuracy:** whether the shot has produced acceptable value over a meaningful sample.
2. **Volume and willingness:** whether the player takes the attempt quickly enough to punish help.
3. **Difficulty and movement:** whether the player can shoot after relocation, a handoff, a screen, or a pull-up rather than only from a stationary catch.
4. **Secondary response:** whether the player can attack a closeout, pass to the next opening, or rescreen when the shot is denied.

The distinctions matter because Dallas's best young player is not yet a proven floor spacer. Flagg's rookie three-point percentage gives opponents a rational reason to test him. His size, driving force, passing, and interior scoring give Dallas reasons not to park him in the corner merely to satisfy a positional template. The offense should arrange shooting around his creation while also giving him repetitions that can improve the shot. That is an allocation problem: development, present efficiency, and lineup function must coexist.

## The shot portfolio begins with advantage creation

The cleanest threes usually arrive after the defense has moved. Irving, when healthy, can force two defenders toward a ball screen, beat a switch, or draw nail help on a drive. Flagg can create pressure through size, transition force, elbow attacks, and developing pick-and-roll work. Sasser may add pull-up pressure, but his small NBA role does not yet prove that he can command the strongest perimeter defender or create efficient offense at high volume. Nembhard has demonstrated organization and passing more clearly than scoring pressure.

This hierarchy determines who should receive stationary opportunities. If Irving or Flagg creates the first advantage, Christie, Washington, Aldama, Thompson, Marshall, or Sasser can become the release valve. If Dallas instead asks three finishers to create independently, their percentages will include more late-clock and self-generated difficulty. Shooting allocation cannot be separated from [[Dallas guard creation after roster turnover]] because the same player can look like a strong spacer beside Irving and a weak primary option when asked to manufacture the opening.

The team should protect two things at once. First, at least one credible initiator should be present in most competitive minutes. Second, that initiator should see at least three defenders who cannot abandon their assignments without paying a price. A vertical threat can count as one of those three: Dereck Lively II or Daniel Gafford makes the rim defender account for a lob even without taking a jumper. But two interior-only players alongside a developing shooter can let the defense crowd the lane unless the action moves one big away from the rim or turns the other into a screener and passer.

## Thompson supplies movement gravity

Thompson's distinctive value is not reducible to a catch-and-shoot percentage. His history and release force defenders to trail him across screens, top-lock routes, communicate exchanges, and resist helping from his body. That movement can open a driving lane before Thompson touches the ball. It can also create a false choice: stay attached and leave Flagg against a tilted floor, or help and concede a rapid three.

Dallas should use this gravity selectively rather than making every possession a Thompson shot hunt. At age 36 entering 2026-27, workload and defensive matchup matter. The best use may be concentrated sequences in which he runs a defender through Flagg or Aldama screens, then exchanges roles. Flagg can screen and short-roll if two defenders follow Thompson. Aldama can pop after a handoff. A center can flip the screen and dive. The goal is to convert movement into a chain of decisions, not merely a difficult attempt.

Thompson also helps diagnose spacing. If a lineup functions only when its oldest movement shooter is sprinting through multiple actions, its base geometry may be fragile. Dallas needs stationary and second-side threats that survive when Thompson rests, when opponents switch his routes, or when his defender successfully denies the catch.

## Aldama changes where a big defender stands

Aldama's 35.0 percent from three in 2025-26 is less important in isolation than the combination of height, attempt rate, and frontcourt position. Nearly five attempts in fewer than 28 minutes communicates willingness. When he plays beside Lively or Gafford, Aldama can pull the second big away from the paint while the center rolls. When he plays center, all five Mavericks can begin outside the lane.

The two uses carry different defensive costs, as [[Santi Aldama in Dallas lineups]] explains. At forward, Dallas preserves traditional rim protection and vertical spacing but may be large and slower on the perimeter. At center, the offense becomes more open while rim deterrence and defensive rebounding must be redistributed. Shooting allocation should not assume that the offensively cleanest shape is the best five-man unit.

Aldama should receive above-the-break attempts, pick-and-pop touches, and trail threes because those shots create the greatest geometric pressure. Corner accuracy still matters, but using a seven-footer exclusively in the corner forfeits his screening and connective passing. If an opponent switches, he can seal or facilitate over a smaller player. If the big stays low, he should shoot. If the defense closes hard, he can drive or make the next pass. These branches turn a three-point threat into an offense rather than a spreadsheet cell.

## Sasser's percentage needs a difficulty test

Sasser's 41.5 percent in 2025-26 is encouraging and potentially important. It came on only 2.8 attempts per game in a 12-minute role, however. Increasing minutes changes more than the number of attempts. The defender may be better, the closeouts longer, the shots later in the clock, and the proportion of pull-ups higher. Dallas should treat Sasser as a shooting-pressure candidate, not pencil his reserve percentage directly into a larger role.

The early evaluation should separate stationary catches, relocation threes, dribble pull-ups, and shots created after he rejects or uses a screen. Catch-and-shoot success would make him a useful partner for Flagg. Pull-up success would have larger structural value because it could punish defenders for going under and help create the first advantage. The passing response matters too. If a second defender steps up, can Sasser deliver to the roller or opposite corner on time? A made pull-up and a forced rotation are different outputs, but both can improve Dallas's geometry.

His size also affects allocation. Sasser and Nembhard together could supply organization and shooting, yet give opponents two small defensive targets. If the pairing forces Dallas to add more defense and size at the other positions, the resulting lineup may remove a shooter elsewhere. Offensive spacing and defensive survivability must be optimized together.

## Flagg needs functional space and developmental shots

Flagg's 29.5 percent rookie mark should neither be ignored nor treated as destiny. Defenses will begin by going under some screens, helping from him in selected lineups, and asking him to convert. Dallas should respond in three layers.

First, keep him involved. A weak-side defender can help more easily from a stationary Flagg than from a Flagg who is screening, cutting, receiving at the elbow, or preparing to attack a closeout. Making him the screener for Irving can force a switch or create a short-roll playmaking chance. Placing him at the elbow can let him see cutters and punish smaller defenders without requiring the three to initiate every action.

Second, preserve real attempts. Dallas should not hide the jumper so completely that development stalls. Open catch-and-shoot threes, especially above-the-break shots that arise naturally from his handling role, are useful repetitions. The meaningful indicators are release confidence, defender distance, shot preparation, and performance by location and action—not one rolling percentage after ten games.

Third, avoid lineups that magnify the weakness. Flagg with two paint-bound bigs and a low-pressure organizer may face a packed lane. The same Flagg with Aldama spacing, Thompson moving, and one vertical center can attack gaps while still using size. This is why [[Cooper Flagg and two-big lineup creation burden]] is a distinct question: “two bigs” can mean one roller plus one spacer, or two defenders stationed close enough to shrink every drive.

## The centers create vertical spacing

Lively and Gafford should not be described simply as non-shooters. Their screens, rolls, lob threat, offensive rebounding, and finishes occupy the rim defender. Vertical spacing can be especially valuable beside Irving, who manipulates the screen defender, and Flagg, who can throw over smaller help.

But vertical spacing has a congestion cost when the ballhandler does not force the defense to react. A center standing in the dunker spot cannot create room by reputation alone. If the guard's defender goes under and the opposing big stays home, the lane remains crowded. Dallas must pair its centers with pull-up pressure, screening angles that create momentum, and weak-side shooting that prevents early tags.

Two-center minutes require a more deliberate solution. One big can screen and dive while the other operates from the elbow, short corner, or as an off-ball screener. The second big must pass, screen, or cut on time; simply occupying the opposite dunker spot invites one defender to guard the rim and monitor two players. Aldama makes large lineups easier because he can stand outside, but Washington and Flagg may also perform quasi-big screening jobs without sacrificing all perimeter skill.

## Five lineup archetypes

### Irving, Thompson, wing, Flagg, Lively

This group has one elite creator, one movement specialist, a large secondary creator, and a vertical target. The fifth player's shooting and defense decide whether opponents can load toward Flagg. Its main risk is health and age: Dallas should not require Irving and Thompson to supply every hard perimeter skill for long stretches.

### Nembhard, Sasser, wing, Flagg, Gafford

This group tests whether organization, pull-up possibility, and Flagg's size can replace Irving's first advantage. It may offer four credible decision-makers around a roller. Its risks are two-small-guard defense and the possibility that opponents ignore Nembhard, switch length onto Sasser, and crowd Flagg.

### Irving, Christie, Flagg, Aldama, Lively

This is the large, skilled base concept. Irving bends the defense; Christie makes quick decisions; Aldama spaces and connects; Lively dives; Flagg attacks the second side. If Christie and Aldama are honored, the geometry is strong. If Irving or Lively is limited, the lineup's defining chain changes.

### Sasser, Christie, Marshall, Washington, Aldama

This centerless bench concept can place five players outside the lane and test Sasser's pull-up role. It may switch defensively and attack a slow center. It can also concede the rim and glass, while lacking a proven elite creator. It is a matchup counter, not a default conclusion.

### Organizer, Thompson, Flagg, Washington, Gafford

This lineup gives the organizer a movement shooter, large initiator, two-way forward, and roller. The spacing can work if Flagg's defender cannot abandon him and Washington shoots willingly. If the defense loads toward Flagg, Dallas may produce orderly possessions that end without sufficient rim or pull-up pressure.

## What Dallas should measure

Team three-point percentage is an outcome, not a diagnosis. Dallas should track the process by lineup and creator:

- defender distance and whether a player actually takes the conceded shot;
- corner, above-the-break, movement, and pull-up attempt shares;
- how often a ball screen draws two defenders or moves the big above the level;
- drives, paint touches, rim attempts, and free throws created before the kickout;
- whether the next receiver shoots, drives, passes, or resets;
- turnover and shot quality after the first closeout;
- offensive rebounding and transition defense sacrificed by five-out groups;
- performance with and without Irving, Flagg, Aldama, and a traditional center;
- opponent coverage, garbage time, and small-sample uncertainty.

The staff should also distinguish developmental from competitive possessions. Flagg needs real shooting and initiation reps. Sasser needs controlled chances to prove pull-up and passing translation. A close fourth quarter may call for the most reliable current arrangement; an early-season stint may rationally test a higher-variance answer. Mixing those objectives without labeling them makes evaluation incoherent.

## Forecast implications

A forecast should not add career three-point percentages and declare Dallas well spaced. It should model role ranges and dependencies. Irving availability changes the difficulty of teammates' attempts. Lively availability changes vertical pressure and lineup size. Aldama's health and position change whether five-out is available. Flagg's shot changes how much help a defense can send. Sasser's role determines whether his percentage is applied to open reserve catches or harder on-ball attempts.

These are pressure points, not excuses for false precision. Scenario branches can represent Irving limited versus available, Lively restricted versus available, and Flagg's shot remaining weak versus becoming credible. Shot volume and location should move with each branch. The model should widen uncertainty when the role is new rather than treating every attempt as an identical Bernoulli trial.

The basketball conclusion is more direct. Dallas has enough shooting skill to build functional space, but not enough that every plausible lineup will space itself. The best configurations combine one proven advantage creator, one source of movement or pull-up gravity, one vertical or five-out big threat, and at least two quick second-side decision-makers. The coaching task is to distribute those functions without sacrificing defense, health, or Flagg's development.
