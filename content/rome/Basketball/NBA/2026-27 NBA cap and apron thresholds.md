The NBA's final 2026–27 system levels put the $164.961 million salary cap at the bottom of a five-line decision map whose higher thresholds govern tax payments and which roster-building tools a team may use.

Up: [[Basketball]]

## The official numbers

On June 30, 2026, the NBA announced the levels that took effect at 12:01 a.m. ET on July 1. These are final league figures, not the estimates that cap sites and reporters used before the league year opened.

| 2026–27 system level | Official amount | Distance from salary cap | What the line primarily controls |
| --- | ---: | ---: | --- |
| Salary cap | $164.961M | — | Ordinary room and the baseline for cap exceptions and maximum salaries |
| Minimum team salary | $148.465M | −$16.496M | The required minimum payroll, equal after rounding to 90% of the cap |
| Luxury-tax level | $200.428M | +$35.467M | Whether a team owes tax, subject to the CBA's tax calculations |
| First apron | $209.015M | +$44.054M | A hard-cap line for specified transactions and a gateway to tighter trade rules |
| Second apron | $221.686M | +$56.725M | A higher hard-cap line for specified transactions plus the most restrictive trade and draft consequences |

The same release set three mid-level exceptions: $15.044 million for the non-taxpayer MLE, $6.064 million for the taxpayer MLE, and $9.366 million for the room MLE. Those amounts matter because an MLE is not simply extra spending permission. Which version a club uses can impose an apron ceiling for the rest of the cap year.

The spacing between the upper lines is also useful. The first apron is $8.587 million above the tax line. The second apron is another $12.671 million above the first apron and $21.258 million above the tax line. A team can therefore be a taxpayer without being an apron team, or be above the first apron without being above the second. Calling every expensive team a “second-apron team” collapses materially different operating regimes.

## Five lines, five different questions

The cap is soft. A team below it ordinarily has room equal to the cap minus its Team Salary. A team above it cannot simply sign any free agent for any amount, but the CBA supplies exceptions: Bird rights, Early Bird rights, non-Bird rights, rookie-scale and minimum-salary exceptions, the several MLEs, and others. This is why most NBA payrolls can exceed the cap without violating it.

The minimum team salary points in the opposite direction. The CBA requires a team to reach a payroll floor. It is not a competitive-balance ceiling and should not be confused with the cap. For 2026–27, $148.465 million is roughly $16.5 million below the cap. A rebuilding club can have meaningful cap room while still satisfying the floor.

The tax level is a payment threshold, not by itself a hard cap. Crossing $200.428 million can create a luxury-tax bill; it does not automatically make the roster illegal. The tax calculation uses its own rules, rates, and repeater status. A team may consciously accept a tax bill while retaining tools that disappear only at an apron.

The aprons are different. They connect transaction choice to a ceiling. Under the NBA's CBA 101 guide, a team may not complete a listed transaction if its adjusted “Apron Team Salary” would exceed the applicable apron immediately afterward. Once it completes such a transaction, it cannot exceed that apron for the remainder of the salary-cap year. This is the mechanism commonly called being hard-capped.

The resulting hierarchy is best read as a permissions map:

1. The cap asks whether the club has ordinary room or needs an exception.
2. The tax line asks whether the club owes a tax payment.
3. The first apron asks whether certain powerful acquisition tools remain available and whether the club has triggered a first-apron hard cap.
4. The second apron asks whether the narrowest trade rules and longer-horizon draft consequences apply, and whether a second-apron transaction has hard-capped the club there.

Those questions overlap, but they are not interchangeable.

## A hard cap is triggered, not universal

“The NBA has a hard cap at $209.015 million” would be wrong. The league has a soft salary cap, while particular actions impose an apron hard cap. The official CBA 101 transaction table identifies the relevant triggers.

At the first apron, the listed actions include using the bi-annual exception, using the non-taxpayer MLE, acquiring a player in a sign-and-trade, signing certain higher-salaried players waived during the regular season, and using the expanded traded-player exception. If a team uses one of these tools, its applicable Apron Team Salary cannot finish the transaction above $209.015 million and its Team Salary cannot later rise above that line during 2026–27. There is a narrow MLE accounting exception: if a team nominally uses the non-taxpayer MLE only for contracts of no more than two seasons whose aggregate first-year salary does not exceed the taxpayer MLE, and it undertakes no other first-apron-triggering transaction, the CBA can deem the team to have used the taxpayer MLE instead if it later exceeds the first apron. That exception is one reason to test the exact contract and sequence rather than infer the hard cap from the name of an exception alone.

At the second apron, the table includes acquiring a player with a pre-existing standard traded-player exception generated in a prior year, acquiring with an aggregated standard traded-player exception, paying cash to another team in a trade, acquiring a player with a traded-player exception generated by sending out a sign-and-traded player, and using the taxpayer MLE. Those actions impose the $221.686 million ceiling.

The ordering can feel counterintuitive. The larger non-taxpayer MLE is a first-apron trigger, while the smaller taxpayer MLE is a second-apron trigger. That is deliberate: the richer exception is available only to a team that stays under the lower line. A club cannot use the larger tool and then spend through the first apron.

Apron status also changes trade math even without a team voluntarily selecting an exception. The CBA's traded-player rules progressively remove flexibility. Above the first apron, the $250,000 salary-matching allowances are reduced to zero. Second-apron teams face still tighter constraints, including the inability to aggregate outgoing salaries to create an aggregated standard traded-player exception. [[NBA apron mechanics and roster building]] develops these roster-building effects; the present note's narrower job is to establish which 2026–27 number governs each decision.

## Team Salary and Apron Team Salary are not always identical

Public cap tables often show several totals because the CBA asks different accounting questions. The official guide defines Apron Team Salary as Team Salary with adjustments: it excludes free-agent amounts and first-round draft-pick holds, includes certain incentive compensation excluded from Team Salary, and makes other CBA-specified changes.

That means a simple sum of guaranteed player salaries may not answer whether a transaction is permissible. Cap holds preserve exceptions and room calculations. Likely and unlikely incentives can be treated differently. Waived salary, roster charges, two-way arrangements, trade bonuses, and timing can also affect the appropriate ledger. A public “payroll” number may be useful without being the controlling apron number.

This distinction is especially important near a line. Suppose a cap site lists $208.8 million, about $215,000 under the first apron. It would be unsafe to conclude that the club can use the full non-taxpayer MLE. The proposed signing itself adds salary; the relevant calculation is Apron Team Salary immediately after the transaction; and the team must preserve room for a legal roster and later moves. Cap management therefore uses a buffer, not merely a screenshot showing one dollar below a threshold.

The CBA also has special rules from the day after the regular season through June 30. For specified transactions, the league examines the following salary-cap year as well as the ending one. The official guide says the projection assumes options are handled in prescribed ways, no additional transactions occur, higher-max possibilities take their specified treatment, and—before the next levels are known—the subsequent cap and aprons equal the current figures. The basic lesson is that an offseason trade can constrain two accounting periods; “the new league year has not started” is not a loophole.

## Why final figures displaced projections

The CBA calculates the cap from projected Basketball Related Income, projected player benefits, and the league's 30 teams. Its public summary describes the cap formula as 44.74% of projected BRI, less projected benefits, divided by 30, subject to adjustments. The other system levels then rise at the same rate as the cap. No system level can fall from the prior year or increase by more than 10%.

Before June 30, independent cap trackers necessarily worked with projections. Spotrac, for example, published a $166 million 2026–27 projection earlier in 2026. The final $164.961 million cap is $1.039 million lower. That difference is small relative to a whole payroll but large at the margin: it can be most of a minimum contract, the difference between retaining and renouncing a cap hold, or the buffer needed to complete a trade.

The correct research practice is therefore temporal. A projection is legitimate when labeled with its date and source. Once the NBA announces final figures, canonical threshold notes and scenario models should replace projected system levels with the official values while preserving uncertainty in team-specific payroll assumptions. Spotrac remains valuable as independent specialist corroboration and for live team ledgers, but the league release controls the system levels.

## Maximum salaries and exception amounts scale from the cap

The cap is also a denominator. The usual first-year maximum salary bands are 25%, 30%, and 35% of the salary cap for players with 0–6, 7–9, and 10 or more years of service, subject to the CBA's higher-maximum and prior-salary rules. Applied mechanically to $164.961 million, those percentages are about $41.240 million, $49.488 million, and $57.736 million. These are teaching values, not a substitute for a contract-specific calculation: a player's service time, eligibility for a higher maximum, 105% prior-salary protection, extension structure, and timing can change the answer.

Many exception scales also grow at the cap's annual rate. The NBA's final release is therefore more than five headline numbers. It resets rookie-scale values, minimums, cash limits, trade allowances, and other dollar amounts governed by the CBA's growth rules. A roster model that updates only “salary cap” while retaining an old MLE or apron can produce internally inconsistent advice.

## A practical classification workflow

For any proposed 2026–27 move, work through the lines in order.

First, establish the controlling ledger and date. Use the team's Apron Team Salary rather than a generic payroll total when testing apron transactions. Mark options, non-guarantees, cap holds, draft holds, incentives, waived money, and incomplete reported deals. Do not treat a verbal agreement as an executed contract.

Second, identify the mechanism. Is the player signed into room, re-signed with Bird rights, acquired through an MLE, claimed on waivers, obtained in a sign-and-trade, or traded into a specific exception? The same incoming salary can be legal under one mechanism and prohibited under another.

Third, identify the threshold the mechanism invokes. Ordinary use of the non-taxpayer MLE is not tested against the tax line; it is a first-apron trigger, subject to the limited taxpayer-sized, two-season deeming rule described above. The taxpayer MLE is a second-apron trigger. A pure Bird re-signing may take a club above an apron even though many outside acquisition routes are unavailable there.

Fourth, calculate the post-transaction position and remaining roster needs. A legal move that leaves no room to reach the minimum roster size may be practically unusable. Include expected minimum contracts and other required charges.

Fifth, separate tax cost from basketball permission. “The owner will pay” answers the tax question, not whether the CBA permits aggregation, a sign-and-trade, or an exception. Conversely, getting below the tax line can save money without being necessary to legalize a move whose controlling ceiling is an apron.

Finally, preserve scenario ranges. Team salary changes with waived guarantees, unlikely incentives, trade bonuses, and order of operations. Near an apron, report the assumed sequence and buffer rather than one falsely exact “space” number.

## Verification matrix for live roster work

The thresholds themselves are now the least uncertain part of a 2026–27 cap analysis. The NBA's June 30 release fixes the five system levels and three MLE amounts, and the league's key-dates page fixes the July 1 start of the cap year and July 6 end of the moratorium. Most remaining uncertainty sits in the transaction ledger. A reported agreement, an official team announcement, a league-approved trade, and a cap-table entry are four different evidentiary states.

For committee work, each input should therefore carry both a status and an effective date:

| Input | Preferred evidence | Safe use |
| --- | --- | --- |
| System level | NBA's final annual cap release | Treat as controlling for the named cap year |
| CBA permission or restriction | Governing CBA, then the NBA's official CBA guide | Identify the transaction mechanism and controlling apron |
| Completed player movement | Team release or NBA tracker explicitly identifying an official deal | Move the player between canonical roster ledgers |
| Reported agreement | Attributable report, clearly labeled | Build a conditional scenario; do not treat it as executed |
| Team salary or apron salary | Reconciled contract ledger with stated assumptions | Estimate room only after checking holds, incentives, waived money, and roster charges |
| Future roster action | Explicit scenario and order of operations | Test legality and flexibility; never present as a completed move |

This matrix resolves a common contradiction in July coverage. A leaguewide offseason page may still label a move “multiple reports” after the participating team has issued its own completion release, or a team roster grid may lag the completed transaction. The canonical article should use the most direct dated evidence for the specific claim rather than treating every NBA-owned page as equally current. In Dallas's July 2026 case, the official acquisition announcement controls the player movement while [[Dallas Mavericks roster and minutes ledger]] preserves the lagging roster display as a provenance warning.

The matrix also prevents cap language from outrunning evidence. Knowing that the first apron is $209.015 million does not prove Dallas is below it, above it, or hard-capped there. That conclusion requires a reconciled Apron Team Salary and the mechanism used in every relevant transaction. Likewise, a public payroll estimate below the second apron does not establish access to the taxpayer MLE if another action already imposed a lower ceiling. The proper output is a conditional statement: *given this ledger, this transaction mechanism, and this order, this threshold controls.*

### Arithmetic recheck

The published differences in this note were independently recalculated from the NBA's final figures on July 10. The first apron minus the tax level is $8.587 million; the second apron minus the first is $12.671 million; the second apron minus the tax level is $21.258 million; and the first apron minus the salary cap is $44.054 million. The minimum team salary is $16.496 million below the cap. These checks confirm the table's subtraction, but they do not substitute for team-specific accounting.

## What the 2026–27 ladder means strategically

The $44.054 million interval between cap and first apron appears large, but it can disappear quickly on a star-heavy roster. Two 30% maximum salaries mechanically consume about 60% of the cap; add a 25% maximum and the nominal core reaches 85% before the rest of the roster. Bird rights allow the team to retain that core above the cap, but the aprons make external improvement progressively harder.

That creates an asymmetry between retention and acquisition. A club may often re-sign its own players and become expensive, yet lose flexible routes for adding outsiders. The CBA does not impose a simple ban on spending. It makes repeated high-end spending increasingly dependent on drafting well, developing minimum-salary contributors, sequencing transactions carefully, and deciding which players merit long-term slots before the roster becomes rigid.

For Dallas or any other contender, the right question is thus not merely “How far below $221.686 million are we?” It is “Which transaction tools does the plan require, which line does each tool invoke, what is the adjusted post-move salary, and what flexibility remains afterward?” Thresholds are constraints on a sequence, not labels attached once to a roster.
