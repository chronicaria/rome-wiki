## Availability is a basketball variable, not a medical prediction

The Mavericks' roster creates an unusually large gap between “who is under team control” and “which version of the team can play together.” Kyrie Irving missed the entire 2025-26 season while continuing recovery from left-knee ACL reconstruction. Dereck Lively II appeared in only seven games before right-foot surgery ended his season. Dallas publicly expects both players to matter in 2026-27, but the available sources do not establish exact return dates, minute limits, back-to-back policy, or probability of recurrence.

That evidence boundary should shape the analysis. This note does not diagnose either player, infer healing from silence, or assign a medically precise games-played distribution. It instead asks what Dallas's basketball structure looks like under observable states. The scenarios are conditional planning tools. They become more or less plausible as Dallas releases milestones such as full practice participation, preseason appearances, restrictions, or official injury designations.

The distinction matters for forecasting. Availability does not merely subtract a player's projected value. It changes the jobs assigned to everyone else, the combinations a coach can use, and the environment in which young players develop. Irving's absence transfers initiation and late-clock creation. Lively's absence transfers rim protection, screening, and vertical spacing. If both are absent, Dallas cannot replace each independently without creating secondary costs.

## What is verified as of July 10

Dallas announced in February 2026 that Irving would not return during the 2025-26 season as he continued ACL recovery. The injury occurred in March 2025, and the long recovery means his absence was a deliberate team decision rather than an ordinary late-season designation. The NBA's Dallas draft profile subsequently described him as expected to be ready for the start of the next season, and Dusty May's introductory material spoke about building with Irving. Those later statements improve the organizational outlook, but they do not demonstrate competitive readiness or an unrestricted workload.

Lively's public baseline is less encouraging. Dallas and NBA reports identify right-foot surgery and a season ending after seven appearances. The prior Dallas briefing records that, as of June 26, he had not progressed to running. That is a dated milestone, not a current diagnosis. It is enough to reject an assumption that he had already completed a normal offseason progression; it is not enough to project a particular return month.

The two cases therefore differ. Irving's team-facing language has shifted toward expected return, after an intentionally prolonged recovery. Lively had a more recent negative functional milestone in late June. Neither case should be flattened into “healthy” or “out.” The honest categories are stages: not cleared for full basketball work, participating with restrictions, available but workload-managed, and available without a publicly stated restriction.

Other players can miss games, of course, but Irving and Lively carry the largest structural leverage. P. J. Washington, Naji Marshall, Santi Aldama, Daniel Gafford, Cooper Flagg, Max Christie, Ryan Nembhard, and Marcus Sasser affect depth and lineup quality. The roster can absorb an isolated short absence from one of them more directly. Irving and Lively define capabilities the roster does not duplicate at the same level. Tyus Jones is not a current Maverick; Dallas waived him on February 28 and Denver signed him on March 5.

## Scenario one: both core veterans are functionally available

In the best basketball scenario, Irving and Lively participate in training camp, enter the regular season without a material public restriction, and remain available often enough to establish lineup continuity. “Functionally available” does not require 82 games or peak-career minutes. It means May can plan rotations around them rather than treating each appearance as a special event.

This version has a coherent starting architecture. Irving supplies first-side advantage creation and late-clock scoring. Flagg can expand as a secondary creator instead of being forced to organize every difficult possession. Lively screens, dives, passes on the short roll, and protects the rim. Washington, Marshall, Christie, Aldama, or another credible wing fills the remaining slots based on shooting and matchup.

The offensive benefit compounds. Irving's defender cannot abandon him, which creates cleaner spaces for Flagg. Lively's lob gravity occupies the low defender, which makes Irving's pocket passing and Flagg's downhill attacks more dangerous. Aldama or Washington can stretch a frontcourt defender away from the paint. Dallas can then use Sasser as a scoring reserve rather than asking him to imitate a primary point guard.

The defensive benefit is equally important. A functional Lively allows perimeter defenders to press higher and lets Flagg take aggressive assignments without every mistake becoming a layup. Gafford can remain a forceful second center rather than carrying all traditional-center minutes. Aldama can be used for his skill in favorable matchups instead of being pressed into every rim-protection possession.

Even this scenario contains uncertainty. Irving will be 34 during the season after an extended absence, so “available” does not guarantee old burst or defensive tolerance. Lively must demonstrate that his foot can tolerate repeated NBA loading. The proper forecast response is not to restore each player's old rate and multiply by optimistic minutes. It is to use a broad effectiveness band inside the healthy-availability branch.

For roster planning, this scenario supports balanced units: Irving–Christie–Flagg–Washington/Aldama–Lively as one conceptual shape, with Marshall, Sasser, Thompson, Gafford, and the remaining forwards supplying matchup depth. The names are illustrative, not a predicted starting five. What matters is the functional distribution: one elite creator, two or three large connectors/defenders, and one rim-running center.

## Scenario two: Irving is available, Lively is delayed or managed

This may be the easiest partial-health scenario for Dallas to survive because the roster has multiple centers. Gafford can start and provide screening, finishing, rebounding, and some rim protection. Powell or another depth big can cover low-leverage minutes. Aldama can play small-ball center when offense is the priority. Morez Johnson or another young center can receive developmental minutes if his roster status and readiness justify them.

The substitution is not neutral. Lively's defensive range and passing ceiling are distinctive. Gafford can protect the rim and finish powerfully but does not reproduce every coverage or connective read. Aldama improves skill and spacing but is not the same last-line defender. A rookie may supply activity while making positioning errors. Dallas can fill the minutes more easily than it can fill the exact role.

With Irving active, however, those compromises are manageable. He can create efficient shots for Gafford and keep offense-first Aldama groups organized. Flagg can spend more energy as a defender and transition force. May can choose center types by matchup instead of choosing them solely to rescue a stagnant offense.

The forecast effect should therefore include a modest direct downgrade plus matchup variance. Against teams that pressure the rim or punish small centers, missing Lively may be severe. Against switching or spacing opponents, Aldama-at-five groups could remain competitive offensively. A single points-per-game deduction would miss that distribution.

The verification triggers are concrete: running, contact work, full practice, preseason participation, and official injury reports. A vague statement that Lively is “progressing” should not move the scenario much unless it identifies the stage. Conversely, an absence from Summer League or voluntary workouts has limited meaning for an established injured player unless Dallas explains it.

## Scenario three: Lively is available, Irving is delayed or managed

This version preserves the defensive skeleton but exposes Dallas's thinnest offensive function. Lively can anchor the paint, Gafford can keep center minutes stable, and the team can build large lineups around Flagg, Washington, Marshall, Christie, and Aldama. Those groups may defend, rebound, and run. The central question becomes who creates a quality half-court shot after the first action is stopped.

Flagg would carry a larger initiation burden. That can accelerate development, but it is not costless. Defenses can load toward him, test his handle, and go under screens if his shooting does not improve. Nembhard can organize the first action and protect the ball, but the evidence does not establish that he reproduces Irving's scoring gravity. Aldama can serve as a second-side passer and occasional handler. Sasser can take pull-ups and attack tilted defenses. Christie and Marshall can drive closeouts. None has Irving's proven ability to repeatedly manufacture advantages against a set defense.

May can mitigate the problem through pace and collective passing. Defensive stops create transition chances before the opponent loads the paint. Early drag screens let Flagg or Sasser attack before matchups settle. Split cuts and movement shooting can create advantages without a traditional point guard. But scheme changes the frequency and location of hard decisions; it does not eliminate the need for someone to make them.

The likely rotation implication is more ballhandling by committee and more shooting around Flagg. Thompson's gravity may earn situational minutes despite defensive decline. Aldama becomes more valuable because he can keep the ball moving from a frontcourt slot. A non-shooting perimeter defender becomes harder to play beside Lively because the lane already contains a center.

This scenario is also where the distinction between “Irving plays” and “Irving restores the offense” matters most. A managed return could involve fewer minutes, fewer back-to-backs, and reduced rim pressure even while he is officially active. Dallas should be modeled with an intermediate branch in which Irving stabilizes some minutes but does not carry his prior offensive workload.

## Scenario four: both Irving and Lively are limited or unavailable

This is the stress case, not a forecast. It reveals why Dallas's apparent depth should not be mistaken for redundancy. Without Irving, the team lacks proven top-end creation. Without Lively, it lacks its highest-upside defensive anchor. Replacing both forces Flagg to solve more possessions while also taking harder defensive responsibility.

A plausible group might use Nembhard or Sasser with Christie, Flagg, Washington or Marshall, and Gafford. It can defend at several spots and maintain vertical pressure, but its half-court ceiling depends heavily on Flagg. Another version could use Aldama beside Gafford to add passing and shooting. A smaller offense-first version could put Aldama at center, but then rim deterrence and rebounding become vulnerabilities. These are functional sketches rather than a claim that an unverified depth chart is settled.

The deepest consequence is role cascade. Nembhard moves from secondary organizer toward a larger creation burden; Sasser moves from reserve scorer toward more primary handling. Flagg moves from developing co-creator toward offensive engine. Gafford moves from a shared-center arrangement toward a heavier workload. Aldama becomes both spacing solution and backup organizer. Christie and Marshall receive more on-ball tasks. Each promotion can reduce the very skill that made the player valuable in the smaller role. Tyus Jones must not appear in this allocation: Dallas waived him February 28, 2026, and Denver officially signed him March 5.

This is why availability shocks are nonlinear. Suppose Irving is worth a certain number of points above a reserve and Lively another number above a backup. Adding those two individual losses understates the combined effect if the backups cannot coexist cleanly or if Flagg's efficiency drops under extra usage. It can also overstate the effect in specific matchups if Dallas discovers a productive Flagg–Aldama offense. Scenario analysis preserves both possibilities.

The model's 28-to-41-win p10–p90 band and four cold-start players are consistent with this structural uncertainty. The low branch is not simply “bad shooting luck.” It can represent the roster spending long stretches without one or both organizing pillars while inexperienced players absorb unfamiliar jobs.

## Scenario five: available bodies, reduced effectiveness

Binary injury models often miss the most plausible middle. Irving and Lively may both be active yet below assumed effectiveness. Irving might protect his knee by reducing rim attacks or defensive change-of-direction. Lively might play shorter stints, avoid some coverage demands, or need recovery days. These examples are basketball possibilities, not claims about their medical state.

This branch matters because official “available” status can tempt forecasters to restore full player value immediately. A better method separates participation, workload, and performance. For each player, ask three different questions:

1. Is he active for the game?
2. How many and what kind of minutes can he play?
3. How effective is he within those minutes relative to the prior projection?

The answers need not move together. Irving could be active for 24 controlled minutes and still transform Dallas's second unit. Lively could start but avoid long closing stints until conditioning returns. A model that records only games played will miss both the value and the limitation.

Evidence should also be weighted by proximity. An official training-camp restriction is more informative than a June optimism quote. Repeated preseason workload is more informative than one practice clip. Regular-season back-to-back decisions are more informative than a generic statement that there is no formal minutes limit. The evidence ladder should update gradually rather than flipping the projection at the first positive headline.

## Converting scenarios into a minutes model

The vault's older roster-context packet assigned Irving 56 expected games at 27.0 minutes and Lively 51 games at 25.8 minutes. Those are model inputs, not verified forecasts. The public evidence is insufficient to replace them with more precise point estimates. It is sufficient to test them against conditional branches.

A practical approach begins with role-minute ranges rather than exact season totals:

| State | Irving role range | Lively role range | Main beneficiaries of transferred work |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Both functionally available | Primary creator; starter workload with possible management | Starting defensive center; shared center workload | Flagg develops as co-creator; Gafford stabilizes bench |
| Irving available, Lively limited | Primary creator | Delayed, restricted, or absent | Gafford center minutes; Aldama small-ball/skill minutes |
| Lively available, Irving limited | Restricted or absent creator | Starting defensive center | Nembhard organization; Flagg initiation; Sasser handling; Aldama passing |
| Both limited | No stable primary creator | No stable defensive anchor | Nembhard, Flagg, Sasser, Gafford, Aldama and young depth all promoted |
| Both active but sub-prior | Participation without full assumed effectiveness | Participation without full assumed effectiveness | Mixed; role cascade is smaller but performance remains uncertain |

For simulation, each branch should carry correlations. Irving's status affects Flagg's usage and the value of finishers. Lively's status affects Gafford's minutes and perimeter defenders' risk tolerance. Their joint absence should not be sampled as two unrelated additive losses. The model can represent this with branch-specific minute matrices and player-effect distributions, then weight branches only when evidence supports probabilities.

Until Dallas supplies more evidence, the branch weights should remain broad and qualitative. Publishing “Irving will play 55 games” would create false certainty. Publishing “if Irving is workload-managed, these four functions transfer to these players” produces a falsifiable and useful planning statement.

## Evidence that should change the outlook

Positive evidence for Irving includes full-contact participation, repeated preseason appearances, normal change-of-direction and rim pressure in games, and no stated restriction beyond ordinary veteran management. Positive evidence for Lively includes progression from running to basketball activity, full contact, repeated practice, and preseason minutes that show he can move through defensive coverages.

Negative evidence includes delayed clearance, repeated non-contact status, an official timetable extending into the season, recurrent soreness identified by the team, or regular absences after return. A single rest day or conservative preseason scratch is weak evidence by itself. The NBA's regular-season injury reports become the authoritative status source once games begin; commentary can explain context but should not override the designation.

Performance evidence must also be interpreted carefully. A few made jumpers do not prove Irving's burst has returned. One blocked shot does not prove Lively can sustain a starter workload. Useful early indicators are role and movement patterns over repeated possessions: Irving's ability to turn the corner, force low-man help, and defend changes of direction; Lively's ability to cover space, screen repeatedly, sprint in transition, and recover between efforts. Any film conclusion should state the sample.

## Why it matters

Dallas's season cannot be understood through a single “healthy roster” depth chart. The team has enough frontcourt inventory to survive a Lively delay better than it can survive an Irving delay, but losing Lively still changes the defense's ceiling. It has enough secondary handling to distribute Irving's minutes, but not enough evidence to claim the creation job is replaced. Flagg is the swing bridge in every branch: better health lets him develop inside a balanced structure; worse health asks him to become the structure.

This scenario frame also disciplines expectations. Organizational optimism is relevant because it signals planning, but it is not clearance. Old injury news is relevant because it sets the last verified milestone, but it is not a permanent status. The forecast should move when the evidence moves, and it should move the entire rotation rather than only the injured player's games-played cell.
