## Direct summary

This ledger answers a narrow but consequential question: **what transaction state can be treated as verified when building a 2026-27 NBA roster, minutes forecast, or team analysis?** It records the event, effective date, evidence tier, resulting roster implication, and any remaining uncertainty. It does not treat a social-media report, an NBA.com roundup of reported agreements, an outdated roster page, or a writer's inference as equivalent to a completed club transaction.

The first fully adjudicated case is Tyus Jones. Dallas's official 2025-26 game notes list **“2/28: Waived Tyus Jones.”** Denver then published a dated club announcement on March 5 saying that the Nuggets had signed him. Denver's official team-information history also labels him **“Signed on 03/05/26,”** and Denver's playoff game notes place the signing in the club's recent-transactions chronology. Those independent official surfaces agree on the sequence. Therefore Jones must receive zero projected Dallas minutes after February 28, 2026. His later Denver status must be evaluated separately because the NBA's July offseason trackers describe a reported one-year return, while the currently indexed Denver roster page labels him a free agent. A reported July agreement is not promoted here to a completed signing without a club announcement or official transaction entry.

**As of:** July 10, 2026, America/New_York. This is a verification state, not a claim that every 2026 offseason move has been collected.

## Why an event ledger beats a roster screenshot

Roster pages are snapshots assembled for presentation. They can lag a waiver, retain a player through caching, switch to offseason “free agent” labels, or omit the effective mechanics behind an acquisition. A transaction is an event with a date and an issuer. The durable research object is therefore not “Jones appears on page X today,” but “Dallas recorded a waiver on February 28; Denver announced a signing on March 5.”

That distinction matters because a roster model is path-dependent. If an old Dallas page or third-party depth chart still contains Jones, a model may allocate him 15–20 minutes, reduce minutes for Ryan Nembhard or Marcus Sasser, and infer a level of ball security that Dallas does not actually possess. The initial identity error then contaminates lineup combinations, usage estimates, guard-depth conclusions, cap assumptions, and even prose about coaching choices. [[Dallas Mavericks roster and minutes ledger]] should consume the verified event chain, not re-litigate it from display pages.

The Denver evidence also demonstrates why a second official source is useful even after a club announcement. The March 5 article is a contemporaneous declaration. Denver's official team information later preserved “Signed on 03/05/26,” and its playoff notes included “3/5/26 ... Signed Tyus Jones” in recent transactions. Agreement across a dated announcement, roster history, and game notes sharply reduces the possibility of a headline mix-up, publication-date confusion, or similarly named player error. It also distinguishes Tyus Jones from Denver forward Spencer Jones, whose contract conversion appears elsewhere in the same transaction chronology.

## Evidence hierarchy

The ledger uses the following order when sources conflict:

1. **Official transaction announcement or official transaction log.** A dated team release explicitly saying “signed,” “waived,” “traded,” or “converted” is the strongest ordinary public evidence of a completed action.
2. **Official game notes or team-information transaction history.** These are retrospective operational records. They are especially valuable for waivers that did not receive a durable standalone news release.
3. **NBA-published official trade announcement or league transaction surface.** This can settle multi-team structure and formal completion.
4. **NBA.com news roundup that explicitly labels a move reported.** This is strong evidence that an agreement was publicly reported, but its own wording prevents treating the contract as officially executed.
5. **Current official roster display.** Useful corroboration, but weaker than a dated event record when caching, free-agency transitions, or page maintenance create a mismatch.
6. **Established reporting and databases.** Valuable for discovering candidates and contract detail, but a discovery lead is not the same thing as official completion.

This hierarchy is not a judgment about journalistic quality. It answers a narrower database question: what public artifact establishes that the team or league actually recorded the roster action? A reporter can be correct hours or days before a club release, yet the proper state remains “reported agreement” during that interval.

## Context and mechanism

Jones's Dallas tenure began amid deadline churn. NBA.com reported that Orlando sent him to Charlotte, which then sent him to Dallas on February 5. Dallas's own February 9 feature described the unusual three-team, two-day movement and identified him as a new Mavericks guard. Denver's March announcement later summarized his 2025-26 Orlando-and-Dallas total as 56 games, including ten starts, and credited him with a 7.68 assist-to-turnover ratio among qualifying players at that moment.

The relevant mechanism on February 28 was a **waiver**, not a trade. A waiver removes the player from the waiving team's active roster and exposes his contract to the league's waiver process; if unclaimed, the player generally becomes a free agent. Public sources reviewed for this ledger establish Dallas's act and Denver's later signing, but they do not establish every intervening procedural detail or the financial terms. The defensible statement is consequently simple: Dallas waived Jones on February 28, and Denver signed him on March 5. It is unnecessary—and unsafe—to invent the exact clearance time, cap treatment, guarantee, or exception used.

The March 5 Denver announcement establishes a signing but does not state contract length or salary. That omission matters in July. A March signing for the remainder of 2025-26 could end at the league-year boundary, making Jones a free agent, while a multi-year agreement would produce a different status. Denver's currently indexed roster page shows “FA / Free Agent” beside Jones, and NBA.com's July 2 offseason roundup says he “returns” to Denver on a reported one-year deal. Together those pages support the interpretation that a new agreement was required, but the conclusion remains an inference unless contract terms or a completed July signing are officially posted.

This is why one player's history can contain three different truth states without contradiction:

- the February 28 Dallas waiver is completed and verified;
- the March 5 Denver signing is completed and verified for the 2025-26 context;
- the July Denver return is reported but not yet verified here as a completed 2026-27 signing.

## How to use this ledger

For a roster snapshot, begin with the last verified completed event for each player, then apply only later events whose evidence clears the same gate. Do not fill silence with a previous team's roster page. Record the as-of timestamp because an offseason ledger can become outdated within minutes.

For projections, convert each row into a state transition:

`Dallas active → waived by Dallas (2026-02-28) → signed by Denver (2026-03-05) → Denver 2025-26 player → offseason free-agent / reported-return state (2026-07-02 reporting)`

That chain should feed [[NBA roster churn as projection uncertainty]], [[Dallas Mavericks 2026-27 roster construction dossier]], and [[Dallas guard creation after roster turnover]]. It does not answer how good Jones was, how Denver used him, or what role he would have in 2026-27. It only prevents those analyses from beginning with the wrong employer.

For cap work, do not infer salary, guarantee, Bird status, exception type, or tax effect from the word “signed.” Those require contract-specific evidence and the applicable collective-bargaining rules. For lineup work, do not interpret a reported future agreement as availability for opening night; execution, medical review, later transactions, and final roster decisions can still matter.

## Why it matters

The Jones correction is small in transaction volume and large in downstream effect. Point guard is a high-leverage role in a Dallas roster already requiring careful distinctions among primary creation, secondary handling, and low-turnover organization. Leaving Jones in Dallas would make the guard room look more stable than the verified roster warrants. It could cause an analyst to understate the creation burden on Kyrie Irving and Cooper Flagg, misclassify Nembhard or Sasser's opportunity, and explain away a roster need that remains real.

It also illustrates a general 2026 offseason research problem. “Official website” is not a single evidentiary category. An official team news release can confirm completion; an official roster can lag or shift labels; an NBA.com news-services story can repeat a report while conspicuously stopping short of official confirmation. Good transaction research preserves those verbs. **Agreed**, **reportedly agreed**, **signed**, **waived**, and **appears on the roster** are not interchangeable.

Maintaining that discipline makes later revisions cheaper. When Denver publishes a new Jones announcement or its transaction history adds a July date, the third row can be promoted with one citation and timestamp. No surrounding prose needs to be rewritten, because the distinction between March completion and July reporting is already explicit.
