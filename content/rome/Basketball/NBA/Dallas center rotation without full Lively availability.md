If Dereck Lively II is unavailable or restricted, Dallas can cover center minutes but cannot replace his whole role with one player; the viable solution is a portfolio built around Daniel Gafford, Santi Aldama, and matchup-specific team responsibilities.

## The question is functions, not 48 minutes

“Who backs up Lively?” is too narrow for the 2026-27 Mavericks. Dallas can put a nominal center on the floor for every possession. Daniel Gafford is a proven NBA finisher and rim protector. Santi Aldama can play offense-first minutes at the five. Depth players can absorb low-leverage work if their final roster status and readiness justify it. The harder question is whether those combinations preserve the things Dallas expects Lively to connect: rim deterrence, pick-and-roll coverage, defensive rebounding, screening, vertical spacing, transition running, and quick decisions after the catch.

Those jobs do not travel together. Gafford covers the most traditional center functions, but his offensive value depends heavily on another player creating the advantage. Aldama improves shooting and passing, but a spaced floor does not by itself deter a drive or finish a defensive possession. A young energy big may rebound and run without having NBA coverage discipline. A large forward can switch for a few possessions without becoming a full-time rim protector.

The sensible rotation therefore changes by health state and opponent. Dallas should begin with Gafford as the stable replacement, use Aldama-at-five to change offensive geometry, and distribute the remaining defensive load across Cooper Flagg, P. J. Washington, and the perimeter. That is a rotation plan, not a claim that the combined group equals Lively.

## What is known about Lively's status

The last cited functional milestone in Rome is not a clearance. Right-foot surgery ended Lively's 2025-26 season after seven appearances. An attributable June 26 report said he was out of the boot and able to walk, swim, and lift but had not been cleared to run. Dallas had earlier discussed training camp as a hope. As of July 10, the evidence collected here does not establish running, contact work, full practice, preseason participation, or an unrestricted workload.

That boundary requires conditional language. This article does not predict that Lively will miss opening night. It asks how Dallas should allocate jobs in three observable states: unavailable, active with a meaningful restriction, and functionally available but not yet ready for his assumed workload. The same alternatives matter if he later misses isolated games.

The distinction also prevents a common projection error. A model can assign Lively zero minutes and hand those minutes to Gafford and Aldama. The arithmetic will sum to 48, yet the basketball forecast can still be wrong because the new groups use different coverages, create shots differently, and expose different teammates.

## Gafford is the base because his role is legible

Gafford supplies the cleanest conventional answer. His 2025-26 NBA line in the current Dallas research recorded 55 games, 21.7 minutes per game, 65.5 percent shooting, 2.5 offensive rebounds, and 1.3 blocks. Those statistics do not measure every defensive possession and should not be treated as proof that he reproduces Lively. They do establish a stable NBA archetype: a physical screen-and-dive center who finishes efficiently near the rim, attacks the offensive glass, and provides real shot-blocking.

When Lively is absent, Gafford should receive the first share of additional center minutes because Dallas does not need to redesign every possession around him. He can run high pick-and-roll, screen in transition, occupy the dunker spot, and punish a switching mismatch on the glass. On defense, he can play conventional drop principles, meet drivers at the rim, and rebound in traffic. These are scalable regular-season tasks.

The costs appear when the burden grows. A heavier workload can reduce the energy that makes Gafford effective as a roller, offensive rebounder, and shot challenger. More minutes also mean more exposure to opponents that pull centers away from the basket or force repeated lateral decisions. Dallas should not assume that a productive 22-minute role scales linearly into every matchup at 32 minutes.

Gafford's offense is also creator-dependent. He bends a defense vertically after a ballhandler first forces two defenders to react. Kyrie Irving is the cleanest source of that advantage, while Flagg, Marcus Sasser, Ryan Nembhard, and other handlers present different levels of proof. If Irving is simultaneously absent or limited, simply increasing Gafford's minutes can place him in units without enough pull-up or rim pressure to activate his finishing. The center rotation and guard rotation therefore cannot be modeled independently.

This leads to a practical rule: pair Gafford's non-Irving minutes with the best available combination of organizer, downhill threat, and shooting. Avoid stacking him with multiple perimeter players whom defenses can ignore. His lack of three-point gravity is not fatal, but it makes the other four players' spacing responsibilities stricter.

## Aldama changes the problem instead of imitating Lively

Aldama offers a different answer. In the cited 2025-26 statistics, he attempted 4.7 threes per game at 35.0 percent and averaged 2.9 assists against 1.3 turnovers. Those rates do not prove he is a full-time center or primary playmaker. They do support two valuable functions: he occupies a defender away from the rim and can continue an advantage through passing rather than only finishing it.

At the five, Aldama can screen and pop, initiate a handoff, receive above the break, or relocate after Flagg drives. A conventional opposing center must decide whether to stay near the rim or honor Aldama's shot. That creates more usable space for Flagg and secondary guards than a traditional center alignment. It also lets Dallas play five players who can plausibly pass on the perimeter.

This is especially useful when the offense stalls without Irving. Dallas cannot manufacture another Irving by changing centers, but it can reduce the congestion facing less proven creators. Aldama can become a release valve after the first action, attack a slow closeout, or move the ball before the defense resets. The center position then contributes to organization rather than waiting for a lob.

The defensive exchange is substantial. Aldama's size does not establish Lively-level rim deterrence, recovery range, or center rebounding. A five-out offense can be excellent while the same lineup gives back points through penetration, fouls, and second chances. Dallas must protect those minutes with a team scheme: Flagg and Washington handling physical matchups, perimeter defenders containing the ball, help arriving early at the nail, and all five players completing possessions on the glass.

Aldama-at-five should therefore be a targeted tool rather than the automatic second unit. It is attractive against a slow center, a switching defense, or a lineup Dallas wants to pull out of the paint. It is less attractive against dominant offensive rebounders, relentless rim pressure, or units that can force Aldama into repeated last-line decisions. The success test must include both offensive spacing and opponent shot quality.

## Flagg and Washington absorb the hidden center work

Small-center groups work only when the forwards accept jobs that disappear from a depth chart. Someone must make first contact with the opposing big, rotate from the weak side, tag the roller, rebound outside his area, and communicate a switch. Flagg and Washington are the logical hinges because they combine size with perimeter mobility, but assigning them those tasks has opportunity costs.

Flagg is central to Dallas's offense and development plan. If he must wrestle with centers, protect the rim, initiate the offense, and create late-clock shots, the lineup may solve Lively's absence by overloading its most important young player. Washington can take more physical assignments and preserve Flagg for transition and creation, but he also needs to maintain spacing and closeout attacking. Neither should be treated as free center labor.

The best Aldama groups will distribute the work. Washington can take the strongest interior opponent while Aldama guards the less threatening frontcourt player. Flagg can roam from a wing, supply secondary rim help, and start transition after rebounds. Guards and wings must prevent straight-line drives so that the back line is rotating to predictable locations rather than emergency situations.

This is also why a defense-first perimeter player can become harder to use in small-center groups. Dallas may need that player's containment, yet if the opponent ignores him, the offense loses some of the spacing benefit gained by moving Aldama to center. The coaching staff must choose which end it is trying to stabilize rather than assuming one lineup solves both.

## A restricted Lively requires shorter, clearer assignments

If Lively is active but restricted, Dallas should resist treating his appearance as restoration of the normal rotation. A 16- or 20-minute version of Lively can still change the defense and make life easier for Flagg, but the remaining center possessions need explicit ownership. Gafford can take the heavier conventional share; Aldama can cover selective offense-first stretches; Lively can be concentrated in the lineups and matchups where his unique functions matter most.

That might mean starting Lively for continuity but closing with Gafford when physical finishing and rebounding are priorities. It might mean using Lively beside the best perimeter defenders so his shorter stint has maximum defensive leverage. It could also mean avoiding long consecutive minutes and protecting him from the most movement-intensive matchup early in a return. These are tactical possibilities, not claims about a medical plan.

The forecast must separate three questions: whether Lively dresses, how many minutes he plays, and how effective or constrained those minutes are. An active player can remain on a workload branch. Conversely, 18 impactful minutes can be much more valuable than a binary model implies because they stabilize specific groups and keep Gafford from carrying every conventional center possession.

## Four rotation families

### 1. Gafford-led conventional rotation

Use Gafford as the main center, surround him with four credible handlers or shooters, and keep Aldama primarily at forward. This is the safest regular-season structure because every role is familiar. It fails when Dallas lacks creation to activate the roller or when opponents pull Gafford into repeated space.

### 2. Split conventional and skilled-center rotation

Use Gafford for roughly half the game and Aldama for selected center stretches. This maximizes stylistic contrast: vertical pressure in one segment and five-out skill in another. It succeeds if Dallas can contain and rebound during the Aldama minutes. It fails if the defensive cost overwhelms the spacing gain.

### 3. Restricted-Lively portfolio

Concentrate Lively's available minutes in high-leverage defensive groups, let Gafford carry most standard center work, and retain a small Aldama-at-five package. This preserves access to Lively's unique tools without pretending the restriction is gone. Its weakness is rotation complexity and the possibility that constant role changes reduce continuity.

### 4. Matchup emergency size

Use two traditional bigs or a depth center only for narrow reasons: foul trouble, end-of-quarter defense, exceptional opponent size, or a rebounding crisis. Lively–Gafford together should remain exceptional because neither creates normal three-point gravity. Extra size can protect the rim while shrinking Dallas's own driving lanes and transition defense.

## How Dallas should evaluate the alternatives

Early lineup plus-minus will be noisy. The useful evidence is functional. On offense, track whether the group reaches the rim, forces low-man help, produces corner threes, and has a second decision-maker after the first action. On defense, track point-of-attack containment, rim attempts allowed, help timing, fouls, and defensive rebounds. Separate opponent quality and garbage time.

For Gafford groups, the main questions are who creates the advantage and whether the spacing around the roll is respected. For Aldama groups, ask whether the offensive gain survives the defensive glass and rim. For restricted-Lively groups, ask whether his movement, repeated effort, and assigned coverages look sustainable across several appearances rather than one highlight.

Dallas should also record role cascades. More Gafford minutes change the backup creator's job. More Aldama-at-five minutes change Washington's matchup and Flagg's help responsibilities. A depth center can reduce physical load on the forwards while reducing offensive skill. Those second-order changes explain why center availability can alter a team forecast more than the replacement player's individual rating suggests.

## Why it matters

Dallas has legitimate frontcourt depth, but it does not have a spare Lively. Gafford is the reliable conventional base; Aldama is the geometry-changing counter; Flagg and Washington make the counter defensively plausible; young depth is insurance until verified otherwise. The coaching problem is to combine these pieces without confusing minutes coverage with functional replacement.

The honest forecast uses branches. If Lively is unavailable, start from Gafford and a selective Aldama package. If he is restricted, concentrate his unique value and leave the rest of the portfolio intact. If he becomes functionally available, reduce the emergency burdens gradually rather than instantly restoring an old minute matrix. Each branch should move guard creation, forward workload, and team defensive assumptions together.
