## The roster must be reconstructed from events, not one webpage

This dossier asks a deceptively basic question: what team has Dallas actually built for 2026-27? As of July 9, the answer cannot be copied safely from the Mavericks' roster grid. The grid was still showing Khris Middleton and AJ Johnson after Dallas had officially sent them away, while omitting newly acquired Santi Aldama and Marcus Sasser. That is a publication-lag conflict, not evidence that the transaction failed. The Mavericks' announcement and the NBA offseason trade tracker identify the completed six-team transaction; those event records control until the roster display catches up.

The verified July 8 Dallas side is straightforward. Dallas acquired Aldama, Sasser, and the NBA rights to Tarik Biberovic. It sent Middleton to Washington and Johnson to Memphis, along with a protected 2030 first-round pick and Dallas-controlled second-round compensation. Biberovic's rights are not the same thing as a roster contract. Reports about a future agreement remain reports until Dallas announces a signing or the NBA transaction record shows it. Any depth chart that automatically inserts him into the rotation is therefore premature.

This distinction matters because three different populations are easily collapsed into one “roster.” First are players under NBA contracts or otherwise displayed by current public roster sources. Second are acquired draft rights, which create negotiation control but no active-player status. Third are Summer League, Exhibit 10, two-way, or camp candidates whose presence does not guarantee a regular-season place. A useful construction analysis must keep those groups separate.

The stable basketball core visible across official and established roster sources is Kyrie Irving, Cooper Flagg, P. J. Washington, Naji Marshall, Max Christie, Klay Thompson, Caleb Martin, Dereck Lively II, and Daniel Gafford. The offseason transaction adds Aldama and Sasser. Dallas's June 29 team report also says it exercised Ryan Nembhard's 2026-27 team option. Tyus Jones is not part of the current group: Dallas's official game notes record that it waived him on February 28, and Denver officially announced his signing on March 5. By contrast, the NBA's pre-draft team profile listed Dwight Powell and Marvin Bagley III as free agents, while ESPN's roster display included Bagley and Moussa Cisse when accessed. The research packet also contains draft or development names including Morez Johnson Jr., Sergio de Larrea, Tobi Lawal, and Vsevolod Ishchenko. Those names and third-party roster-grid appearances should not all be treated as equal-status members of the opening-night team; contract type and final roster disposition remain verification tasks.

The resulting conclusion is narrower but more reliable than a twenty-name depth chart: Dallas has a confirmed star-development centerpiece in Flagg; a high-leverage but medically uncertain primary creator in Irving; several proven forwards and centers; and a group of secondary guards whose ability to organize an NBA offense is not established at lead-creator scale.

## The construction problem begins at point guard

Irving is the roster's only demonstrated high-end half-court initiator. That does not mean every possession must begin with him. Flagg's rookie production—21.0 points, 6.7 rebounds, and 4.5 assists in the baseline research packet—supports a larger year-two creation role, and Dusty May has publicly emphasized pace, passing, and using Irving as a foundational veteran. But “can create some offense” and “can stabilize an NBA offense against set defenses” are different jobs.

Irving's last competitive game preceded ACL reconstruction, and Dallas announced in February 2026 that he would miss the rest of 2025-26. The team's newer offseason language treats him as part of May's foundation, while an NBA draft profile said he should be ready for the start of the season. Those are encouraging organizational signals, not a verified workload guarantee. They do not establish that he can immediately absorb his old minutes, usage, burst, or back-to-back schedule.

Sasser helps the guard inventory without resolving the central question. He is a smaller scoring guard with NBA experience, useful pull-up potential, and a plausible second-unit role. The acquisition gives Dallas another ballhandler and shooter, but the evidence does not warrant treating him as a full substitute for Irving's manipulation of switches, late-clock shot creation, or playoff-caliber decision making. Nembhard may organize units after Dallas exercised his option; Christie can defend wings and attack advantages; Flagg can become a point-forward hub. Each covers a portion of the job. None is a verified replacement for a healthy Irving's full creation burden. See [[Dallas guard creation after roster turnover]] for the corrected post-Jones guard map.

That makes guard creation a portfolio problem. Dallas should ask which combinations supply four functions: bringing the ball up under pressure, bending the first defender, making the second read, and producing a credible shot late in the clock. A Jones-led group may be orderly but still lack Irving's scoring pressure. A Sasser–Christie pairing may provide handling and defense but need Flagg's passing. A Flagg-led bench group may need Thompson or Aldama to pull help defenders away. A Nembhard-led group may also organize possessions while remaining less forceful at the rim. The roster can cover the functions collectively, but the combinations are sensitive to shooting and health.

## Frontcourt abundance is both insurance and congestion

Dallas has several real frontcourt options: Flagg, Washington, Marshall in larger wing configurations, Aldama, Lively, Gafford, Powell, and possible depth from Bagley, Morez Johnson, or Cisse depending on final status. This is valuable because Lively played only seven games in 2025-26 before foot surgery, and late-June public reporting in the research briefing said he had not yet progressed to running. A team with that medical uncertainty should carry more than one playable center.

Yet extra bigs do not automatically produce a good defense. Lively offers the highest theoretical combination of rim protection, vertical spacing, mobility, and short-roll passing. Gafford supplies force at the rim, offensive rebounding, screening, and dependable regular-season center minutes. Powell is a veteran systems player. Aldama is the most important geometry changer because he can handle, pass, and shoot at forward or small-ball-center size. Washington can defend across frontcourt matchups and is a credible spacer. Morez Johnson projects as an energetic defender and rebounder but enters with rookie uncertainty.

The problem is overlap. Two traditional non-shooting centers cannot share ordinary NBA minutes without severely compressing the offense. Flagg's rookie three-point percentage, cited in the Dallas briefing at 29.5 percent, means Dallas cannot yet assume that every Flagg-plus-center lineup has four respected shooters. If Washington, Marshall, or a young guard is also ignored, the lane narrows and Irving or Flagg sees extra bodies before creating an advantage.

Aldama changes those constraints more than his raw scoring average suggests. NBA.com lists 14.0 points, 6.7 rebounds, and 2.9 assists in 43 games for Memphis in 2025-26. At seven feet, his willingness to pass and operate away from the rim can allow Dallas to keep size without assigning every frontcourt player to the dunker spot. He can play beside Lively or Gafford, replace them in offense-first stretches, or join Washington and Flagg in a nominally centerless skill lineup. The defensive viability of the last option is matchup-dependent; Aldama should not be described as a direct substitute for Lively's rim deterrence.

This is why a roster that appears “too crowded” up front may be rational. The players are not interchangeable. Lively is the ceiling defender, Gafford the sturdy vertical center, Aldama the skill-and-spacing connector, Washington the two-way forward hinge, and the younger bigs insurance and development bets. The coaching challenge is not simply allocating minutes fairly. It is choosing which property the lineup needs and accepting the property it gives up.

## Flagg is the bridge between timelines

The franchise's construction has two clocks. Irving, Thompson, Powell, and the established centers are oriented toward immediate competence. Flagg, Christie, Morez Johnson, Sasser, and other young guards represent development and optionality. Aldama and Washington sit between those groups: experienced enough to help now, young enough to remain relevant through Flagg's rise.

Flagg makes the clocks compatible because he does not need to be hidden while he develops. His rookie scoring and playmaking establish a high baseline, and his size lets May test him at several functional positions. But the roster should not use versatility as an excuse to leave him without a defined developmental burden. His most valuable year-two questions are whether defenses must respect his three-pointer, whether he can create efficient advantages against set half-court coverages, and whether he can carry difficult defensive assignments without blunting his offense.

The ideal veteran structure helps answer those questions rather than merely chasing a few extra wins. Irving can teach Flagg how to manipulate help and can reduce late-clock burden. Lively can supply a lob threat and defensive backstop. Washington and Aldama can preserve size while opening passing lanes. Christie and Marshall can take perimeter assignments. If those players are available, Flagg receives demanding repetitions inside coherent lineups. If they are not, he may accumulate counting production while being forced into tasks that obscure which skills are actually improving.

## The transaction price reveals the front office's bet

Dallas did not acquire Aldama merely by clearing a stale veteran contract. The official trade accounting includes AJ Johnson and meaningful draft compensation, including a protected 2030 first. The protection matters, but so does the principle: the front office exchanged future optionality for a frontcourt player who better fits Flagg's age line and skill environment.

That bet can succeed in several ways. Aldama can become a high-minute connector, improve non-Irving offense, and make larger lineups playable. Sasser can become a trustworthy rotation guard. Biberovic's rights can eventually yield a player without requiring an NBA roster commitment today. The bet can also disappoint without any player being “bad”: Aldama's defensive limitations could restrict postseason matchups, Sasser might remain a small scoring specialist, and the rights may never turn into an NBA contributor.

The deal also clarifies the new leadership's approach after the February Anthony Davis trade and May's hiring. Dallas appears to be moving away from dependence on aging, famous names and toward a wider band of playable size around Flagg. That is an interpretation, not an official declaration, and it should be tested against subsequent contracts. A large expenditure on another aging star would complicate it. For now, the Aldama move is consistent with a roster that wants to compete while reopening a younger timeline.

## A role-range map, not a false depth chart

The following construction map intentionally uses roles rather than exact minutes:

| Function | First option if available | Secondary coverage | Main constraint |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Primary half-court creation | Kyrie Irving | Flagg by committee with Sasser or Nembhard | Irving health; secondary guards do not duplicate his scoring pressure |
| Wing initiation | Cooper Flagg | Aldama, Marshall | Spacing around the initiator |
| Point-of-attack/wing defense | Christie, Marshall, Flagg | Washington, young guards | Keeping enough creation on court |
| Rim protection | Lively | Gafford | Lively recovery and workload |
| Vertical spacing | Lively, Gafford | Powell | Non-shooting overlap |
| Frontcourt shooting/passing | Aldama, Washington | Flagg, Thompson in wing-sized groups | Defensive rebounding and rim protection |
| Movement/gravity shooting | Thompson, Aldama | Christie, Sasser | Whether shooting survives defensive targeting |
| Developmental upside | Flagg | Christie, Sasser, Morez Johnson and young guards | Role stability and NBA readiness |

This map supports several plausible identities. A healthy version can start Irving with two large wings, Flagg, and one center, creating a balanced defense with enough playmaking. A conservative-health version can make Flagg the central organizer and use Aldama as the second frontcourt passer. A defense-first version can deploy Christie, Marshall, Flagg, Washington, and Lively, but it must prove it can score. An offense-first version can use Irving, shooting guards, Flagg, Aldama, and one mobile big—or Aldama at center—while accepting more pressure at the rim.

The key is that Dallas does not possess one obvious five-man answer. Its roster is a menu of tradeoffs. That can be a strength under a coach who identifies matchup-specific functions, or a weakness if rotation choices are driven by reputation and nominal position.

## Why it matters

The Mavericks' 34.4-win model prior is not contradicted by the roster's surface talent. It is a skeptical median for a team whose best creator and best defensive center have not yet demonstrated current availability, whose second-year star may be asked to scale rapidly, and whose depth is stronger at forward and center than at lead guard. The roster has a path above that median because Flagg can improve, Irving can return effectively, Lively can restore the defense, and Aldama can solve real spacing problems. It has a path below it because the same four propositions are correlated: missing creators worsens spacing, missing rim protection forces less skilled defensive lineups, and injuries push young players into unstable roles.

The next good update is not a speculative trade target. It is a transaction-reconciled contract table, followed by evidence from camp and preseason about which players actually perform the four creation functions and which frontcourt pairings preserve space. Until then, role ranges are more honest than a precise depth chart.
