Dallas can build several credible frontcourts around Cooper Flagg, but no combination simultaneously maximizes shooting, creation, rebounding, and rim protection; the useful question is which tradeoff each game state requires.

## Current state as of 2026-07-09

**Official:** Dallas announced on July 8 that it acquired Santi Aldama, Marcus Sasser, and the NBA rights to Tarik Biberovic in a six-team trade. The transaction puts Aldama into a frontcourt already built around Flagg, P. J. Washington, Dereck Lively II, and Daniel Gafford. The official roster grid had not yet caught up when the local [[Dallas Mavericks roster and minutes ledger]] was checked, so this article uses the transaction announcement for the event and avoids pretending every camp or depth player has a settled contract and rotation role.

**Evidence:** The established players offer visibly different production profiles. Aldama played 43 games for Memphis in 2025-26 and averaged 14.0 points, 6.7 rebounds, and 2.9 assists in 27.9 minutes while making 35.0 percent of 4.7 three-point attempts per game. Gafford played 55 games for Dallas, averaging 9.5 points and 6.9 rebounds in 21.7 minutes; he took no threes and made 65.5 percent of his field-goal attempts. Lively played only seven games before right-foot surgery, and an NBA.com player update said on June 26 that he had not progressed to running. Flagg's local research baseline records a productive rookie year but only 29.5 percent from three.

**Projection:** Those facts do not establish a starting five or closing lineup. They establish the constraints. Dallas has two conventional rim-running centers, one seven-foot skill forward who can sometimes function as a center, a versatile two-way forward in Washington, and a developing star whose eventual position may be less important than the jobs assigned around him. Training camp, Lively's recovery, shooting response, and opponent type will decide which theoretical groups are actually playable.

## Geometry means preserving routes, not collecting size

Lineup geometry is the location and movement of threats. A possession needs an initiator who can bend the defense, passing outlets that punish help, a route to the rim, and enough spacing that two offensive players do not invite one defender to guard both. Defense needs a credible last line, enough size to finish possessions, and perimeter mobility that keeps the last line from facing a constant two-on-one. A roster can have excellent individual players and still fail these tests when their preferred spaces overlap; see [[Lineup fit is not the sum of player value]].

Dallas's central collision is straightforward. Lively and Gafford are most dangerous near the rim as screeners, lob targets, and offensive rebounders. Flagg is most valuable when he can attack a tilted floor rather than drive through a crowd. If his jumper does not yet command tight coverage, his defender can shade toward a center in the dunker spot. Add another weakly respected perimeter shooter and a nominally large lineup becomes functionally small: the offense has less usable court because three defenders can occupy the paint and elbows.

The opposite tradeoff appears when Dallas removes the conventional center. Aldama can stand above the break, screen and pop, pass from the slot, or relocate after initiating an action. Washington can defend larger opponents while remaining an outside threat. Flagg can operate as a handler or screener. That shape opens routes, but it also asks someone to absorb center tasks: contain the ball in pick-and-roll, deter shots at the rim, box out a true five, and protect the defensive glass. “Five-out” describes offensive locations, not defensive competence.

The roster therefore should not be sorted only into power forwards and centers. It is better read as a set of frontcourt functions:

| Player | Clearest demonstrated function | Geometry gained | Main unresolved cost |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Cooper Flagg | large initiator, transition force, multipositional defender | creates from a frontcourt slot and can screen or handle | whether defenses respect the jumper; year-two creation efficiency |
| P. J. Washington | two-way forward hinge | lets Dallas retain size without occupying the rim | shooting consistency and creation burden in low-skill groups |
| Santi Aldama | shooting and passing connector | pulls a big away from the lane and adds a second-side decision maker | whether center minutes hold up defensively in difficult matchups |
| Dereck Lively II | mobile rim protector, lob threat, connective passer | anchors the defense while creating vertical space | health, conditioning, and post-surgery workload |
| Daniel Gafford | physical screener, finisher, rebounder, rim protector | provides reliable vertical force and regular-season center minutes | no shooting gravity and less connective range than the ideal Lively outcome |

This table is a role map, not a ranking. Gafford's lack of shooting can be acceptable when four teammates stretch or create. Aldama's extra skill can be less valuable when the opponent relentlessly attacks the rim. Washington can be the essential hinge in one group and redundant in another. The geometry depends on the other four players and on the possession's objective.

## Flagg plus one traditional center is the balanced base

The most coherent default shape uses Flagg with exactly one of Lively or Gafford, plus three perimeter players who collectively provide handling, shooting, and point-of-attack defense. This preserves a vertical threat without placing two non-shooting centers in the lane. It also keeps Flagg in the frontcourt while allowing him to initiate offense against a spread floor.

With Lively, the theoretical ceiling is highest. He can screen for Kyrie Irving or Flagg, dive behind the defense, and pass after catching short of the rim. Defensively, his mobility and length should allow the perimeter to apply more pressure and should give Flagg more freedom to take an aggressive wing assignment. But this is partly a projection from Lively's pre-injury tools and earlier work, not evidence about his July 2026 capacity. Seven games in 2025-26 and the June non-running milestone make any normal-workload assumption premature.

With Gafford, the shape is easier to trust as a minutes base. His 2025-26 line—55 games, 21.7 minutes, 65.5 percent shooting, 2.5 offensive rebounds, and 1.3 blocks per game—shows a player who can absorb real center possessions, finish rather than initiate, and generate extra chances. Those numbers do not prove the lineup will be good, and field-goal percentage for a low-range finisher is not equivalent to self-created efficiency. They do show a stable archetype. Dallas knows where Gafford needs to stand and what advantages a ballhandler must create for him.

The shooting test in either version is demanding. If Flagg remains lightly guarded at the arc, the other three players cannot merely be nominal shooters. At least two need to command immediate closeouts, and the ballhandler must punish a defender who goes under a screen. Washington or Aldama can occupy the forward slot, but stacking Flagg, a center, and a perimeter non-shooter would narrow the driving lanes. This is why a defense-first five can look impressive by name yet struggle to create against a set defense.

The distinction between Lively and Gafford also matters after the first action. An ideal Lively possession can continue through a short-roll read or handoff. A Gafford possession more often asks a guard to finish the creation chain by delivering a lob, pocket pass, or dump-off. That makes Gafford especially dependent on Irving or another defender-bending initiator. It does not make Gafford inferior in every setting: his physicality, screening, and offensive rebounding may be exactly what a second unit needs. It means Dallas should not copy the same playmaking assumptions from one center to the other.

## Aldama is the geometry changer, not a universal center

Aldama's value begins before he touches the ball. A seven-footer who attempted 4.7 threes per game at 35.0 percent forces a different coverage decision from Gafford, who attempted none. The defense must decide whether its big can leave Aldama at the arc to help on Flagg or Irving. Even imperfect shooting has geometric value when the volume and release create a credible consequence for abandonment.

His 2.9 assists per game and low 1.3 turnovers also support a connector role. Those box-score rates do not prove elite playmaking, but they are consistent with a frontcourt player who can receive, move the defense with one dribble, and find the next option. That matters when Irving sits or when Flagg is learning to solve loaded help. Dallas can use Aldama as the release valve above the break, run a handoff, or let him attack a rotating big instead of asking a low-creation wing to improvise.

Beside Lively or Gafford, Aldama converts a two-big lineup from two interior finishers into one interior finisher plus one perimeter connector. That is the credible form of “playing big.” A conceptual Irving–wing–Flagg–Aldama–center group can field enormous size without assigning both frontcourt partners to the paint. It can also overload rebounding and create cross-matches in transition. The cost is perimeter defense: the selected wing and Flagg may need to take the hardest creators, while Aldama must survive in space and the center must cover mistakes.

At center, Aldama creates the cleanest offensive floor. A Flagg–Washington–Aldama frontcourt can run actions in which any member handles, screens, pops, or makes the next pass. It can force a conventional center away from the basket and offer Flagg an empty lane. But describing Aldama as a small-ball five should not smuggle in an unsupported claim that he supplies Lively's rim deterrence. The lineup needs a plan for penetration and rebounding: switch more actions, keep a strong wing at the nail, pressure the ball, and gang rebound. Some opponents will defeat that plan with size, offensive boards, and repeated rim attacks.

Aldama-at-five is therefore best understood as a targeted offensive tool. It may close against a switching opponent when Dallas needs a basket, rescue non-Irving minutes, or force a slow center into uncomfortable space. It should not automatically become the default simply because it looks modern. Its success must be measured at both ends and against opponent quality.

## Washington determines whether large lineups stay functional

Washington is the hinge because his role changes without requiring a substitution. Beside a conventional center, he can defend the larger forward, rebound down, and stand beyond the arc. Beside Aldama, he can take more of the physical defensive work. In a centerless group, he may become the nominal five on one possession while Flagg or Aldama handles the next matchup. That flexibility allows Dallas to change coverage without announcing the change through personnel.

His offensive importance is more conditional. In an Irving-led unit, Washington does not have to create the first advantage; he must make open shots, cut behind ball watching, and attack a closeout. In a group without Irving, asking Washington to become another primary creator can reveal the difference between versatility and advantage creation. Dallas should protect his connective value rather than treating “can do several things” as evidence that he should do everything.

Washington also helps answer the rebounding problem in skilled groups. Aldama and Flagg provide size, but centerless possessions are completed only when someone makes first contact with the opposing big and the remaining players pursue the ball. Washington's ability to take that contact may allow Flagg to release in transition or initiate after securing the rebound. If Dallas instead asks Flagg to absorb the strongest center and create every half-court advantage, the lineup may solve spacing by overloading its most important young player.

## Two traditional centers should be an exception

There is little offensive rationale for ordinary Lively–Gafford minutes. Both derive value from screening, rolling, finishing, and occupying the rim; neither pulls a defender beyond it. Playing them together would allow an opponent to station two large defenders near the basket, go under actions involving Flagg if his jumper remains uncertain, and shrink the space available to Irving. Offensive rebounding could improve, but it would come with poor floor balance and fewer clean drives.

There are narrow cases for the pairing: protecting the rim on an end-of-quarter possession, matching an opponent with exceptional size, surviving foul trouble at forward, or using a brief zone behind them. Those are tactical exceptions, not a rotation identity. Even then, Dallas would need elite shooting and handling from the other three spots, and transition defense could become fragile if both bigs crash the glass.

The rookie and depth frontcourt pieces should be evaluated by the same functional test. A young rebounder or rim protector may eventually substitute for a center function, but activity does not create shooting gravity, and summer or college evidence does not guarantee NBA coverage discipline. Until contract status, camp role, and NBA readiness are verified, they belong in the insurance and development category rather than in a precise minute allocation.

## Four lineup families and their falsification tests

The names below describe structures, not predicted units.

### 1. Balanced vertical lineups

Shape: one primary creator, two credible perimeter defenders/shooters, Flagg, and Lively or Gafford.

The goal is to combine downhill creation with a lob threat and real rim protection. It works if Flagg can attack a single gap, the perimeter defenders are respected, and the center can defend repeated actions. It fails if opponents ignore two shooters, if Lively cannot carry the movement load, or if the non-Irving creator cannot bend the defense.

### 2. Large skilled lineups

Shape: Irving or another organizer, a wing, Flagg, Aldama, and one traditional center.

The goal is to preserve size while adding a second frontcourt passer and shooter. It works if Aldama's defender stays attached and the wing can handle the hardest perimeter assignment. It fails if opponents pull the center into space, target Aldama, or make the group too slow to contain transition.

### 3. Centerless creation lineups

Shape: multiple handlers or shooters around Flagg, Washington, and Aldama.

The goal is five-out space, switching optionality, and a half-court environment in which Flagg can attack without a stationary paint defender. It works if Dallas keeps the ball in front, rebounds collectively, and turns the spacing into rim attempts rather than passive jump shooting. It fails when a dominant interior opponent creates fouls and second chances or when Dallas's extra space does not produce an actual first advantage.

### 4. Defense-first lineups

Shape: large defensive wings, Flagg, Washington, and Lively.

The goal is to pressure the ball, switch selectively, and protect the rim behind length. It works if defense creates transition and at least two perimeter players make defenses pay for helping. It fails when a set defense packs the lane, goes under screens, and forces low-efficiency late-clock creation. The group's success cannot be judged only by defensive possessions; an inability to score eventually degrades transition defense too.

These tests are more useful than assigning a universal grade. They tell Dallas what to watch in camp and preseason: who receives tight closeouts, who initiates after the first action breaks, whether the center can recover across multiple efforts, and who ends possessions with a rebound.

## Health changes geometry nonlinearly

Lively's status is not simply a center-minute subtraction. If he is unavailable, Gafford moves toward heavier traditional-center work, Aldama receives more offense-first center possessions, and the margin for using a non-shooting perimeter player shrinks. Dallas may preserve regular-season competence, but it loses the frontcourt piece with the best theoretical combination of rim protection, mobility, vertical spacing, and connective passing.

Irving's status also changes the frontcourt even though he is a guard. A healthy elite creator makes Gafford's finishing more valuable, creates easier lob routes for Lively, and allows Washington or Aldama to remain connectors. Without Irving, Dallas needs more handling and passing from Flagg and Aldama, and every cramped lineup becomes harder to justify. The two health questions are correlated at the lineup level: losing the creator reduces the value of vertical finishers, while losing the defensive anchor may force more skilled but weaker defensive groups.

That is why [[Dallas Mavericks availability scenarios]] should govern any forecast. A single healthy-roster depth chart hides both workload uncertainty and role cascade. Dallas should maintain separate minute matrices for Lively available, limited, and unavailable states, then adjust the compatible frontcourt families inside each branch.

## What would count as evidence

The first meaningful evidence will be functional rather than rhetorical. For Lively, progression to running, unrestricted basketball activity, contact work, full practice, and repeated preseason minutes would narrow the health uncertainty. For Flagg, defender behavior matters more than a few makes: do teams go under, stunt from him, or stay attached? For Aldama, watch whether he closes as a forward or center and which opponents Dallas protects him from. For Gafford, watch whether May trusts his center minutes when Irving sits or primarily pairs him with the best pick-and-roll creator.

Lineup plus-minus from a handful of preseason possessions will not settle the question. The initial sample should be used to identify roles, coverages, and opponent responses, not to declare winners. A credible in-season conclusion will require repeated combinations against comparable competition, with garbage time and injury-distorted units separated.

## Why it matters

Dallas's frontcourt depth is real, but depth is not redundancy. Lively and Gafford cover vertical center minutes in different ways. Aldama changes where defenders must stand. Washington makes multiple structures defensively plausible. Flagg connects all of them while developing into a larger creation role. The coaching task is to select a frontcourt property—rim protection, force, skill, mobility, or spacing—without accidentally surrendering two others.

The best default is Flagg plus one conventional center, surrounded by enough respected shooting and handling. Aldama beside that structure offers a credible large-skill variation; Aldama at center offers a targeted offensive counter. Lively–Gafford should remain exceptional. Every conclusion beyond those principles depends on health, defender response, and live lineup evidence that did not yet exist as of July 9.
