Dusty May gives Dallas a coherent starting vocabulary—pace, read-and-react spacing, multiple ball screens, decisive passing, selective switching, and shared accountability—but none of those ideas has yet been demonstrated against NBA competition.

## Why it matters

May's appointment changes the most important interpretive question around Dallas. The roster is no longer being judged only as a collection of players; it is being judged as material for a first-time NBA head coach translating a successful college program. If reporting collapses May's stated preferences, Michigan's actual methods, and forecasts for Dallas into one bucket called “the system,” it will manufacture certainty before Dallas has supplied evidence. A cleaner framework makes the coming season useful: each possession can confirm, complicate, or reject a specific hypothesis.

## Evidence ladder

This note uses five labels deliberately:

- **Stated intent** means something May or Dallas basketball leadership publicly said about the intended program. It establishes priorities, not execution.
- **Prior-system evidence** means an official Michigan source describing or documenting what May's college team did. It establishes precedent under different rules and personnel, not a Dallas scheme.
- **NBA translation inference** is a reasoned connection between that precedent and the Mavericks' roster or league environment. It is analysis, not reported fact.
- **Forecast** is a falsifiable expectation about what Dallas may do or what outcome may follow. It should be tested against possessions and lineups.
- **Unknown** means the public record does not yet support an answer. Naming an unknown is preferable to filling it with coaching cliché.

## Current state — as of July 10, 2026

**Official:** Dallas hired May on June 23 after he coached Michigan to the 2026 national championship and a 37–3 season. He has not coached an NBA game, and Dallas has not yet supplied a regular-season possession sample from which to infer its actual scheme. His introductory comments emphasized collaboration, communication, development, and adapting with experienced NBA assistants. President of basketball operations Masai Ujiri publicly associated May's prior teams with size, pace, speed, and toughness.

**Evidence from Michigan:** Michigan's own 2025–26 game notes described a fast transition-oriented, read-and-react offense built on spacing, long-range shooting, several ball-screen actions, and passing from good shots to better shots. The same primary document described an active man-to-man defense that switched the four perimeter positions on and off the ball while usually keeping the center in more traditional coverage. Michigan finished 37–3 and won the national championship, but college results do not measure whether identical rules will work in the NBA.

**Inference:** Dallas hired a coach whose prior principles fit several needs in the current roster: more early offense, more shared creation around [[Cooper Flagg year-two development|Cooper Flagg]], clearer roles for an unusually deep frontcourt, and a defensive language that can use large wings without asking every center to defend identically. The fit is plausible. It is not yet an observed Dallas identity.

**Forecast:** Dallas is more likely to preserve May's decision rules—advance the ball, maintain spacing, create a second action, exchange comparable assignments—than to reproduce Michigan's action menu at the same frequencies. The first preseason possessions can weaken this forecast; regular-season possessions against prepared NBA defenses will be stronger evidence.

**Unknown:** Dallas has not established a public possession-level baseline for May's offense or defense. The final staff responsibilities, lineup rules, center coverages, and hierarchy of creators remain unsettled in the sources used here.

## The useful question is translation, not imitation

“What system will May run?” sounds precise but invites a false answer. A coach does not carry a fixed college playbook into the NBA like a file copied between computers. College and NBA basketball differ in spacing, defensive three seconds, game length, schedule density, scouting repetition, player authority, shot-making, and the consequences of a small matchup disadvantage. The relevant question is which principles survive those changes and how May adjusts their implementation to Dallas's personnel.

That distinction matters because the most portable elements are usually not named sets. They are decision rules: run when an advantage is available; preserve width around a ball screen; move the defense before attacking its final coverage; empower a capable shooter to take an open shot; exchange assignments only when both defenders can execute the handoff; and define roles clearly enough that a deep roster does not become a collection of overlapping claims on the same space.

The less portable elements are the exact frequency of a college action, a college matchup's preferred coverage, and a rotation pattern built for a shorter game and a different calendar. Dallas should therefore be evaluated by whether it implements May's prior logic, not whether a possession resembles Michigan frame for frame.

That method also protects against reverse engineering a philosophy from wins. Michigan's 37–3 championship season is verified achievement, but the record alone does not identify which features caused those wins or which will transfer. Talent, opponent quality, shooting variance, health, and tournament structure all sit between a principle and an outcome. The championship raises the importance of studying May; it does not eliminate the translation problem.

## Pace should mean earlier advantages, not merely shorter possessions

Ujiri identified pace and speed as characteristics of May's basketball. Michigan's own description placed transition opportunities at the front of its offense. For Dallas, that should not be reduced to a target number of possessions per game. A team can play quickly and still generate bad offense if it attacks before spacing is established, turns the ball over, or asks the wrong player to make the first decision.

The productive version of pace has three layers.

First, Dallas can advance the ball before the defense assigns matchups. Flagg, Naji Marshall, P. J. Washington, Santi Aldama, and capable guards can push or pass ahead rather than waiting for a point guard to collect every outlet. That makes versatility functional: a rebound becomes an initiating event, not merely the end of a defensive possession.

Second, early offense can flow directly into a ball screen, handoff, or empty-side action. The defense then has to communicate while retreating instead of loading against a stationary creator. This is particularly important while Kyrie Irving's availability and workload remain uncertain. Dallas cannot replace Irving's handle and shot-making by declaring somebody else an equivalent isolation scorer, but it can reduce the number of possessions that require elite late-clock rescue.

Third, pace must include the speed of decisions after the initial rush ends. A pass that arrives half a second earlier can matter more than a player running faster. May's Michigan materials repeatedly stressed passing from a good shot to a better one and shooting open attempts decisively. Dallas's test will be whether the ball continues to move after an advantage is created, without turning “unselfishness” into purposeless extra passing.

This creates measurable questions for the first months: How often does Dallas attack before the defense is set? Who advances after a defensive rebound? Does early offense produce rim attempts, open threes, and free throws, or merely faster contested shots? What happens when an opponent stops the first action? Pace is successful only when it improves the quality or resilience of the possession.

The evidence boundary is clear. Ujiri's language and Michigan's game notes support **stated intent** and **prior-system evidence** for pace. The claim that Dallas's forwards will push rebounds is an **NBA translation inference** based on their skills. The expectation that early actions will reduce late-clock dependence on Irving is a **forecast**. The actual initiator distribution, turnover cost, and shot-quality effect are **unknowns** until games produce data.

## Read-and-react requires shared reads and protected spacing

“Read-and-react” can sound like freedom from structure. In practice it demands more common structure, not less. Five players must recognize the same trigger, preserve useful distances, and know which teammate fills the space that another vacates. Otherwise freedom becomes two cuts into one lane, a screen with no angle, or a drive toward a waiting helper.

Dallas has reasons to pursue this style. Flagg's passing can be used from several locations rather than only at the top. Aldama can connect actions and draw a larger defender away from the rim. Washington and Marshall can attack tilted defenses. Irving, when available, can alternate between initiating and finishing rather than carrying the entire organizational burden. Centers can screen, dive, re-screen, and create second-side opportunities without needing post touches to remain involved.

The roster also makes spacing discipline difficult. Dallas can plausibly play combinations containing two centers, a power forward, or several players whom a playoff defense may selectively help from. The name of the position does not determine the geometry. The defense cares about shooting credibility, release speed, cutting timing, screening, and whether the ball handler can punish a switch.

May's college history with large lineups is therefore relevant as a problem-solving clue, not proof of an NBA answer. He has experience arranging multiple big players within an offense. Dallas still must decide which big is the screener, which occupies the dunker spot, which can lift or pop, and which defender the opponent will use as its roaming helper. Those answers can change by lineup and opponent. [[Dallas frontcourt geometry]] owns the detailed combinations; the coaching principle here is that role clarity must be possession-specific.

The forecast is conditional: read-and-react organization should help Dallas only if the players share the same reads and the lineups contain enough shooting or compensating movement. If two-big units repeatedly place a defender in the lane without punishment, “freedom” will not explain away the geometry. Whether May treats those possessions with a substitution, a different alignment, or a different role for Flagg is unknown.

## Multiple ball screens can distribute work without pretending creation is equal

Michigan's primary materials described several ball-screen actions within May's offense. Dallas can use that family of actions to distribute decisions, but it should distinguish different forms of creation.

Irving bends a set defense because he can turn the corner, stop into a shot, finish through traffic, and punish a switch. Flagg can leverage size, passing, and developing scoring pressure. Marcus Sasser or Ryan Nembhard may supply narrower guard functions. These are complementary capabilities, not interchangeable labels. Tyus Jones cannot be part of the solution: Dallas waived him on February 28 and Denver signed him on March 5, a sequence preserved in [[Dallas guard creation after roster turnover]].

A varied screen game can make the distinctions useful. Dallas can set a high screen for Irving, use Flagg as the screener and short-roll decision-maker, let Aldama pop, use a center in delay action, or begin with an off-ball exchange that disguises who will handle. The point is not novelty. It is forcing the defense to reveal a coverage and then placing the right decision-maker at the next point of advantage.

This is where coaching translation will become visible. NBA opponents will switch more selectively, put their least mobile defender into a preferred matchup, top-lock shooters, pre-switch before the screen arrives, and shrink the floor from a designated non-shooter. A college action that produced a clean first read may produce only a temporary mismatch in the NBA. May's offense will need counters that are simple enough to execute under schedule pressure and flexible enough to survive opponent-specific coverage.

The most important evaluation is what Dallas does after the first action fails. Does it enter a second screen with time remaining? Can Flagg make the next pass from the middle? Does the weak side move in coordination? Or does the possession revert to a difficult isolation? [[Dallas guard creation after roster turnover]] examines the personnel side; May's task is to create a chain of decisions that reduces dependence on a single savior without denying who the best creator is.

## The defensive clue is selective switching around a protected five

Michigan's 2025–26 notes described switching among four perimeter positions while generally keeping the center in traditional coverage. That is a more informative clue than saying May “switches.” It suggests a defense willing to exchange similar assignments while preserving a distinct job for the rim-protecting five.

Dallas has several wings and forwards who could support that logic. Flagg, Washington, Marshall, Max Christie, Caleb Martin, and Aldama offer different combinations of size and mobility. A lineup with enough comparable defenders can switch an off-ball exchange without losing its shape. It can also keep the center closer to the rim, where Dereck Lively II or Daniel Gafford supplies more value than when stranded against a guard in space.

But selective switching creates three hard problems.

The first is communication. A switch is valuable only if both defenders make it on time. Late exchanges concede slips, open threes, or two defenders following the same player. May's emphasis on connectedness has a tactical counterpart: teammates must communicate before the action determines the outcome.

The second is the weak-side rebound. A perimeter switch can leave a smaller player responsible for a larger cutter or offensive rebounder. Dallas's size helps, but the center cannot protect the rim and collect every miss alone. The low man and the defender at the nail must finish the possession.

The third is coverage diversity. An NBA playoff opponent can hunt the one pairing Dallas does not want to exchange. A rigid switch rule will expose the roster's least compatible matchup. Dallas may need to switch some groups, show and recover with others, play the center at different depths, or scram a small defender out of the post after an initial exchange.

Consequently, the first reliable evidence will not be a coach's promise of defense. It will be the lineup-specific rules visible over repeated possessions: which positions exchange, how the center handles the ball screen, where help comes from, and whether the defense completes the play with a rebound.

Here again, the distinction prevents overclaiming. Four-position switching with a more traditional center is **prior-system evidence** from Michigan, not a promise Dallas made. Applying that structure to Lively or Gafford is an **NBA translation inference**. Expecting Dallas to begin with selective rather than universal switching is a **forecast**. The exact coverage call, exception list, and opponent adjustments are **unknown**.

## Flagg is the central development test

Ujiri has tied May's reputation to player development, and Dallas's future makes Flagg the highest-leverage test. Development here should not mean adding more touches indiscriminately. It should mean increasing the number of possessions in which Flagg recognizes and executes the right high-value decision while the defense gives him progressively less time.

May can place Flagg in several learning environments. As a transition handler, he reads whether to attack, advance, or organize. As a screener, he reads two defenders and makes the short-roll pass. As a wing initiator, he learns how defenders load to his drive. As an off-ball player, he learns when to cut, screen, relocate, or punish a mismatch. Defensive assignments can similarly move from broad versatility toward specific high-value jobs.

The danger is confusing versatility with unlimited responsibility. If Flagg is asked to rebound, initiate, create late-clock offense, guard the opponent's best wing, and cover lineup spacing problems at once, his counting statistics may rise without creating the cleanest development path. Dallas should identify which responsibilities are foundational and which are emergency substitutions for roster weaknesses. [[Cooper Flagg year-two development]] establishes the player-specific evidence and measurement plan.

## Role clarity is more important because the roster is deep

May arrives with a public reputation for connecting players and securing buy-in. Dallas will test that skill differently from college. NBA roles interact with contracts, careers, veteran standing, health management, and matchup-based rotation changes. The roster also contains many credible players competing for fewer coherent combinations.

Clear roles need not mean fixed minutes. They mean a player can explain what earns his minutes, what decisions the lineup needs from him, and how the job changes when personnel changes. For example, a forward may be a spacer beside two interior players, a screener beside Irving, and a secondary handler when Flagg rests. Those are different instructions even if the box score calls all three appearances “power forward.”

May's staff composition will matter. At his introduction he said he expected a mixture of college experience, NBA experience, and some retention, while Ujiri emphasized the importance of NBA experience around a first-time NBA head coach. Assistants influence coverage installation, opponent scouting, development routines, and communication between the head coach's principles and NBA-specific detail. Until the staff and division of responsibility are fully established, strong claims about Dallas's exact scheme are premature.

## A falsifiable evaluation plan

The right way to cover the new regime is to pre-register questions rather than declare success from training-camp language.

### Offense

- Does Dallas create more early advantages without increasing live-ball turnovers?
- Who initiates after rebounds, and does that choice improve transition spacing?
- Do the first and second ball-screen actions connect, or does the offense stall after the initial read?
- Which lineups maintain credible shooting around a rim-running center?
- Does Flagg's larger role improve team shot quality, or simply transfer difficult possessions to him?
- How far does the offense fall when Irving sits or is limited?

### Defense

- Which positions switch on and off the ball?
- What coverage does each center play, and how does it change by opponent?
- Can Dallas protect the rim without conceding uncontested pull-up threes?
- Do perimeter exchanges create rebounding mismatches?
- Which lineups can survive opponent attempts to hunt a single defender?
- Are fouls and offensive rebounds the hidden price of added aggression?

### Development and rotation

- Are roles legible when the roster is healthy, and do they remain legible when injuries force changes?
- Does May use depth to sustain pace and defensive effort, or does constant mixing prevent continuity?
- Which young players receive repeatable developmental tasks rather than scattered minutes?
- Can the staff correct a failed coverage or spacing rule across the compressed NBA schedule?
- Does Dallas preserve multiple ways to win when the preferred style is denied?

These questions keep result and mechanism separate. A team can win a game because it shoots unusually well while executing poorly, or lose while generating a sustainable process. Over time, possession evidence, lineup data, health context, and opponent quality can distinguish noise from identity.

### Evidence checkpoints

Training camp can establish vocabulary and role language, but it cannot validate competitive effectiveness. Preseason can reveal alignments, likely initiators, and default coverages, but experimental lineups and uneven opposition limit outcome claims. The first 15–20 regular-season games can show repeated rules and failure modes; even then, schedule and health require context. Playoff or late-season games against opponents with multiple scouting opportunities provide the strongest test of counter structure.

No single checkpoint should be asked to answer every question. A press conference is suitable evidence for intent. Film is suitable evidence for execution. Lineup and possession data are suitable evidence for frequency and results. Player interviews may clarify roles but remain perceptions rather than proof that the system works.

## What would count as confirmation or disconfirmation

May's intended identity would receive early confirmation if Dallas consistently enters offense before the defense is organized, sustains spacing through a second action, gives Flagg purposeful decisions, switches comparable defenders without losing the center's rim protection, and maintains clear roles across lineup changes. None requires Dallas to copy Michigan's exact formations.

It would be challenged if “pace” produces rushed low-value shots, “read-and-react” produces ambiguous spacing, shared creation leaves nobody able to break the defense, switching creates repeated rebounding failures, or depth results in unstable roles. The sharpest warning would be a system that works only when Irving solves late possessions individually. Irving brilliance can coexist with good coaching, but it cannot be the only counter.

The fair baseline is patience without vagueness. May has a demonstrated college record, an intelligible set of prior principles, and a Dallas roster with several plausible points of fit. He also has no NBA head-coaching sample. Every claim beyond that should be labeled as a hypothesis and tested against the possessions Dallas actually plays.
