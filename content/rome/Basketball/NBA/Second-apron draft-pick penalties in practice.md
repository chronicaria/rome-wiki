The second-apron draft rule first freezes a distant first-round pick, then either releases it after three compliant seasons or moves it to the back of its round after two further second-apron seasons within a four-year test window.

Up: [[Basketball]]

Related: [[2026-27 NBA cap and apron thresholds]] · [[NBA apron mechanics and roster building]]

## The rule in one timeline

Beginning with the 2024–25 cap year, the NBA tests a team's Apron Team Salary at a precise moment: the start of its last regular-season game. If the total exceeds the second apron then, the team's first-round pick in the seventh draft following the end of that cap year becomes the “Frozen Pick.” It cannot be traded.

The next four cap years form the resolution window. Two mutually exclusive outcomes are possible:

- If the team is over the second apron at the same measurement point in at least two of those four years, the Frozen Pick moves to the end of the first round in its draft and remains untradeable.
- If the team is at or below the second apron in at least three of those four years, the pick is unfrozen on the day after the regular season ends in the third compliant year. Its eventual draft position is then determined normally.

This is often compressed to “three times in five years.” That shorthand is directionally useful but incomplete. The first overage creates the freeze. The relevant pick is tied to that trigger year. The next four seasons decide its fate, and the league tests the specific Apron Team Salary at the start of the final regular-season game—not an average payroll, opening-night payroll, tax bill, or postseason roster.

## Mapping trigger years to frozen drafts

The CBA's wording is “the seventh Draft following the last day of such Salary Cap Year.” The official guide supplies the anchor example: finishing 2024–25 over the second apron freezes the 2032 first-round pick.

| Trigger cap year | Seventh draft after that cap year | Four-year resolution window |
| --- | ---: | --- |
| 2024–25 | 2032 | 2025–26 through 2028–29 |
| 2025–26 | 2033 | 2026–27 through 2029–30 |
| 2026–27 | 2034 | 2027–28 through 2030–31 |

For 2026–27, the final second apron is $221.686 million. A team whose Apron Team Salary exceeds that number at the start of its last regular-season game triggers a freeze on its own 2034 first-round pick. “Exceeds” matters: the official summary says a team at or below the line counts as compliant. A dollar over and a dollar under fall on opposite sides of the test, even though real teams preserve larger buffers because accounting can move.

The mapping also explains why a new frozen pick can arise in consecutive seasons. A team over in 2024–25 can freeze 2032; if it is over again in 2025–26, that season can both count as the first of two repeat overages for the 2032 penalty and create a new 2033 freeze with its own later window. The CBA is not a single clock attached permanently to a franchise. Each trigger cap year can attach a separate clock to a separate distant pick.

## Freeze is not forfeiture

A frozen pick still belongs to the team unless some other valid prior transaction controls it. Freezing removes the ability to trade it; it does not immediately transfer the selection to the NBA, another team, or the end of the round. The club can ultimately make the pick if it stays frozen, subject to the later position penalty.

This distinction matters in trade reporting. Saying a team “lost its 2032 first” overstates the immediate consequence. Its asset has become illiquid. It cannot be attached to a star trade during the freeze, and its contingent value is impaired because repeated overages may force it backward, but the team has not forfeited the player selected with it.

Nor is the moved pick necessarily literally No. 30 in every possible draft. The official CBA 101 guide calls it the final pick in the first round, but then addresses multiple penalized teams: if more than one Frozen Pick is moved in the same draft, those teams select at the end of the first round in inverse order of their winning percentage in the regular season immediately before that draft. Expansion, forfeitures, or multiple penalties can make casual “pick 30” language imprecise. “End of the first round” is the durable formulation.

The position penalty is especially severe because it disregards the team's normal draft slot. Imagine a distant roster collapse that would ordinarily produce a lottery selection. If that pick has been moved under the apron rule, poor performance does not restore its lottery position. The CBA assigns it to the penalty block at the back of the first round. The delayed design prevents a wealthy contender from treating today's excess spending only as an owner's cash cost and leaving all basketball downside in the present.

## A worked 2024–25 example

Use the CBA's own trigger year. Team A finishes 2024–25 above the second apron at the measurement point. Its 2032 first is frozen. Now classify the next four years with `O` for over and `C` for at or below.

### Path 1: `O, O, C, C`

The first two following seasons are both over. Once the second repeat overage occurs, Team A has been a second-apron team in the trigger year plus at least two of the next four. Its 2032 first moves to the end of the first round and stays untradeable. The team need not wait for all four years to elapse because the penalty condition can no longer be avoided.

### Path 2: `C, C, C, O`

After the third compliant season, the pick is unfrozen on the day following the last day of that regular season. Its position is no longer restricted by this freeze. The fourth year's later outcome does not reverse the already satisfied resolution for this particular trigger, though a later overage can independently create another Frozen Pick.

### Path 3: `O, C, C, C`

The pick remains frozen through the first two compliant years. One overage has occurred, so the club still needs a third at-or-below result. It earns that result in the fourth following year, at which point the pick unfreezes. This shows why “get below once and the pick is free” is false.

### Path 4: `C, O, C, O`

The second repeat overage arrives in the fourth following season. The Frozen Pick moves to the end. The compliant seasons do not erase the two overages because the rule counts results within the four-year window; they need not be consecutive.

There is no unresolved fifth pattern after four binary tests. At least two of the four are over, producing the penalty, or at least three are at or below, producing release. The thresholds are complements.

## Measurement and planning are different

The final regular-season-game test gives a front office a planning deadline, but it does not make a last-day escape easy. Contracts are guaranteed or partially guaranteed on their own schedules. Trades end at the February deadline. Waiving a player can leave salary on the books. Incentives and other adjustments affect the relevant Apron Team Salary. A team cannot assume it can “fix the number in April” merely because the formal test is late.

The test is also not the ordinary luxury-tax calculation. For the draft rule, the official guide explicitly uses Apron Team Salary. That adjusted measure excludes some amounts found in Team Salary and includes certain incentives that Team Salary excludes, along with other CBA adjustments. A public tax payroll, cash payroll, or list of guaranteed salaries is therefore not conclusive.

For the 2026–27 trigger, a club should model at least three ledgers:

1. the current Apron Team Salary under executed contracts;
2. the expected final-game figure after incentives, roster changes, and retained dead money;
3. the downside figure if uncertain bonuses or replacement signings count more heavily than expected.

Only the first is directly observable at a moment in time. The other two should be scenarios, not fabricated precision.

## How overlapping freezes compound

Consider Team B above the second apron in 2024–25 and 2025–26, then compliant in 2026–27. The first overage freezes 2032. The second overage does two jobs: it is one of the two additional overages that could move 2032, and it independently freezes 2033. The compliant 2026–27 year counts toward releasing both picks, but the windows differ:

- 2032 looks at 2025–26 through 2028–29.
- 2033 looks at 2026–27 through 2029–30.

If Team B is over again in 2027–28, the 2032 pick has now accumulated two overages in its four-year window and moves to the back. For 2033, however, 2027–28 is only the first overage following its trigger; that pick's outcome remains open. The same season can therefore finalize one penalty, continue another, and—because it is itself a trigger year—freeze yet another distant pick.

This rolling structure is the rule's most important practical feature. It makes a multi-year second-apron plan path-dependent. “We can afford two years” is not enough; executives must specify which two years, which already-frozen picks they affect, and whether the club can accumulate three compliant results inside every open window.

It also changes the value of flexibility before a championship core ages. The frozen selection lies seven drafts beyond the trigger, when current stars may be retired or elsewhere. If moved to the end, the penalty can hit the rebuilding version of the franchise rather than the roster that created it. That intertemporal transfer is intentional deterrence but produces a hard governance question: how much future optionality should a club spend for a present title probability?

## The trade-market effect arrives before draft night

Moving a pick backward in 2032 or 2034 is remote. Freezing it has an immediate effect because teams ordinarily trade first-rounders far into the future, subject to the Stepien rule and other limitations. The seventh-year pick is often the newest first-round asset to enter the trade window. Freezing it removes a valuable endpoint from packages for stars.

The effect can propagate. The Stepien rule generally prevents leaving a team without a future first-round pick in consecutive drafts. If the distant endpoint is unavailable, protections and swaps involving nearby years can become harder to structure. A club may still trade other picks, swaps, or incoming selections it owns, depending on the details, but it loses one piece of scheduling flexibility. The penalty is therefore not merely the expected difference between a lottery pick and a late pick. It is also the option value of deploying the asset years earlier in a trade.

Outside teams price this uncertainty too. A counterparty cannot accept a pick the CBA says the original team may not trade. If a related swap or conveyed asset depends on the original team's selection, counsel must determine how the frozen-pick rule and the existing transaction interact. Public asset charts are helpful orientation, but only the executed trade terms and league treatment settle complicated encumbrances.

This is one reason independent specialist reporting focuses on “available to trade,” not just “owned.” ESPN's draft-asset coverage, for example, has described Phoenix's 2032 first as frozen and ineligible to be traded. Ownership, tradability, and projected position are three separate fields.

## Actual first-cycle evidence

The rule began with 2024–25, so its long-run position penalties cannot yet have reached the affected drafts. Contemporary specialist coverage reported Boston, Phoenix, and Minnesota as finishing that cap year over the second apron, freezing their 2032 first-round picks. ESPN's asset analysis specifically identified Phoenix's 2032 first as frozen; later reporting has similarly discussed Boston's 2032 freeze.

These examples corroborate the CBA's timeline, but they should not be stretched into claims about final pick position. A 2032 selection is not moved merely because it was frozen in 2025. Its team must also be over in at least two of the four cap years from 2025–26 through 2028–29. Likewise, a club that cut salary after the trigger did not retroactively avoid the freeze; it began accumulating the compliant seasons required for release.

Team identities and cap accounting can change after published reports, so the canonical fact is the rule and measurement. Any live team ledger should cite a dated source or official league determination rather than infer final apron status from a midseason cap table.

## Common misconceptions

### “Crossing the apron once sends the pick to No. 30”

No. The initial overage freezes the seventh-draft pick. Movement requires at least two additional overages in the next four cap years. And “end of the first round” is more exact than a universal No. 30.

### “One year below unfreezes it”

No. Release requires at least three of the next four cap years at or below the second apron. One compliant season helps but does not finish the test.

### “The test is three consecutive years over”

No. The two further overages may occur anywhere within the next four cap years. Consecutiveness is not required.

### “The team loses the pick immediately”

No. The team retains ownership but cannot trade the Frozen Pick. It may ultimately select a player with it, from the end-of-round position if the penalty matures.

### “Dropping below at the trade deadline automatically solves it”

Not necessarily. The controlling measure is Apron Team Salary at the start of the last regular-season game. Transaction accounting, incentives, dead salary, and later signings still matter. Dropping below in a reliable final ledger can produce one compliant year, not erase prior triggers.

### “Second apron means luxury tax”

Second-apron teams are above the tax line because the second apron is higher, but the draft penalty is not calculated from the tax bill. It uses the separate Apron Team Salary and second-apron threshold.

## A front-office decision rule

Before choosing to finish a cap year over the second apron, a team should value four costs separately.

First is the cash cost: salary plus luxury tax. Second is current transaction rigidity: aggregation, exceptions, incoming salary, cash in trades, and other tools can be restricted. Third is immediate asset illiquidity: the newly frozen seventh-draft first cannot be traded. Fourth is contingent draft-position loss if the club repeats often enough.

The third and fourth should not be collapsed into a single expected pick value. Illiquidity matters now, while the position penalty may never mature. Even after the CBA unfreezes a pick, ordinary trade restrictions—including the Stepien rule and any pre-existing conveyances—still apply. A reasonable decision tree asks:

- Which pick will this cap year's overage freeze?
- Which older frozen-pick windows does this year count toward?
- What is the earliest year in which each pick can unfreeze or become penalized?
- Does the competitive plan require the frozen pick in a trade before then?
- Can the club plausibly record three compliant seasons in every open four-year window without dismantling the core?
- How sensitive is that plan to maximum-salary escalators, options, draft hits, and replacement costs?

A one-year championship push can be rational if the incremental title probability is real and the club has a credible exit. Repeatedly saying “one more year” without mapping overlapping windows is not a strategy. The rule is designed to make persistence, rather than a single expensive season, carry the deepest sporting penalty.
