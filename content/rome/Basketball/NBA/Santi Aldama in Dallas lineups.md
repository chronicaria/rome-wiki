Santi Aldama gives Dallas a seven-foot shooter, connector, and mismatch attacker whose best base role is skilled forward beside a true center; using him at center can unlock five-out offense, but it transfers rim protection, physical rebounding, and containment duties to the rest of the lineup.

## Current state as of 2026-07-10

Dallas officially announced on July 8 that it had acquired Aldama, Marcus Sasser, and the NBA rights to Tarik Biberovic in a six-team transaction. The official NBA player page now identifies Aldama as a Dallas forward-center and lists him at 7 feet and 215 pounds. In 43 games for Memphis in 2025-26, he averaged 14.0 points, 6.7 rebounds, and 2.9 assists in 27.9 minutes. He made 35.0 percent of 4.7 three-point attempts per game and averaged 1.3 turnovers. Those are useful role indicators, not proof that a particular Dallas lineup will work.

The current evidence also contains an important availability qualification. NBA.com's player news records that Aldama underwent a season-ending arthroscopic procedure and received an orthobiologic injection for discomfort in his right knee in March 2026. The transaction confirms that Dallas acquired him; it does not by itself establish an unrestricted training-camp workload. This article therefore maps basketball fit while keeping health and minute capacity conditional. [[Availability risk in NBA projections]] applies before any exact rotation forecast.

The position label “forward-center” is accurate but incomplete. At forward, Aldama can add size and skill while a conventional center performs the hardest interior defensive jobs. At center, he changes the offensive geometry more dramatically because every Dallas player can begin away from the rim. The same move removes the conventional back-line defender. His position is therefore not a fixed identity; it is a transfer of responsibilities among five players.

## The transferable package

The most portable Aldama skill is not merely three-point accuracy. It is the combination of height, willingness to shoot, and competence after the catch. A reluctant 35-percent shooter may not move a defense. A seven-footer who attempts nearly five threes in fewer than 28 minutes makes abandonment carry a visible cost. Dallas can place him above the break, in the corner, or as a screener who pops, forcing an opposing big to choose between protecting the lane and arriving at the arc.

That gravity should help Cooper Flagg and Kyrie Irving in different ways. Flagg needs driving lanes while his own jumper develops into a shot opponents must honor. Irving, when available, can punish the moment Aldama's defender shows at the ball. A traditional center can still occupy the dunker spot or roll through the lane because Aldama does not need the same space. This is why Aldama can make a large lineup functionally spacious rather than simply large.

His passing is the second transferable skill. The 2.9-assist, 1.3-turnover line does not establish primary creation; assists depend on role and teammate shotmaking, and turnovers omit possessions in which a player never tries the difficult pass. It does support a narrower conclusion: Aldama can keep an advantage alive. He can catch above the break, reverse the ball, make a high-low entry, hit a cutter, or take one dribble and deliver to the next opening. Dallas's guard inventory makes that connective competence valuable because [[Dallas guard creation after roster turnover]] distinguishes creating the first advantage from extending one. Aldama is much more credible in the second job.

The third transferable skill is attacking a displaced matchup. When a smaller defender switches onto him, Aldama can use his reach around the rim or pass over the top. When a slow big closes out, he can put the ball down and make the defense rotate again. Neither action requires him to become a high-volume isolation scorer. It asks him to punish the compromise the opponent already made to survive the first action.

He also offers functional rebounding size. His 6.7 rebounds per game show participation, not dominance, and should not be read without minutes, role, and team context. Still, a seven-foot forward can finish defensive possessions, reach balls smaller spacers cannot, and let Dallas retain size when one frontcourt player leaves the paint. The question at center is not whether Aldama can rebound at all; it is whether the five-man group can make first contact, keep an opposing center off the glass, and collect misses after he has helped away from his man.

Finally, Aldama's skills are symmetric enough to make him a useful tactical bridge. He can screen or handle, space or cut, make the next pass or finish. That does not mean he is equally good at every task. It means Dallas can change an action without changing personnel. A possession can begin with Aldama spacing beside a Lively ball screen, continue with Aldama receiving the release pass, and end with him screening for Flagg. That elasticity is especially useful when a defense switches the original matchup or denies the first entry.

## Forward is the stable base role

Aldama's most defensible default is power forward beside Dereck Lively II or Daniel Gafford. In this structure, the center supplies vertical spacing, screening force, rim protection, and the first body on an opposing five. Aldama supplies horizontal spacing and secondary decisions. The jobs complement rather than duplicate each other.

Beside Lively, the theoretical ceiling is unusually connective. Lively can roll, catch short, and pass; Aldama can pop or become the next receiver. An Irving–Lively pick-and-roll can leave Aldama one pass away against a rotating defense. A Flagg–Aldama action can pull both frontcourt defenders away from Lively, leaving a lob or offensive-rebound route behind them. Defensively, Lively's length and mobility can cover some penetration while Aldama uses his size against the less forceful frontcourt matchup.

That formulation is conditional on Lively's recovery. Dallas's existing research records only seven Lively appearances in 2025-26 and no progression to running as of a June 26 NBA update. It would be circular to call Aldama a safe forward because Lively solves the defense and then assume Lively is available. Dallas needs separate rotation branches for Lively unrestricted, limited, and unavailable.

Beside Gafford, the division is simpler. Gafford is a physical screener, rim finisher, offensive rebounder, and conventional back-line defender. Aldama can occupy the perimeter and deliver entries or dump-offs without competing for Gafford's preferred space. The limitation is creation after the first action. Gafford generally needs a handler to produce the opening; Aldama can extend it but should not be presumed to manufacture Irving-level pressure. Without Irving, Dallas may need Flagg or a guard to bend the defense before the frontcourt partnership becomes dangerous.

Forward minutes also offer a cleaner defensive assignment. Aldama can begin on a less explosive forward, use his length in passing lanes, and switch selected actions without being the permanent rim protector. The center remains behind the play if Aldama is beaten. Dallas can ask him to execute a coverage rather than cover every failure. This distinction matters more than whether a lineup card writes “PF” or “C.”

The cost is perimeter speed. A Flagg–Aldama–center frontcourt is enormous, but one of Flagg or the selected wing will often need to defend the opponent's best creator. If Aldama is pulled into repeated high screens or forced to chase a movement shooter, Dallas may have size without enough containment. The large-skilled group therefore needs point-of-attack resistance, not just nominal wings. Max Christie, Naji Marshall, or another credible defender may be more important to the lineup than adding a fourth scorer.

## Center is an offensive counter, not an automatic promotion

Moving Aldama to center removes the stationary interior teammate and can create a true five-out shape. A Flagg–P. J. Washington–Aldama frontcourt allows every member to handle, screen, pop, cut, or pass. Irving can attack without a traditional rim runner occupying the lane. Flagg can receive a ball screen from Aldama and force the opposing center to defend in space. Washington can take the stronger interior matchup while Aldama pulls the other big away from help.

The tactical value is clearest against a slow center or a switching defense. Against a slow center, repeated pick-and-pop actions make the defender cover more ground and can turn a paint protector into a closeout defender. Against switching, Aldama can seal a smaller player or facilitate from above the defense while Dallas identifies the mismatch. In late offense, having five players able to catch outside the lane reduces the chance that an opponent can guard two Dallas players with one helper.

But “center” includes defensive jobs that shooting does not solve. Someone must contain the ball in pick-and-roll, protect the rim when the first line breaks, tag cutters, box out the opposing five, and finish the possession. Aldama's height can help; his listed 215-pound frame and forward-oriented role do not support treating him as equivalent to Gafford or Lively against every interior matchup. Dallas may need Washington or Flagg to take the strongest big, which shifts wear and foul exposure onto players with major offensive responsibilities.

Centerless defense is therefore a system rather than an individual bet. Dallas can switch more often to keep Aldama out of deep drop. It can pressure the ball so drivers do not arrive downhill. A strong nail defender can shrink gaps, and all five players can rebound toward the ball. Those answers have costs: switching can create post mismatches, pressure can allow back cuts, nail help concedes kick-out threes, and gang rebounding slows transition offense. The lineup has succeeded only if the extra offensive space exceeds the defensive price.

Opponent type should determine the dose. Aldama-at-five is more plausible against a spacing center who does not punish switches or an opponent whose rim pressure comes from slower creators. It is less plausible against an elite offensive rebounder, a powerful post scorer, or relentless downhill guards. It may be useful for a short scoring stretch without being sustainable for a 30-minute center workload. Calling the lineup “small ball” can obscure this: Dallas remains physically tall, but it is small in the specific functions of mass, rim deterrence, and box-out leverage.

## Four useful lineup families

### Irving, two-way wing, Flagg, Aldama, Lively

This is the high-skill large lineup. Irving creates the first advantage; Lively rolls; Aldama spaces and connects; Flagg attacks the second side; the wing takes the hardest perimeter assignment. It has the clearest two-big logic because only one big lives at the rim. Its failure modes are Lively's availability, insufficient wing shooting, and too much perimeter responsibility for Flagg.

### Irving, shooter, Flagg, Washington, Aldama

This is the offense-first centerless group. It gives Irving maximum driving space and allows three large frontcourt players to exchange screening and handling roles. Washington should absorb the most physical defensive matchup, while Flagg supplies help range. It can close when Dallas needs a basket, but an opponent with interior force can turn every stop into a rebounding test.

### Organizer, Christie, Marshall, Aldama, Gafford

This is a possible non-Irving bridge. Christie and Marshall provide defensive coverage and can attack advantages; Gafford supplies a target and rim protection; Aldama becomes the release valve. The group depends on the organizer creating enough pressure and on at least two perimeter defenders being respected as shooters. If the first action produces no advantage, Aldama's connection skills cannot substitute for primary creation.

### Multiple guards, Flagg, Aldama

This is a pace-and-skill bench concept rather than a fixed five. Aldama can trail into a three, screen for a small guard, or initiate a handoff while Flagg attacks a cross-match. The group may score before the opposing center establishes paint position. If it fails to generate early offense, its defensive size and rebounding must survive the next half-court possession. Pace is a way to exploit Aldama's range, not a cure for missing rim protection.

## Limitations Dallas should not explain away

First, shooting gravity must be measured through defender behavior, not reputation. Aldama's 35.0 percent in 2025-26 is credible enough to demand attention, but a short slump, slower release under pressure, or opponent-specific willingness to concede the shot can change the geometry. Dallas should track whether defenders stay attached, how far they help, and whether Aldama converts above-the-break attempts when involved as a screener.

Second, connective passing is not primary creation. Aldama can improve a possession without being the player who reliably defeats a set defense late in the clock. Dallas should avoid making his versatility the answer to its guard shortage. The useful question is whether his passing reduces the burden on creators, not whether it eliminates the need for them.

Third, positional versatility can become defensive ambiguity. If Aldama plays forward, Dallas must identify which perimeter assignment he can hold without forcing constant help. If he plays center, it must identify the rim-protection and rebounding plan. “Can play either spot” is not evidence that either deployment works against a specific opponent.

Fourth, health precedes tactical optimization. A March knee procedure makes availability, lateral movement, conditioning, and contact tolerance live questions. Dallas should not infer October capacity from a July transaction announcement. The first meaningful updates are clearance, full practice participation, preseason movement, and workload without recurrence—not a projected minute total.

## Evaluation plan

Dallas can learn more by tracking jobs than by reacting to early lineup plus-minus. For forward minutes, watch whether Aldama's defender remains attached, whether the traditional center still has a clear rolling lane, and whether the group contains the ball without placing Aldama in repeated emergency rotations. For center minutes, track rim attempts allowed, offensive-rebound rate, foul pressure, and which Maverick takes the opposing five.

On offense, distinguish first advantage from advantage extension. Record whether Aldama's screen forces two defenders to the ball, whether he attacks the resulting closeout, and whether his pass creates an immediate shot or merely resets the possession. Track turnovers, but also possessions in which he declines a useful advantage. The goal is not to maximize touches; it is to learn which touches convert his unusual size-skill combination into team leverage.

Samples should be separated by opponent quality, garbage time, Irving availability, Lively availability, and Aldama's position on defense. A nominal Aldama-at-center lineup in which Washington guards the opposing five is different from one in which Aldama provides the back line. Aggregating them would hide the very role distinction Dallas needs to test.

## Why it matters

Aldama expands Dallas's menu because his forward skills travel across lineup types. Shooting changes where a big defender stands. Passing preserves an advantage. Size allows him to finish and rebound without requiring every possession to be designed for him. These qualities should help beside either traditional center and can create a particularly useful developmental environment for Flagg.

His center minutes are more conditional. They can create Dallas's cleanest offensive floor and punish opponents that cannot defend in space, but they do not recreate Lively's rim protection or Gafford's physical center work. The honest hierarchy is therefore forward as the base role, center as a targeted counter, and full-time center as an unproven proposition.

That distinction keeps expectations disciplined. Aldama does not need to solve every Dallas weakness to justify his role. If he makes large lineups spacious, improves non-Irving possessions as a connector, and gives the coaching staff a credible five-out changeup, he has transferred his most valuable skills. Whether Dallas can protect those skills from postseason defensive targeting will be learned through health, assignments, and repeated lineup evidence.
