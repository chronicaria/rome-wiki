Duke enters 2026–27 with one proven returning college rim protector in Patrick Ngongba II and several plausible secondary answers, but its actual paint defense will depend more on foul control, point-of-attack containment, help timing, and defensive rebounding than on raw block totals.

## The July answer is a hierarchy, not a guarantee

As of July 10, 2026, Patrick Ngongba II is Duke's strongest established rim-protection answer. Duke's official 2025–26 totals credit him with 43 blocks in 701 minutes, or 1.1 per game, while playing 21.9 minutes per game. Those totals prove that he produced blocks against college competition in Duke's system. They do not prove that he can anchor the paint for 30 minutes, cover every ball-screen scheme, or remain available against every matchup. His 90 fouls—about 5.1 per 40 minutes—are the central qualification.

The alternatives broaden the menu without settling it. Reported transfer Drew Scharnowski produced 1.3 blocks per game at Belmont and earned Missouri Valley all-defensive recognition. Duke signee Maxime Meyer carries a 7-foot-3 wingspan, a 9-foot-5 standing reach, and official international and circuit block production. Cameron Williams brings a 7-foot-1.5 wingspan and reported 2.6 blocks per game in both his high-school junior season and Adidas 3SSB play. Reported addition Joaquim Boumtje Boumtje recorded 2.1 blocks per game at the 2026 FIBA U17 World Cup, including a seven-block performance against Japan.

Those are distinct evidence classes. Ngongba's sample comes in the ACC and Duke's scheme. Scharnowski's comes against older college players, but at a different competitive level and in a different defense. Meyer and Williams have official prospect measurements and pre-college production. Boumtje Boumtje has a spectacular recent international youth sample, not a college sample. Combining their block averages into a claim that Duke is “loaded with rim protection” would erase the very translation questions that matter.

The defensible summer hierarchy is therefore:

1. **Ngongba is the presumptive primary anchor**, subject to foul sustainability and expanded-minute evidence.
2. **Scharnowski is the experienced changeup**, with secondary-rim-protection evidence but an unresolved lone-center ceiling against ACC size.
3. **Meyer is the clearest developmental length bet**, with tools and youth production that must survive college screening, strength, and decision speed.
4. **Williams and Boumtje Boumtje are mobile secondary candidates**, whose value may come as much from early help and lineup versatility as from playing conventional drop center.
5. **Dame Sarr and Duke's large wings are part of the system**, because low-man help and rebounding often determine whether the nominal center can contest at all.

This is not a depth chart. It is an allocation of defensive risk.

## What rim protection actually includes

A block is the most visible result and one of the least complete descriptions. Functional rim protection begins before the shot. The on-ball defender influences the drive. The screen defender establishes a depth. The weak-side defender tags the roller. The back-line player decides whether to meet the ball, protect the lob, or remain attached to a shooter. After the contest, somebody must secure the rebound. A blocked shot recovered by the offense is not a finished stop; a drive discouraged before the restricted area may be excellent rim protection without appearing in a box score.

For Duke, five mechanisms should be kept separate.

**Deterrence** is the offense declining a rim attempt because the center is positioned early. It can be measured through opponent decisions and rim-attempt frequency, not blocks alone.

**Contest quality** is whether the defender arrives square, stays vertical, avoids unnecessary body contact, and forces a difficult release. A made contested layup may still reflect good process; an opponent miss after an uncontrolled fly-by may not.

**Coverage execution** is whether the big protects the correct threat. In drop, that means containing the handler while preserving the roller. At the level, it means influencing the ball and recovering before the pocket pass becomes a four-on-three. On a switch, it means delaying penetration long enough for the shell to reorganize.

**Foul economy** is availability. A center who blocks two shots but commits two avoidable first-half fouls may weaken the next 15 minutes. Vertical contests, early positioning, and disciplined hands can be more valuable than an additional event play.

**Possession completion** is the rebound or controlled loose ball. When Ngongba leaves his matchup to contest, the low man or weak-side wing inherits a box-out. The center should not be blamed for every offensive rebound created by his help, nor credited with a stop that teammates fail to finish. [[Duke 2026-27 defensive rebounding]] and rim protection are two stages of one possession.

## Ngongba's opportunity is to scale without losing availability

Ngongba's 2025–26 record supplies Duke's best starting point. In addition to 43 blocks, the official totals show 5.8 rebounds per game and 70 offensive rebounds. His size, experience, and minutes in Duke's terminology make him the player most capable of organizing the back line immediately. That continuity matters: an early screen call can give a guard the half-step needed to recover, and an early low-man call can prevent two helpers from rotating to the same threat.

The constraint is mathematical as well as tactical. Ninety fouls in 701 minutes scale to more than five per 40. Simply projecting his per-minute blocks and rebounds into a larger role ignores that a player cannot supply 30-minute anchoring if foul accumulation repeatedly changes his aggression or removes him. Duke needs fewer late reaches, fewer contests begun from poor position, and fewer offensive fouls that consume the same five-foul budget.

His best base may be the contain-and-recover structure described in [[Duke 2026-27 ball-screen coverage menu]]. Keeping Ngongba closer to the basket uses his established interior presence while allowing the guard to recover over the screen. But “drop” is not one fixed location. Against an elite pull-up shooter, too deep a drop concedes rhythm jumpers. Against a vertical roller, too high a position opens the lob. Ngongba must calibrate his depth to the handler, screener, clock, and help location.

The decisive development is therefore processing rather than a higher block average. Can he call the screen before contact? Can he retreat while staying square? Can he show his body to the handler without losing the roller behind him? Can he contest with the correct hand and then find a rebounder? Can he switch late in the clock without opening an immediate drive? These actions would allow Scheyer to vary coverage while preserving Ngongba's strongest function.

The failure mode is equally concrete. If guards repeatedly die on screens, Ngongba will defend two players. If he steps up late, he will either concede the pass or foul. If the weak-side wing fails to tag, the rotation will look like a center error even when the chain broke elsewhere. Evaluating him honestly requires logging the first breakdown, not merely the final shot.

## Scharnowski changes the coverage, not just the name

Scharnowski's Belmont record makes him more than an emergency reserve. Belmont's dated March 4 ledger recorded 39 blocks and 23 steals in 29 appearances, and later reporting lists 1.3 blocks per game with all-defensive recognition. Those figures support defensive activity and mobility. They do not establish that a listed 6-foot-9, 230-pound forward can absorb every high-major center as Duke's only back-line anchor.

His most promising rim-protection value may be preventative. A mobile big who shows higher, switches selectively, or disrupts the pocket pass can keep the ball from arriving cleanly at the rim. That is a different mechanism from Ngongba's more conventional interior presence. Duke should not force both players into identical coverage and then compare block rates.

As the lone five, Scharnowski must prove four things: hold position against stronger finishers, contain a guard for the required number of dribbles, recover to the play without fouling, and rebound after leaving his matchup. A successful switch that ends in a putback is an incomplete possession. His Belmont rebounding and event production offer a credible hypothesis, but ACC opponents will test the strength and length translation.

Beside Ngongba, Scharnowski could become a mobile first responder while Ngongba remains nearer the basket. The pairing may let Duke place Scharnowski on a screening four, switch selected actions, and use Ngongba as the final rim defender. It also creates a new vulnerability: a skilled stretch four can pull Scharnowski away, while offensive spacing at the other end may limit how often Duke can use the group. Two bigs create useful rim protection only if assignments remain clear. Both cannot chase the same block, and neither can assume the other will collect the rebound.

The correct preseason question is not whether Scharnowski is “a rim protector.” It is which threats he can remove, in which coverage, against which position type.

## The freshmen offer different defensive bets

Meyer owns the most traditional developmental profile. Duke officially lists his 7-foot-3 wingspan and 9-foot-5 standing reach and reports 3.2 blocks per game on the Adidas 3SSB circuit. FIBA's player ledger gives him a seven-game 2025 U19 World Cup sample with 1.3 blocks per game. Reach gives him error margin around the basket, but it cannot make the initial decision. College teams will involve him in repeated screens until he proves he can identify the action, set his depth, and recover without opening the roller.

His early playable package should be narrow: call the coverage, protect the rim vertically, run to the proper defensive spot, rebound his area, and avoid fouls that come from chasing blocks. If that package holds, Meyer can supply real reserve-center minutes without needing a polished offensive role. If it does not, his measurements will remain developmental evidence rather than current lineup value.

Williams offers a more portable but less conventional path. Duke's signing release supplies the 7-foot-1.5 wingspan and reports 2.6 blocks per game in two different pre-college settings. The same release describes perimeter skill and multi-position defense. That combination makes him a candidate to help from the weak side, switch some actions, and recover across space. It does not establish that he can anchor the paint against college centers.

Williams may be most valuable when another big handles the primary center assignment. From the four, he can tag a roll, contest from the side, and use length without absorbing every direct screen. To make that role functional, he must also rebound after helping. A highlight block from the weak side can conceal an abandoned shooter or a lost box-out. His test is complete-possession versatility, not event production.

Boumtje Boumtje arrives, if Duke's reported addition is reflected in the final roster, with the loudest recent youth result. FIBA credits him with 15 blocks in seven games at the 2026 U17 World Cup, and his seven blocks against Japan set a USA single-game record at the event. He also collected 10.9 rebounds and 1.7 steals per game, evidence of broad activity rather than blocks alone.

The age and competition boundary must remain visible. U17 opponents do not reproduce the screening angles, strength, pace manipulation, or shooting of veteran college teams. Boumtje Boumtje's first Duke question is whether he defends the action before the shot: communicates the screen, contains the ball, recovers to the roller, and secures the rebound. His shooting could make small or mobile lineups easier to play offensively, but defensive lineup access still must be earned possession by possession.

## Wings determine whether the big can protect the rim

Duke cannot solve paint defense by selecting a center and absolving the other four players. Point-of-attack containment determines how many emergencies reach the back line. A guard who sends the handler toward the planned help buys the big time. A guard who concedes the middle forces the center to protect a pull-up, rim finish, roller, and corner pass simultaneously.

Large wings such as Sarr carry the next obligation. Sarr's 14 blocks in Duke's 2025–26 totals show some event production, but his larger value may be as the low man: tag the roller when Ngongba engages the ball, contest a pass or finish, then recover or find a body. Williams or another long wing could perform a similar function. The metric is not only blocks. It is how often the low man arrives early enough that the center can make one decision instead of two.

This relationship also explains why a nominally smaller lineup can sometimes protect the rim well. If switching removes the initial advantage, the offense may never generate a clean downhill drive. Conversely, a large lineup can protect the rim poorly if slow containment creates repeated rotations. Size determines the available tools; coordination determines whether they are used.

## Four lineup structures and their tradeoffs

### Ngongba with four perimeter players

This is the clearest baseline. Ngongba owns the primary contest and back-line calls. A large wing owns the low-man tag and weak-side rebound. Guards chase or contain according to the coverage. The structure maximizes role clarity and likely gives Duke its most conventional deterrent.

Its weakness is dependence on Ngongba's availability. Foul trouble or fatigue can force an abrupt scheme change. It also asks the perimeter to keep the first action manageable; otherwise Ngongba becomes the solution to every error.

### Scharnowski at five

This group can defend farther from the basket, show higher, or switch more selectively. Its rim protection would come partly from preventing clean entries. The group should be judged through opponent rim frequency, complete defensive possessions, and rebound rate rather than comparing Scharnowski's blocks directly with Ngongba's.

### Two true frontcourt players

Ngongba plus Scharnowski, Meyer, Williams, or Boumtje Boumtje can create a primary and secondary rim defender. The benefit is layered help and rebounding size. The cost is coverage of a mobile four and possible offensive congestion. Duke should use the pairing when the defensive problem justifies the exchange, not because more height automatically means a better defense.

### Wing-at-five or switch-heavy lineups

A small group cannot imitate deep-drop protection. It must flatten actions, consume clock, front mismatches, scram a small defender out of the post, and rebound collectively. Sarr, Williams, or another large wing could serve as the back-line communicator. This structure is most credible late in the clock or against five-out opponents. It becomes fragile if the opponent reaches the rim before the switch structure is set or repeatedly wins the offensive glass.

## Facts, inferences, and forecasts

### Established facts

- Duke's 2025–26 totals record 43 blocks, 5.8 rebounds per game, and 90 fouls in 701 minutes for Ngongba.
- Belmont's official and contemporary records show meaningful block, steal, and rebounding production for Scharnowski, plus all-defensive recognition.
- Duke's signing release gives Meyer and Williams verified measurements and reported pre-college block production.
- FIBA verifies Meyer's seven-game 2025 U19 sample and Boumtje Boumtje's seven-game 2026 U17 sample, including the latter's 15 blocks and seven-block game.
- Duke's public roster page had not yet become a complete named 2026–27 ledger when accessed July 10.

### Reasonable inferences

- Ngongba should open as Duke's primary back-line anchor because he owns the strongest relevant college and system evidence.
- Scharnowski may create more value through mobility and coverage flexibility than through static drop protection.
- Meyer has the clearest developmental profile for conventional reserve rim protection.
- Williams and Boumtje Boumtje may be most useful initially as mobile or secondary helpers rather than sole anchors.
- Duke's collective paint defense will rise or fall with perimeter containment, low-man timing, and rebounding.

### Forecasts that require game evidence

- Ngongba will reduce his foul rate enough to sustain a materially larger role.
- Scharnowski can anchor against ACC centers as the only big.
- Any freshman's youth production will translate immediately to prepared high-major offense.
- Duke can play two bigs without conceding too much perimeter defense or offensive spacing.
- A wing-at-five group can survive the defensive glass over a meaningful sample.

## The verification ledger

Duke should evaluate rim protection at the possession level once games begin. Record the coverage, the first breakdown, the primary helper, the shot location, contest type, foul cause, and rebound result. Count drives abandoned or redirected before the rim. Separate blocks kept in bounds from blocks returned to the offense. Record whether the big protected the roller, the handler, or neither. Attribute offensive rebounds created by help rotations to the entire chain.

For Ngongba, the decisive indicators are fouls per 40, minutes against elite opponents, rim deterrence, vertical-contest rate, and defensive rebounds after his own contest. For Scharnowski, track center matchups, successful high shows or switches, recovery to the roller, and rebounding after movement. For Meyer, Williams, and Boumtje Boumtje, track call accuracy, late rotations, avoidable fouls, and whether the same task survives against starters rather than reserves.

Opponent context is essential. Blocks against an undersized nonconference team cannot settle ACC translation. A low block total against a team that never challenges the rim may reflect deterrence. A high total in a game full of conceded paint touches may reflect repeated emergency opportunities. The ledger should explain mechanism rather than award a winner.

Duke has enough length and experience to construct several credible paint-defense plans. It does not yet have evidence that all of them work. The safest July projection is a Ngongba-centered base, a Scharnowski changeup, and carefully bounded freshman or wing responsibilities. The ceiling comes if those roles become interchangeable without losing communication. The floor drops if Duke mistakes block totals for a connected defense or asks Ngongba to repair every perimeter mistake while carrying a five-foul limit.

## Open questions

- Can Ngongba reduce avoidable fouls while playing more minutes and defending more than one ball-screen coverage?
- Does Scharnowski's mobility and event production translate into lone-center rim protection against ACC strength?
- Which freshman can execute the back-line call, vertical contest, and rebound as one repeatable sequence?
- Will Duke's best low-man helper be Sarr, Williams, another large wing, or a second big?
- Can two-big groups defend stretch fours without surrendering the offensive spacing needed to remain playable?
- How often can Duke use wing-at-five switching before defensive rebounding erases the advantage?
- What does opponent rim frequency show after the first five competitive games, and how much of it reflects deterrence rather than shot luck?
