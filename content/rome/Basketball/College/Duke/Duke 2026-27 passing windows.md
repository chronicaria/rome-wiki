Duke's 2026–27 passing problem is not simply finding one point guard; it is creating windows early enough, wide enough, and from enough different locations that several decision-makers can keep an advantage alive.

## The window is the unit of analysis

As of July 10, 2026, Duke's public roster evidence supports a passing ecosystem more confidently than it supports a single dominant creator. The program has officially described a team with five returners, three transfers, and a highly regarded freshman class, but its live roster page still showed 2025–26 when checked. The completed-season statistics establish that returning guard Cayden Boozer recorded 115 assists against 55 turnovers, Patrick Ngongba II had 64 assists from the center position, and Caleb Foster supplied veteran handling beside 39 made threes. Official prior-school statistics establish that transfer big Drew Scharnowski averaged 2.6 assists per game at Belmont. Those facts identify possible passers. They do not yet prove that Duke can consistently produce the space in which their passes become useful.

A passing window is the temporary lane between the ball and a productive receiving point. It may lead directly to the rim, to a roller, to a shooter, or merely to the next player who can attack a shifted defense. The window has four properties: **location**, **width**, **duration**, and **value after the catch**. A technically completed pass can still be a failure if it arrives late, forces the receiver away from the rim, or transfers the ball without transferring an advantage. A risky pass can be correct when the window is brief and the reward is a layup. Evaluating Duke by assists alone would erase these distinctions.

The central July hypothesis is that Duke should build its offense around *manufactured windows* rather than asking one guard to improvise them on every possession. Screens, cuts, seals, handoffs, pace, and alignment can force defenders to reveal space. The passer then needs to recognize which window opened, deliver on time, and resist the more spectacular pass when a simpler continuation is available. This framing connects [[Duke 2026-27 turnover economy]] to shot quality: ball security is not minimizing ambitious passes; it is matching pass difficulty to the actual window.

## Five windows Duke should deliberately create

### The entry window

The first test is whether Duke can enter an action cleanly against denial. An entry is not administrative. A wing catch one step farther from the basket changes the angle of a ball screen; a delayed post feed lets help establish position; a floating reversal gives a closeout time to arrive under control. Opponents described in [[How opponents pressure Duke's initiators]] can damage a possession before the nominal action begins simply by making the ballhandler retreat or by pushing every catch toward the sideline.

Boozer's 2.09 assist-to-turnover ratio in 2025–26 makes him the clearest returning candidate to stabilize this phase, but his task should extend beyond bringing the ball over half court. Duke can use shallow cuts, guard-to-guard pitches, and early drags so that the entry itself changes a matchup. Foster's shooting makes him useful on either side of the pass: he can initiate, or his relocation can pull a defender away from the next window. Freshman Deron Rippey Jr. has an authoritative USA Basketball profile as a guard committed to Duke, but there is no college-possession evidence yet that establishes how quickly he will diagnose ACC denial.

The coaching test is concrete: does Duke begin its first consequential action with at least 20 seconds on the shot clock and the intended spacing intact? If the answer is no, later assist totals may conceal an offense repeatedly solving self-created emergencies.

### The pocket and short-roll window

Ball screens create Duke's most obvious vertical window. When the on-ball defender trails and the big steps toward the ball, a pocket pass can reach Ngongba or Scharnowski before the low defender rotates. The width of that lane depends on screen angle, ballhandler pace, the screener's departure, and the shooting credibility behind the help. It closes when the handler takes an extra dribble, the roller leaves too early, or a weak-side defender can stand in the lane without consequence.

Ngongba's official 2025–26 line—.606 field-goal shooting, 70 offensive rebounds, and 64 assists—supports more than a finishing role. His passing total is evidence that possessions need not end when he catches between the arc and rim. The important projection, however, is not that he becomes a hub by declaration. It is that Duke can pass him into a four-on-three situation and measure what follows: immediate finish, one-dribble force, corner delivery, dunker-spot feed, or reset. The receiver's decision is part of the original pass's value.

Scharnowski offers a different geometry. Belmont's official statistics list 78 assists in 30 games, 2.6 per game, alongside 10.7 points and 6.0 rebounds. That is a meaningful college passing signal for a big, but not proof of ACC translation. As a short roller he may receive slightly farther from the basket and use processing rather than burst. Duke should test whether his passes arrive before longer defenders recover and whether the lineup around him preserves two genuine outlets. In two-big units, one big must occupy or move the low defender; otherwise the pocket catch merely places the ball in congestion.

### The post-entry and split-cut window

Ngongba's interior efficiency gives Duke a reason to feed the post, especially after switches. Yet a post entry is vulnerable to fronting, a baseline trap, or a guard digging from the nearest shooter. Duke can enlarge the window by entering from below the free-throw-line extended, screening the fronting defender, or using high-low alignment. The pass should reach Ngongba where he can score without first backing down a recovered defense.

The second window opens after the catch. Perimeter players can split cut, screen for one another, or relocate behind a dig. This converts an interior touch from an isolation into a decision node. Foster's .398 three-point shooting in 2025–26 gives defenders a reason to hesitate before helping from him. Other returners and freshmen will have to earn the same reaction. If a defender can dig off a non-shooter and recover comfortably, the apparent kickout exists but has little value after the catch.

Scharnowski may also create elbow-to-post windows. A high-low pass over a front can punish switching or denial without demanding that a guard thread the ball from the sideline. But this alignment requires timing: the low big must seal while the high big still owns a clear sight line. Holding the ball turns a seal into wrestling and lets the weak side collapse.

### The baseline and corner window

Paint pressure is most valuable when the ballhandler can see the help defender's origin. A drive that forces the low man to tag the roller should expose either a corner shooter or a baseline cutter. This is where size and vision matter differently. A larger wing such as Dame Sarr may see over a compact defense, while a smaller guard may create a lower passing angle by getting closer to the rim. Neither advantage matters if the receiver stands still behind a defender's shoulder.

Duke should pair the baseline drift with the drive rather than treat it as improvisation. When a driver reaches the lane line, the corner player can drift toward the baseline to widen the passing angle; when the defender turns fully toward the ball, a cut behind the head can create a layup. The passer must distinguish the two. Throwing to the original corner spot after the teammate has drifted is a live-ball turnover. Passing to a cutter who has not actually cleared the help is worse than keeping the ball.

This window is especially important for John Blackwell. Wisconsin's official statistics establish his high-volume scoring and prior playmaking base, but Duke needs to learn what happens when elite help commits to his drive. His scalable outcome is not simply more assists. It is forcing the low defender to choose, then placing the pass where the recipient can shoot or finish without gathering into a recovery. The developmental measure should separate reads made on balance from bailout passes made after the lane has closed.

### The second-side window

The best first pass often does not produce the shot. A pocket pass may draw the low defender; the roller then finds a wing; that wing attacks the closeout or makes one more pass. Duke's roster appears suited to this chain because Boozer, Foster, Blackwell, Ngongba, and Scharnowski can plausibly occupy different links. The risk is that “shared creation” becomes sequential turn-taking rather than connected advantage.

A second-side window is usually wider but shorter than it looks. The receiver catches against a moving defense, yet every pause allows the shell to reset. Duke's wings therefore need a three-option rule on advantage catches: shoot a clean attempt, attack the defender's momentum, or move the ball immediately. Extra dribbles without new pressure destroy the value produced upstream.

Boozer may be especially valuable here. His official assist and turnover record was built in a complementary role rather than as the only creator. Receiving after Blackwell or a big has bent the defense could let him use size and decision control without requiring him to win every first matchup. Foster can similarly move between organizer and finisher. The formation is less important than ensuring that the best quick reader receives the second pass while the best shooter or cutter occupies the next outlet.

## Who owns which read

The July evidence supports provisional ownership, not fixed positions. Boozer has the strongest returning possession-management evidence and should own difficult entries, pressure releases, and many second-side connections. Foster can initiate basic actions, punish under-help as a shooter, and attack a tilted closeout. Blackwell should create the first defensive commitment and prove that his passing scales when help is faster and teammates are more talented. Ngongba should own roll catches, post decisions, and high-low seals. Scharnowski should be tested as a handoff, elbow, and short-roll connector.

The freshmen remain projections. Rippey's guard identity supports early testing as an organizer, not an assumption that he is immediately Duke's primary point guard. Cameron Williams and Bryson Howard may supply large-wing sight lines and receiving gravity, but high-school reputation cannot verify college processing speed. The correct early-season question is not “Can the freshman pass?” It is “Which read can he execute repeatedly against a scouting report?” A player may be ready for the corner lift before the weak-side skip, or for the simple pocket pass before rejecting the screen and reading a rotating center.

## Lineup architecture changes the pass

In a three-guard lineup, Duke can place two release valves around the initiator. Pressure against Blackwell becomes less damaging if Boozer and Foster exchange into visible passing lanes rather than wait at static spots. The cost is that one guard must create value without the ball through screening, cutting, or gravity. Three handlers do not automatically create three windows.

With Ngongba as the lone big, Duke can use conventional spread ball screens and place four possible receivers above or outside the lane. This is the clearest environment for pocket passes and corner decisions. Its effectiveness depends on whether the weakest shooter is positioned as an active cutter or screener instead of becoming the defender who tags the roll for free.

With Scharnowski at center, passing can originate at the elbow through delay actions and dribble handoffs. The ball may move more, but circulation is useful only if it produces a defender facing the wrong direction or two defenders guarding one action. Duke should count rim pressure and open attempts generated from the hub, not touches at the hub.

The Ngongba–Scharnowski pairing creates high-low and interior-offensive-rebound possibilities, while narrowing certain drive-and-kick windows. To function, the big away from the ball must screen, occupy the dunker spot dynamically, or lift high enough to move his defender. If both big defenders can see ball and assignment from the paint, the lineup has converted passing talent into smaller windows.

## A verification ledger for the season

The first official roster update can verify membership and listed size, but not decision quality. Preseason practice comments identify intended roles, not successful ones. Exhibitions reveal alignments and preferred actions, yet opponent quality can make windows appear wider than they will be in ACC play. Competitive possession coding is the necessary next layer.

For each half-court possession, the Duke desk should identify the intended first action, whether the entry arrived on time, which defender first committed to the ball, which window opened, whether the pass arrived before recovery, and the receiver's next action. Record failed reads as well as turnovers: a missed roller that becomes a contested make is still a passing failure; a correct corner pass that produces a missed open three is still good process. Add pass destination and delivery type—one hand, two hand, bounce, hook, overhead, or jump pass—only when those details explain whether the window was actually usable.

Four rates would make the framework operational:

1. **On-time entry rate:** intended actions begun with spacing intact and at least 20 seconds remaining.
2. **Advantage-transfer rate:** passes after a defensive commitment that leave the receiver with a shot, drive, or immediate two-on-one.
3. **Window-loss rate:** possessions in which a visible productive pass disappears because of an extra dribble, hold, or inaccurate delivery.
4. **Receiver-conversion rate:** useful outcomes after an on-time pass, separated into rim attempt, open three, foul, continued advantage, reset, and turnover.

These measures should be split by passer, receiver, lineup, coverage, and opponent quality. A raw aggregate can flatter a player who makes safe reversals while hiding the guard asked to attempt narrow, high-value reads. Conversely, turnover rate without window difficulty can punish the only player generating rim chances. Film and outcome must remain connected.

## What success would look like

Duke does not need every player to become an advanced creator. It needs enough complementary readers that a defense cannot end the possession by removing one route. Against ball pressure, a guard-to-guard outlet should preserve the entry. Against a hard hedge, a big should become a short-roll decision-maker. Against deep drop, the handler should reach a pull-up or pocket lane without drifting sideways. Against switching, the group should feed the seal or attack the mismatch before the defense scrams out. Against compact help, the baseline and second-side windows must turn paint pressure into valuable catches.

The strongest version of this team would therefore have a hierarchy of *reads* rather than a monopoly of touches. Blackwell could force the first commitment, Boozer make the second decision, Ngongba finish or connect, and Foster punish the final rotation; on the next possession those jobs could rotate. The weak version would feature the same players exchanging the ball without changing the defense, then asking one scorer to rescue the clock.

Passing windows make that distinction observable. They prevent a made shot from excusing a late read and a missed shot from condemning a correct one. More importantly, they show how roster construction becomes offense: size changes sight lines, shooting changes help distance, screening changes duration, and decision speed determines whether temporary space becomes a durable advantage.

- Can Blackwell deliver to the corner or opposite slot before elite help recovers, rather than only after his scoring path closes?
- Does Ngongba's passing scale from post and connective possessions to repeated short-roll four-on-three reads?
- Can Scharnowski's 2.6 assists per game at Belmont translate when ACC defenders shorten the window with greater length and recovery speed?
- Which freshman read becomes dependable first: pressure release, pocket pass, baseline delivery, or second-side skip?
- In Ngongba–Scharnowski lineups, can Duke create high-low value without losing the drive-and-kick windows needed by its guards?
