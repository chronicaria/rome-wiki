## A role map, not a summer depth chart

This page is a **projection as of July 10, 2026**, not a report of assignments made in practice. Duke's official roster selector still displayed the completed 2025–26 team when checked. Duke has officially described the next roster in aggregate—five returners, three transfers, and a highly ranked recruiting class—and independent reporting supplies a likely name-by-name group. Those sources establish different things. The official Duke release is strong evidence for the team structure and the named returning scorers. CBS Sports is independent evidence for newcomer identities and prior production. Neither source can tell us who will initiate the first possession, which freshmen will survive defensive targeting, or who will close a one-possession game.

The safest analysis therefore begins with offensive work rather than nominal positions. A functional possession needs someone to organize the entry, someone to force the first defensive concession, someone to make the next read, players who maintain useful space, a screener or interior partner who changes the defense's geometry, and a player who converts the advantage. One player can do several jobs, and a job can move between players during the possession. What cannot happen is for a five-man group to omit a job because every player has been described as “versatile.”

The completed 2025–26 team also sets the replacement problem. Duke's official statistics show Cameron Boozer supplied 22.5 points, 10.2 rebounds, and 4.1 assists per game while Isaiah Evans made 101 three-pointers. Boozer could create pressure, connect the offense, finish, and recover misses; Evans widened the floor before touching the ball. Their departure removes complementary sources of gravity. The 2026–27 offense cannot replace that merely by adding the points per game of its newcomers. It must reconstruct the sequence that produced good shots.

The assignments below use three evidence labels. **Demonstrated** means a player performed the job in meaningful college competition, although translation to Duke usage remains open. **Projected** means prior production, role, or profile supports a reasonable July hypothesis. **Unresolved** means public evidence cannot yet justify the assignment. These are role labels, not player rankings.

## Job one: organize the possession

The organizer gets Duke into its action on time, recognizes the coverage, and protects the possession before an advantage exists. This is different from leading the team in scoring. Against pressure, organization may mean advancing the ball without needing a rescue timeout. Against a switching defense, it may mean calling the correct matchup into an action. After an offensive rebound, it may mean deciding immediately whether to attack or reset.

Cayden Boozer has the strongest **demonstrated continuity** case. Duke's official 2025–26 totals credit him with 115 assists against 55 turnovers, or about 2.1 assists for every turnover. That does not make him the automatic point guard, but it establishes real possession-management value beside his brother's high usage. His best version keeps Duke connected through pace changes: advance early when the floor is open, feed an advantage when it appears, and avoid consuming dribbles simply to prove authorship.

Caleb Foster also has a **demonstrated veteran** claim. His prior Duke experience and shooting make him plausible both as the entry organizer and as the recipient of an early reversal. The July question is not whether he has ever handled the ball; it is whether he can sustain efficient organization against the best pressure on Duke's schedule while remaining valuable away from it. John Blackwell is a **projected co-organizer**, not because scoring volume automatically equals point-guard play, but because any high-usage creator must read the help he attracts. Deron Rippey Jr. arrives with a reported point-guard profile, yet college organization remains **unresolved** until his decision speed survives older, stronger defenders.

The likely design is shared organization with clear possession-level authority. Boozer or Foster may initiate one trip, Blackwell the next, and a freshman another. But the lineup still needs one player to know the clock, call the entry, and prevent two teammates from coming for the same handoff. Shared responsibility works only when responsibility is visible.

## Job two: bend the first line

The advantage creator makes the defense change shape. A downhill drive can do it; so can a hard closeout attack, a post seal, a transition mismatch, or a ball screen that forces two defenders toward the ball. This is the role most easily confused with “best scorer.” Efficient difficult-shot making matters, but the repeatable value is producing a second defender's commitment.

Blackwell owns the clearest **demonstrated perimeter-volume** case. CBS reports 19.1 points, 5.1 rebounds, and 2.3 assists per game at Wisconsin in 2025–26, with nearly 39 percent shooting from three in its accompanying coverage. That establishes scoring pressure in a major conference. It does not establish that Blackwell should dominate the ball at Duke or that every Wisconsin shot transfers unchanged. His most scalable Duke role is to attack the first vulnerable seam, then recognize whether the correct result is his finish, the roller, or a weak-side pass.

Patrick Ngongba II supplies **demonstrated interior pressure**. Duke's official totals list 10.1 points in 21.9 minutes per game on .606 field-goal shooting, with 70 offensive rebounds. A deep seal, forceful roll, or recovered miss can bend a defense without a guard winning isolation. Ngongba's role matters especially when opponents switch smaller players or overplay the perimeter. His 64 assists also suggest he can continue some possessions after the defense converges, though the public box score alone cannot prove every short-roll read.

Foster and Boozer are **projected secondary benders**. Their path is not necessarily higher dribble volume. Foster can punish a hard closeout or use the threat of his catch-and-shoot jumper to enter the lane. Boozer can attack a tilted floor or turn an early mismatch into a post entry. Rippey, Cameron Williams, and Bryson Howard may eventually create first advantages, but their freshmen translation is **unresolved**. Recruiting reputation is a prior; the decisive evidence is whether they reach useful space without turning a difficult drive into a dead possession.

The failure mode is a lineup full of organizers and connectors but no player who forces rotation. If opponents switch, go under, or sit in gaps without consequence, clean passing can become lateral passing. Every viable group needs at least one demonstrated or rapidly emerging bender.

## Job three: make the second read

The first creator rarely defeats an elite defense alone. Once help arrives, a connector must make the next correct decision before the defense recovers. This can be a pocket pass, an extra pass, a short-roll delivery, a baseline cut, or an immediate attack against a closing defender. The job rewards speed and accuracy more than possession count.

Boozer and Foster are **demonstrated guard connectors** because they have already played complementary Duke roles around a centerpiece. Their value increases if Blackwell starts the action: one can become the second-side handler rather than a spectator. Dame Sarr is a **projected large-wing connector**. His 6-foot-8 frame and prior starting experience give him a platform, but size helps only when decisions arrive on time. If a defender helps off him, Sarr must shoot, cut, screen, or move the ball before the gap closes.

Drew Scharnowski has perhaps the most intriguing **demonstrated prior-role, projected-translation** case. CBS reports 2.6 assists per game at Belmont alongside 10.7 points and 6.0 rebounds. A passing big can turn a handoff, elbow catch, or short roll into a branch point and let Duke create width through movement rather than five-out shooting. The uncertainty is competition and lineup context. ACC length will compress windows more quickly, and playing beside Ngongba could give Scharnowski's defender more freedom to retreat. His passing is evidence; its Duke-scale effect is still a test.

The connective standard should be visible on film: receive with an advantage, decide before the defense resets, and leave the next teammate in a better position. Assist totals alone miss decisive swings that lead to the assist, and they overcredit passes followed by difficult shotmaking.

## Job four: preserve useful space

Spacing is the defense's response, not the offense's formation. A player can stand beyond the arc and still shrink the floor if his defender ignores him. Another can create space without shooting by screening, cutting behind help, or moving the ball instantly. Duke's task is to surround its creators and interior finishers with enough credible punishment that help becomes expensive.

Foster has the strongest **demonstrated returning shooting** case: Duke's official totals show 39 makes on 98 attempts (.398) in 2025–26. Blackwell brings a **demonstrated prior shooting threat** according to independent reporting, though his shot mix at Duke may change. Sarr made 21 of 65 threes (.323), while Boozer made 10 of 33 (.303). Those figures do not render either unusable, but they do not establish strong gravity. Opponents may initially help from them until repeated willingness, accuracy, and quick decisions change the scouting report.

Williams, Howard, and Rippey are **projected spacers with unresolved college gravity**. The distinction matters. A freshman may have good shooting mechanics yet hesitate against fast closeouts, or may make open shots without being guarded as a movement threat. Duke should not count a prospect's reputation as a made college three. Conversely, early misses should not erase good process if defenders still chase the player and the attempts are clean.

Ngongba and Scharnowski illustrate non-shooting space. Ngongba can widen a driving lane through a screen that forces contact, then occupy the rim vertically. Scharnowski can pull a center into a handoff decision or find a cutter from the elbow. A two-big lineup is viable only if those actions prevent both defenders from camping in the lane. The test is not how the lineup is drawn; it is where the defenders put their feet.

## Job five: screen, seal, and move the defense

Screening is offensive creation performed without the ball. The screener changes the on-ball defender's path, forces the big to declare a coverage, and can generate a mismatch before becoming a finisher or passer. A poor screen lets the defense guard two players with two defenders. A good one can momentarily require three.

Ngongba is the **demonstrated Duke baseline**. His size, efficient finishing, offensive rebounding, and emerging passing make him the clearest conventional ball-screen partner. His assignment should vary with coverage: roll hard when the lane opens, re-screen against a guard who recovers, seal a switch, or catch and move the ball when the low defender commits. Foul sustainability and conditioning matter because a larger offensive role is valuable only if he remains available defensively.

Scharnowski is the **projected movement screener**. He could operate through delay actions, dribble handoffs, slips, and short rolls, especially as a center with four perimeter players. Beside Ngongba, he may need to screen away, exchange sides, or work from the elbow so that the pair produces motion rather than congestion. Sarr and the large freshmen are **projected ghost-screen and mismatch-screen candidates**: a wing who screens for Blackwell can induce a switch, then pop, slip, or attack a scrambled closeout. Whether Duke uses those options will depend on shooting respect and decision-making.

This job should be evaluated beyond screen assists. Track whether the screen makes contact, changes the coverage, creates a favorable matchup, or frees a cutter. The action succeeds when the defense concedes something, even if the screener never receives the ball.

## Job six: finish the advantage and recover misses

An offense can execute the first five jobs and still waste the possession. Finishers convert at the rim, make the open three, draw fouls, or turn the first miss into another attack. This job also clarifies why role maps must include rebounding: the player who crashes from the weak side can complete an advantage that initially produced only a contested layup.

Ngongba is the strongest **demonstrated interior finisher**, supported by his .606 field-goal percentage and offensive-rebound volume. Blackwell is the leading **demonstrated perimeter scorer**, but his Duke efficiency will depend on how many attempts come after an advantage rather than before one. Foster is a **demonstrated catch finisher from three** based on the completed season. Scharnowski is a **projected cutter, roller, and second-chance finisher** whose Belmont production must translate against larger defenders.

Sarr and the freshman wings can become decisive here without leading usage. A 6-foot-8 wing who cuts when his defender turns his head, finishes through contact, and occupies the offensive glass can punish the exact help that Blackwell or Ngongba creates. Boozer's best finishing contribution may be selective: attack a gap, use size against a smaller guard, or take the open shot without stopping the ball. The team should distinguish missed good outcomes from bad decisions. An open corner three after paint touch is usually evidence of a functioning possession even when it misses; a crowded pull-up before the defense shifts usually is not.

## Lineup architectures to test

The most balanced July baseline is **Blackwell plus one organizer, two credible space/connect players, and Ngongba**. It gives the offense a proven perimeter scorer, a separate possession manager, a conventional interior partner, and three players capable of making the second read. Its swing variable is whether the nominal wings create enough gravity that opponents cannot crowd Blackwell and Ngongba simultaneously.

A **three-guard structure** with Blackwell, Boozer, and Foster offers the strongest demonstrated organization. It can move initiation between players and reduce the effect of pressure aimed at one handler. Its challenge is not ballhandling but size allocation and role clarity: one guard must screen, cut, or space rather than waiting for a turn. A freshman can displace one of the veterans by creating more first-line pressure, but only if he preserves the group's decision quality.

A **Scharnowski-at-center movement group** could emphasize handoffs, early offense, and five-man connectivity. Its offensive ceiling depends less on Scharnowski becoming a high-volume shooter than on his defender being unable to sit at the rim. Four perimeter partners must cut and relocate around his catches. The cost belongs chiefly on defense and the glass, but offensively the test is whether passing produces rim attempts rather than aesthetically pleasing circulation.

The **Ngongba-Scharnowski two-big group** can create seals, high-low reads, and offensive rebounding. It is the lineup most likely to be mislabeled from formation alone. If Scharnowski's defender retreats and Ngongba's defender stays home, the lane can vanish. Duke needs screening exchanges, elbow decisions, and strong weak-side shooting to make the pairing functional. It should be an opponent-specific tool until the defense proves it must guard both bigs outside the restricted area.

A **large-wing pressure group** with Sarr and one or more freshmen can preserve size while surrounding a single big. Its offensive question is decision speed. Long wings who shoot, cut, and swing immediately can see over rotations and punish switches. Long wings who hesitate give the defense time to reload. The lineup should be judged by paint touches and advantage continuation, not by how imposing it looks walking onto the floor.

## What would verify or falsify the map

The first official 2026–27 roster should resolve membership and listed measurements, not roles. Preseason comments can reveal intended experiments but are weak proof. Exhibitions can show who initiates and which combinations the staff wants to inspect; unusual opponents and scripted substitution patterns limit inference. Competitive games provide the first serious evidence.

For each lineup, the Duke desk should record: who starts the possession; who produces the first two-defender reaction; what happens on the next catch; which defender is allowed to help; who screens and how the coverage responds; and where the shot or turnover comes from. Add late-clock ownership, turnover type, offensive-rebound responsibility, and free-throw shooting for closing groups. This chain is more diagnostic than raw usage or plus-minus.

The map should change when a mechanism repeats. If Boozer organizes cleanly against several pressure teams, that is stronger than a single high-assist game. If opponents repeatedly go under Sarr and clog Ngongba's rolls, his spacing classification must remain limited even after a hot night. If Rippey forces two defenders and finds the second side against veteran competition, he has earned more than a prospect label. If Scharnowski's handoffs generate advantage without compromising finishing, his passing becomes a Duke mechanism rather than a Belmont résumé line.

## Why it matters

Duke has enough reported talent to invite the wrong kind of certainty. A summer rotation can make every player look additive because it does not force the five-man group to divide actual possessions. The offensive role map imposes that constraint. It asks who organizes, who bends, who connects, who spaces, who screens, and who finishes—and whether the same lineup can cover all six without asking three players to do the same thing.

That framework also makes development legible. Blackwell does not need to repeat 19.1 points to succeed if his pressure creates better team shots and he remains dangerous off the ball. Boozer can become more valuable without scoring much more if he organizes pressure possessions and attacks selective gaps. Sarr can transform the offense by turning ignored space into immediate cuts and decisions. Ngongba can increase impact through screens and passes that never enter his scoring average. Freshmen can earn roles through one dependable action before they become complete creators.

Most importantly, the map preserves uncertainty honestly. July 2026 evidence supports candidates and experiments, not a finished offensive identity. Duke's strongest offense may be distributed without being leaderless: multiple initiators, one clear decision-maker on each possession, and enough complementary jobs that the defense cannot remove everything with one coverage.

## Open questions

- Who owns organization when Duke faces sustained full-court pressure rather than ordinary half-court defense?
- Can Blackwell force help and make the next read often enough to be both Duke's leading scorer and a scalable creator?
- Which two wings create genuine off-ball gravity around Ngongba rather than merely occupying perimeter spots?
- Does Scharnowski's passing translate quickly enough to make him a second offensive hub, and can that role coexist with Ngongba?
- Which freshman first demonstrates one repeatable action against veteran high-major competition?
- Who owns the late-clock possession, and does that player change according to coverage?
- Can Duke's most productive offensive group also defend and rebound well enough to close elite games?
