Duke's turnover economy is the full exchange of possessions lost, possessions forced, and value created after each event—not simply a target for fewer turnovers.

## The accounting question

As of July 10, 2026, Duke has not played a 2026–27 possession. The useful evidence comes from completed 2025–26 seasons: Duke's official cumulative ledger, Wisconsin's official ledger for incoming scorer John Blackwell, and individual totals for Duke returners. Those records can define a baseline. They cannot establish next season's turnover rate, rotation, pressure response, or defensive scheme.

“Turnover economy” describes more than ball security. On offense, a turnover ends a possession without a field-goal attempt and may expose an unbalanced floor. On defense, a forced turnover can create an attack before the opponent organizes. But the exchange is not symmetrical. A dead-ball traveling violation lets the defense set; a live-ball pass intercepted near midcourt may produce a layup. A steal that leads to a missed contested shot has less value than one that creates a rim attempt. A team can win raw turnover margin and still lose the economic exchange if its mistakes are more damaging or its takeaways are poorly converted.

The framework therefore asks four separate questions:

1. What share of Duke's possessions ends in a turnover?
2. What share of opponent possessions does Duke end with a turnover?
3. Where and how do those turnovers occur?
4. What scoring value follows before the floor resets?

Only the first two can be reconstructed cleanly from a cumulative box score. The latter two require play-by-play and film classification. This distinction prevents a public statistic from carrying more tactical meaning than it contains.

## Definitions and arithmetic

Raw turnovers per game are pace-dependent. A team playing 75 possessions has more opportunities to turn the ball over than a team playing 62. For a transparent possession estimate, this article uses

$$\widehat{Poss}=FGA+0.475(FTA)-ORB+TO.$$

The coefficient estimates how many possessions free throws consume; it is not a claim that every trip to the line uses 0.475 possessions. Technical fouls, and-ones, one-and-ones, and multi-shot fouls make the relationship irregular. Offensive rebounds are subtracted because they extend the same possession rather than begin another. Turnovers are added because they end possessions without appearing among field-goal attempts. Team and opponent estimates can differ slightly because this is an approximation and because end-of-period and team-rebound bookkeeping are imperfect. The figures below should therefore be read as reproducible estimates, not official NCAA efficiency ratings.

Estimated offensive turnover percentage is

$$TO\%=100\times\frac{TO}{\widehat{Poss}}.$$

The same calculation applied to opponent totals estimates the defensive turnover percentage. Their difference, opponent $TO\%$ minus Duke $TO\%$, is a pace-adjusted turnover-rate margin. Positive is favorable. Raw turnover margin remains useful, but it answers a narrower question: how many more recorded turnovers did one team commit?

Duke's official 2025–26 totals were 2,198 field-goal attempts, 840 free-throw attempts, 471 offensive rebounds, and 405 turnovers across 38 games. Substitution gives

$$2{,}198+0.475(840)-471+405=2{,}531.0$$

estimated possessions, or 66.6 per game. Duke's estimated offensive turnover percentage was therefore

$$100\times405/2{,}531.0=16.0\%.$$

The same official ledger gives Duke's opponents 2,225 field-goal attempts, 507 free-throw attempts, 357 offensive rebounds, and 439 turnovers:

$$2{,}225+0.475(507)-357+439=2{,}547.8.$$

That produces an estimated opponent turnover percentage of 17.2 percent. The rate differential was approximately **plus 1.2 percentage points** for Duke. The raw ledger tells the same directional story: Duke committed 405 turnovers and opponents committed 439, a favorable margin of 34, or 0.9 per game. Duke also recorded 280 steals. Because opponent turnovers exceeded Duke steals by 159, 36.2 percent of opponent turnovers were recorded as Duke steals; the rest arose from violations, offensive fouls, lost balls without an awarded steal, shot-clock events, or other bookkeeping categories.

These calculations were checked directly against the published totals. Rounded intermediate values were not used in the final division. The possession estimates differ by 16.8 over 38 games—less than half a possession per game—which is a reminder that the formula is an estimator rather than a literal possession count.

## What the 2025–26 baseline does and does not say

Duke's prior team protected the ball reasonably while forcing slightly more mistakes than it made. Its official page also reports 634 assists against 405 turnovers, a 1.6 assist-to-turnover ratio, and a points-off-turnovers edge of 13.5 to 11.0 per game. Across 38 games, those displayed averages imply roughly a 95-point advantage in points off turnovers, although the page provides the category as rounded per-game values rather than an exact cumulative total. That is genuine evidence of a favorable exchange.

It is not a portable 2026–27 forecast. Cameron Boozer recorded 157 assists and 95 turnovers; Isaiah Evans had 48 and 43. Their departures remove 205 of the team's 634 assists and 138 of its 405 turnovers, but subtraction does not reveal what happens next. Vacated possessions will be reassigned, returning players' roles will change, and newcomers will face different decisions. “Returning turnover total” is especially misleading because players who remain will not repeat the same minutes or usage.

The returning guard evidence is encouraging but bounded. Caleb Foster finished with 92 assists and 36 turnovers, a simple ratio of 2.56. Cayden Boozer had 113 assists and 51 turnovers, or 2.22. Together they supplied 205 assists against 87 turnovers. Those totals support [[Duke 2026-27 backcourt creation|a distributed organizational layer]]. They do not show which passes came against traps, which mistakes became runouts, or whether either player can preserve the ratio with greater creation responsibility.

Nor should assist-to-turnover ratio be treated as a universal decision-quality score. A guard can protect his ratio by making only safe passes while failing to create an advantage. A high-usage scorer can make the correct attacking read and still accrue more turnovers because he faces more help defenders. Assists depend on teammates making shots; turnovers do not. The ratio is descriptive context, not a complete efficiency metric.

## What Blackwell's Wisconsin context contributes

Wisconsin offers a useful second prior because Blackwell played a major offensive role there. The Badgers' official 2025–26 totals were 2,171 field-goal attempts, 684 free-throw attempts, 359 offensive rebounds, and 314 turnovers in 35 games. The same formula yields

$$2{,}171+0.475(684)-359+314=2{,}450.9$$

estimated possessions and a 12.8-percent offensive turnover rate. Opponents produced an estimated 2,445.5 possessions from 2,141 field-goal attempts, 639 free-throw attempts, 348 offensive rebounds, and 349 turnovers, for a 14.3-percent turnover rate. Wisconsin's approximate differential was plus 1.5 percentage points. Its official page reports 9.0 turnovers per game, 1.8 assists per turnover, and a 13.6-to-10.2 points-off-turnovers advantage.

Blackwell contributed 79 assists and 57 turnovers while scoring 19.1 points per game. His individual assist-to-turnover ratio was 1.39, lower than the team ratio, but that comparison requires role context. Nick Boyd recorded 149 assists and 59 turnovers and led Wisconsin in scoring. Blackwell worked inside a two-creator structure on a team that rarely turned the ball over. His record supports the proposition that he can carry scoring volume without the entire offense becoming careless. It does not prove he was the sole organizer or that Duke can import Wisconsin's 12.8-percent estimated rate.

This is the central projection boundary. Foster, Boozer, and Blackwell have each produced positive individual assist-to-turnover balances in completed college seasons. Duke can plausibly field two experienced decision-makers together. But lineup spacing, screen personnel, opponent pressure, freshman minutes, and role expansion will determine whether those priors combine cleanly. Adding three favorable ratios does not create a team forecast.

## The three costs of an offensive turnover

The first cost is the lost shot opportunity. A turnover before a shot prevents Duke from testing its shooting, drawing a foul, or creating an offensive rebound. This is why turnover percentage belongs beside shot quality and rebounding: all three determine how many chances a possession has to produce points.

The second cost is floor balance. [[How opponents pressure Duke's initiators]] distinguishes denial, presses, ball-screen blitzes, switches, and gap loading because they create different mistakes. A live-ball turnover by a high guard can leave only one defender between the ball and rim. A bad interior pass may at least leave several Duke players behind the ball. The box score counts both as one turnover; the transition burden does not.

The third cost is role distortion. If one handler struggles against pressure, Duke may bring a second guard toward the ball. That can solve the advance but remove spacing or a downstream receiver. If a big cannot make the short-roll pass, the primary guard may avoid an otherwise productive action. A turnover problem can therefore reduce offensive options even on possessions that do not end in a turnover.

Duke's internal review should classify giveaways as dead-ball or live-ball, then by stage: inbound, advance, entry, first action, advantage pass, finish, or late clock. It should also identify the decision type—handle loss, inaccurate pass, illegal screen, charge, travel, offensive foul away from the ball, or shot-clock violation. The purpose is not to excuse mistakes. It is to assign the repair correctly. Extra ballhandling work does not fix a screener's timing; a safer passing rule does not fix poor receiver spacing.

## Forcing turnovers without gambling away the defense

Defensive turnover rate also needs mechanism. Steals can come from point-of-attack pressure, a dig at the post, a nail helper, a deflection in a passing lane, or a rotation behind a trap. Non-steal turnovers may reflect disciplined containment just as much as aggression: a charge, five-second call, travel, or shot-clock violation can arise because all five defenders held their assignments.

Duke's 280 steals in 2025–26 show event production, but they do not prove that every aggressive action was profitable. A failed reach can concede a paint touch; an overplayed passing lane can trigger a backdoor cut; a trap can create four-on-three. The proper denominator is not steals alone. It is the whole exchange: turnovers forced, shots conceded when pressure fails, fouls created, and transition value after recovery.

The 2026–27 projection should therefore begin with constraints rather than a promised pressure identity. Lineups with more guard speed may be able to raise the pickup point. Larger groups may shrink passing windows without gambling. A mobile frontcourt may support trapping; a traditional rim protector may favor conservative containment. [[Duke 2026-27 defensive role map]] should own the assignment details once the roster and game evidence are firm. Turnover economy supplies the test: does the chosen pressure create more possession value than it surrenders?

## A 2026–27 measurement plan

The first public dashboard should retain the basic accounting: offensive turnover percentage, opponent turnover percentage, rate differential, raw margin, steals, and points off turnovers. Each should be shown for the season and a rolling window, with opponent and game-state context. A single high-turnover opener should not define the team; a low-error night against passive pressure should not settle the question.

Film review should add five fields unavailable in the cumulative ledger:

- live-ball versus dead-ball giveaways;
- location and action at the turnover;
- which stage of the possession failed;
- shot quality allowed within the ensuing transition window;
- shot quality Duke created after a takeaway.

The useful offensive target is not zero turnovers. An offense that never risks an advantage pass may also create few rim attempts and free throws. Duke should instead seek a stable rate while distinguishing productive aggression from preventable loss. A pass that attempts to punish two defenders can be conceptually sound and poorly delivered. Repeated failures still require adjustment, but their solution may be timing or spacing rather than abandoning the read.

Lineup comparisons should wait for adequate possessions and common opponent types. If a Blackwell-Foster group posts fewer turnovers than a Blackwell-Boozer group, the difference may reflect role, matchups, or who shares the frontcourt. The initial question should be mechanical: does the group enter offense on time, force a rotation, and make the next pass without exposing transition? Efficiency numbers can then confirm or challenge the film.

The strongest 2026–27 economy would combine three features: Foster and Boozer preserving their demonstrated connective control, Blackwell translating high-volume scoring without becoming an overburdened sole initiator, and a defense that forces mistakes without sacrificing its shell. That is a hypothesis, not a preseason conclusion. The completed-season arithmetic establishes favorable priors and a measurement baseline. The coming possessions must supply the answer.
