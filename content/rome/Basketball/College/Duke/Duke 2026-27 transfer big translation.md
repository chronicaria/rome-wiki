Drew Scharnowski gives Duke a proven interior finisher, passer, rebounder, and disruptive defender, but his Belmont production translates only if Duke treats him as a mobile connector rather than assuming that a 6-foot-9 forward automatically supplies shooting or full-time center protection.

## Direct summary

As of July 10, 2026, **Drew Scharnowski** is the one incoming Duke transfer who fits this article's “big” category. Duke has officially said that the 2026–27 team welcomes three transfers, but the program's public roster materials reviewed for this article do not individually name all three. Scharnowski announced that he would join Duke, according to contemporary reporting, and Belmont's official records establish his origin-school identity and performance. The most careful present wording is therefore that Scharnowski is the strongly established reported transfer big, while enrollment, eligibility, health, and his eventual Duke role remain to be verified on Duke's completed roster.

The translation case begins with an unusual statistical combination. The *News & Observer* reported that Scharnowski averaged 10.7 points, 6.1 rebounds, 2.6 assists, and 1.3 blocks as a redshirt sophomore, earning first-team All-Missouri Valley Conference and MVC all-defensive honors. Belmont's March 4 game notes, with 29 games then recorded, showed 68.4 percent shooting inside an overwhelmingly interior shot diet, just five three-point attempts, 43.8 percent free-throw shooting, 76 assists, 51 turnovers, 39 blocks, and 23 steals. Those numbers support four sturdy conclusions: he finished extremely well around the basket, passed meaningfully for a frontcourt player, created defensive events, and did not demonstrate floor-spacing range. They do **not** prove that he can reproduce the same efficiency against ACC size, defend every college center, or share the floor comfortably with every non-shooting teammate.

His best Duke pathway is therefore neither “backup center” nor “stretch four” as a fixed label. It is **mobile frontcourt connector**: screen, roll, cut, seal, pass quickly, rebound, switch or show when the matchup permits, and keep possessions alive without requiring plays to be called for him. He can help a talented lineup become coherent. Whether he can stay on the floor in Duke's highest-leverage combinations will depend on the exact spacing and rim-protection costs around him.

## Identity and evidence boundary

Scharnowski is listed by Belmont at 6-foot-9 and 230 pounds, from Burlington, Illinois, and was a redshirt sophomore in 2025–26. Belmont says he redshirted in 2023–24, then averaged 5.5 points and 3.0 rebounds across 25 appearances in 2024–25. The progression to an all-conference role the next season matters. Duke is not importing a one-month hot streak or a player who was hidden on a bench; it is importing a frontcourt player who converted a developmental season into a major role on a 26-win Belmont team.

There is a small but important source discrepancy to preserve rather than erase. Belmont's official March 4 notes were produced before the season was complete and list 29 games, 10.9 points, and 6.0 rebounds at that cutoff. The later *News & Observer* account reports the final-season averages as 10.7 points and 6.1 rebounds. These figures are compatible with an additional postseason sample changing the averages. This article uses the later reported final averages for the season summary and the dated Belmont ledger for detailed shot, passing, turnover, steal, and block counts. It does not silently mix the two into a fictional final stat line.

The destination evidence also needs disciplined wording. Scharnowski told the *Chicago Sun-Times* that playing at Duke had been a dream and described Duke's belief in his fit. The *News & Observer* reported that he announced his Duke decision on Instagram. Those are strong, direct and contemporaneous indicators of intent. Duke's June 22 release independently confirms that three transfers are entering the roster, but it does not attach names to that count. Until Duke supplies a current named roster or an individual announcement, “reported Duke transfer” is more exact than presenting the roster ledger as already complete.

## What the Belmont record actually establishes

### Interior efficiency is the clearest bankable skill

At the March 4 cutoff Scharnowski had made 128 of 187 field-goal attempts, a 68.4 percent rate. Only five attempts came from three. Removing that tiny perimeter sample leaves 127 makes on 182 two-point attempts, just under 70 percent. That is not merely acceptable finishing; it is the statistical signature of a player whose offense lived in high-value interior locations and converted them exceptionally well.

The mechanism still has to be separated from the percentage. An official total does not tell us how many baskets were dunks, slips, post seals, cuts, transition runs, offensive rebounds, or short-roll catches. It does tell Duke what not to do: do not evaluate him as though he created 10.7 points through self-contained half-court scoring. His efficiency will transfer best when guards draw two defenders, wings cut at the right time, and Scharnowski receives the ball while the defense is already rotating. If Duke parks him against a set defender and asks for repeated late-clock post creation, the Belmont field-goal percentage is a poor forecast.

This distinction makes screening quality central. A mobile big can score without holding the ball by creating contact, changing the ballhandler's path, and then arriving in the pocket before the low defender recovers. Scharnowski's finishing makes the roll a credible threat; his passing makes the short roll potentially more valuable than the shot itself. The sequence can create a layup, a corner pass, a high-low feed, or one more reversal. Duke should judge the possession by the advantage he produces, not only by whether he receives an assist.

### The passing is real enough to shape a role

Belmont's dated ledger recorded 76 assists against 51 turnovers in 29 games. The final reported average of 2.6 assists is unusually useful for a frontcourt translation because it points to repeated connective responsibility, not a single highlight. Eight assists at Valparaiso and nine against Murray State in the game-by-game record show that the passing ceiling appeared in actual games.

An assist-to-turnover ratio above one is not proof of primary playmaking, especially because official assists do not capture missed reads and turnovers include offensive fouls or lost rebounds. It does establish that Scharnowski can be given decisions. Duke can test him as an elbow hub, a short-roll passer, a dribble-handoff partner, and a high-low facilitator beside [[Patrick Ngongba II]]. These jobs matter because a non-shooting big cannot be allowed to become stationary. Passing turns occupied space into active space.

The speed of the read will be more important than its ambition. Against quicker, longer defenders, a pass available for a full second in the Missouri Valley may be available for half a second in the ACC. The relevant preseason test is whether Scharnowski can catch, scan, and move the ball before the defense resets. A spectacular assist paired with two delayed possessions is less valuable than five immediate decisions that keep the advantage alive.

### Rebounding supplies value, but context sets the ceiling

The final reported 6.1 rebounds per game and five 10-rebound games in Belmont's notes establish consistent frontcourt activity. The March 4 ledger divided his 173 rebounds into 60 offensive and 113 defensive, a notably large offensive share. That distribution fits the interior role: he operated near the rim, pursued extra possessions, and could punish opponents that lost contact.

Duke should not translate the raw average directly into an ACC rebounding guarantee. Team pace, shot locations, teammate assignments, opponent size, and minutes all change opportunity. What travels more reliably is the habit: make first contact, pursue outside the restricted area, and convert offensive boards into fast finishes or kickouts. In two-big groups, Duke also needs to avoid sending both frontcourt players to the glass without transition balance. Scharnowski's offensive rebounding is an asset only when the other four players understand the coverage behind it.

### Defensive events suggest versatility, not automatic anchoring

Scharnowski's 1.3 blocks per game and MVC all-defensive selection are meaningful evidence. Belmont's 29-game ledger also shows 39 blocks and 23 steals, with four games of at least three blocks. This is a broader activity profile than that of a player who merely stands under the rim. His listed mobility, steals, and passing background support testing him in aggressive coverages: showing at the level, switching selectively, recovering after a hedge, and rotating from the weak side.

But blocks do not settle the hardest Duke question: can he anchor the paint as the only big against elite size? At 6-foot-9 and 230 pounds, he has legitimate frontcourt mass but is not automatically a full-time drop center. Rim defense also includes deterrence, verticality, positioning, defensive rebounding, and avoiding fouls. Belmont's March 4 totals show 73 fouls in 29 games, or roughly 2.5 per game in fewer than 22 minutes. That is not disqualifying; it does mean foul sustainability should be measured when his assignments become larger and his minutes more consequential.

The safest projection is coverage flexibility in selected matchups. Alongside Ngongba, Scharnowski may guard the more mobile frontcourt player, participate in switches, and arrive as a secondary rim protector. As the lone big, he may enable pace and five-position activity but surrender size or back-line certainty. Those are lineup exchanges, not verdicts on his talent.

## The two constraints Duke cannot wish away

### He has not established shooting gravity

Five three-point attempts in 29 games are not a meaningful perimeter sample. A player can improve over an offseason, but improvement is a future event, not evidence already in the record. Defenses should be expected to help off Scharnowski until he demonstrates otherwise in games. That affects every two-big combination and every drive by a guard who needs an open lane.

Duke can create spacing without pretending he is a shooter. Use him as the screener rather than the stationary corner player. Let him begin at the dunker spot, cut behind help, exchange with a wing, or operate at the elbow where a defender's retreat opens a handoff. Pair him with four credible shooters when he plays center. If he plays with Ngongba, place movement around their complementary actions instead of leaving both on the same block. [[Duke 2026-27 shooting and spacing]] should treat Scharnowski as a source of screen and cut gravity until shot evidence changes the classification.

Any preseason three-point clips should be treated as evidence of practice, not proof of game gravity. The update threshold should be explicit: repeated attempts, stable mechanics, and enough makes that opponents actually change their coverage. One made three can decide a game but cannot alone rewrite the scouting report.

### Free throws threaten the most natural offensive role

The March 4 ledger lists 60 makes in 137 free-throw attempts, 43.8 percent. The volume is substantial, which confirms that Scharnowski generated contact; the conversion rate is a major constraint. An interior finisher who draws fouls but converts fewer than half can lose much of the efficiency his field-goal percentage appears to promise. Opponents may also become more willing to foul him intentionally in late-clock or late-game situations.

Free-throw performance can change, and the later final ledger may move the exact percentage slightly. The responsible July conclusion is not that he will remain a 44-percent shooter. It is that Duke must verify improvement before designing closing offense around his rim pressure. Track mechanics and results over a meaningful competitive sample, then distinguish three questions: can he stay above the opponent's intentional-foul threshold, can he finish through contact to reduce reliance on the line, and can Duke substitute offense for defense without damaging continuity?

The free-throw issue also interacts with passing. A short-roll connector can release the ball before a foul, keeping the possession in a stronger shooter's hands. That is not an argument for passivity; it is another reason his best role may be making the correct next decision rather than forcing a shot through two bodies.

## Three viable Duke lineup translations

### 1. Lone-big connector

With four perimeter players, Scharnowski can screen, roll, facilitate, and chase offensive rebounds without another interior teammate occupying the lane. This gives Duke its cleanest offensive translation of his Belmont strengths. His passing can punish aggressive ball-screen help, and his mobility can support switching or higher ball-screen coverage.

The cost is defensive anchoring. Duke must establish whether he can protect the rim, finish possessions on the glass, and absorb center contact without fouling. This version is likely matchup-dependent until proven otherwise. It could be powerful against mobile or undersized opponents and vulnerable against dominant post size.

### 2. Two-big connector beside Ngongba

Ngongba supplies the more conventional interior anchor while Scharnowski becomes the mover: screen away, facilitate from the elbow, cut behind help, and guard the more mobile forward. The combination could improve rebounding and give Duke two finishers around the rim. Scharnowski's passing may also make high-low entries and split actions more functional than a conventional two-post alignment.

Spacing is the immediate price. If neither big pulls a defender beyond the lane, Duke's guards can encounter a crowded paint. Movement must therefore be continuous and purposeful. One big screens while the other occupies help; one rolls while the other lifts; the ball changes sides before the defense can load up. The pairing should be evaluated by paint quality and turnover rate, not by whether its nominal size looks imposing.

### 3. Reserve stabilizer and matchup closer

Scharnowski need not start to become consequential. A veteran reserve big can stabilize freshman-heavy groups, preserve passing when starters rest, and let Scheyer change coverages without reconstructing the entire lineup. Some nights his mobility may make him the best closing defender. Other nights free throws, spacing, or opponent size may remove him from the final offense.

This variable role is not evidence of failed translation. It may be the point of the transfer. Duke's projected roster contains multiple frontcourt possibilities; a useful connector allows the staff to choose the one that solves the current matchup. The evaluation should ask whether his minutes improve lineup function, not whether his Belmont starting status carries over.

## Facts, inferences, and tests

### Established facts

- Belmont listed Scharnowski as a 6-foot-9, 230-pound redshirt sophomore forward in 2025–26.
- Belmont recorded a developmental jump from 5.5 points and 3.0 rebounds in 2024–25 to first-team All-MVC and all-defensive recognition in 2025–26.
- Final reporting lists 10.7 points, 6.1 rebounds, 2.6 assists, and 1.3 blocks per game.
- Belmont's March 4 ledger shows extreme interior efficiency, almost no three-point volume, meaningful passing, defensive events, offensive rebounding, and poor free-throw conversion.
- Scharnowski publicly chose Duke, according to his comments and contemporary reporting; Duke separately says its roster includes three transfers.

### Reasonable inferences, not settled facts

- His finishing and passing should translate best through screens, rolls, cuts, handoffs, and short-roll decisions.
- His mobility and event production make him a candidate for multiple defensive coverages.
- He can complement Ngongba if Duke solves the pair's offensive spacing.
- His most valuable role may vary by opponent rather than conform to a fixed starter/backup hierarchy.

### Claims that require game evidence

- That he can defend high-major centers as Duke's only big.
- That he has developed credible three-point gravity.
- That his free-throw shooting has improved enough for late-game offense.
- That his interior finishing rate survives longer, more athletic defenders.
- That a Scharnowski–Ngongba pairing creates more through passing and rebounding than it concedes through spacing.

## A practical translation scoreboard

Duke can keep the evaluation honest by recording mechanisms rather than narrative impressions.

1. **Finishing:** rim attempts, assisted share, offensive-rebound putbacks, and conversion against set versus rotating defense.
2. **Decision speed:** time from catch to shot, pass, or drive; short-roll assists; potential assists; and turnovers caused by delayed reads.
3. **Spacing effect:** defender location when Scharnowski is off the ball, paint touches created or blocked, and the quality of two-big possessions.
4. **Screen value:** contact quality, re-screens, slips, and the advantage produced even when he never touches the ball.
5. **Defense:** opponent rim attempts, fouls, defensive-rebound completion, coverage errors, successful switches, and recovery distance.
6. **Pressure points:** free-throw rate and percentage, intentional-foul decisions, and performance against size.

This scoreboard protects Scharnowski from two bad evaluations. The first would call him an immediate star because his Belmont field-goal percentage is spectacular. The second would call him unplayable because he does not shoot threes or free throws well. His actual Duke value will sit in the interactions: whether passing converts non-shooting minutes into organized offense, whether mobility offsets some lack of center size, and whether lineups around him cover the remaining constraints.

Scharnowski's translation is ultimately a test of Duke's ability to preserve a transfer's defining advantages without importing his old environment whole. Belmont gave him interior touches, connective decisions, and a platform for defensive activity. Duke can offer stronger surrounding creation and more lineup variety, but it will also present faster closeouts, larger finishers, and less tolerance for spacing errors. If he screens, passes, finishes, rebounds, and defends with speed, he does not need to imitate a shooting five. If Duke assigns him a static label and waits for the old numbers to recur, it will miss the reason the profile is interesting.
