## The baseline is strong, but it does not transfer automatically

As of July 10, 2026, Duke has confirmed the broad construction of the next roster but has not published a complete 2026–27 roster page or competitive lineup evidence. Its June 22 announcement says five players return, three transfers arrive, and the recruiting class ranks second nationally. The same release names Cayden Boozer, Caleb Foster, Patrick Ngongba II, and Dame Sarr among the returners. Assigning any of those players a fixed rebounding job in July would go beyond the evidence. What can be established is the scale of the possession-ending work Duke must redistribute.

Duke’s official 2025–26 totals show 1,058 defensive rebounds while opponents collected 357 offensive rebounds. Using the NCAA.com definition—team defensive rebounds divided by team defensive rebounds plus opponent offensive rebounds—Duke secured about 74.8 percent of available defensive rebounds. That is a much better baseline than raw rebounds per game because it asks what share of actual rebound chances Duke won. The Blue Devils also held opponents to 39.3 percent shooting and 30.6 percent from three. Those figures describe a defense that created many misses and usually finished the possession.

But the personnel carrying that result changed. Cameron Boozer alone recorded 261 defensive rebounds. The four returners named in Duke’s June release totaled 396: Ngongba had 116, Sarr 115, Foster 96, and Cayden Boozer 69. In other words, those four produced only 37.4 percent of Duke’s defensive rebounds even though they are the most securely identified pieces of the returning core. The arithmetic is not a forecast. Minutes, roles, teammates, opponent shots, and team-rebound credits will all change. It simply identifies a genuine workload gap.

The wrong response is to multiply every returner’s 2025–26 average by more minutes and declare the problem solved. Rebound totals are shaped by where a player is stationed, which teammate contests, whether the defense switches, the location of the miss, and who is instructed to release. Duke must replace an ecosystem, not a column in a box score.

## A defensive rebound is a five-player sequence

The common image of rebounding is the tallest player jumping highest. The possession usually turns earlier. Five connected jobs determine whether a miss becomes Duke’s ball.

First, the on-ball defender influences the shot. Staying attached to a pull-up shooter can create a miss but also carry that defender away from the rim. Containing a drive may keep the center on his own man and improve the rebounding structure. Losing the ball cleanly forces a big to contest, removes him from his box-out, and starts a rotation behind him.

Second, somebody contests without abandoning the possession. Ngongba may be the most likely primary rim contestant in conventional groups, but a contest and a rebound are often competing tasks. If he steps across the lane to challenge a driver, the offensive center gains inside position unless a wing rotates down. A blocked or altered shot can therefore be a poor rebounding situation unless the next assignment is explicit.

Third, the nearest non-contesting defender makes first contact. This is the least visible job and often the most important. The defender should find the opponent before tracking the ball, establish legal leverage, and prevent a direct path to the restricted area. The player who makes that contact may never receive statistical credit. A guard can create a teammate’s rebound by checking a larger wing; a center can seal the opponent’s best rebounder while a guard collects the ball.

Fourth, a designated pursuer attacks the ball. Long misses from three do not always favor the center. They often reach the elbows, wings, or free-throw line. The weak-side guard or wing must remain involved instead of assuming the frontcourt will clean up. Foster’s 96 and Sarr’s 115 defensive rebounds from last season establish that they have collected meaningful volume in Duke games. They do not prove how much was contested or which future lineups best use them.

Fifth, the group secures control before running. A rebound is not complete if the ball is tipped twice, stripped, or knocked out to another shooter. The collector needs two hands when traffic demands it, strong outlets when the advantage is real, and restraint when bodies surround the ball. Duke’s transition ambitions should begin after possession is established, not a step before.

This sequence explains why “four crash, one releases” is useful as a starting rule but insufficient as a full scheme. The identity of the release player can change with the shot location and matchup. The center who contests may need a wing to replace him. A guard stationed in the weak-side corner may be the best long-rebound pursuer. The important feature is that all five players can name their responsibility before the shot goes up.

## What the returning evidence does and does not say

Ngongba is the clearest returning interior rebounder. His official line—70 offensive and 116 defensive rebounds in 701 minutes—shows productive involvement in a limited workload. It also comes with 90 personal fouls. More minutes could produce more team rebounding, but only if he stays available and preserves sound contest position. Chasing blocks or arriving late can increase both foul risk and the need for teammates to cover his man. The meaningful development question is not whether he can average a larger round number. It is whether Duke can finish possessions when he is the primary helper and whether he can remain on the floor against different spacing structures.

Sarr’s 115 defensive rebounds in 893 minutes make him an important bridge candidate. A large wing beside one center can perform the low-man rotation, hit an opposing big, and pursue from the weak side. Yet that role cannot simply be assigned from his listed size. If Duke asks Sarr to defend an opponent’s primary large creator far from the basket, his matchup may pull him out of the rebounding picture. If he functions as the weak-side helper, he may be in better position to finish. His rebound contribution will therefore depend on matchup ownership as much as effort.

Foster’s 96 defensive rebounds are a useful reminder that guards matter. A lead guard who rebounds can start transition without an outlet and can collect misses that escape the paint. But Duke should not treat every guard rebound as surplus value. If Foster is assigned to chase a shooter through multiple screens, he may finish possessions farther from the ball. If he helps tag a roller, he must recover into contact rather than watch the shot. His 2025–26 injury interruption also makes future workload and movement questions matters for current evidence, not assumption.

Cayden Boozer’s 69 defensive rebounds came alongside 113 assists and an expanded initiation role. The tension is structural: sending a guard early can improve transition spacing, while holding that guard in the rebounding shell can help end possessions. Duke need not choose one rule for every game. Against a poor offensive-rebounding opponent, a guard release may be rational. Against a team that sends three to the glass, the same choice may concede the very advantage Duke hopes to run from.

Duke’s official aggregate announcement confirms three transfers but does not name them. Independent roster reporting identifies John Blackwell and Drew Scharnowski among the additions and reports previous-season averages of 5.1 and 6.0 total rebounds per game, respectively. Those numbers are relevant priors, not Duke assignments. Blackwell’s guard rebounding suggests physical participation; Scharnowski’s frontcourt production suggests another possible contact-and-pursuit option. Neither number reveals how their old teams distributed contests and releases, and neither establishes translation against ACC size. Other reported newcomers should remain outside any firm ownership chart until Duke publishes the complete roster and the team supplies competitive evidence.

## How projected lineup structures change the jobs

### Ngongba with four perimeter players

This is the easiest structure to describe and the most dangerous to oversimplify. Ngongba would plausibly anchor the paint and take the primary center matchup. If he contests, the weak-side forward must meet the opposing center before pursuing the ball. The two nearest guards must check perimeter crashers and cover long rebounds. One player may release, but only after the opponent’s offensive-rebounding plan is identified.

The benefit is clear role geometry: one interior anchor and four players who can close space. The risk is overloading that anchor. Repeated middle penetration forces Ngongba to leave his matchup, which turns every rebound into a rotation test. A lineup can improve its rebounding without changing personnel simply by containing the ball better. Conversely, a nominally big group can rebound poorly if the center contests every drive and the wings never replace him.

### Scharnowski as the lone center

If independent reporting is reflected on Duke’s final roster, a Scharnowski-at-five group could change where the first contest occurs. More aggressive screen-level defense might keep the handler out of the lane, but it can also pull the center above the foul line. That shifts the possession-ending burden toward Sarr, another large wing, and the guards.

The alternative is a deeper coverage that preserves the center near the rim. Whether that is viable depends on Scharnowski’s comfort, opponent pull-up shooting, and Duke’s screen navigation. His Belmont rebounding average cannot choose the coverage. The early test is whether he can move from the screen action into first contact without losing balance or position against stronger players.

### Ngongba and Scharnowski together

A two-big lineup appears to solve rebounding by adding size, but it creates assignment questions. One big will often contest while the other becomes the primary box-out player. If both chase the ball, an offensive rebounder can slip behind them. If neither can defend a stretch four, the opponent may drag one away from the rim and neutralize the apparent advantage.

The pairing’s best rebounding version would be complementary: the helper contests, the weak-side big seals the most dangerous opponent, and a guard pursues the released ball. It could be especially useful against teams that post two frontcourt players or send multiple bodies to the glass. Its cost may appear at the other end through spacing and transition speed, so the correct comparison is total lineup value, not rebound count in isolation.

### Large-wing or switch-heavy groups

Small lineups cannot outsource the glass. Their case is that switching or pressure prevents a clean initial advantage, leaving all five defenders close enough to make contact. The group fails if players celebrate a successful switch while a larger opponent seals inside. It also fails if the nominal center contests at the rim and four perimeter players release.

These groups should use gang rebounding as a mechanism, not a slogan. The nearest defender hits, the next pursues, and the remaining players protect against a tip-out. Switches must be followed through to the end of the possession: a guard switched onto a center may need immediate help from a wing, while the big switched outside may become the long-rebound pursuer. This structure can work late in the shot clock or against five-out spacing, when traditional size is less useful. It remains a hypothesis until Duke closes possessions against strong offensive-rebounding teams.

## Metrics that can distinguish mechanism from noise

The season’s primary team measure should be defensive rebound percentage:

$$
\text{DRB\%}=\frac{\text{team defensive rebounds}}{\text{team defensive rebounds}+\text{opponent offensive rebounds}}.
$$

It controls for the number of missed shots better than defensive rebounds per game. Even that rate needs context. A team that forces many difficult threes may face more long and unpredictable rebounds. A switching lineup may prevent rim attempts but create cross-matches when the shot rises. A deep-drop lineup may keep its center near the rim yet allow guards to pull up, changing miss location. Opponent quality and shot profile matter.

Lineup-level tracking should add three film-coded questions. Who made the primary contest? Who made first contact with the opponent’s most dangerous rebounder? Where did the ball land? Those observations distinguish a center failing to secure a reachable rebound from a wing failing to rotate after the center helped. They also reveal whether an individual’s high total came from uncontested collections or from winning physical chances.

Useful supporting measures include opponent offensive rebounds after paint contests, rebounds allowed after switches, long-rebound recoveries, and second-chance possessions that begin with a missed box-out rather than an unlucky bounce. Team rebounds and dead-ball rebounds should be separated from player-controlled rebounds. Five-game rolling samples can show emerging patterns, but opponent styles must be noted; one game against an extreme crash team can distort a short window.

The most important caution is causal. If a smaller lineup posts a weak rebounding rate, size may be the cause—or repeated penetration may have forced its largest defender to help. If a two-big lineup dominates the glass, it may have benefited from poor opponent shooting locations rather than superior technique. Film and rate statistics must answer each other.

## Alternatives and decision rules

Duke does not need one universal rebounding identity. It can choose among three broad policies.

A conservative policy keeps four players in the rebounding shell and releases one guard. This protects the baseline and is the safest July expectation. An aggressive transition policy releases two when the shot and opponent permit, accepting some extra glass risk for early offense. An opponent-specific policy changes the release number based on who crashes, who shoots, and which Duke player is pulled into the contest.

The opponent-specific version is likely the strongest long-term answer, but it demands communication. Players must recognize whether the opposing point guard retreats, whether a corner shooter crashes, and whether a stretch big pulls Duke’s center away. Until those reads become reliable, a simple conservative rule may produce fewer failures.

Scheyer’s substitution decisions should also respond to the type of failure. A lost rebound caused by no contact is different from a well-defended tip that falls to the offense. Repeated leaks require a rule change; repeated failure to move a larger opponent may require different personnel. A single high rebound total should not protect a lineup if it surrendered easy second chances, and one unlucky bounce should not erase a structurally sound possession.

## What would count as a successful translation

The goal is not to reproduce Cameron Boozer’s 261 defensive rebounds through one player. It is to preserve a high team share of available rebounds while allowing different lineups to defend as intended. Ngongba’s minutes could grow, Sarr and the guards could absorb more pursuit, transfers could supply physicality, and freshmen could earn bounded jobs. None must become a singular savior if the assignments connect.

By the first major November tests, Duke should be able to answer four questions with film rather than projection. When the center contests, who replaces him? Which guard or wing is allowed to release? Can the small group complete a switch with a rebound? Can the two-big group defend enough space to keep its rebounding advantage relevant?

If Duke remains near its prior defensive-rebound baseline across quality opponents and lineup types, the possession-ending system will have translated. If the aggregate rate looks acceptable only because the biggest lineup dominates weak opponents, scalability remains unresolved. If Duke forces misses but repeatedly concedes second shots, the defense is not finished, regardless of blocks or initial shot quality.

Defensive rebounding is therefore the final test of [[Duke 2026-27 defensive role map|Duke’s defensive role map]]. Containment protects the center. The contest activates the low man. First contact frees a pursuer. Secure possession enables transition. The roster has plausible candidates for every job, but July evidence justifies only a framework. The season must reveal who can complete it together.
