Jon Scheyer's first four Duke teams show a stable preference for size, rebounding, and low-mistake offense, but the way those strengths were expressed changed sharply with personnel.

Up: [[Duke men's basketball 2026-27]]

The safest way to describe a coach's system is to begin with what his teams repeatedly accomplish, then ask which lineup structures make those outcomes possible. That is especially important with Jon Scheyer. Duke's roster turned over heavily between his first four seasons as head coach, and public team statistics do not identify a play call or coverage. They can establish the result of a scheme—shot mix, ball security, rebounding, opponent shooting, and scoring—but they cannot prove, by themselves, whether Duke used a particular ball-screen coverage on a particular possession.

This note therefore treats the completed 2022-23 through 2025-26 seasons as a historical record, not as a claim about the unseen 2026-27 team. It separates three evidence levels: official season totals; Duke's own retrospective descriptions; and tactical interpretation consistent with those facts. It does not present reconstructed film language as official truth.

These are descriptive, not pace-adjusted, figures calculated from Duke's official totals. Yet they identify the main story. Scheyer's debut team won with defense, defensive rebounding, and a large front line while producing only 6.8 made threes per game. The second team became more efficient and protected the ball much better. The third combined high-volume perimeter shooting, elite field-goal efficiency, low turnovers, and historically stingy opponent shooting. The fourth lost some three-point accuracy but overwhelmed opponents on the glass while retaining strong two-way efficiency.

The continuity is not a single favorite action. It is lineup construction that protects several possession-level advantages at once: size without conceding too much skill, enough passing to avoid dependence on one conventional point guard, and enough defensive length to finish stops with a rebound. The variation is how each roster paid for those advantages.

## First season: size and the possession floor

Scheyer's 2022-23 team was young, frequently disrupted by injuries, and built around a large frontcourt. Duke's official coaching biography says the team emphasized defense and rebounding, ranked in the national top 20 in nearly every rebounding statistic, and was top 30 defensively. The raw record supports that description: opponents shot .406 overall and .305 from three, while Duke won the rebounding battle by 7.9 per game and blocked 4.5 shots per game.

Offensively, this was not yet the spaced, high-throughput version associated with 2024-25. Duke shot .335 from three, made 6.8 threes per game, and committed 12 turnovers per game. Kyle Filipowski and Dereck Lively II gave the roster different frontcourt functions; the broader point is that the team could create value through size, second chances, interior finishing, and foul pressure when perimeter creation was uneven. A large offensive-rebounding base also created a possession floor: a mediocre first shot did not always end the trip.

That floor came with a scalability question. Playing multiple non-elite shooters can compress driving lanes, make post touches easier to crowd, and leave a team vulnerable when an opponent both rebounds and forces the ball toward low-value shooters. The correct historical claim is not that one spacing problem caused every loss. It is that the roster's greatest strengths—frontcourt size and rebounding—could conflict with maximum half-court spacing. Scheyer's later teams did not abandon size; they found different ways to attach skill to it.

## Second season: ball security and five-man skill

The 2023-24 team illustrates why lineup scalability is more than “going small.” Duke still played through Kyle Filipowski, who averaged 16.4 points, 8.2 rebounds, and 2.8 assists, but surrounded him with more perimeter shooting and multiple handlers. Jared McCain shot .414 from three, Jeremy Roach .429, Caleb Foster .406, and Tyrese Proctor .352 on high volume. Team three-point accuracy rose to .377, assists increased, and turnovers fell from 12.0 to 9.7 per game.

The structural gain was not merely shot making. A big who can pass, face the basket, or operate away from the rim changes the geometry of a lineup. Guards can cut behind overplays; handoffs and ball screens can flow into one another; and a defense cannot automatically solve the possession by stationing its center in the lane. Several perimeter players could initiate, so pressure on one nominal point guard did not necessarily end the action.

The season also warns against equating offensive versatility with complete two-way scalability. Duke allowed opponents to shoot .430 overall, worse than in Scheyer's other first four seasons, and the rebound margin fell to +5.1. The team still reached the Elite Eight and held Houston to 51 points in the Sweet 16, demonstrating that a season average does not dictate every matchup. But across the full sample, the roster's spacing and ball security advanced faster than its ability to suppress opponent field-goal percentage.

This is the first reusable Scheyer principle: a lineup is not scalable merely because all five players can score. It must preserve point-of-attack resistance, rim protection, and possession completion when opponents increase physicality and target the weakest defender.

## Third season: the two-way convergence

The 2024-25 team is the strongest evidence for Scheyer's desired two-way architecture. Duke scored 83.2 points per game while allowing 62.8. It shot .493 overall and .386 from three, made 394 threes, assisted 17 baskets per game, and turned the ball over only 9.3 times. Opponents shot .384 overall and .311 from three. Duke's official retrospective notes that the team led the ACC in both scoring offense and scoring defense and posted the best assist-to-turnover ratio in program history.

Personnel explains why apparently competing goals could coexist. Cooper Flagg supplied creation, passing, rebounding, weak-side defense, and transition pressure from a large forward position. Kon Knueppel combined shooting with secondary playmaking. Tyrese Proctor offered an experienced guard defender and shooter. Sion James added strength and connective passing. Khaman Maluach gave the defense vertical size without demanding post-up volume. The roster could field size at every position while keeping enough shooting and decision-making on the floor.

That combination made the offense difficult to reduce to one action. If a defense loaded toward Flagg, shooting and secondary attacks remained. If it switched, Duke could seek a size mismatch. If it stayed attached to shooters, the rim and offensive glass remained available. Again, those are structural implications of player skill sets and team outcomes, not a claim that Duke called the same read every time.

Defensively, length mattered because it widened the range of viable answers. A lineup with mobile size can switch selectively, contest without overhelping, recover to shooters, and survive a temporary mismatch long enough for the possession to end. Maluach's rim presence and Flagg's range reduced the cost of aggressive perimeter positioning. Duke also rebounded well enough to convert those contests into stops.

The Final Four loss does not erase the architecture, nor does the architecture prove invulnerability. Late-game offense, foul decisions, opponent shot making, and execution in a tiny sample can decide a knockout game. “Scalable” should mean that a lineup retains multiple paths against elite opposition, not that it guarantees a title.

## Fourth season: rebounding as an organizing advantage

In 2025-26, Duke again finished 35-3, won both ACC titles, earned the NCAA tournament's No. 1 overall seed, and reached the Elite Eight. The team shot .491 overall but only .347 from three, below the previous two seasons. It nevertheless scored 81.6 points per game because it shot well inside the arc, reached the free-throw line, passed effectively, and created an enormous possession advantage on the glass. Duke outrebounded opponents by 11.0 per game, 40.2 to 29.3.

Cameron Boozer's official line—22.5 points, 10.2 rebounds, 3.5 assists, and .556 shooting—captures the frontcourt hub at the center of this version. Isaiah Evans increased from 13.6 minutes and 6.8 points per game in 2024-25 to 28.2 minutes and 15.0 points in 2025-26, a useful example of continuity producing a larger role. Patrick Ngongba II, Maliq Brown, and other large forwards and centers helped make rebounding a collective identity rather than a single-star statistic.

The tactical lesson differs subtly from 2024-25. The third Scheyer team had the cleanest balance of shooting, creation, length, and low turnovers. The fourth showed that elite lineup functionality need not depend on elite team three-point accuracy if the group creates extra shots, draws fouls, and defends. But the tradeoff remains visible: the closer a tournament opponent can come to neutralizing the glass and shrinking driving space, the more valuable off-the-dribble shooting and fast decisions become.

## A practical lineup-scalability test

The historical record suggests evaluating a Duke lineup through six linked questions rather than by positions alone.

### 1. Where does the first advantage come from?

A group needs at least one reliable source of rotation: a guard beating the first defender, a forward creating from the elbow, a post mismatch, movement shooting, or transition pressure. “Five skilled players” is too vague. The analyst should identify the mechanism and ask whether a top opponent can switch, deny, or load up against it.

### 2. What happens after the first pass?

Tournament defenses often concede the first action and test the second decision. Duke's best groups under Scheyer have included connectors who can catch, drive, pass, or shoot without resetting the possession. Assist totals and turnover rates are imperfect but useful checks on whether the ball keeps moving without becoming careless.

### 3. Can two weak shooters share the floor?

This is contextual rather than categorical. Two limited shooters may be playable if one screens, finishes, passes, and dominates the glass while the other creates advantages or cuts intelligently. They become costly when both allow defenders to occupy the lane without consequence. The question is not the label “non-shooter”; it is what each defender is permitted to do.

### 4. Who contains the ball?

Size behind the play does not excuse repeated straight-line penetration. A scalable lineup needs at least one credible point-of-attack matchup and enough surrounding mobility that opponents cannot simply screen that defender away and attack the next weakest link.

### 5. What is the back-line answer?

Traditional rim protection, switchable size, early help, and charge-taking can all protect the basket, but each changes the shots conceded elsewhere. Official opponent field-goal and block totals describe outcomes; possession review is required to attribute them to a coverage.

### 6. Who ends the possession?

Scheyer's four teams make rebounding impossible to treat as an afterthought. A forced miss is not a stop until the defense controls it. Conversely, offensive rebounding can stabilize an offense when initial shooting deteriorates. The scalable lineup is the one that preserves enough size and physicality to finish possessions without destroying spacing or transition defense.

## What is stable, and what remains personnel-dependent

Four seasons support several modest conclusions. Scheyer has consistently built large teams. His best groups pair that size with multi-position skill rather than choosing between “big” and “small.” Rebounding is a repeated source of margin. Ball security improved dramatically after his first season and remained a strength. Defensive form has changed with available rim protection, mobility, and perimeter resistance, while the 2024-25 and 2025-26 teams demonstrate that elite defense can coexist with very different offensive shot-making profiles.

What the history does not establish is a fixed 2026-27 scheme. The new roster must earn its own description. Summer measurements, recruiting labels, and past team averages cannot tell us which five players communicate best, which ball-screen answer the staff will prefer, or who can make the second-side read against pressure. [[Duke lineup scalability]] should therefore remain a recurring empirical question: test each lineup against the six functions above, over meaningful possessions and opponent types, rather than assigning it an identity in July.

## Why it matters

This framework turns “Scheyer offense” and “Scheyer defense” from aesthetic labels into falsifiable questions. It also prevents a common analytical mistake: crediting every strong outcome to coaching design while blaming every weakness on personnel, or vice versa. Roster construction defines available solutions; teaching and deployment determine how consistently those solutions appear; player execution determines whether the possession works.

For the [[Duke men's basketball 2026-27|2026-27 season]], the relevant baseline is not that Duke will replay 2024-25 or 2025-26. It is that Scheyer's strongest teams have preserved creation, shooting, size, defense, and rebounding simultaneously. Any proposed rotation should be judged by which of those functions it retains, which it trades away, and whether the trade is acceptable against the opponent in front of it.
