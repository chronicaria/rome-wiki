Duke’s 2026–27 defense should be built as a chain of assigned jobs—ball containment, screen navigation, wing matchup absorption, back-line organization, rim protection, and possession-ending rebounding—rather than as a collection of long athletes assumed to be interchangeable.

## The map is a projection, not a depth chart

As of July 10, 2026, Duke has officially confirmed the architecture of its roster more securely than the full individual ledger. The program’s June 22 release says five players return, three transfers arrive, and the incoming recruiting class ranks second nationally. It names returning scorers Cayden Boozer, Caleb Foster, Patrick Ngongba II, and Dame Sarr. Duke’s public roster page still displays 2025–26, while independent and prior-school sources identify newcomers including John Blackwell, Drew Scharnowski, Jacob Theodosiou, Deron Rippey Jr., Cameron Williams, and Bryson Howard. This note therefore treats Duke’s aggregate roster structure and completed Duke statistics as verified, newcomer identities as reported or independently corroborated where noted, and all 2026–27 assignments as tactical inference.

That separation matters especially on defense. Listed height, recruiting rank, steals, and blocks can establish useful priors, but none proves that a player can execute Duke’s calls against a specific opponent. Good defense is relational. A guard’s screen navigation determines how long the center must contain the ball. The center’s positioning determines whether the weak-side wing must tag the roller. That tag determines whether another defender must split two shooters. The possession ends only when somebody secures the rebound. One failure can make the next player appear responsible even when the chain broke earlier.

The correct summer question is not “Who are Duke’s five best defenders?” It is “Which combinations can assign every necessary job without hiding two weak links or overloading one anchor?” The role map below answers provisionally. It does not predict starters or exact minutes.

## The six jobs every lineup must cover

### Point-of-attack containment

The first defender must influence the ball before the screen or drive produces a clean angle. Containment does not require winning every isolation. It requires sending the handler toward the planned help, avoiding unnecessary opening of the hips, recovering after a change of direction, and preventing a routine action from becoming a two-on-one.

Foster is the most plausible returning stabilizer for this job because he has accumulated multiple seasons in Duke’s terminology and has the size to take more than one guard type. That is an inference from experience and physical profile, not a verified claim that he was or will be Duke’s best stopper. His 2025–26 season was interrupted by injury, so current movement and availability must be established rather than assumed.

Boozer offers another experienced option. His sophomore leap should be evaluated through whether he can stay attached to quick guards without conceding the middle, absorb contact at the screen, and recover without fouling. Blackwell’s reported 5.1 rebounds per game at Wisconsin suggest useful physical involvement, but rebounding production does not establish point-of-attack defense. His projected offensive burden also creates a resource question: Duke may want him on a dangerous scorer in selected possessions, yet asking him to chase the opponent’s lead guard all night could tax his late-game creation.

Rippey has the clearest projected upside as a pressure defender among the reported freshmen, but that label remains a recruiting-level prior. USA Basketball verifies his guard identity and national-team background, not college screen navigation. Early minutes should be read through repeatable tasks: does he angle the ball toward help, get skinny over screens, remain in the play after contact, and avoid gambling himself behind the possession? A highlight steal is much weaker evidence than five consecutive correct containments.

### Screen navigation and coverage execution

Point-of-attack defense and screen navigation overlap but are not identical. A defender can move laterally well in space and still lose contact at the screen. Duke’s guards must recognize the coverage before impact: chase over in drop, lock-and-trail a shooter, switch when called, peel-switch after a drive, or recover to the original assignment after a show.

Foster and Boozer have the strongest continuity case because they have already operated inside Scheyer’s system. Continuity reduces the language burden, though it does not guarantee execution. Theodosiou, if his reported transfer status is reflected on Duke’s final roster, brings college experience but must learn new calls. Blackwell faces the same translation from Wisconsin. Their prior minutes show that they have defended college actions; they do not show that every habit matches Duke’s rules.

Freshman wings Williams and Howard may have enough size to switch some guard-wing actions and reduce the need to chase. That possibility should not become a universal “switchability” label. A successful switch requires early communication, correct leverage, and survival after the offense attacks the new matchup. Switching merely to avoid navigating a screen can concede a seal, a driving angle, or an offensive rebound. Duke should use switching as a coverage with a purpose, not as a synonym for athleticism.

### Large-wing matchup absorption

Sarr is the most obvious candidate to take the opponent’s biggest perimeter creator. Duke listed him at 6-foot-8 in 2025–26, and the completed season gives him a year of ACC possessions plus 14 blocks in the official totals. Those facts support length and some defensive event production. They do not reveal whether he can consistently contain a primary creator, navigate repeated ball screens, or defend a skilled four without surrendering the glass.

His role matters because matchup absorption protects the rest of the structure. If Sarr can guard a large initiator without automatic help, Foster or Boozer can take a smaller guard, Blackwell can conserve some creation energy, and Ngongba can stay closer to the rim. If Sarr is better as a weak-side disruptor than as a primary stopper, Scheyer must assign the hardest creator elsewhere and preserve Sarr’s length for rotations.

Williams, Howard, and Sebastian Wilkins are projected alternatives, not established answers. A large freshman who survives the initial drive but misses the next off-ball exchange has not completed the job. Their pathway should be bounded: take a similar-sized wing, maintain gap position, execute the low-man rotation, and box out. Expanded assignments can follow when those four actions become dependable. Defensive reputation from high school can identify candidates; it cannot settle the hierarchy.

### Nail help, low-man rotation, and off-ball communication

The most important defender on many possessions is neither on the ball nor at the rim. The nail helper discourages a direct drive while remaining able to recover to a shooter. The low man tags a roller or cutter when the center steps toward the ball. The next defender must then cover two threats until the shell unscrambles. These tasks demand anticipation and communication more than a box-score event.

Boozer’s passing vision on offense suggests—without proving—potential value as an off-ball reader. Foster’s system experience makes him a plausible organizer from the perimeter. Sarr’s length gives him recovery margin if his first step is correct. Blackwell’s guard rebounding can become valuable after he stunts and recovers. But every assignment remains an inference until film shows the timing.

This is also where young lineups are most likely to break. A freshman can make the correct first rotation and still abandon the corner too far, tag with the wrong body position, or watch the shot instead of finding a rebounder. Duke should judge off-ball growth by whether teammates avoid emergency rotations. Fewer spectacular recoveries can indicate a better-connected defense if the initial positioning removed the emergency.

### Back-line organization and rim protection

Ngongba is the strongest established candidate to anchor the baseline defense. Duke’s official 2025–26 totals credit him with 1.1 blocks per game, 5.8 rebounds in 21.9 minutes, and 90 fouls in 701 minutes. The blocks and size show real rim activity; the foul total, about 5.1 per 40 minutes, identifies the constraint. An anchor who cannot remain available cannot stabilize every lineup.

His job is larger than blocking shots. In drop coverage he must choose a depth that discourages the ball handler without releasing the roller. He must call the screen early, square the drive, contest vertically, and then locate the ball or a body. Against empty-corner pick-and-roll, his help arrives from a different place. Against five-out spacing, he may have to show higher or switch briefly before recovering. The 2025–26 totals cannot establish mastery of this menu; they merely provide the best completed Duke baseline on the projected roster.

Scharnowski offers a reported alternative. CBS’s roster tracker lists 1.3 blocks and 6.0 rebounds per game in 21.8 minutes at Belmont. Those figures show frontcourt events and production, not ACC-caliber anchoring. The translation questions are precise: can he hold his position against stronger finishers, contain a guard for two dribbles, communicate the weak-side rotation, and rebound after leaving his man? If yes, Duke can change its offensive and defensive geometry when Ngongba sits. If not, Scharnowski may be more viable beside Ngongba than as the only rim protector—provided he can defend a four in space.

Reported young bigs Maxime Meyer and Joaquim Boumtje Boumtje should remain outside the verified hierarchy until Duke publishes its final roster and competitive evidence exists. Height is not rim protection. The first useful indicators are early talk, verticality, foul avoidance, screen-call volume, and possession-ending rebounds.

### Defensive rebounding and transition release

Rebounding is the final defensive role, not a separate box-score category. Duke lost Cameron Boozer’s large share of 2025–26 rebounds. Ngongba can absorb more only if his minutes and foul sustainability rise. Scharnowski’s reported Belmont production and Blackwell’s Wisconsin guard rebounding help on paper. Sarr and every large wing must convert size into contact and possession.

The center often cannot collect the rebound because he is contesting. That makes the weak-side wing and guard responsible for hitting the offensive rebounder before pursuing the ball. A lineup can force a difficult miss and still defend badly if three players leak into transition. Conversely, Duke’s desire to play faster should clarify rather than weaken assignments: designate one release player, require the other four to rebound or establish contact, and change that rule only for opponent-specific reasons.

Team defensive-rebound percentage will be more informative than any one player’s average. Film should separate uncontested collections from contested wins, misses lost after the center helps, and long rebounds created by high-volume three-point opponents. Smaller units must prove that extra speed or switching produces enough difficult shots to compensate for reduced size.

## How the assignments fit common lineup structures

### Ngongba with four perimeter players

This is the clearest baseline. Ngongba owns back-line calls, rim contests, and the primary center matchup. Sarr, if included, takes the largest wing and supplies low-man length. Two of Foster, Boozer, Blackwell, and Rippey divide point-of-attack and screen-navigation work. The fifth player must rebound rather than function as a protected scorer.

The structure’s strength is legibility: the perimeter can chase over screens knowing a true center is behind it. Its weakness is dependence. If the guards repeatedly concede clean middle penetration, Ngongba must choose between pull-up, roller, and corner. Fouls follow, and the weak side enters constant rotation. This group’s defensive ceiling is set as much by its second-best ball defender as by its center.

### Scharnowski at center

A Scharnowski-at-five unit can plausibly play higher at the level of the screen, switch selected actions, or use more aggressive shows if his mobility translates. That could keep the ball from reaching the paint cleanly. It also moves the rebounding burden outward. Sarr or another large wing becomes essential as a secondary rim helper and glass finisher.

This lineup should not copy Ngongba’s coverage automatically. If Scharnowski’s best skill is movement and communication rather than deep-drop deterrence, Duke should exploit the difference. The evidence gate is opponent quality: successful possessions against mid-major reserves or in an exhibition cannot settle whether the coverage holds against elite pull-up shooting and vertical threats.

### Ngongba and Scharnowski together

The two-big structure can put Ngongba on the primary center and use Scharnowski as a second rebounder and helper. It may be valuable against post size, two-big offenses, and elite offensive-rebounding teams. The danger is defending a skilled four. If Scharnowski cannot contain that player outside, Duke may have to switch Sarr or a guard onto the four and hide Scharnowski elsewhere, unraveling the presumed size advantage.

The pairing also needs explicit transition assignments. Two bigs crashing or occupying the lane can leave a slow floor balance after a turnover. On defense, both cannot chase the same block. The weak-side big must protect the rebound or the lob while the primary helper engages the ball.

### Small or switch-heavy groups

A true wing-at-five group is the highest-variance possibility. Its purpose would be to remove the first advantage through switching, pressure, or early rotation—not to imitate traditional rim protection without a center. Sarr or another large wing would organize the back line; all five would have to rebound. The lineup should be judged by the entire possession: a clean switch followed by a post mismatch and offensive rebound is not a success.

Small groups may be useful late in the clock, when there is less time to exploit a mismatch; against five-out opponents that pull Ngongba away from the basket; or when Duke needs pressure and turnover creation. They are less credible as a default until someone proves capable of absorbing center contact and the team closes the glass collectively.

## A provisional ownership table

The most defensible July assignment is a hierarchy of responsibility, not a grade:

- **Primary back-line anchor:** Ngongba, demonstrated at Duke but limited by foul sustainability and unresolved coverage range.
- **Secondary center and change-of-coverage candidate:** Scharnowski, demonstrated as a productive Belmont big but unverified in an ACC defensive role.
- **Large-wing assignment leader:** Sarr, supported by size and Duke experience but not yet proven as an every-possession stopper.
- **Perimeter continuity organizers:** Foster and Boozer, supported by system experience but requiring current health, containment, and screen-navigation evidence.
- **Physical scoring guard with rebounding value:** Blackwell, supported by major-conference production; hardest-matchup capacity and Duke-system execution remain unverified.
- **Pressure and speed candidate:** Rippey, a reported freshman whose pathway depends on disciplined containment more than steals.
- **Large-wing depth candidates:** Williams, Howard, and Wilkins, whose college defensive roles remain unknown.
- **Experienced contingency guard:** Theodosiou, if officially rostered; prior college minutes do not determine his Duke matchup tier.
- **Developmental rim depth:** Meyer and Boumtje Boumtje, reported additions with no justified 2026–27 defensive assignment yet.

This map intentionally leaves multiple jobs without a single permanent owner. Duke may assign by opponent: Foster on a movement shooter, Sarr on a large initiator, Boozer on a connector, and Blackwell on the least taxing guard one night; the next opponent may require a different arrangement. Flexibility is valuable only when communication preserves the chain.

## What to verify once games begin

The first five competitive games should establish who receives the opening assignment on three archetypes: a quick ball-screen guard, a large scoring wing, and a stretch four. Opening assignments are not final judgments, but they reveal intent. Substitution patterns after defensive errors reveal which mistakes the staff considers tolerable.

Possession tracking should record screen coverage, first breakdown, helper, result, and rebounder. It should distinguish a made difficult jumper from a failed possession and an opponent miss after a blown rotation from a successful stop. For Ngongba and Scharnowski, track fouls by cause: late position, illegal screen-defense contact, reach, vertical contest, or offensive foul. For guards, track whether they die on screens, recover, or execute the coverage. For wings, track tag-and-recover timing and box-outs.

The November schedule will supply better stress tests than summer projections. Duke’s official announcements include high-level opponents with different offensive structures. A defense that handles one star guard may still fail against five-out spacing or offensive rebounding. The map should change when a player repeatedly performs a job against quality opposition, not after one highlight or one poor box score.

## Why it matters

Duke’s roster appears deep enough to create many plausible lineups, but defensive role clarity determines whether that depth becomes adaptability or confusion. Scheyer can change centers, add size, pressure the ball, or switch more often only if every group knows who contains, who helps, who protects the rim, and who rebounds. The team does not need five individually dominant defenders. It needs five defenders whose jobs complete one another.

The central dependency is clear. Ngongba supplies the best established interior baseline, but his value scales only if perimeter defenders reduce emergency rotations and he reduces fouls. Sarr supplies the cleanest large-wing bridge, but his value scales only if he can execute both primary matchups and weak-side work. Foster and Boozer supply continuity, but continuity matters only if it becomes reliable containment and communication. The newcomers enlarge the menu; they do not arrive with roles already proven.

If Duke identifies two dependable point-of-attack defenders, one large-wing absorber, one communicative anchor, and four credible rebounders in each important lineup, its depth can support opponent-specific coverage without sacrificing its floor. If those jobs concentrate in only three players, opponents will hunt the other two and force Scheyer to choose between offense and defense. That is the defensive question the season must answer.

## Open questions

- Who receives Duke’s first assignment against an elite small ball-screen creator?
- Can Ngongba reduce his foul rate while defending higher or switching selectively?
- Is Sarr more valuable as the primary large-wing stopper or as a weak-side helper whose length stays near the action?
- Which two guards can share the floor without creating a point-of-attack target?
- Can Scharnowski anchor an ACC defense at center, or is his best defensive role beside Ngongba?
- Which freshman first earns trust through correct rotations and rebounding rather than event plays?
- Can a switch-heavy closing group finish possessions against elite offensive rebounding?
