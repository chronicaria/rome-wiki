## What this ledger can and cannot say in July

As of July 10, 2026, Duke has published a 2026–27 schedule but its roster page still displays the 2025–26 team. A June 22 Duke release confirms the new roster's architecture—five returners, three transfers, and a highly ranked freshman class—without supplying a complete official name-by-name ledger on the roster page. That mismatch sets the first rule for any rotation projection: use official evidence for what Duke has confirmed, identify independently reported additions as reported, and do not turn summer uncertainty into exact minutes.

A rotation is not merely a ranking of players. It is the answer to several changing questions: who starts possessions, who creates the first advantage, who preserves spacing, who defends the point of attack, who protects the rim, who rebounds after the contest, and who can perform those jobs together. A player can be eighth in raw minutes yet indispensable against a particular opponent. Another can start because his skill balances the opening group but sit during a different closing situation. This ledger therefore tracks roles, combinations, and evidence thresholds rather than declaring a permanent one-through-five order.

Three labels govern the current entry. **Established Duke function** means the player performed the job across meaningful 2025–26 Duke minutes. **Transferable prior function** means the player performed it in college elsewhere but must show that it survives a new role and competitive environment. **Developmental possibility** means recruiting evidence, physical tools, or a small sample makes the role plausible, but Duke game evidence has not established it. None of those labels guarantees a starting place. They describe how much confidence the public record supports.

The official 2025–26 statistics give the returning core a real baseline. Patrick Ngongba II averaged 10.1 points, 5.8 rebounds, and 2.0 assists in 21.9 minutes while shooting .606 from the field. Caleb Foster averaged 8.3 points and 2.8 assists, made 39 of 98 threes, and started 30 games. Cayden Boozer averaged 7.7 points and 3.0 assists, appearing in all 38 games and starting 11. Dame Sarr started 30 games, averaged 6.4 points and 3.8 rebounds, and made 43 of 133 threes. Those figures establish experience and certain functions; they do not automatically assign 2026–27 hierarchy.

## The returning floor

Ngongba is the clearest established interior anchor. He supplied efficient finishing, offensive rebounding, screening size, some passing, and normal starting-center experience. His path to a larger role is plausible, but the same official totals show 90 fouls in 701 minutes—about 5.1 per 40 minutes. A projection that simply multiplies his per-minute production into 30 minutes ignores the constraint most likely to prevent that scaling. His rotation checkpoint is therefore not points per game. It is whether he can defend more possessions, cover more opponent structures, and finish them with rebounds without losing availability to fouls or fatigue.

Foster offers the strongest returning combination of experience, secondary initiation, and demonstrated shooting. His .398 three-point percentage is useful evidence, especially because lineups around an interior center need defenders to honor the perimeter. Yet percentage alone does not settle his role. Volume, shot type, health, ability to attack a hard closeout, and defensive assignment determine whether he is a starting connector, a second-unit organizer, or a closing guard. His ledger entry should rise when he owns difficult possessions without sacrificing off-ball value, not merely when he makes several early threes.

Boozer supplied a different connective profile. His 113 assists against 51 turnovers are evidence of organization and ball security, while his .303 three-point percentage prevents the ledger from assuming he creates the same spacing as Foster. The central question is whether he becomes enough of a scoring threat that defenders cannot duck under actions or leave him to crowd another creator. His passing can stabilize a group containing scorers, but the best version of that role also punishes the defense for treating him only as a facilitator.

Sarr is the returning large-wing bridge. At 6-foot-8 on Duke's 2025–26 roster, he can theoretically preserve size while defending across wing matchups. His 43 made threes show real willingness and his 37 blocks show weak-side activity, but .323 shooting does not yet guarantee gravity against elite opponents. His route to stable minutes runs through decisions that do not require high usage: defend the correct assignment, rebound outside his area, cut when ignored, move the ball before help resets, and make enough open shots that a defender cannot live in the lane.

Sebastian Wilkins is identified by independent roster reporting as the fifth returner after a redshirt year, while Duke's June release confirms five returners in aggregate. Until the official 2026–27 roster page resolves the ledger, his exact competitive role remains developmental. A redshirt year may improve readiness, but time in the program is not game proof. His first meaningful evidence will be the position groups he practices with, the defensive assignments he receives in public competition, and whether his size translates into rebounding and switchable defense without compromising spacing.

## Transfer roles should begin as ranges

Independent reporting identifies John Blackwell from Wisconsin, Drew Scharnowski from Belmont, and Jacob Theodosiou from Loyola Maryland as Duke's three transfers; Duke's own release confirms the count but the accessible roster page had not yet confirmed the full list as of this review. The ledger can use their prior college production as evidence of transferable functions while withholding claims about starting status or exact minutes.

Blackwell has the strongest reported claim to primary scoring responsibility. Existing roster reporting credits him with 19.1 points, 5.1 rebounds, and 2.3 assists per game at Wisconsin in 2025–26. High-major scoring volume is meaningful: he has already carried difficult possessions against major-conference athletes. The translation question is whether Duke asks him to reproduce the same usage or to create more efficiently inside a deeper team. A high-volume transfer can improve a lineup by bending the defense, but he can also flatten it if every possession becomes a handoff to the established scorer. The ledger should track how often Blackwell initiates, how he responds to a second defender, and whether he remains valuable when Foster, Boozer, or a freshman starts the action.

Scharnowski's reported Belmont line—10.7 points, 6.0 rebounds, 2.6 assists, and 1.3 blocks in 21.8 minutes—suggests a frontcourt connector rather than a simple reserve center. Passing can give Duke a second hub in handoffs, delay actions, and short rolls. His role range spans backup five, mobile change-of-pace center, and possible partner for Ngongba. Each demands different proof. At center he must protect the rim and rebound against larger competition; beside Ngongba he must defend a four and keep the lane functional. Prior blocks and assists justify the experiment, not the conclusion.

Theodosiou is the least appropriate player for a confident July role declaration because accessible public evidence is thinner and Duke's official roster page has not yet resolved membership. Experienced guard depth can matter during injury, foul trouble, practice, and schedule compression even if it does not yield a normal eight-man role. The ledger should not confuse “possible depth value” with “projected rotation lock.” Official enrollment, roster listing, and preseason usage are necessary next steps.

## Freshmen enter through jobs, not rankings

Independent reporting and the existing Duke corpus identify Deron Rippey Jr., Cameron Williams, Bryson Howard, Max Meyer, and Eric Boumtje Boumtje among the incoming freshman group. Their recruiting status supplies a prior about talent; it does not supply a college role. The rotation should ask which job each can perform against an opponent who scouts weaknesses.

Rippey's quickest route appears to be two-way point-guard work: advance the ball, create an initial advantage, defend the point of attack, and organize without excessive turnovers. Duke's returning guards mean he does not need to own every possession, but that also means he must contribute when the ball starts elsewhere. His early-minute test is whether he can defend veteran guards and make fast second-side decisions, not whether a highlight proves he can beat one defender.

Williams and Howard project as large perimeter options who could solve multiple lineup constraints if their shooting, handling, and defense translate together. The operative word is “if.” A large wing who spaces the floor and guards can unlock Ngongba-centered lineups; one who is ignored or misses rotations can compress the offense or weaken the back line. Their ledger should record opponent quality, shot willingness, closeout decisions, defensive matchups, and rebounding rather than points alone.

Meyer and Boumtje Boumtje represent young frontcourt size. Height and recruiting reputation do not establish rim protection, because the college job depends on early positioning, screen coverage, verticality, communication, foul avoidance, and securing the rebound. Duke's veteran interior options reduce the need to force either into a role before he is ready. Developmental minutes can still be valuable, but they should not be counted as a stable competitive rotation until normal opponent possessions demonstrate readiness.

## Provisional role bands

The safest July band is **established returning core**: Ngongba, Foster, Boozer, and Sarr. They have already played substantial Duke minutes, so the question is role expansion and combination fit rather than basic college survival. This band is not a promise that all four start or close.

The next band is **experienced transfer competition**: Blackwell and Scharnowski have reported production that should compete immediately for major functions, while Theodosiou's depth role requires stronger official and usage evidence. Blackwell can contend for primary creation; Scharnowski can contend for frontcourt connectivity and reserve-center minutes. Both must translate rather than merely import their old statistics.

The third band is **freshman role competition**: Rippey, Williams, Howard, Meyer, and Boumtje Boumtje can win jobs through function-specific evidence. The perimeter freshmen have clearer paths to scalable minutes if they defend and decide quickly. The young bigs have a narrower immediate pathway because Ngongba and Scharnowski can absorb veteran frontcourt responsibility, but foul trouble or matchup needs could change that.

Wilkins sits between the returning and developmental bands. Program continuity matters, but a redshirt season supplies practice familiarity rather than public competitive evidence. He can move rapidly if exhibitions and early games show a dependable large-wing job.

## Lineup experiments the ledger should preserve

The first baseline experiment is **Blackwell plus one returning organizer, two scalable wings, and Ngongba**. It gives Duke a demonstrated scoring creator, secondary handling, size, and an established center. Its stress points are whether the wings create enough gravity and whether the perimeter contains the ball well enough to keep Ngongba out of repeated emergency rotations.

The second is **two organizers with Blackwell off or on the ball**. Pairing Foster, Boozer, or Rippey can reduce the burden on any one handler and create more entries into the offense. The cost appears when two defenders can go under screens or help from low-gravity players. The ledger should distinguish lineups that merely have multiple ballhandlers from lineups in which each handler forces a defensive response.

The third is **Scharnowski at five with four perimeter players**. This can create handoff and passing offense while changing ball-screen coverage. Its success should be measured through rim attempts conceded, defensive rebounding, fouls, and the quality of shots created after Scharnowski touches the ball—not through pace or aesthetics alone.

The fourth is **Ngongba and Scharnowski together**. This could improve rebounding and frontcourt passing against large opponents. It can also crowd driving space or expose a big to a skilled four. Opponent behavior is the test: if one defender lives in the paint without punishment, the lineup is not functionally spaced no matter where players stand.

The fifth is a **large-wing pressure group** with Sarr and one of Williams, Howard, or Wilkins. Its purpose is to defend more actions without creating obvious targets and to rebound with only one conventional big. It becomes a closing option only if at least one large wing supplies reliable shooting or quick connective play.

## How the schedule should change the rotation

Duke's official schedule already lists Michigan State on November 10, Illinois on November 17, Washington State on November 21, UConn on November 25, Florida on December 1, Michigan on December 21, and Gonzaga on February 20. The ACC has also announced an 18-game structure with home-and-away games against North Carolina and Virginia as Duke's repeat opponent. Those games are not interchangeable rotation tests.

Early lower-pressure games, once added, can reveal role assignment but may exaggerate efficiency against overmatched opponents. Michigan State and Illinois arrive early enough that Duke's freshmen and transfers will face physical and tactical stress before a permanent hierarchy is likely. UConn and Florida can test whether creation and defensive rebounding survive elite pressure. Michigan and Gonzaga occur late enough to show whether Scheyer has consolidated the group or retained matchup flexibility.

The ledger should therefore record the reason for a change. A player receiving fewer minutes because of foul trouble is not necessarily falling in the hierarchy. A second big used against a large opponent is not automatically the new default. A freshman closing one game because the team needs shooting is meaningful evidence of trust in that circumstance, not proof of universal closing status.

## Update protocol once public competition begins

For every game, record starters, first substitutions, the primary initiators, center pairings, closing group, and any role that persists through opponent adjustment. Pair those observations with the reason visible in the possession record: shooting gravity, turnover control, screen defense, rim deterrence, rebounding, foul pressure, or a specific matchup.

Use at least three to five games before describing a stable efficiency trend, unless Scheyer publicly announces a role or the substitution pattern changes unambiguously. One hot shooting night is not a new identity. One benching after two mistakes is not necessarily a demotion. A repeated closing assignment against strong opponents is stronger evidence than a nominal start followed by limited second-half use.

Raw plus-minus should remain subordinate to mechanism. Short lineup samples are shaped by opponent bench units, shooting variance, and game state. The ledger should ask why a group succeeded: did it reach the rim, force rotations, protect the ball, prevent paint touches, or finish rebounds? If the answer is only that contested threes happened to fall, preserve the result without promoting a theory.

Health and availability require the strictest source boundary. Record only what Duke, the athlete, or strongly sourced reporting establishes. Never infer a diagnosis from video, and never convert “did not play” into an injury claim without confirmation. A role range can narrow because of verified availability; it cannot be built on medical speculation.

## Why it matters

A disciplined rotation ledger prevents two opposite errors. The first is premature certainty: assigning exact minutes in July and then interpreting every event as confirmation. The second is empty agnosticism: refusing to identify any hierarchy despite meaningful evidence. Duke already has established returning functions, reported transfer production, and a schedule that will test different lineup structures. Those facts justify hypotheses, but not a final depth chart.

The central 2026–27 problem is allocation. Duke appears to have more plausible contributors than normal rotation places, while replacing the extraordinary production of Cameron Boozer and the shooting volume of Isaiah Evans. Scheyer must find enough creation without monopolization, enough shooting without sacrificing defense, and enough size without shrinking the floor. A good ledger makes those tradeoffs visible and lets later evidence correct the projection in place.
