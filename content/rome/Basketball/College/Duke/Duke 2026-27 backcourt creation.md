Duke can replace a heliocentric creator by assigning different guards the first touch, first advantage, and second read—but only if every lineup preserves both rim pressure and a clear late-clock hierarchy.

## The question is not who plays point guard

As of July 9, 2026, Duke has announced the architecture of its roster more clearly than its complete individual ledger. The program says five players return, three transfers arrive, and a highly ranked freshman class joins them; its public roster page still displays 2025–26. Independent reporting identifies a crowded guard group built around Wisconsin transfer John Blackwell, returners Caleb Foster and Cayden Boozer, reported freshman addition Deron Rippey Jr., and reported Loyola Maryland transfer Jacob Theodosiou. This article treats Duke’s team-level counts and its returning players as official, while preserving the reported status of individual newcomers until the complete roster appears.

That boundary matters because the backcourt problem is already easy to misstate. Duke does not merely need to choose a point guard. It must replace the sequence of advantages that Cameron Boozer and Isaiah Evans helped create from other positions in 2025–26. Cameron Boozer could draw attention with scoring and then pass over or through the defense. Evans’s shooting punished help and widened the floor before a guard ever dribbled. Their departures remove both a frontcourt hub and high-volume perimeter gravity. Giving one incoming guard a “primary creator” label does not recreate those effects.

The stronger design is distributed but not egalitarian. “Distributed” should mean that multiple players can begin or extend a possession, not that every player receives an identical number of touches. A functional possession still needs an order:

1. an initiator gets Duke into the action and attacks the first coverage;
2. a pressure point—drive, screen, seal, cut, or post touch—forces the defense to move;
3. a second reader attacks the rotation before it recovers;
4. a designated closer owns the possession if the chain produces nothing.

This division separates initiation from creation. A player can safely cross half court and call a set without bending the defense. Another can bend it without being the nominal point guard. A third can be Duke’s best decision-maker only after the defense has already shifted. The offense succeeds when Scheyer aligns those jobs; it fails when several competent handlers take turns dribbling against a set defense.

## What the completed-season evidence actually says

Blackwell has the strongest demonstrated scoring burden. Wisconsin’s official 2025–26 ledger credits him with 19.1 points in 33.7 minutes per game, 96 made threes on 38.9-percent shooting, 79 assists, and 57 turnovers across 34 games. The scoring, three-point volume, and free-throw accuracy establish that he can carry difficult shot volume in a major conference. The 79 assists—2.3 per game—do not prove that he should be Duke’s sole table-setter. They instead suggest a more precise role: first-side scorer who can force the defense to commit, with another guard available to convert the response.

Blackwell also shared Wisconsin’s offense with Nick Boyd, who led the Badgers in scoring and recorded 149 assists. That context is useful. Blackwell’s value did not require exclusive control of every possession. Duke can preserve the part of his Wisconsin role that is most portable—shot creation, pull-up gravity, and foul pressure—without asking him to reproduce every organizational task formerly supplied by a playmaking forward.

Foster supplies a different type of proof. Duke’s official 2025–26 totals list 92 assists against 36 turnovers in 33 games, along with 39-of-98 three-point shooting (39.8 percent). His season was interrupted by injury, so the sample should not be converted into a durability assumption. Yet the assist-to-turnover relationship and catch-and-shoot accuracy support a stabilizer role: Foster can start a possession without wasting it, play away from the ball when Blackwell attacks, and receive a reversal without allowing the defense to reset.

Cayden Boozer’s official line is more aggressive as a passer: 113 assists and 51 turnovers across 38 games, with 7.7 points per game and 30.3-percent three-point shooting. His 3.0 assists per game were the highest among Duke’s returning guards. The tension is visible in the same ledger. Boozer has demonstrated organizational passing, but his perimeter percentage does not yet guarantee that a defense will chase him over every screen or remain attached when he gives the ball up. His sophomore creation question is therefore not “Can he pass?” It is whether his handle, finishing, or pull-up threat can make the defense help soon enough for those passes to become more damaging.

Theodosiou, if and when his Duke status is officially reflected, would add experienced creation of another kind. Loyola Maryland’s official biography credits him with 13.1 points, 3.6 rebounds, and 2.7 assists in 27 games in 2025–26, with 74 assists and 68 turnovers. That is real college responsibility, but it came in a different competitive and spacing environment, and the turnover count cautions against calling him a low-error organizer. His most plausible value is not automatic lead-guard status. It is optionality: another player who has seen help, operated under pressure, and can absorb possessions if injury, foul trouble, or matchup needs rearrange the main rotation.

Rippey is the largest upside variable and the least demonstrated at this level. USA Basketball lists him as a 6-foot-2 guard committed to Duke and describes his participation in the junior national-team environment. That supports his identity and guard profile, not immediate college efficacy. High-school burst and point-guard reputation may translate into the downhill pressure this roster needs, but college defenders will test his handle with physicality, retreat coverage, switches, and crowded gaps. Until competitive possessions exist, he is a plausible change-of-pace initiator rather than a proven solution.

## The backcourt should be built in pairs

The most important unit is not “the starting point guard.” It is the primary two-guard pairing, because a distributed offense needs one player to pressure the first defender and another to punish the next decision. Duke has several pair types, each with a distinct logic.

### Blackwell and Foster: pressure plus control

This is the cleanest veteran baseline. Blackwell can begin from a ball screen, a handoff, or an empty-side action with permission to score. Foster can space one pass away, lift behind a drive, or receive the ball on the second side. If opponents blitz Blackwell, Foster becomes the release valve who can organize four-on-three play or immediately enter a second action. If opponents switch, Foster’s presence lets Blackwell attack the mismatch without also carrying responsibility for every other player’s placement.

The pairing’s risk is insufficient downhill pressure if Blackwell settles for difficult jumpers and Foster remains purely connective. Duke should judge it by paint touches and forced rotations, not by how calmly it enters sets. Foster’s 2025–26 three-point accuracy helps spacing, but his 58.2-percent free-throw mark on 55 attempts makes him a provisional rather than automatic late-game ball security answer. One completed-season percentage is not destiny; it is a reason to verify who should receive intentional fouls and finish possessions.

### Blackwell and Boozer: scoring plus live-dribble passing

Boozer gives Blackwell a more overt passing partner. He can bring the ball up, use Blackwell as an off-ball threat, and reverse the relationship on the next possession. A promising structure would let Boozer initiate early offense, then flow into Blackwell attacking a tilted defense rather than a stationary one. In the half court, Blackwell can draw the strongest guard defender while Boozer probes the weaker matchup.

The defensive response will depend on Boozer’s scoring threat. If opponents go under his screens and load toward Blackwell, the pairing can become cramped. Duke would then need to use Boozer as a cutter, screener, or dribble-handoff hub rather than leaving him stationary above the break. His 81.3-percent free-throw shooting is a useful closing-game indicator, but late-game inclusion will also depend on whether defenses can ignore him before the foul occurs.

### Foster and Boozer: continuity without a guaranteed bend

This returning pair should understand Scheyer’s terminology and teammates’ timing better than any newcomer pairing. Their official combined 205 assists against 87 turnovers in 2025–26 establish a meaningful base of decision-making. But assist totals partly reflect a prior offense organized around Cameron Boozer’s gravity. The pair must show it can generate the first defensive rotation rather than merely deliver the ball to someone who already does.

Its best use may be to stabilize minutes when Blackwell rests, especially if a wing or center becomes the pressure point. A Patrick Ngongba II seal, a Dame Sarr cut, or a designed freshman-wing action can bend the defense; Foster and Boozer can then organize entries and continuations. Asking this pair to create every advantage from high ball screens would place the offense’s most uncertain task on players whose strongest evidence is connectivity.

### Rippey beside a veteran: speed with a safety rail

If Rippey’s downhill ability translates, he should not have to prove it first in five-out late-clock isolation. Pairing him with Foster or Boozer provides an organizer who can call the action and receive the ball back. Pairing him with Blackwell provides a scorer who occupies help and can rescue an empty drive. In either case, Rippey’s initial job can be narrow: advance after rebounds, turn the corner against a bent floor, and make the first simple help read.

That scaffolding is not an argument for permanently limiting him. It is a way to distinguish college-ready pressure from college-ready orchestration. If he consistently reaches the paint without losing the ball, Scheyer can expand his coverage reads and late-clock ownership. If he struggles against size, Duke can retain his transition value without allowing every freshman mistake to become a structural crisis.

## Actions that make distributed creation coherent

Personnel alone will not create the chain. Duke needs actions that exchange roles during the possession.

**Advance-and-return** can give Boozer, Foster, or Rippey the initial advance before Blackwell receives the ball on the move. The first guard does not disappear after passing; he cuts through, screens, or relocates so his defender cannot load toward Blackwell. This is a simple way to let an organizer initiate while a scorer creates.

**Guard-guard screening** can force switches before the main ball screen arrives. If Foster screens for Blackwell, the defense must decide whether to switch a larger or weaker guard onto the scorer. If Boozer or Rippey slips the screen, the second guard can catch with movement and attack a temporary gap. The action also prevents the non-initiating guard from becoming a spectator.

**Re-screen sequences with Ngongba** can make the center a pressure multiplier. The first screen reveals coverage; a quick re-screen changes the angle before help is fully organized. Blackwell can attack to score, while Foster or Boozer rotates behind the play as the next passer. If the defense traps, Ngongba’s short-roll decision becomes the bridge between first and second creation. This is distributed creation across positions, not merely among guards.

**Second-side handoffs** can turn a safe reversal into a new attack. When the first ball screen produces no shot, the ball should reach a guard moving into a handoff rather than standing above the arc. Foster’s shooting makes the defender’s route consequential. Boozer can use the handoff to reach a passing lane. Rippey can use it to gain a step without needing to beat a set defender from rest.

**Empty-corner and inverted actions** should be matchup tools, not the entire identity. Emptying a side gives Blackwell or Rippey a cleaner downhill read. Using a large wing or Scharnowski as the handler can make a guard the screener and force unusual coverage decisions. But every novelty must preserve the underlying sequence: someone forces help, someone reads it, and someone remains available when the defense takes away the first two options.

## Rotation rules are more important than minute forecasts

A summer minute projection would manufacture certainty, but several rotation rules can already be stated as testable hypotheses.

First, Duke should keep two credible initiators on the floor whenever possible. “Credible” does not mean two traditional point guards. Blackwell plus Foster qualifies; Boozer plus a wing who can create may qualify; a guard plus a passing frontcourt player may qualify. The rule exists to prevent pressure defense from erasing the offense by denying one player.

Second, every two-guard group needs a source of first advantage. Foster and Boozer may supply organization, but if neither bends the defense, their lineup needs Ngongba’s interior pressure, a dynamic wing, or Rippey’s burst. Conversely, a Blackwell-Rippey pairing could have ample pressure but need a low-error connector elsewhere.

Third, at least one guard must remain useful without the ball. Blackwell’s 96 made threes at Wisconsin and Foster’s 39.8-percent Duke shooting provide the clearest completed-season evidence. Boozer’s cutting and screening can compensate when the shot is not respected. Rippey’s off-ball value is unknown. A distributed system collapses if “sharing” merely alternates which guard stands idle.

Fourth, the closer must be named before the possession begins. This need not be the same player every night. Blackwell has the strongest scoring case; Foster has the strongest returning record of ball security; Boozer combines passing with the best 2025–26 free-throw percentage among that returning trio. Matchup and development can change the answer. What Duke cannot afford is spending the final eight seconds deciding who owns the shot.

## How to tell whether the system works

Traditional assists will not be enough. Duke’s evaluation should separate four events: who brought the ball into the action, who created the paint touch or double-team, who made the pass that extended the advantage, and who finished. The same possession may credit four different players. That is the point.

Early-season film should track whether Duke enters its first action before the defense is set; how often the first action produces a paint touch, two defenders on the ball, or a clean post catch; whether the second side attacks within roughly one beat; and which player receives the ball when the shot clock falls below eight. Turnovers should be classified rather than merely counted. An aggressive pass after forcing help is different from losing the ball before the offense begins.

Lineup evidence also matters. Does Blackwell’s efficiency hold when Foster initiates? Does Boozer create better shots after giving the ball up and getting it back? Can Rippey force elite defenders to retreat? Does a Foster-Boozer group survive without Blackwell? Can Duke maintain its creation structure against switching, trapping, and full-court pressure? Answers across opponent types will reveal more than a single hot shooting night.

The best outcome is not five players averaging similar assists. It is an offense with multiple entry points and a stable decision tree. Blackwell can be the leading scorer without becoming heliocentric. Foster can have fewer touches than a conventional senior point guard while organizing the most delicate possessions. Boozer can become more valuable by converting passing into pressure. Rippey can grow into responsibility through bounded tasks. Theodosiou can provide experienced contingency creation without needing a promised role.

The failure mode is equally clear: nominal balance without anyone compelling help. If Duke records clean passes around the perimeter but few paint touches, its distribution is cosmetic. If Blackwell must rescue every late clock, the roster has recreated heliocentrism by necessity. If several guards require the ball and none punish help away from it, depth becomes redundancy. The season’s central backcourt test is therefore not whether Duke has enough handlers. It is whether those handlers can create one continuous advantage together.

## Open questions

- Which guard creates Duke’s first reliable paint touch against switching defenses?
- Can Blackwell preserve his scoring efficiency while making faster passes against traps and nail help?
- Does Boozer’s sophomore scoring pressure grow enough to make his demonstrated passing more valuable?
- Is Foster fully available, and which on-ball and off-ball balance best uses his shooting and turnover control?
- How quickly can Rippey translate downhill pressure without becoming a turnover target against veteran guards?
- Who owns the final eight seconds, and does that hierarchy change by opponent or remain stable?
