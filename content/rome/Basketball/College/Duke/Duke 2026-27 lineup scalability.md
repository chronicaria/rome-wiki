Duke's deepest 2026–27 lineups will matter only if they can preserve creation, spacing, size, defensive containment, rim protection, and rebounding as opponents change the problem.

Up: [[Duke men's basketball 2026-27]] · Related: [[Duke 2026-27 role inventory]] · [[Jon Scheyer's tactical evolution at Duke]]

As of July 9, 2026, Duke has more plausible rotation players than verified five-man solutions. The program officially says five players return, three transfers arrive, and the nation's second-ranked recruiting class joins them. It names Cayden Boozer, Caleb Foster, Patrick Ngongba II, and Dame Sarr among the returners, but the live roster page still displays 2025–26. Independent reporting supplies the likely newcomers, while games have supplied no evidence at all about how those newcomers function together. A scalable-lineup analysis must therefore define the test before it declares the answer.

## Scalability is the ability to survive a change in the question

A lineup scales when its virtues remain useful against stronger, more varied opposition. Dominating an overmatched team through size is not proof that the same group can defend five shooters. Playing five skilled perimeter players is not proof that they can finish possessions against an elite offensive-rebounding frontcourt. The relevant question is not whether a group can succeed in one environment. It is how many high-level environments it can enter without surrendering a fatal weakness.

For Duke, six functions form the basic test. The lineup must create an advantage against a set defense; maintain enough width that the advantage has somewhere to go; contain the ball before the defense collapses; protect the rim when containment fails; end the possession with a rebound; and make the next decision quickly enough to exploit rotation. No lineup will maximize all six. Scalability is the art of keeping each above an acceptable floor while changing which one receives emphasis.

This distinction matters because the projected roster is modular. John Blackwell brings the clearest record of high-volume college scoring. Foster and Boozer bring Duke experience and secondary initiation. Ngongba supplies an established interior anchor. Sarr provides large-wing versatility. Drew Scharnowski is reported as a multipurpose frontcourt transfer, while freshmen such as Deron Rippey Jr., Cameron Williams, and Bryson Howard offer possible creation or wing upside. The pieces appear to cover every job. The hard part is placing five together without two solutions producing a new problem.

## The baseline: two handlers, one interior anchor, two large connectors

The most conservative scalable structure begins with two credible handlers, Ngongba at center, and two wings large enough to defend and rebound. It is a structure rather than a predicted starting five. Blackwell can provide first-option pressure; one of Foster, Boozer, or Rippey can organize the second side; Sarr or another large wing can defend across matchups; and Ngongba can screen, finish, rebound, and protect the paint.

The appeal is redundancy. Pressure on one handler does not end the possession. A trapped ball screen can flow into a second creator. A switch onto Ngongba can create a seal rather than merely reset the offense. Larger wings reduce the need to overhelp and give Duke more bodies capable of finishing the defensive possession.

The risk is shooting geometry. Two handlers are useful only if the off-ball handler is guarded. A large wing is useful only if his defender cannot camp in the lane. Ngongba's interior value increases when four teammates stretch help decisions; it decreases when multiple defenders can ignore the perimeter. Sarr shot 32.3 percent from three in the 2025–26 figures cited by CBS, which is evidence of an unresolved constraint, not a forecast that he will remain there. Boozer's value likewise depends on whether his scoring pressure makes his passing more dangerous. The baseline group scales only if at least four positions force honest defensive attention.

Defensively, this structure has a plausible floor. Foster, Boozer, and Sarr have program experience; Ngongba has protected the interior in meaningful games; Blackwell brings major-conference experience. But reputation cannot answer the central coverage question. If the guards cannot stay attached at the point of attack, Ngongba must step higher, the low defender must tag earlier, and a large wing's rebounding value is spent in rotation. The lineup's size then becomes recovery size rather than deterrence.

## The large lineup: winning the possession twice

Duke's frontcourt depth creates the possibility of playing Ngongba beside Scharnowski or another large forward. The purpose should not be to look imposing. It should be to create two specific advantages: survive the first defensive action without exposing the rim, then dominate the rebound that ends or extends the possession.

On defense, a second big can allow Ngongba to remain closer to the basket while a more mobile partner handles screening actions or tags rollers. On offense, two screeners can create high-low seals, second-side handoffs, and offensive-rebounding pressure. Scharnowski's reported 2.6 assists and 1.3 blocks per game at Belmont suggest the outline of a connector who can pass and provide some back-line value, though those figures do not establish ACC translation or a particular coverage.

The spacing cost is obvious. If neither frontcourt player commands a defender outside the lane, Blackwell's driving windows shrink and Boozer's paint-touch development becomes harder to evaluate. One answer is functional spacing rather than stationary shooting: a high-post passer, a short-roll decision, a corner exchange, or a screen that forces two defenders to occupy one action. Another is to stagger the bigs, using one as the active screener and the other along the baseline rather than placing both near the lane line.

That still must be proven against switching. An opponent may put a mobile forward on Ngongba, switch the initial action, and help from the least dangerous shooter. The large lineup then needs either a reliable post mismatch or rapid screening and cutting to punish the switch. If it cannot generate that punishment, the defensive rebounding gain may not repay the lost offensive width.

The correct early-season measure is not raw plus-minus. It is whether the group creates rim attempts, free throws, and offensive rebounds without an accompanying spike in turnovers or opponent transition. A two-big lineup that retrieves misses but cannot retreat after live-ball mistakes may win the glass and lose the game environment.

## The mobile-center lineup: exchange rim security for decision speed

Scharnowski or a developing young big could theoretically play center without Ngongba. That changes the lineup's operating system. A mobile or passing five can screen farther from the basket, facilitate handoffs, and create space for cutters. Four perimeter-capable teammates may allow Duke to attack a defense before it establishes help.

The trade is interior certainty. Scharnowski's Belmont statistics indicate broad contribution, but college levels and roles differ. Protecting the rim in limited minutes or a different league does not guarantee anchoring a Duke defense against elite size. The group may need stronger point-of-attack defense so the center is asked to contest rather than erase. It may also need gang rebounding, because pulling the five away from the rim improves offensive movement while removing a natural rebounder from prime position.

This lineup is most likely to scale against opponents that use mobile centers, switch frequently, or force traditional bigs into space. It is least likely to scale against a dominant post scorer or multiple elite offensive rebounders unless the wings provide extraordinary support. The staff should evaluate it as an exchange rate: how much extra creation and spacing does the mobile five produce for every unit of rim and rebounding security surrendered?

## The maximum-creation lineup: solve pressure before it starts

A group featuring three credible initiators—perhaps Blackwell with two among Foster, Boozer, and Rippey—could make Duke difficult to press or trap. The first ball handler would not have to win every matchup. Duke could reverse the ball, attack a tilted floor, and keep a creator in each corner of the possession.

This structure addresses the deepest uncertainty in a post-centerpiece team. Distributed creation succeeds when attacks are chained, not alternated. Blackwell might force the first rotation; Boozer could read the tag; Foster could attack the closeout; a wing could cut behind help. Three handlers increase the probability that the chain reaches a capable decision-maker.

They also make hierarchy harder. If every guard is comfortable initiating but only one consistently forces help, the offense may spend the clock exchanging control without changing the defense. Roles have to be asymmetrical: a primary attacker, a secondary organizer, and a connector who can shoot, cut, or defend without the ball. Those roles may change by matchup, but they cannot be undefined within a possession.

The defensive cost depends on the third guard's size and resistance. Sarr is officially listed by Duke as a guard/forward and offers a way to keep perimeter skill without becoming conventionally small. A freshman wing could eventually do the same. A three-small-guard version, however, may invite opponents to screen for a preferred matchup or post a weaker defender. Duke would then need aggressive switching, scram switches, or early help, each of which creates a rebounding or shooting consequence.

The maximum-creation group should be tested against full-court pressure, switching defenses, and late-clock possessions. Its success criterion is not total assists. It is whether Duke reaches an advantage earlier, maintains it through the second and third decision, and still fields enough defenders to avoid becoming a matchup target at the other end.

## The maximum-defense lineup: stops still require an exit

Duke can imagine a large defensive group built around Ngongba, Sarr, and the best available perimeter stoppers. Such a unit may switch more matchups, protect the rim, and rebound. It could be valuable after timeouts, late in halves, or against an opponent whose best player must be denied the ball.

But defense-only lineups often fail at the exit. A stop has value only if the rebound is secured and the resulting offensive possession can cross half court, create a quality attempt, and avoid feeding transition the other way. If the strongest defensive five includes two players opponents ignore, Duke may repeatedly defend for 25 seconds only to take a congested shot. That workload eventually degrades the defense it was designed to maximize.

The solution is not necessarily substituting a pure shooter. It may be ensuring that the defensive group contains one organizer, one advantage creator, and one interior release valve. Ngongba can be the release valve through seals or screens. Blackwell can be the creator if he holds up defensively. Foster or Boozer can stabilize entry. The remaining spots can emphasize defense as long as they cut and pass decisively.

This is why lineup labels should describe functions, not player archetypes. A “defensive” player who makes quick offensive decisions may scale better than a nominal two-way scorer who stops the ball. Likewise, a shooter who prevents a defender from helping may improve team defense indirectly by reducing live-ball turnovers and opponent transition.

## Closing lineups need a possession hierarchy

Closing is not the same problem as starting. Opponents switch more readily, hunt weaker defenders, deny first options, and manipulate foul and clock states. A closing group needs a clear first action, a counter, dependable free-throw handling, and no defender who can be attacked without response.

Blackwell's reported 19.1 points per game at Wisconsin make him the leading candidate to begin a difficult possession, but prior scoring volume does not automatically make him Duke's sole closer. A scalable close could use Blackwell to create the initial bend, Ngongba to screen or seal, and a returning guard to control the second action. Sarr or a freshman wing might close if his defensive size and offensive spacing pass the opponent-specific test.

The key is a hierarchy that does not become predictability. Teammates should know where the first advantage is expected, while the defense should not know which capable player finishes it. Duke's recent centerpiece teams could begin with a singular star. This team may need a rehearsed chain. If the first action fails, the ball should reach a second creator with enough time to act; if a switch creates a mismatch, the group should know whether to attack it immediately or flow into the counter.

Defensive substitutions can be useful, but repeated offense-defense exchanges are constrained by dead balls and can disrupt rebounding assignments. The ideal closer is not simply the five best names. It is the five-player group with the fewest ways to be made unplayable in the current game.

## A practical evidence ledger

Early lineup results will be noisy. Shooting variance, opponent quality, score state, and tiny samples can make a weak process look dominant or a strong process look broken. Duke should be evaluated through repeated mechanisms before net-rating language becomes persuasive.

For each meaningful group, the public ledger should track the initiating structure, number of credible shooters, center role, ball-screen answer, primary rebound assignments, and opponent type. It should then ask several possession-level questions. Did the lineup create a paint touch or force help? Did the next player act before the defense recovered? Could the guards contain the first action? Who tagged the roller, and who rebounded after the tag? Did the opponent hunt one defender? Did Duke's own miss lead to vulnerable transition?

Three to five games are a sensible minimum before calling a statistical direction a trend, and even that sample must be opponent-aware. A declared role change or repeated tactical pattern can be described sooner, but its consequences should remain provisional. Raw plus-minus should never carry the argument alone; it records what happened during a shared interval, not why it happened or which player caused it.

The nonconference schedule supplies unusually rich stress tests. Duke's official June 22 release says Michigan State, Illinois, UConn, Florida, Michigan, and Gonzaga were all top-three NCAA seeds and final-AP top-20 teams in 2026. Their 2026–27 identities will change, so last season's style cannot simply be copied forward. Still, the sequence gives Scheyer chances to test size, pressure response, travel, rebounding, and late-game adaptability before March. ## What would falsify the scalability thesis

The optimistic thesis is that roster depth allows Duke to change lineup shape without falling below the championship floor in any essential function. Several findings could falsify it.

If the large lineups cannot create width, their defense and rebounding will be too expensive. If smaller groups cannot finish possessions, their creation advantage will leak away on the glass. If Blackwell is the only player who reliably forces help, distributed creation will be descriptive language rather than an operating reality. If Ngongba must remain on the floor for both rim protection and offensive structure, Duke's apparent frontcourt optionality will narrow. If the best defenders are ignored offensively or the best scorers are hunted defensively, closing choices will become opponent-controlled.

The thesis strengthens if several different groups produce the same essentials by different means. A large group might win through rebounds and interior pressure; a smaller group through decision speed and spacing; a defensive group through containment and transition; a closing group through a stable creation chain. Diversity is valuable when it creates answers, not when it merely increases the number of combinations.

## Why it matters

Lineup scalability is the bridge between roster talent and tournament reliability. A six-game NCAA run presents different bodies, coverages, tempos, and whistle environments with little preparation time. A team built around only one favorable matchup can be excellent and still fragile. A team with several complete shapes can absorb an opponent's adjustment without abandoning its own standards.

For the 2026–27 Blue Devils, scalability is also the clearest way to evaluate Jon Scheyer's roster-construction bet. The group appears designed around continuity, transfer experience, and freshman optionality rather than one unquestioned organizing star. That design succeeds only if overlapping skills become complementary lineups. Depth on a roster sheet is insurance. Scalable depth is the ability to solve the next basketball problem without creating a larger one.
