Duke's 2026–27 ball-screen defense should be a small, connected menu chosen by handler, screener, spacing, and lineup—not a single universal coverage or a collection of surprise calls.

## The evidence boundary

As of 2026-07-10, Duke has not published a 2026–27 defensive playbook, coverage frequency, summer-practice report, or settled matchup chart. Its June 22 release says that five players return and specifically names Cayden Boozer, Caleb Foster, Patrick Ngongba II, and Dame Sarr among the returning scorers. It also says three transfers and the nation's second-ranked recruiting class join them. The public roster page still displays 2025–26. Those official facts establish continuity, turnover, and broad roster shape; they do not establish what Jon Scheyer has installed for the next season.

Scheyer has, however, described principles that make a bounded projection possible. In January 2025 he said Duke's defensive work centered on guarding the ball, protecting the paint, covering for one another, and improving five-man rotations. He also connected size to the ability to switch while warning that switches still have to end in post defense and box-outs. After Duke beat North Carolina in February 2025, he praised the team's talk and switching and called that game one that specifically demanded Maliq Brown. Those comments support a **personnel- and opponent-dependent** view of coverage, not a claim that switching is Duke's permanent default.

The completed 2025–26 totals provide another constraint. Duke held opponents to 63.6 points per game, .393 shooting, and .306 from three while posting a plus-11.0 rebound margin. Ngongba recorded 90 personal fouls in 701 minutes. Those are team outcomes and a center workload signal, not ball-screen tags. They support preserving an elite defensive chain and reducing unnecessary emergency contests; they cannot reveal how often Duke dropped, switched, showed, or trapped.

Accordingly, everything below is an **analytical framework, not a staff claim**. It proposes the smallest menu that could fit the verified roster functions, assigns each branch a purpose, and states what future game evidence would confirm or reject it.

## One menu, four decisions

Coverage names can create false precision. “Drop” is not one location, and “switch” is not complete when two defenders exchange assignments. Every call must answer four questions:

1. **Where should the ball go?** The point defender must influence the handler toward the intended boundary, screen, or helper.
2. **Who owns the roller?** The center, low man, or switching guard needs a defined responsibility before the screen lands.
3. **What pass is temporarily conceded?** Every coverage spends something: a pull-up, short roll, skip, seal, or pocket of space.
4. **How does the possession end?** A forced miss followed by a lost rebound is an incomplete coverage.

This makes communication the common skill. Foster, Boozer, and Sarr possess Duke continuity; Ngongba is the returning center with the most direct back-line responsibility. Newcomers may add speed, physicality, or mobility, but prior-school production cannot prove command of Duke's calls. The practical goal is not to teach every player every answer at equal depth. It is to establish a default, two credible changes, and an emergency answer that the five players on the floor can execute together.

## Coverage one: contain-and-recover drop

Drop should be the baseline candidate when Ngongba plays center, especially against handlers who do not force the big above the foul line. The on-ball defender chases over or trails according to the shooter's threat; Ngongba contains the drive and roller while retreating to keep both in view; the low man tags only as deeply as necessary; and the guard recovers onto the handler's back hip.

The value is role clarity. Ngongba remains closer to the rim, the weak side can stay nearer shooters, and Duke can preserve rebounding shape. It also protects against the lob if the guard stays involved. But “deep” cannot become passive. A handler who walks into an uncontested rhythm pull-up has dictated the possession. Ngongba's level should move with the matchup, screen angle, and clock: lower against a reluctant shooter, higher against a balanced pull-up threat, and more aggressive late in the clock when a short-roll pass has less time to develop.

The point defender determines whether drop works. If he dies on the screen, Ngongba guards a pull-up, drive, and roll simultaneously. The low man then commits early, a corner opens, and the center may foul while trying to erase the first mistake. This is why [[Duke 2026-27 point-of-attack defense]] treats screen survival as more valuable than steal totals. Drop is not a hiding place for weak containment; it is a partnership whose margin depends on rear pressure.

Drop should be rejected or raised when a handler repeatedly makes clean pull-ups, when a screener is a dangerous pop shooter, or when an empty corner removes the normal low tag. The adjustment need not be a new identity. Duke can change the guard's route, move Ngongba closer to the level, weak the action toward a boundary, or reassign the tag before abandoning the coverage entirely.

## Coverage two: switch selected matchups

Switching is the cleanest way to remove the immediate two-on-one, but it merely exchanges a ball-screen problem for matchup, post, and rebounding problems. Duke should switch when the two defenders can survive the resulting assignments and when the remaining three players understand the next likely attack.

Sarr's verified 6-foot-8 listing and a year in Duke's system make him a plausible switching piece against large initiators and guard-wing screens. Foster and Boozer bring returning guard size. Ngongba's ability to switch onto a creator for a full possession remains an open question, and no public 2026–27 evidence answers it. The useful distinction is therefore **one-through-four switching**, **late-clock switching**, and **center-involved switching**. Success in the first category cannot be assumed to prove the third.

The switch must be early and physical. A late call leaves both defenders with the handler and releases the screener. An upright guard concedes a deep seal to a center. A big who opens the wrong hip gives the handler a direct drive. Away from the ball, Duke must decide whether to stay home, dig at the mismatch, or scram a smaller defender out before the entry. Scheyer's public emphasis on five-man coverage applies exactly here: the visible exchange is only the beginning.

Rebounding is the decisive audit. Switching often pulls the center away from the rim and leaves a guard boxing out a larger roller. The nearest wing must crack back, the guard must make first contact, and somebody must secure the ball before leaking out. A contested miss plus an opponent putback should be coded as a failed possession even if the initial switch looked clean. This connects the menu directly to [[Duke 2026-27 defensive rebounding]].

Selected switching becomes especially useful late in the shot clock, when the offense has less time to enter the post or run a second action. It can also deny a favorite handler-screener partnership for a short stretch. It should not become an automatic response to every screen merely because a lineup looks long on paper.

## Coverage three: show-and-recover at the level

A mobile center or forward-at-five lineup may support a show, hedge, or brief two-to-the-ball appearance before the screener defender recovers. The purpose is to turn the handler sideways, delay the attack, and allow the point defender to reattach without permanently changing matchups.

This branch can punish a handler who depends on getting downhill immediately. It can also move a comfortable pull-up shooter away from his preferred spot. The cost is the space behind the showing big. The screener can short roll into a four-on-three; the weak-side low man must tag; and the next defender must split two shooters until the big recovers. A slow or decorative show is the worst of both worlds: it pulls the center from the rim without actually changing the handler's path.

Drew Scharnowski or another more mobile frontcourt option could make this coverage plausible, but that is a translation hypothesis, not a verified Duke role. His prior-school statistics cannot establish ACC recovery speed or Duke communication. The first tests are simple: does the show force the handler away from the middle, does the big recover before the short roller attacks, and does the rotation end with a rebound?

Show-and-recover also places a physical demand on the on-ball defender. He cannot assume the big has taken his assignment. He must get back in front or onto the rear hip, then communicate the big home. If the guard takes a wide route or stops pursuing, Duke remains with two defenders on the ball and one defender covering both roller and corner.

## Coverage four: blitz as a targeted changeup

A blitz commits both screen defenders to the handler long enough to force a pass, retreat dribble, or pickup. It is the highest-risk branch because the offense plays four-on-three if the ball escapes cleanly. Duke should reserve it for a defined reason: a handler with a vulnerable release, a late-clock possession, a sideline trap, an unexpected after-timeout change, or a lineup with enough length and anticipation behind the play.

The trap must take away vision rather than merely add bodies. The guard closes the escape route; the big arrives under control with high hands; the nearest defender protects the short roll; and the weak side zones two players until the rotation resolves. If the handler splits the trap or throws an uncontested pocket pass, the call has failed before the shot.

Blitzing can also protect a weaker individual matchup by preventing a star handler from choosing the attack. Yet repeated blitzes reveal the rotation and can expose a young back line. Duke's strong 2025–26 defensive results do not prove that the new roster can reproduce Maliq Brown's anticipation after his departure. The relevant 2026–27 evidence will be how quickly the third defender arrives and whether the fourth and fifth defenders exchange without losing the rim or corner.

The most useful outcome measure is not turnovers forced. A blitz that produces a rushed reset with eight seconds left has succeeded even without a steal. A blitz that produces a deflection but leaves the offense with an open corner three has not necessarily succeeded. Process grades should record release time, short-roll catch location, rotation integrity, shot quality, and rebound.

## Boundary call: weak or ice the side ball screen

Side ball screens deserve a boundary rule rather than an entirely separate system. Duke can “weak” the handler toward his less comfortable hand or ice the action by keeping the ball on the sideline and below the screen. The point defender jumps the screener's high side; the big contains the baseline lane; and the weak side prepares for the roller or drift pass.

The sideline acts as an extra defender, but the angle must be won before the screen. If the handler rejects cleanly into the middle, the entire coverage is behind the play. Empty-corner spacing also changes the help map: with no corner offensive player, the roller may have more space and the nearest tag may come from farther away. Duke should therefore make the call conditional on handler hand, screen angle, and weak-side personnel rather than using “ice” as a reflex.

This branch can fit any of the four main coverages. Duke can ice into a low contain, switch a re-screen, show at the sideline, or trap near the boundary. Its value is reducing the handler's choices before the screen, not adding terminology.

## Match the call to lineup and opponent

With Ngongba at center, a contain-and-recover base offers the clearest July projection. It keeps the returning rim protector near his strongest verified area and limits how often smaller defenders must solve post mismatches. Selected switches can change the picture late in the clock or against compatible screeners. Higher shows and blitzes should require evidence that the back line can rotate without driving up Ngongba's foul exposure.

With a more mobile center, Duke can move the screen defender higher or switch more. That potential comes with a glass tradeoff. If the coverage disrupts the first pass but scatters the rebounders, it has not improved the defense. A two-big group could place Ngongba near the rim while another frontcourt player handles a mobile screener, but skilled fours can invert that plan and drag both bigs into space.

Small or wing-at-five lineups should switch with a purpose: flatten the initial action, consume clock, and pressure the next pass. They cannot imitate Ngongba drop protection. All five must front or scram post mismatches, stunt without overhelping, and rebound down a position. Their success standard should be opponent points per complete possession, not the aesthetic cleanliness of the switch.

Opponent archetype matters as much as Duke personnel. A pull-up guard calls for a higher level or switch. A downhill passer may invite a lower contain that removes the lob. A popping center strains drop and tests communication on switches. A short-roll playmaker punishes blitzes. A weak shooter can be sent under until a made-shot sample or tactical adjustment forces change. The menu exists to remove an opponent's best branch while accepting the least damaging alternative.

## A possession-level November audit

The official nonconference schedule names Michigan State, Illinois, Connecticut, Florida, Michigan, Gonzaga, and Washington State, offering different early tests. Reputation should not pre-grade them. For each meaningful ball screen, chart:

- handler, screener, Duke defenders, and lineup;
- location and spacing: middle, side, empty corner, transition drag, or re-screen;
- called coverage when visually identifiable, otherwise “uncertain”;
- guard route and screen survival;
- big's level and first responsibility;
- first helper and first advantage conceded;
- shot quality, foul, turnover, or reset;
- whether Duke secured the rebound.

Four diagnostics matter more than raw points allowed. **Containment integrity** asks whether the handler reached the intended space. **Two-on-two integrity** asks whether the guard and big made one player guard two threats. **Rotation integrity** asks whether the first helper created an unguarded second pass. **Possession completion** asks whether Duke rebounded without avoidable fouling.

Samples should be split by coverage and opponent archetype, not pooled into one defensive grade. Ten drops against a non-shooting guard do not establish success against an elite pull-up creator. A late-clock switch is not comparable to an early-clock center switch. Unclear possessions should remain unclear; public video rarely reveals the call with certainty.

The evidence should also distinguish result from execution. A made contested pull-up can be a successful drop possession. A missed open corner three can expose a failed blitz rotation. A steal after two players collide may conceal a broken call. Repeated mechanisms across several strong opponents should drive any revision to the menu.

## What would falsify this framework

The projection that Ngongba lineups begin from contain-and-recover weakens if Duke consistently starts him at the level, switches him across multiple actions, or uses another center as the primary anchor. The projection that Sarr and the larger guards enable selected switching weakens if Duke protects them from screen actions or repeatedly scrams immediately. The mobile-center show hypothesis fails if the short roll routinely reaches the paint before recovery. The blitz changeup should be discarded if escape passes create clean four-on-three attacks more often than the pressure delays possessions.

More broadly, the menu fails if players cannot communicate its branches. A theoretically ideal call executed late is worse than a simpler call executed by all five. Duke's 2026–27 defense does not need maximum variety. It needs enough variety to prevent an elite handler from solving one picture all night, while preserving the verified program priorities of paint protection, collective coverage, and rebounding.

That is the central distinction: coverage versatility is not the number of calls available. It is the ability to change one answer without breaking the other four jobs.
