Duke’s deep 2026–27 roster should be projected by required basketball functions—advantage creation, spacing, connective play, point-of-attack defense, rim protection, and rebounding—rather than by a premature one-through-five depth chart.

## Why roles are more informative than positions

As of July 9, 2026, Duke has officially announced only the roster’s aggregate structure: five returners, three transfers, and the nation’s second-ranked freshman class. Independent reporting supplies a likely name-by-name group, but the official roster selector still displays 2025–26. Any minutes table would therefore manufacture precision twice—first by treating reported membership as a final official ledger, and then by converting summer reputation into exact playing time.

A role inventory asks a safer and more revealing question: what must a championship-level five-man unit be able to do, and which players have evidence that they can perform each task? Modern positions are bundles. A nominal guard may initiate, space, or defend a wing. A center may finish, pass, screen, or protect the rim. Duke’s roster is unusually rich in players who can straddle those bundles, which makes a rigid depth chart especially misleading.

The inventory below separates three evidence levels. “Demonstrated” means the player has performed the function in substantial college competition, though not necessarily at the same usage or against the same schedule. “Plausible” means prior role, physical profile, recruiting evidence, or limited college evidence supports the possibility. “Unknown” means public evidence reviewed here cannot justify the assignment. These labels are not grades and will change once Duke publishes the final roster and plays games.

## Primary advantage creation

Every high-level offense needs someone who can bend a set defense. The action may be a ball screen, isolation, post touch, transition attack, or designed mismatch, but the result must force a second defender to react. Duke lost Cameron Boozer’s frontcourt creation and Isaiah Evans’s shooting volume. The new roster distributes the replacement burden.

John Blackwell has the strongest demonstrated high-usage case. CBS reports 19.1 points per game at Wisconsin in 2025–26, along with 5.1 rebounds and 2.3 assists. That establishes that he can carry scoring volume in a major conference. It does not establish that he should monopolize Duke possessions. The useful test is whether he can generate efficient first advantages against elite switching and pressure while also functioning when Foster, Boozer, or Rippey starts the action.

Patrick Ngongba offers a different source of pressure. His 10.1 points in 21.9 minutes per game, reported by CBS, came through interior finishing and a developing all-around role. A stronger seal, short roll, or offensive rebound can force help just as surely as a guard drive. The constraint is that post and interior creation needs adequate perimeter spacing. Ngongba’s development becomes more valuable when Duke can punish the defender who leaves a shooter to crowd him.

Cayden Boozer and Caleb Foster have demonstrated college initiation, but neither has yet been publicly established as a high-volume primary scorer for this roster. Boozer’s 3.0 assists per game and expanded postseason role suggest organizational value. Foster’s experience makes him a plausible late-clock stabilizer. Deron Rippey Jr. arrives with the profile of a natural point guard, while Cameron Williams and Bryson Howard arrive with high-end scoring reputations. For each freshman, the primary-creation label remains plausible rather than demonstrated until college possessions show that the handle, decision speed, and finishing survive physical pressure.

The ideal outcome is not necessarily one permanent “alpha.” It could be a hierarchy with Blackwell as the default creator, Ngongba as an interior pressure point, and two or three guards or wings who can attack a tilted floor. The danger is committee offense: several capable handlers moving the ball without anyone consistently forcing rotation.

## Secondary initiation and connective passing

Deep teams become coherent when players who do not start the action can extend it. Secondary initiation includes attacking a closeout, making the next pick-and-roll read, feeding the post, advancing in transition, and relocating the ball before the defense resets.

Foster and Boozer are the clearest demonstrated guard connectors among the returners. Their experience beside higher-usage teammates matters because Blackwell’s arrival should not require either to become stationary. Foster can organize a possession and defend across guard sizes. Boozer’s passing can link transition and half-court play. Their development question is whether each can create enough direct scoring pressure that defenders honor the pass fake and close space.

Dame Sarr is a crucial bridge candidate. His 6-foot-8 frame allows Duke to keep size on the floor, and his prior starts establish that he can survive normal college rotation demands. Yet a large wing becomes an offensive multiplier only when he quickly reads the second side. If his shooting remains inconsistent, decisive cuts, handoffs, and extra passes are the mechanisms that prevent a defender from treating him as a place to help.

Drew Scharnowski’s Belmont line—10.7 points, 6.0 rebounds, and 2.6 assists according to CBS—suggests frontcourt connectivity. His assists are particularly relevant because a passing big can turn a handoff or short roll into a branching action. Translation questions remain: can he make the same reads against longer defenders, and can he defend well enough at center or shoot well enough beside Ngongba to keep that passing on the floor?

The most scalable Duke lineups will likely contain at least three competent connectors. This reduces turnover risk and prevents opponents from solving the offense by denying one guard. It also makes role acceptance easier: a player can meaningfully influence a possession without receiving an isolation.

## Shooting and spacing

Spacing is not a roster’s listed positions; it is the defense’s belief about what happens if it leaves a player. Duke needs enough credible shooting around Ngongba and its attacking guards to open the lane. The loss of Evans’s volume makes this a construction question, even if several individual percentages look adequate.

Blackwell’s shooting must be evaluated in role context: pull-up attempts and late-clock shots are harder than stationary catches. Foster has demonstrated catch-and-shoot utility, though his health and volume need current evidence. Sarr shot 32.3 percent from three as a freshman according to CBS. That is not so poor that improvement is impossible, nor so strong that defenses must already treat him as elite gravity. Boozer’s value will similarly depend on whether defenders can go under screens or help off him without cost.

The freshmen may change the calculation. Williams and Howard have scoring reputations, but recruiting rank cannot substitute for college shot selection and accuracy. Rippey’s fit beside another initiator improves if he commands respect away from the ball. Bigs such as Meyer or Boumtje Boumtje would create exceptional flexibility if they can space, but no such assumption should be made from size or highlights alone.

The key lineup test is not “How many shooters are on the roster?” It is “Can this five-man group keep both corners occupied, punish under coverage, and still rebound and defend?” A nominal four-shooter lineup may have weak gravity if opponents ignore two low-volume players. A three-shooter lineup may still work if Ngongba’s screening and Scharnowski’s passing repeatedly create layups. Game evidence must distinguish those cases.

## Point-of-attack defense and wing containment

Duke’s offensive depth will matter less if guards cannot keep the ball out of the paint. Point-of-attack defense includes screen navigation, lateral containment, early transition matching, and communicating switches. It is particularly important for lineups with a traditional center, because every clean blow-by forces Ngongba to choose between the ball and the rim.

Foster, Boozer, Sarr, and Blackwell all have the size to participate in versatile perimeter groups, but size is not a coverage. Sarr’s length may let him defend larger creators. Foster’s experience can stabilize assignments. Boozer’s sophomore development should be tested against quick lead guards. Blackwell’s offensive burden raises a familiar two-way question: can he take the hardest perimeter matchup without reducing his creation late in games?

Rippey’s path to early minutes may depend as much on defense as playmaking. Freshman guards often create offensive flashes before they consistently navigate screening angles and off-ball exchanges. Williams and Howard could give Duke large switchable lineups if they defend with discipline. Again, athletic reputation is only a prior. The first meaningful evidence will be whom Scheyer trusts against veteran nonconference guards and which mistakes trigger substitutions.

A useful rotation should include at least two credible ball containments in most lineups. One can be hidden through matchup selection; two weak points invite opponents to screen until they find the desired defender. Duke’s roster seems physically capable of avoiding that problem, but summer physical profiles cannot prove possession-level execution.

## Rim protection, screen defense, and defensive rebounding

Ngongba is the most established center candidate. His experience provides a baseline in drop coverage, switching decisions, vertical contests, and defensive rebounding. More minutes could increase his total impact, but only if foul rate, conditioning, and matchup flexibility permit. The correct developmental question is whether Duke can keep him on the floor across different opponent structures, not whether his per-minute production can be mechanically extrapolated.

Scharnowski offers a contrasting frontcourt option. His reported 1.3 blocks in 21.8 minutes at Belmont suggests some rim activity, while his passing may enable smaller, more mobile offensive groups. He could play beside Ngongba, replace him, or shift between roles depending on shooting and defensive evidence. Each arrangement changes responsibilities: a two-big group must defend in space and preserve the glass; a small-ball group must protect the rim collectively.

Meyer and Boumtje Boumtje add reported size, but young centers should not be assigned a defensive role on height alone. Rim protection requires arriving on time without fouling, controlling the rebound after the contest, and communicating before the drive. Wilkins, Sarr, Williams, and Howard may provide weak-side size or small-ball rebounding, yet those possibilities remain practice and game questions.

Rebounding deserves separate emphasis. Cameron Boozer’s departure removes 10.2 rebounds per game according to CBS. No single replacement has to reproduce that number, but Duke must replace the possessions. Ngongba’s minutes could rise; Blackwell is a strong rebounding guard by his reported 5.1 average; Scharnowski averaged 6.0. Large wings can finish the job by boxing out and collecting long misses. A modular roster becomes less flexible if every smaller lineup concedes second shots.

## Transition play and pace control

Depth can support faster play because more players can absorb high-intensity minutes. Boozer and Rippey should be capable of advancing the ball, while Blackwell and the freshman wings may attack unsettled defenses. Sarr’s size makes him a potential grab-and-go or lane-running threat. Ngongba can create early seals before the defense loads the paint.

But pace is not simply speed. The best teams choose when to attack and when to organize. Veteran guards Foster and Boozer can help Duke avoid turning depth into rushed possessions. Scharnowski’s outlet and connective passing may also matter. The useful measure will be the quality of early offense: rim attempts, open threes, and advantageous cross-matches, not merely a higher possession count.

## Provisional functional tiers

No exact minute projection is warranted, but a provisional functional map helps organize future evidence.

Blackwell is the leading demonstrated scoring creator. Ngongba is the leading demonstrated interior anchor. Foster, Boozer, and Sarr are returning two-way connectors with different development needs. Scharnowski is a demonstrated mid-major frontcourt contributor whose Duke role depends on translation. Rippey, Williams, and Howard are high-upside freshmen competing to supply creation, shooting, and perimeter defense. Wilkins is a redshirt development returner. Meyer and Boumtje Boumtje are young size prospects. Theodosiou is reported experienced guard depth whose official status and role need clarification.

This map intentionally allows overlap. Blackwell may be both creator and rebounder. Sarr may be both stopper and connector. Scharnowski may be both backup center and passing four. Rippey may earn a starting role or develop behind veterans. The categories describe routes to value, not ceilings.

## Rotation-building experiments worth tracking

The first experiment is a two-initiator baseline: Blackwell with one of Foster, Boozer, or Rippey. It tests whether Duke can maintain creation without overloading one guard. The second is a large wing shell featuring Sarr and one of Williams or Howard, which tests switchability and spacing. The third is Ngongba with Scharnowski, which tests whether frontcourt passing and rebounding outweigh possible spacing costs. The fourth is Scharnowski at center with four perimeter players, which tests pace and switchability against rim protection.

The fifth experiment is a veteran closing group. Duke’s deepest theoretical lineup is not necessarily its best final-five. Late-game basketball values trust, foul shooting, communication, and the ability to generate a shot against a defense that knows the play. Foster’s experience, Blackwell’s scoring, Boozer’s organization, Sarr’s size, and Ngongba’s interior presence form one plausible foundation, but freshmen or Scharnowski can displace any member by supplying a missing function.

These experiments should be logged as questions, not predictions. The nonconference schedule—Michigan State, Illinois, UConn, Florida, Michigan, and Gonzaga among the official high-level opponents—will stress different functions. A lineup that survives one matchup may fail another. Scalability means preserving enough core strengths while changing one or two jobs.

## Why it matters

### What evidence should change this inventory

The official roster publication should first resolve membership, class, and size. Preseason comments can clarify intended experiments, though coaches’ summer praise should not be treated as proof. Exhibitions can show lineup combinations and broad responsibilities but are weak efficiency samples. The first three to five competitive games can establish role allocation; the major November opponents can test whether those roles survive pressure.

Player-specific updates should be tied to observable tasks. For Blackwell: share of possessions initiated, rim pressure, passing against help, and off-ball shooting. For Ngongba: minutes, foul discipline, coverage range, and passing. For Foster and Boozer: on-ball burden, turnover control, and point-of-attack assignments. For Sarr: shooting gravity, closeout decisions, and large-wing defense. For freshmen: role stability against quality opponents, not isolated highlights.

The inventory should be revised when functions change, not after every box-score fluctuation. A declared starter is state. A new closing role sustained across several games is state. One hot shooting night is evidence, not a new identity.
