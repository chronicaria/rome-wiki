Duke's 2026–27 spacing problem is not whether the roster contains shooters, but whether its best creators, defenders, and rebounders can share the floor while forcing every help defender to pay a cost.

## The evidence boundary comes first

As of July 10, 2026, Duke's official roster page still displays the 2025–26 team. Duke has officially said that five players return, three transfers arrive, and a highly ranked freshman class joins them, and it names Cayden Boozer, Caleb Foster, Patrick Ngongba II, and Dame Sarr among the returners. Independent reporting supplies a plausible individual ledger. This article can therefore analyze the functions that reported players would need to provide, but it cannot present the summer roster as a final enrollment or availability record.

That caution is especially important with shooting. A percentage compresses different shots into one number. A stationary corner three created by a paint touch is not the same task as a pull-up against a switch with six seconds left. Volume matters because defenses may tolerate a good percentage from a player who rarely shoots. Release speed matters because a slow shooter may not punish a short closeout. Location matters because above-the-break range changes where a screener can operate. Passing and driving matter because a defender who runs a player off the line must still be punished.

Spacing is consequently a five-player relationship rather than an individual trait. A lineup can contain three respectable percentage shooters and still feel cramped if two players occupy the same interior corridor, one perimeter player is ignored, and nobody turns the resulting help into a second advantage. Another lineup can survive with only average shooting if its screeners, cutters, handlers, and offensive rebounders force defenders into costly choices. The useful July question is not “Who shot well?” It is “Which combinations can create and preserve space against an opponent that has scouted their limits?”

## What Duke must replace

The departures of Cameron Boozer and Isaiah Evans remove two different kinds of gravity. Cameron Boozer demanded attention through scoring, rebounding, post seals, and passing. His presence could pull a second defender toward the lane even when he did not take a perimeter shot. Evans supplied more conventional perimeter pressure: CBS's roster tracker reports that he made 36.1 percent of his threes in 2025–26 while nearly doubling his attempt volume from the prior season. Defenders had to account for where he stood before the ball arrived.

Replacing their points is not the same as replacing their spatial effects. John Blackwell may absorb scoring possessions, but a guard initiating a ball screen does not reproduce the weak-side gravity of a high-volume wing. Patrick Ngongba can create interior pressure, but two defenders can crowd him if adjacent players are treated as reluctant shooters. A deeper roster can distribute production and still produce worse geometry if the most trusted defensive lineup cannot stretch the floor.

Duke's returning nucleus gives Scheyer continuity with the offense's terminology and timing. It does not guarantee continuity of spacing. Players can know where to stand while opponents choose not to guard them. The first job of the new offense is to decide which players create space through shooting, which create it through movement or screening, and which lineups must compensate for a player whom defenses help away from.

## The three kinds of shooting Duke needs

The first need is **stationary gravity**. A player in the corner or one pass away must make a helper reluctant to tag Ngongba's roll, load against a drive, or dig at a post touch. The shot does not need to be self-created, but it must be taken quickly enough and often enough that the defense believes leaving is dangerous. Foster and Blackwell are plausible sources because their college experience gives opponents real film to scout. Sarr's reported 32.3 percent as a freshman is a development baseline, not proof of either failure or gravity. The freshmen remain projections until college volume exists.

The second need is **movement and relocation shooting**. Static spacing breaks down after the first action. A shooter who drifts from wing to corner behind a drive, lifts when a big rolls, or sprints into transition can extend an advantage without handling the ball. Evans's departure makes this job particularly important. If no one inherits it, Duke may create an initial paint touch only to find the kick-out target standing where a rotating defender can recover. Movement shooting also tests conditioning, footwork, and decision speed that a high-school percentage does not capture.

The third need is **on-ball shot creation**. Opponents will go under ball screens against handlers who cannot punish the gap, switch against players who cannot create separation, and load the lane late in the clock. Blackwell's 19.1 points per game at Wisconsin establish that he carried major-conference scoring volume, but the available roster tracker does not by itself divide his value into pull-ups, catches, rim attempts, and free throws. Duke must learn which parts scale when he shares touches. Foster, Boozer, and incoming guards may also take pull-ups, yet the correct standard is not whether they can make one. It is whether the shot is efficient enough, at enough volume, to alter coverage.

These categories overlap but should not be merged. A strong standstill shooter may not be comfortable sprinting off a handoff. A pull-up creator may have ordinary catch mechanics because he prefers rhythm dribbles. A freshman may arrive with deep range but require a slower release that high-major closeouts expose. Lineup construction improves when the staff knows which kind of shooting each action actually demands.

## Blackwell's gravity depends on role translation

Blackwell is the clearest reported candidate to organize Duke's scoring hierarchy. CBS reports 19.1 points, 5.1 rebounds, and 2.3 assists across 34 games for Wisconsin in 2025–26. Those numbers establish usage and production, not a complete shot-quality profile. At Duke, he may receive fewer total attempts but more efficient catches because other handlers and Ngongba draw attention. He may also face more targeted pressure if opponents regard him as the only proven high-volume perimeter scorer.

His highest-value spacing contribution may come without the first dribble. If Blackwell can command a hard closeout after Foster, Boozer, or a freshman initiates, Duke can use him as a second-side attacker rather than asking him to solve every loaded defense. A closeout against a credible shooter creates a choice: surrender the three, rotate and open a driving seam, or switch early and expose a mismatch elsewhere. Blackwell's rebounding also gives Duke a way to play smaller perimeter groups without entirely conceding the glass.

The failure mode is role congestion. If Blackwell needs the ball, another guard needs the ball, and a non-shooting big occupies the paint, the nominal creation depth can reduce usable space. The preseason test should therefore include possessions in which Blackwell starts off the ball, relocates after passing, and attacks a tilted defense. A successful transfer is not merely the same player reproducing Wisconsin's volume in a Duke uniform; it is a player preserving threat across more contexts.

## The returners determine the floor

Foster, Boozer, Sarr, and Ngongba are officially identified by Duke as returning members of the prior team's top six scorers. Their shared experience means Duke can build spacing rules around known habits rather than introduce every read from scratch.

Foster's value is that he can connect initiation and spacing. When another guard starts the action, he must remain dangerous enough that his defender cannot become a free helper. When he starts it, a credible pull-up discourages under coverage and a quick pass lets Duke attack the second side. His experience also makes him a candidate to stabilize late-clock possessions. The open question is current shot volume and health, not whether a summer reputation can be treated as evidence.

Boozer's 3.0 assists per game in CBS's tracker establish meaningful connective responsibility. His spatial value will rise if defenders cannot duck under his screens or abandon him after he gives up the ball. Even without elite shooting, he can punish help through cuts, quick re-screens, and immediate extra passes. But those alternatives work only when timed. A cut into Ngongba's roll corridor can make spacing worse; a delayed extra pass lets the defense reset. His sophomore development should therefore be read through decisions as much as percentage.

Sarr is the roster's clearest case for separating percentage from gravity. CBS reports 32.3 percent from three as a freshman. That number is neither a reason to declare him a non-shooter nor a reason to assume a leap. Opponents will consider attempt rate, location, and release confidence. If they help off him, Duke can use his 6-foot-8 frame as a cutter, screener, or offensive rebounder, but every alternative changes someone else's location. If he becomes a credible quick-trigger catch shooter, large lineups become much easier to build because he can provide wing defense without shrinking the floor.

Ngongba creates space by occupying defenders inside. A hard screen that forces two defenders toward the ball, a roll that pulls a low man into the lane, or a deep seal that demands a dig can generate open perimeter shots. Yet his interior value is conditional on the kick-out chain. If the first receiver hesitates or the next player is ignored, opponents can crowd him without paying. Duke should evaluate his passing after the catch alongside his scoring. A center who finds the weak-side shooter before the defense recovers changes spacing even without attempting a three.

## Transfers and freshmen change the lineup math

Drew Scharnowski's reported Belmont line—10.7 points, 6.0 rebounds, 2.6 assists, and 1.3 blocks in 21.8 minutes—suggests connective frontcourt value. His passing could create space through handoffs, short rolls, and quick reversals. Whether he can play beside Ngongba depends heavily on shooting behavior and defensive matchup. If neither big pulls a defender away from the lane, the pairing must compensate with screening angles, high-low passing, offensive rebounding, and three perimeter players whom opponents cannot ignore. If Scharnowski can operate at the arc as a real decision-maker, two-big lineups gain a branching hub.

The reported freshman class adds theoretical shooting and size, but recruiting rank is not a spacing metric. High-school defenses, three-point lines, teammates, and closeout speed differ. Cameron Williams, Deron Rippey Jr., Bryson Howard, and reported young bigs should be evaluated by repeatable college tasks: do they take the available catch without an unnecessary dip, relocate after the pass, recognize when a defender goes under, and attack a wild closeout without driving into the next helper? A prospect can be a gifted scorer and still need time to perform those jobs inside a five-man offense.

Freshmen can nevertheless solve a structural problem quickly if one earns trust as a two-way wing. A large player who defends, rebounds, and commands a closeout lets Scheyer keep size without sacrificing a driving lane. The competition should not be framed as which freshman scores the most in early exhibitions. It is which one preserves the most lineup functions at once.

## Four lineup spacing tests

The **single-big, four-perimeter group** is the cleanest baseline. Ngongba or Scharnowski screens and rolls while Blackwell shares the perimeter with three guards or wings. The group should create driving lanes, but only if the fourth perimeter player is guarded. Its defensive and rebounding cost must also be measured. Spacing that produces one extra open three but concedes repeated offensive rebounds is not automatically scalable.

The **two-big group** tests whether passing and physicality can substitute for a fifth shooter. Ngongba and Scharnowski might screen for one another, work high-low actions, or place one big in a handoff hub while the other seals. The weak-side perimeter players must stay mobile because a stationary alignment lets both big defenders protect the lane. This group could punish small opponents on the glass, but elite switching teams may compress it unless one big forces respect outside.

The **three-guard creation group** places Blackwell with two of Foster, Boozer, or Rippey. Its promise is that every closeout becomes another initiation. Its risk is that all three prefer the ball and none supplies enough off-ball gravity. The diagnostic possession begins after the first pass: does the first handler relocate, screen, or stand? Can the second handler attack without the other two drifting toward the ball?

The **large-wing group** pairs Sarr with one or more freshman wings. It offers switchability and rebounding, but its offensive viability depends on quick decisions. If the wings hesitate, defenders can shrink the paint. If they shoot, cut, or swing immediately, length becomes an offensive advantage because Duke can see and pass over rotations. This group may be essential against the large nonconference opponents on [[Duke 2026-27 schedule map]].

## How to measure spacing once games begin

Three-point percentage should be recorded, but it should not lead the evaluation. The first layer is attempt behavior: catch-and-shoot attempts per opportunity, location, release speed, and whether players turn down clean shots. The second is coverage response: who is helped off, which ball handlers see under coverage, and which screeners draw two defenders. The third is possession consequence: paint touches, rim attempts, turnovers caused by congestion, offensive rebounds, and the quality of shots created after the first rotation.

Samples must remain bounded. One hot game does not create gravity if opponents continue to ignore the player. One cold game does not erase it if defenders still attach to him and open the lane. Three to five games can begin to show behavior, but opponent quality and scheme matter. Duke's early sequence against Michigan State, Illinois, UConn, and Florida should reveal whether spacing mechanisms survive length, physicality, scouting, neutral courts, and a true road environment.

Film notation can make the evaluation falsifiable. For every half-court possession, record the nominal alignment, the defender who first leaves his assignment, the offensive response, and the final advantage. That method distinguishes a missed open three from a broken spacing possession: the former may reflect shooting variance; the latter may reflect a player standing in the wrong window or failing to make the next read.

## Why it matters

Duke's depth becomes championship depth only when its best players fit together. Shooting is the bridge between talent collection and functional lineups. It determines whether Ngongba receives single coverage, whether Blackwell attacks tilted floors, whether large defensive groups can remain playable, and whether Scheyer can change personnel without rebuilding the offense each time.

The encouraging case is that Duke has several routes to adequate space: veteran guards, a high-usage transfer, large wings with development potential, a passing transfer big, and freshmen who may add shotmaking. The caution is that each route remains conditional. Prior percentages must translate to new attempts, young players must make fast reads, and the lineups that shoot best must still defend and rebound.

The season should therefore treat spacing as an evolving map rather than a preseason grade. Every game can update who commands respect, which pairings produce congestion, and which compensating actions work. The answer may not be five shooters. It may be four credible threats and one elite interior pressure point, or three shooters plus screening, cutting, passing, and offensive rebounding that make helping expensive. What matters is whether the defense is forced to choose—and whether Duke recognizes the choice quickly enough to punish it.
