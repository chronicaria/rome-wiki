Four contrasting Duke cases show why player development must be calibrated against age, role, opportunity, competition, and selection rather than inferred from a scoring jump or draft result.

Up: [[Duke men's basketball 2026-27]]

[[Duke player development and NBA translation framework]] establishes the measurement problem: a player arrives with prior ability, enters a particular roster, receives a role, and keeps developing after he leaves. This note applies that framework to four deliberately different cases from Jon Scheyer's first four seasons. It is not a ranking of players or a causal estimate of Duke's coaching effect. It is a set of controls—examples that expose what a loose development claim would miss.

The cases are Tyrese Proctor, whose three-year Duke career shows role continuity and gradual skill consolidation; Isaiah Evans, whose second-year expansion separated shooting percentage from shooting responsibility; Patrick Ngongba II, whose larger role tested whether elite finishing could survive more varied work; and Sion James, whose transfer season demonstrates development through role compression. Together they make a better calibration set than four lottery freshmen because they vary the starting point and the task.

## What a calibration case must control

A useful before-and-after comparison needs at least five controls.

First is **age and prior experience**. A third-year guard should normally process college defenses faster than a freshman because he has seen more possessions, even if no single coaching intervention can be isolated. Second is **opportunity**. Minutes can rise because a starter leaves, because the player improved, or both. Third is **role difficulty**. Maintaining efficiency while moving from occasional spot-up attempts to scouted movement shooting can be progress even when percentage falls. Fourth is **team context**. Better spacing and passing can create cleaner shots; weaker spacing can depress the same player's outcomes. Fifth is **selection**. Duke recruits prospects already likely to improve and sends its best performers to the NBA quickly. Counting only successes exaggerates any program effect.

Official box scores can establish minutes, attempts, assists, turnovers, rebounds, and outcomes. They usually cannot establish why a change occurred. Possession review, coach testimony, and opponent context are needed for mechanism. The cases below therefore use official statistics to bound the claim and treat tactical explanations as interpretations to be tested.

## Tyrese Proctor: development without a dramatic box-score leap

Proctor is valuable because he stayed three seasons and occupied versions of the same broad job: organize possessions, create some advantages, space the floor, and defend guards. He did not follow the easiest development narrative, in which points rise monotonically and a prospect becomes a different player every winter. His Duke record instead asks whether a player's decision quality and role reliability can improve while his basic statistical shape remains recognizable.

As a freshman in 2022–23, Proctor entered a young, large roster whose offense often ran through Kyle Filipowski and Jeremy Roach. The immediate question was not whether Proctor could post a star scoring average. It was whether he could help move Duke from initial organization to a usable advantage against ACC pressure. A freshman guard's mistakes occur at high leverage: a late entry, a loose retreat dribble, or a missed weak-side read can consume the possession even if it never becomes a turnover.

By 2024–25, the official season totals show Proctor starting 38 games, averaging 29.9 minutes and 12.4 points, shooting .405 from three, and recording 83 assists against 57 turnovers. Those numbers describe a credible high-level guard but not a heliocentric creator. That distinction is the calibration. Cooper Flagg carried unusual creation responsibility, while Kon Knueppel and Sion James could keep an advantage moving. Proctor's value was partly that Duke did not need him to dominate every possession. He could supply the first action, punish help with shooting, defend, and return to the ball later.

A lazy evaluation could call the absence of a huge scoring explosion stagnation. An equally lazy positive evaluation could point to a deep tournament run and declare that Duke “developed a winner.” The better claim is narrower: across repeated college seasons, Proctor accumulated evidence of functional versatility and lower-friction decision-making inside increasingly talented lineups. To attribute a specific improvement, the film record should show a repeated change—for example, earlier recognition of a tag defender or better balance entering pull-ups—not merely the team's success.

Proctor also illustrates survivor and opportunity effects. A guard who remains at Duke for three seasons receives far more observable college repetitions than a one-year lottery prospect. That does not mean the program failed to develop the freshman or uniquely developed the returner. It means confidence in the description is greater because the sample and range of contexts are larger.

## Isaiah Evans: percentage fell while responsibility rose

Evans is the cleanest warning against treating shooting percentage as the skill itself. Duke's official 2024–25 totals show him making 62 of 149 threes, .416, in 13.8 minutes per game with three starts. In 2025–26 he started all 38 games, averaged 28.2 minutes and 15.0 points, and made 101 of 280 threes, .361. His three-point percentage fell 5.5 points while his makes, attempts, minutes, starts, and scoring responsibility expanded sharply.

Which season is better evidence of shooting? The freshman percentage is evidence that Evans converted a high share of a smaller set of attempts. The sophomore volume is evidence that Duke and opponents treated the shot as a central function over a complete season. Neither alone tells the full story. The relevant possession questions are whether he took more attempts on the move, against tighter closeouts, earlier in actions, or after creating separation; whether misses clustered against particular opponent types; and whether his gravity opened actions for teammates.

Volume changes the defensive contract. An occasional shooter may receive attempts because a defender helps elsewhere. A featured shooter is scouted, chased, top-locked, and forced to put the ball on the floor. Even if the release is mechanically unchanged, the decisions surrounding it become harder. The second-year assignment can therefore reveal development in conditioning, relocation, shot preparation, and closeout response that percentage alone obscures.

Evans's case also controls for opportunity. The departures from the 2024–25 team removed enormous usage and shooting. More minutes were available. His ability to fill them is evidence of readiness, but the vacancy itself is not a skill. A careful note must separate “Duke needed more Evans” from “Evans became capable of more.” The overlap is the meaningful part: he converted available responsibility into 101 made threes while staying on the floor for more than twice as many minutes per game.

The next layer requires film. Did he defend well enough to preserve those minutes? Did his assist and turnover decisions improve when defenders ran him off the line? Did his shot quality decline because of harder attempts or because the offense generated fewer clean ones? Until those questions are classified, the responsible conclusion is that his role and demonstrated shooting volume grew; the precise size and cause of underlying skill growth remain uncertain.

## Patrick Ngongba II: efficiency under a broader task

Ngongba offers the interior counterpart. In 2024–25 he averaged 10.5 minutes and 3.9 points, shooting .719 from the field. In 2025–26 he averaged 21.9 minutes and 10.1 points, shooting .606. His attempts nearly tripled, and the official totals recorded 64 assists against 43 turnovers in the larger role. The raw field-goal percentage declined, yet .606 on expanded work remained highly efficient.

For a low-minute center, an extreme field-goal percentage may reflect a narrow shot diet: dunks, rolls, dump-offs, and putbacks created by others. Those are valuable skills, not statistical tricks. Screening, timing, hands, positioning, and finishing convert team creation into points. But a larger role asks whether the center can preserve those functions while adding touches, facing scouting, defending without fouling, and making decisions away from the rim.

The assist total does not prove that Ngongba became a hub. It does show that his offensive record cannot be reduced to 188 field-goal attempts. To identify development, analysts should classify elbow handoffs, short-roll passes, post reads, and immediate kickouts. A pass that keeps the offense on schedule may be a meaningful improvement even if it does not produce an assist. Conversely, a higher assist average could simply reflect more minutes and better shooters.

His case also exposes the interaction between physical availability and skill evidence. Centers face foul, fatigue, and contact constraints that can cap their repetitions. Doubling minutes is not just a larger statistical sample; it tests whether screening force, rim running, defensive positioning, and rebounding persist through longer shifts and more opponent adjustments. A decline from an unsustainably clean finishing diet should not automatically be called regression.

For 2026–27, Ngongba should be evaluated against the expanded sophomore baseline, not against the .719 freshman percentage. The falsifiable questions are functional: can he remain a high-efficiency finisher while handling more passing responsibility, protect the rim without losing rebounding position, and stay playable against teams that pull a center away from the basket? Those tasks connect development directly to [[Duke 2026-27 frontcourt combinations]].

## Sion James: transfer development through subtraction

James prevents the calibration set from becoming only a returner study. He arrived with extensive Tulane experience, then averaged 8.6 points, 4.2 rebounds, and 2.9 assists while shooting .516 and starting 32 games for Duke in 2024–25. A points-per-game comparison with his prior role would miss the purpose of the transition.

A veteran transfer on a roster with Flagg, Knueppel, Proctor, and Khaman Maluach did not need to reproduce his former possession share. He needed to compress his game without erasing its strongest functions. That meant making fast connective decisions, guarding physically demanding assignments, finishing advantageous possessions, and avoiding the temptation to reclaim usage merely because he had once handled it.

Role compression can be developmental because subtraction is a skill. A player learns which prior actions survive when teammates are better, spacing changes, and every opponent has size. He may score less while becoming more legible to a professional team that would never ask him to be a primary college scorer. The causal boundary remains important: Duke supplied the new environment, but James arrived with years of accumulated skill and experience. His season is evidence of successful translation into a narrower role, not proof that Duke created the underlying abilities.

The James case also warns against evidence-free leadership claims. Age and starts do not reveal private locker-room impact. Public evaluation can credit observable communication, assignment difficulty, minutes in trusted lineups, and direct testimony. It should not convert “veteran” into an all-purpose explanation for team coherence.

## What the four cases allow us to say

Together, the cases support four bounded conclusions.

First, role expansion is evidence only when paired with maintained function. Evans and Ngongba did not merely receive minutes; they continued to convert shots efficiently enough to sustain much larger assignments. Second, declining percentage can coexist with development when volume and difficulty rise. Third, stable scoring can coexist with development when decisions, shooting gravity, defense, or reliability improve. Fourth, a smaller role can represent successful transfer integration when it preserves a player's most portable value.

They do not establish a Duke development effect relative to other programs. That would require a comparison group matched on age, recruiting profile, prior production, role, health, and professional opportunity. It would also need to include players whose Duke roles stalled, not only visible successes. A serious program-level estimate might compare multi-year guards and bigs across peer programs, but public box-score data would still struggle to measure defensive decisions and coaching exposure.

The cases therefore function as diagnostic checks for future notes. If an article says a player improved, ask which task improved. If it cites percentage, ask how attempts and attention changed. If it cites minutes, ask what vacancy appeared. If it cites draft position, ask what the player already was on arrival. If it cites team wins, ask what individual mechanism connected the player to those wins.

## Why it matters for 2026–27

The current Duke roster requires this discipline because its organizing roles are unsettled in July. Returners may gain responsibility, transfers may lose usage, and freshmen may initially contribute through narrow functions. Those are different developmental paths and should not be graded with one statistic.

For each player, the committee should preserve a dated baseline before games begin: likely first function, limiting skill, role competition, and the first observable test. The baseline must then survive into later revisions so prediction is not silently rewritten as hindsight. Three-to-five-game samples can identify possible role changes; stronger trend language should wait for repeated evidence and opponent context.

This method also improves tactical analysis. Player development changes which lineups Scheyer can use. A wing who makes faster closeout decisions can unlock large groups. A center who passes from the short roll can punish pressure. A guard who becomes reliable off the ball can share the floor with another initiator. The value lies not in declaring that a player “made a leap,” but in showing which new team solution became available.
