Duke can preserve spacing, rim protection, and rebounding in 2026–27 by treating Patrick Ngongba II as the safest single-big anchor, Drew Scharnowski as a change-of-geometry five, and every two-big lineup as a conditional tool whose viability depends on real shooting gravity and perimeter containment.

## The frontcourt problem is three problems at once

As of July 9, 2026, Duke has announced the architecture of its new roster but has not published a complete official 2026–27 player page. Duke says five players return, three transfers arrive, and the incoming recruiting class ranks second nationally. Its June 22 release names Patrick Ngongba II among four returning top-six scorers but does not identify the transfers or freshmen individually. Reputable reporting identifies Drew Scharnowski as a Belmont transfer and Maxime Meyer and Joaquim Boumtje Boumtje as incoming young bigs. Those reported identities support lineup hypotheses, not declarations about enrollment, availability, or roles.

Within that boundary, Duke's frontcourt question is unusually clear. A useful combination must do three jobs that can pull against one another. It must create or preserve offensive space, deter or contest shots at the rim, and finish defensive possessions with a rebound. Adding height can help the second and third jobs while hurting the first. Removing a center can widen the lane while making every perimeter mistake more expensive. The best answer is therefore unlikely to be one permanent pairing.

The completed 2025–26 season supplies a demanding baseline. Duke's official statistics show a 35–3 team that outrebounded opponents 40.2 to 29.3 per game, held opponents to .393 shooting, and allowed only 63.6 points per game. That team also lost Cameron Boozer, whose official line included 10.2 rebounds per game, and Isaiah Evans, who made 101 threes. The new group must replace both possession completion and perimeter gravity. It cannot assume that returning size alone recreates the old balance.

This note evaluates combinations rather than producing a depth chart. “Spacing” means more than a listed three-point percentage: it includes screening, passing, cutting, and whether a defender feels free to abandon his assignment. “Rim protection” includes positioning and deterrence as well as blocks. “Rebounding” includes box-outs and team possession completion, not merely who collects the ball. Public box scores can establish prior production; the claims about how players might fit together are projections to be tested.

## Ngongba as the single-big anchor is the safest baseline

The most evidence-backed starting structure is Ngongba with four perimeter players. Duke lists him at 6-foot-11 and 250 pounds on its still-current 2025–26 roster. Its official season totals show 10.1 points, 5.8 rebounds, 2.0 assists, and 1.1 blocks in 21.9 minutes per game. He shot .606 from the field, collected 70 offensive rebounds in 32 games, and committed 90 fouls. These facts establish efficient interior production, real work on the offensive glass, some passing responsibility, and a foul load that constrained his minutes. They do not prove that he can simply scale the same rates to 30 minutes.

Offensively, a single-big Ngongba group offers the cleanest division of space. He can screen for John Blackwell, Caleb Foster, Cayden Boozer, or another handler, then roll, seal, or make a short pass while the other three players occupy the arc and cutting lanes. The spacing benefit does not require Ngongba himself to become a volume shooter. It requires the surrounding four to punish help and Ngongba to convert the advantages created by his screen. His 64 assists against 43 turnovers in 2025–26 are encouraging evidence that he was more than a terminal finisher, but they are not a complete short-roll scouting report.

This structure also places Duke's strongest established interior body closest to the basket. If the guards contain the ball, Ngongba can play a relatively bounded job: manage the screen action, stay connected to the roller, contest vertically, and rebound. If the first defender dies on every screen, he must repeatedly choose between a pull-up, a lob, and a weak-side rotation. No center can erase that geometry. The perimeter shell is therefore part of the rim-protection plan, not a separate unit.

Rebounding is the principal concern. Ngongba's 5.8 rebounds came in fewer than 22 minutes, but Duke loses Boozer's 389 total rebounds. The four perimeter players cannot release automatically in transition. Blackwell's reported 5.1 rebounds per game at Wisconsin and Dame Sarr's 3.8 at Duke suggest possible support, while larger wings could add length. Yet the decisive evidence will be behavior: does the weak-side wing hit a body, do guards pursue long misses, and does Duke control the ball after Ngongba leaves his man to contest?

The most balanced projected version pairs Ngongba with three credible shooters or quick decision-makers and one large wing. Blackwell supplies creation and guard rebounding. Foster's official 39-for-98 three-point season (.398) offers demonstrated catch-and-shoot value, although his low free-throw percentage and prior foot injury belong to other role questions. Sarr, Cameron Williams, Bryson Howard, or another large wing can supply defensive size if that player commands enough offensive respect. Cayden Boozer can organize the group, but his official .303 three-point percentage means a Boozer-Sarr-Ngongba trio would need cutting and screening to keep defenders from shrinking the lane. It may work; it should not be called spaced merely because three players stand outside.

## Scharnowski at center changes the geometry

Scharnowski's most interesting projected role is not “backup Ngongba.” It is a center who can alter how the offense connects. CBS Sports reports that he averaged 10.7 points, 6.0 rebounds, 2.6 assists, and 1.3 blocks in 21.8 minutes at Belmont. Those numbers describe a productive mid-major frontcourt player with passing and rim activity. They do not establish that the same defense or efficiency translates against ACC length, nor do they establish reliable perimeter gravity.

At center, Scharnowski could operate through dribble handoffs, delay actions, slips, and short-roll passes. A big who can catch above the foul line and find cutters makes the center's defender defend decisions rather than simply wait at the rim. With four perimeter players, Duke could use Blackwell's scoring pressure while preserving a second source of playmaking behind the first line. This is spacing by connectivity: the paint opens because the ball moves and the defense must honor multiple cuts, not necessarily because five players are elite shooters.

The defensive exchange is harder. Scharnowski's reported blocks provide evidence of rim events, but blocks per game do not tell whether he can anchor drop coverage, absorb contact without fouling, or deter drives he never has to contest. At Belmont, the opponent size, pace, and spacing menu differed from Duke's November and ACC tests. A Scharnowski-at-five group therefore needs stronger point-of-attack containment and more collective rebounding than a Ngongba group. Its advantage may be mobility and passing; its risk is asking a connector to become a full-time back-line eraser.

The best use could be opponent-specific. Against a slower center who struggles away from the rim, Scharnowski can pull the matchup into handoffs and screening actions. Against a five-out offense, his mobility may help Duke avoid placing Ngongba in repeated long recoveries. Against elite offensive-rebounding size, however, the group must prove it can finish possessions. Duke should compare not only points scored during these minutes but also opponent rim attempts, fouls drawn, offensive rebounds conceded, and how often help rotations force corner threes.

## Ngongba and Scharnowski together can work only if one becomes a spacer

The Ngongba-Scharnowski pairing is the clearest route to maximum veteran frontcourt size. It could place two rebounders near the action, preserve an experienced rim protector, and use Scharnowski's passing as a bridge between the post and perimeter. It could also congest every drive and give an opponent a comfortable defender to station in help.

There are several ways to create functional space without pretending both bigs are established shooters. Scharnowski can screen for Ngongba before flowing into a handoff. Ngongba can occupy the dunker spot while Scharnowski catches at the elbow. Either can screen away for a wing, forcing both defenders to communicate. High-low passing can punish fronting. Offensive rebounding can compensate for somewhat worse initial shot quality. All of those mechanisms are real possibilities, but none creates gravity automatically. If an opponent simply backs away from Scharnowski and crowds Ngongba, Duke needs a repeatable answer.

The 2025–26 statistics also caution against projecting Ngongba as the spacing big. He attempted 31 threes and made eight (.258). The attempts matter because they show the shot existed in his role; the percentage and low volume do not support treating it as a present strength. Summer improvement is possible, but the evidentiary threshold should be opponent behavior over competitive games, not an isolated make or practice report.

Defensively, the pairing's logic is stronger. Ngongba can guard the primary center and remain the main rim anchor. Scharnowski can take a less stationary frontcourt assignment, rebound from the weak side, and supply a second contest when Ngongba is drawn into ball-screen action. The pairing may also protect Ngongba from some direct physical burden. But two bigs do not guarantee good defense. If neither can stay with a skilled four, Duke may concede open threes or force guards into long rotations. If both chase the same block, the rebound can still go to the offense.

The two-big group therefore projects as a situational weapon rather than the default closing lineup. It is most plausible against opponents that play two interior threats, dominate the offensive glass, or cannot punish a slower four. It is least plausible against teams with four shooters and a dynamic pick-and-roll guard. To earn broader use, it must pass four tests: one big draws a defender outside the restricted area; both make fast passing decisions; Scharnowski can defend a college four or Duke can change coverages around him; and the rebounding advantage is large enough to justify any loss of pace or driving room.

## Large-wing lineups are the likeliest compromise

Duke may preserve the three required functions most often by playing one true big with a large wing rather than two centers. Sarr is the only such bridge with a completed Duke season. Official totals list him at 6-foot-8 with 6.4 points and 3.8 rebounds per game, 21 made threes on 65 attempts (.323), and 20 blocks. The profile suggests length and some weak-side defensive activity, while the shooting remains unresolved as genuine gravity.

A Ngongba-Sarr frontcourt can keep a traditional rim anchor, assign Sarr to larger wings, and ask four players to rebound. On offense, its success depends on what Sarr does when ignored. A credible catch-and-shoot improvement would be the simplest answer. Timely cuts, screening, handoffs, and immediate closeout attacks can also punish help. Standing stationary while his defender digs at Ngongba cannot.

Williams, Howard, or Sebastian Wilkins might offer another large-wing answer, but current public evidence is thinner. Recruiting reputation and physical measurements are priors, not college production. A freshman who shoots but misses rotations may improve spacing while damaging rim protection. A rangy defender who is ignored offensively may reproduce the same compression problem. Their early frontcourt minutes should be evaluated through role execution: shooting willingness, decision speed, box-outs, weak-side positioning, and the ability to defend without fouling.

Scharnowski can also fill the large-wing slot beside Ngongba, but that is the two-big problem in another name unless he defends fours and pulls his defender away from the paint. Position labels do not solve geometry. Opponent behavior does.

## Small-ball lineups trade deterrence for pressure

Duke can plausibly play without Ngongba or another traditional center by using Scharnowski or a large wing at five. A true wing-at-five unit would maximize speed, switching, and driving room. It could place Blackwell with four players capable of handling or attacking a closeout, then use aggressive perimeter defense to prevent the ball from reaching the rim cleanly.

That structure should remain a short-burst projection until Duke proves the glass. Small lineups protect the rim by changing where and when the attack occurs: switching away the initial advantage, digging at post entries, rotating early, or forcing turnovers. They do not remove the need for a final contest and rebound. Against a powerful offensive-rebounding team, the lineup might produce a good first defensive action and still lose the possession.

The relevant comparison is net exchange, not whether small ball looks faster. Does added spacing produce more rim attempts and open threes than the lineup concedes through opponent rim pressure and second shots? Can Duke switch without creating an automatic post mismatch? Can a large wing box out the opposing five while a guard retrieves the ball? These questions make small ball a matchup tool, especially when trailing or facing five-out offense, rather than an assumed endgame solution.

## Meyer and Boumtje Boumtje should be treated as developmental variables

Independent roster trackers identify Meyer and Boumtje Boumtje as young frontcourt additions, but Duke's accessible official roster had not confirmed the complete 2026–27 ledger by the as-of date. It would therefore be irresponsible to assign either a rotation or a specialized skill. Height, recruiting rank, and highlight clips cannot establish college rim protection.

For either young big, the first trustworthy indicators are mundane: screen angle, sprint consistency, communication, verticality, foul avoidance, and whether a box-out ends with team possession. Offensive spacing can come from clean screens, quick rolls, and passing even before a three-point shot becomes credible. Defensive value can come from correct positioning even without a block. If one becomes ready sooner than expected, Duke gains injury insurance and can keep a true center on the floor through foul trouble. If not, the roster's veteran Ngongba-Scharnowski axis prevents development from becoming emergency dependence.

## A provisional combination hierarchy

The highest-floor combination is **Ngongba plus a large wing and three guards/wings with credible shooting or rapid decision-making**. It keeps Duke's best established rim anchor, gives the defense size at four, and can rebound collectively. Its swing skill is the large wing's gravity.

The best change-of-pace combination is **Scharnowski at five with four perimeter players**. It can increase passing, movement, and matchup flexibility. Its swing skills are Scharnowski's ACC rim defense and the group's defensive rebounding.

The highest-rebounding experimental combination is **Ngongba plus Scharnowski**. It may punish large opponents and create high-low or handoff offense. Its swing skills are frontcourt spacing and Scharnowski's defense against fours.

The maximum-spacing emergency combination is **a wing or mobile Scharnowski at five with four creators/shooters**. It can switch and pressure the rim offensively. Its swing skills are every form of interior defense: post survival, help timing, fouling, and rebounding.

The hierarchy is provisional because the official roster, health, and competitive evidence are incomplete. It is not a predicted rotation. Scheyer's most scalable solution may change by opponent and game state, and the closing group need not be the starting group.

## What should be measured once games begin

Raw plus-minus will be too noisy to settle these questions early. The Duke desk should log each frontcourt combination by opponent type and measure the mechanisms beneath the result.

For spacing, track whether defenders go under screens, leave the nominal four to help, or keep both feet in the paint; the quality of shots after a post touch or short roll; and whether Duke produces rim attempts without surrendering transition balance. Three-point percentage matters, but a small early sample can disguise whether the defense respected the shooter.

For rim protection, track opponent attempts at the rim, shooting fouls, lob concessions, and rotations that force a kick-out. Blocks are events, not the full job. A possession in which Ngongba deters a drive and a guard concedes a late-clock jumper is successful interior defense even if Ngongba records nothing.

For rebounding, separate Ngongba or Scharnowski's individual totals from team completion. Record opponent offensive rebounds after the center contests, rebounds lost to unboxed weak-side crashers, and whether smaller groups compensate by forcing harder initial shots. Offensive rebounding should also be weighed against transition defense; sending both bigs can create second chances while exposing the other end.

Finally, track foul sustainability. Ngongba's 90 fouls in 701 minutes—about 5.1 per 40 minutes—are the clearest numerical reason not to equate strong per-minute production with a guaranteed larger role. This calculation comes from Duke's official totals; it is descriptive, not a forecast. If his positioning and conditioning improve enough to preserve defensive impact without fouling, every other frontcourt choice becomes easier. If foul trouble persists, Scharnowski or a young big must absorb genuine anchor minutes rather than optional experiments.

## Bottom line

Duke does not need one pairing to maximize spacing, rim protection, and rebounding at all times. It needs a dependable baseline and credible counters. Ngongba as the lone center offers the strongest established baseline because he combines efficient finishing, offensive rebounding, passing growth, size, and prior Duke defensive experience. Scharnowski offers the most interesting counter because his passing can widen offensive possibilities, but his rim defense and rebounding must translate. Playing them together raises Duke's physical ceiling while placing the greatest burden on spacing. Playing smaller raises offensive and switching possibilities while distributing rim protection and rebounding across all five players.

The decisive variable may be the nominal power forward. If Sarr or another large wing becomes a respected shooter, fast connector, and reliable rebounder, Duke can remain big without becoming cramped. If no large wing supplies that bridge, Scheyer will have to choose more explicitly between Ngongba's interior floor and a smaller group's offensive width. The roster appears to contain several possible answers. July evidence does not yet establish which one survives elite competition.

## Open questions

- Does Scharnowski command enough perimeter respect to play beside Ngongba against a switching defense?
- Can Ngongba reduce his foul rate while sustaining his defense over a larger minute load?
- Which large wing becomes the best bridge among shooting gravity, weak-side rim help, and defensive rebounding?
- Can a Scharnowski-at-five group protect the rim and glass against elite high-major size?
- Will Meyer or Boumtje Boumtje be officially listed and ready to supply normal rotation minutes, or will their first season be primarily developmental?
- Which combination closes against a five-out opponent, and which closes when Duke must secure one defensive rebound?
