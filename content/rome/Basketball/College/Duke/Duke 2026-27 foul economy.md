Duke’s 2026–27 foul economy will be the two-sided exchange between pressure created, free-throw value converted, fouls conceded, and the availability preserved for its most important players.

## The exchange, not the whistle count

As of July 10, 2026, Duke has not played a 2026–27 possession, and its official roster page has not yet supplied a final name-by-name roster. The completed 2025–26 Duke ledger nevertheless establishes a strong team baseline: Duke attempted 840 free throws while opponents attempted 507, made 612 while opponents made 361, committed 588 personal fouls while opponents committed 689, and played 38 games. Those are unusually favorable raw differentials. They do not by themselves identify which actions drew the fouls, when Duke reached the bonus, which calls were offensive, or which defenders caused the underlying breakdown.

“Foul economy” is therefore broader than free-throw margin. On offense it includes the ability to force legal defenders into losing choices, earn efficient free throws, place an opponent in the bonus, and preserve aggression without accumulating charges or illegal screens. On defense it includes denying the rim without gifting points, choosing when physicality is worth the risk, and keeping essential players available. A foul can be productive, neutral, or destructive depending on the possession state. Stopping an uncontested layup without creating an and-one may be rational; reaching 35 feet from the basket is usually not. An offensive foul costs both a possession and a personal foul. A shooting foul against an elite free-throw shooter may turn a difficult attempt into highly efficient offense.

The 2026–27 question is not whether Duke can “win fouls.” It is whether the roster can preserve the prior team’s pressure advantage after losing the two players who took nearly half its free throws, while reducing the foul exposure of the centers most likely to anchor the defense.

## A reproducible 2025–26 baseline

Duke’s official totals permit four simple rates. Offensive free-throw-attempt rate, defined here as free-throw attempts divided by field-goal attempts, was

$$840/2{,}198=0.382.$$

The opponent rate was

$$507/2{,}225=0.228.$$

This is not a possession rate and should not be read as the share of trips that ended at the line. One shooting foul can produce one, two, or three attempts; an and-one adds an attempt to a made field goal; bonus administration also matters. The ratio is useful because it scales foul pressure to shot volume using public totals. Duke generated approximately 38.2 free-throw attempts per 100 field-goal attempts and allowed 22.8, a favorable difference of 15.4 attempts per 100 field-goal attempts.

The raw scoring result was similarly large. Duke made 251 more free throws than its opponents, a difference of 6.6 points per game before accounting for any downstream bonus or availability effects. Duke’s opponents committed 101 more personal fouls, or 2.7 more per game. These figures describe a team that attacked more than it bailed opponents out. They do not prove that foul differential caused the 35–3 record, and they should not be isolated from Duke’s shooting, rebounding, turnover, and defensive advantages.

The distinction between attempts and conversion matters. Duke shot .729 at the line; opponents shot .712. Duke’s advantage came primarily from creating 333 more attempts, not from a dramatic accuracy edge. That is important for projection. Free-throw pressure is a repeatable offensive function only if new players can recreate the rim attacks, interior seals, offensive rebounds, closeout drives, and late-game ball responsibility that produced it. The departed Cameron Boozer attempted 280 free throws and Isaiah Evans attempted 114. Together they accounted for 394 of Duke’s 840 attempts, 46.9 percent. Simply subtracting their attempts would be misleading because their possessions will be redistributed, but the concentration identifies a real replacement problem.

The returning totals are uneven. Patrick Ngongba II attempted 131 free throws and made 87 (.664), the strongest returning volume. Cayden Boozer made 61 of 75 (.813). Dame Sarr made 33 of 62 (.532), and Caleb Foster made 32 of 55 (.582). Those figures are facts about completed attempts, not stable forecasts: roles, health, shot mix, and sample size will change. They do show that Duke’s established returners combine meaningful foul pressure with substantial conversion uncertainty. A team can reach the line frequently and still fail to harvest the full value.

## The rules shape the economy

The NCAA’s 2025–26 and 2026–27 men’s rules cycle matters because the basic environment is scheduled to carry into this season. The NCAA’s approved continuous-motion change permits an offensive player who ends his dribble while going toward the basket and absorbs contact to complete the pivot or step he is on to finish the shooting motion. This widens the circumstances in which contact during a genuine attack can yield a counted basket or shooting free throws relative to the prior, more restrictive interpretation. It rewards players who maintain a coherent finishing action through contact; it does not turn every bump after a dribble into a shooting foul.

The NCAA also retained the two-half structure for 2026–27. In May 2026, its men’s rules committees said a possible move to quarters remained under study and specifically identified the resetting of team fouls as a strategic issue. That means a four-quarter reset should not be smuggled into a Duke forecast. Under the current men’s structure, team-foul accumulation across each half changes the value of later common fouls, so the time and identity of early fouls matter even when they produce no immediate free throws.

Other foul types should remain separated in analysis. A player-control foul ends Duke’s possession and charges the player; it is not merely a missed opportunity to draw a defensive foul. A Flagrant 1 brings two free throws and possession to the offended team, which makes reckless contact much more expensive than the personal-foul column alone indicates. Technical and dead-ball events also distort any attempt to infer shooting pressure directly from aggregate free-throw attempts. The public cumulative ledger is the starting account, not a complete officiating taxonomy.

## Replacing offensive pressure

The most credible newcomer pressure source is John Blackwell. Wisconsin’s official 2025–26 ledger credits him with 183 free-throw attempts and 158 makes, an .863 rate. That combination is valuable in two different ways. The attempt volume establishes that he repeatedly reached the line in a high-major role; the accuracy makes fouling him an expensive defensive answer and supports late-game ball responsibility when opponents foul intentionally. His shooting also creates a plausible mechanism: defenders who chase him over screens or close hard to the arc can expose driving lanes.

The inference must remain bounded. Blackwell’s Wisconsin usage will not automatically transfer to Duke. Sharing creation with Foster, Boozer, Ngongba, and incoming guards may reduce his attempts even if his efficiency improves. Conversely, a better-spaced second side may let him attack more moving defenders and draw cleaner fouls. The relevant early evidence is not whether he repeats 183 attempts. It is whether his foul pressure survives across on-ball and off-ball roles: ball-screen drives, closeout attacks, transition advances, cuts, and late-clock possessions.

Ngongba supplies the clearest returning interior route. His 131 attempts in 701 minutes indicate frequent contact relative to his playing time. Post seals, rolls, dump-offs, and offensive rebounds can all force a defender to contest from poor position. Yet his .664 conversion reduced the payoff, and a larger offensive role cannot be considered separately from his defensive foul burden. Using him to create contact is attractive only if the extra physical work does not accelerate fatigue or reduce his availability at the other end.

Cayden Boozer’s .813 mark gives Duke a returning reliable converter on lower volume. The developmental question is whether he can raise pressure without losing connective value. More downhill attacks may yield free throws, but forcing drives into established help can also produce charges and turnovers. Foster and Sarr have a different burden: their completed percentages do not justify treating a trip to the line as an automatic optimal outcome. They should still attack advantages—passing up a layup merely to avoid free throws would be irrational—but late-game lineup and ball-placement choices must account for actual conversion evidence.

Drew Scharnowski’s Belmont ledger creates the sharpest fit tension. He attempted 140 free throws in 656 minutes, strong evidence that his interior game generated contact, but made only 61 (.436). A defender may prefer fouling him to conceding a finish, especially late. Duke can still profit from his pressure through opponent foul accumulation, offensive organization, and successful makes, but his free-throw result changes closing offense and two-big spacing decisions. Improvement is possible; preseason hope is not evidence of it.

## Defensive foul sustainability is a lineup resource

Duke committed only 588 personal fouls in 38 games, 15.5 per game, while opponents committed 18.1. The team result was excellent. The individual distribution still exposes the central 2026–27 constraint. Ngongba recorded 90 fouls in 701 minutes, approximately 5.14 per 40 minutes. Scharnowski recorded 73 in 656 minutes at Belmont, approximately 4.45 per 40. These rates do not predict disqualification in a new role, but they warn against multiplying either player’s production into a larger minute load without an availability adjustment.

The cause matters. A center foul can begin at the point of attack. If a guard loses the handler cleanly, the big may confront the ball and roller at once; a late rotation then appears in the box score as the center’s foul. [[Duke 2026-27 point-of-attack defense]] therefore belongs inside foul analysis. Better screen navigation and containment can lower back-line exposure even if Ngongba’s individual technique does not change. The reverse is also true: a lower foul rate produced by passive rim defense would not be progress.

Film review should divide big-man fouls into at least six mechanisms: late help, failed verticality, reach after losing position, moving-screen offense, rebound displacement, and coverage-specific contact away from the rim. Each implies a different repair. Better footwork may solve late position. Clearer guard-big communication may solve coverage hesitation. Screening angles and stillness may solve offensive fouls. Telling a player simply to “be less aggressive” can remove the rim protection or screen force that made him valuable.

Guard and wing fouls require the same discipline. Perimeter defenders can crowd without purpose, grab after being screened, reach from behind, or arrive late as secondary defenders. Large wings may absorb difficult matchups legally and thereby save the center from emergency decisions. Freshmen may have the physical tools to pressure the ball but not yet the college timing to do so without accumulating fouls. The first-month question is which players can apply force while preserving the shell, not which player records the most steals.

## Bonus timing and opponent targeting

Aggregate fouls conceal timing. Four defensive fouls in the first six minutes of a half can change how aggressively Duke guards for the next fourteen. The same four spread across a half may have little strategic effect. Duke should log the time of every team foul, the player’s foul number, the immediate penalty, and the next several opponent actions. Opponents may deliberately attack a defender carrying two first-half fouls or four late fouls, even if the attack does not immediately draw another.

Opponent targeting creates a lineup tax. If Ngongba acquires two early fouls, Scheyer may turn to Scharnowski or an unproven young center. If both experienced bigs have elevated rates, the nominal depth does not guarantee stable rim defense. A two-big lineup can provide rebounding insurance while putting both primary centers at risk simultaneously. Conversely, staggering them can preserve an anchor but may limit experiments in passing and size. Foul state should therefore be recorded alongside every lineup evaluation; a group used only because the preferred center had three fouls is not evidence of an equal preseason hierarchy.

On offense, bonus awareness should influence action selection without making Duke predictable. Once an opponent’s key defender or team enters a vulnerable state, repeated screening can force that defender to navigate contact and make decisions. Posting a smaller defender, involving a foul-prone big in ball screens, and sending cutters through physical gaps can create legal pressure. Hunting a whistle rather than completing the play is the failure mode. The offense should force a difficult defensive choice and remain prepared to score when no call comes.

## Fact, inference, and forecast

The **facts** are the completed ledgers and current rules environment. Duke created a 333-attempt and 251-made-free-throw advantage in 2025–26. Its opponents committed 101 more personal fouls. Blackwell arrives with a reported Duke transfer identity and an official Wisconsin record of high-volume, high-accuracy foul shooting. Ngongba and Scharnowski each produced useful frontcourt offense while recording more than four fouls per 40 minutes in their prior environments. The returning Duke free-throw percentages range widely.

The strongest **inferences** are structural. Duke has a plausible perimeter replacement for some departed foul pressure in Blackwell and an established interior source in Ngongba. It also has an availability problem concentrated at center and a conversion problem in several plausible lineups. Better perimeter containment could improve center foul sustainability. Better spacing could improve offensive foul pressure. Neither mechanism is established for 2026–27 before games are played.

The restrained **forecast** is that Duke can remain favorable in foul economy without matching the extraordinary prior raw differential. Blackwell’s pressure and accuracy, Ngongba’s interior contact, Boozer’s conversion, and roster depth provide credible paths. But losing Cameron Boozer’s 280 attempts is material, and lineups featuring low-percentage foul shooters may invite different defensive choices. The defense’s ceiling also depends on whether Ngongba can preserve verticality and availability across a larger role. A preseason prediction of another plus-333 attempt margin would be false precision.

## A 2026–27 foul dashboard

Duke’s public-season review should retain the raw basics: free throws attempted and made, opponent attempts and makes, personal fouls, and foul differential. It should add free-throw-attempt rate for both teams, free-throw points per estimated possession, and player fouls per 40 minutes. None should be used alone.

Possession coding should add:

- foul type and whether it was offensive or defensive;
- shooting, bonus, technical, flagrant, or no-immediate-shot consequence;
- action and court location;
- primary breakdown and helper involved;
- player foul state and team foul state;
- whether a basket was prevented, counted, or conceded as an and-one;
- whether the foul changed the next lineup or coverage;
- free-throw shooter and conversion.

The dashboard should distinguish process from whistle variance. A clean rim attempt through body contact may be good offense even without a call. A defender who establishes legal position and is still called for a block may have executed the intended technique. Over a larger sample, Duke should ask whether it repeatedly creates advantageous contact, avoids disadvantageous contact, and keeps its best combinations available. Complaining about isolated calls supplies no transferable answer.

Four checkpoints deserve special attention. After the first exhibition, identify who owns rim pressure and which centers can defend without repeated emergency contact. After the first five games, compare lineups by foul type rather than raw count. After the high-level November and December games, test whether Blackwell’s pressure and Ngongba’s foul sustainability survive elite size and switching. After six ACC games, assess bonus timing, opponent targeting, and whether any free-throw percentage change has enough attempts to be meaningful.

The best Duke foul economy would not merely repeat a large margin. It would create contact through real advantages, convert enough of the resulting attempts to punish tactical fouling, keep opponents defending under bonus pressure, and preserve Ngongba and the other essential defenders for closing possessions. That is a connected basketball system. The 2025–26 totals show what a favorable exchange looks like; the 2026–27 roster must build its own mechanisms.
